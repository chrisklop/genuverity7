TL;DR

FALSE — Fabricated without evidence

Instagram accounts spread baseless claim that Buc-ee's CEO donated $1 million to ICE. Viral posts reached 283,000+ likes before company denial. No evidence exists in FEC filings, public records, or government contracts. Real $1M donation went to Texas flood relief. Hoax emerged during anti-ICE boycott wave, exploiting legitimate activism.

Executive Summary

In late January 2026, a completely fabricated claim spread across social media alleging that Buc-ee's CEO Arch "Beaver" Aplin III donated $1 million to U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE). [1] The hoax originated from Instagram accounts @floridafoos and @mexicanworldstarfoo, which posted the claim without any sources, citations, or evidence. [2] One viral Instagram post garnered more than 283,000 likes, while another accumulated over 90,000 likes, sparking widespread backlash and calls for boycotts. [3]

Buc-ee's General Counsel Jeff Nadalo swiftly denied the claim: "The circulating rumors about Beaver donating to ICE are entirely false. Neither the company nor anyone associated with Buc-ee's has made any donations to ICE." [4] An exhaustive search of public records, FEC filings, and government databases revealed no evidence supporting the allegation. [5] The hoax emerged during intense anti-ICE activism following fatal shootings in Minneapolis, exploiting legitimate boycott campaigns targeting companies with actual ICE contracts. [6]

Origin: The Birth of a Hoax

The hoax originated from two Instagram accounts: @floridafoos and @mexicanworldstarfoo. These accounts posted claims that Arch "Beaver" Aplin III "reportedly donated $1 million to support ICE operations" without providing any footnote, source, link, or evidence for the allegation. [2] The accounts simply fabricated the claim and called for a boycott of Buc-ee's.

@floridafoos describes itself as the "#1 Source For News, Music, & Viral Clips" despite providing zero documentation for its explosive allegation. [1] The posts went viral in late January 2026, spreading across Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, and X (Twitter) within days.

One Instagram post reached more than 283,000 likes and nearly 13,000 comments, while another accumulated over 90,000 likes. [3] Facebook posts approached nearly 1,000 likes each by February 4. [3] The combination of a polarizing political issue, a beloved brand, a specific dollar amount, and heightened anti-ICE activism created perfect conditions for rapid spread before fact-checking could catch up.

The Perfect Storm: Political Context

The hoax emerged during the most intense period of anti-ICE activism in recent U.S. history. On January 7, 2026, ICE agent Jonathan Ross fatally shot Renée Nicole Macklin Good, a 37-year-old American woman, in Minneapolis. [6] On January 24, Alex Pretti was shot multiple times and killed by Customs and Border Protection agents. [13]

These deaths sparked massive "National Shutdown" and "ICE OUT" strikes on January 30–31, 2026, with hundreds of demonstrations nationwide. Activists called for "no school, no work, no shopping" to protest Trump administration immigration policies. [14] Over 300 "ICE Out of Everywhere" protests were organized across the U.S. [16]

In Minneapolis, businesses closed, students walked out of classes, and Bruce Springsteen performed at a Concert of Solidarity and Resistance at First Avenue. [14] Hundreds of protesters braved frigid winter temperatures, chanting "ICE out!" in major cities including Los Angeles, San Francisco, and New York City. [15]

What Buc-ee's Actually Donated

Buc-ee's did donate $1 million in 2025—but to flood relief, not ICE. In August 2025, Arch "Beaver" Aplin III presented the donation on NBC's Today Show to the Community Foundation of the Texas Hill Country for relief efforts after devastating July 4 floods that killed over 130 people. [9] [10]

According to Al Roker, who joined Aplin in presenting the checks, it was "the biggest donation ever seen in the history of the segment's 23 years." [10] Buc-ee's also partnered with country musician Robert Earl Keen for a benefit concert on August 28, 2025, that raised $3 million for flood victims. [21] The Community Foundation ultimately raised $60 million through its Kerr County Flood Relief Fund. [22]

The hoax gained plausibility because Arch Aplin does have a documented history of major political donations to Texas Republicans. Nonprofit government transparency group OpenSecrets tracked nearly 100 donations made by Aplin and Buc-ee's executives to politicians across a nearly 30-year timeline from 1996 to 2024. [12]

Aplin's biggest donations went to Gov. Greg Abbott ($575,000) and Lt. Gov. Dan Patrick ($250,000). [11] In 2021 alone, just the first 6 months, Arch Aplin donated $485,000 to Republicans in Texas, including another $250,000 to Greg Abbott. [11] Other recipients included Attorney General Ken Paxton, Rep. Randy Weber, Gov. Ron DeSantis, and even former President Barack Obama in 2004. [12]

By targeting a CEO with known conservative political donations during a period of heightened ICE enforcement under a Republican administration, the hoax creators exploited the plausibility gap—the claim felt believable even without evidence.

Evidence of Falsehood

Official Company Denial

Buc-ee's General Counsel Jeff Nadalo issued multiple statements categorically denying the claim:

"The circulating rumors about Beaver donating to ICE are entirely false. Neither the company nor anyone associated with Buc-ee's has made any donations to ICE."

[4]

Despite the massive traction the claims received, "the origin of the controversy remains a mystery. An exhaustive search of public records and social media trends failed to identify a specific catalyst or piece of evidence that triggered the allegations." [5]

Key Record Searches Conducted:

  • FEC (Federal Election Commission) filings: No ICE donations
  • Transparency USA (Texas state donations): No ICE donations
  • OpenSecrets donor database: No ICE donations [12]
  • Government contract databases: No Buc-ee's contracts with ICE
  • Corporate SEC filings: Buc-ee's is privately held, but political/agency donations are public record—none found

Buc-ee's remains a privately held entity. Aplin co-owns the company with Don Wasek, and the pair operates the brand without outside investors. This private structure means the company is not required to disclose the same level of financial data as public corporations, though political or agency-level donations are typically a matter of public record. [4] No such ICE donation appears in any public record.

Why This Matters

Weaponization of Legitimate Activism: The hoax hijacked real corporate accountability campaigns targeting companies like Palantir ($30M ImmigrationOS contract), Microsoft ($20M ICE contract), and Amazon (AWS cloud infrastructure). By fabricating a claim against Buc-ee's, the hoax creators diluted the credibility of legitimate boycott movements.

Economic Harm Potential: With 283,000+ likes and widespread calls for boycotts, the hoax posed real economic threats to Buc-ee's, a privately held company that employs thousands across Texas and the Southern U.S.

Erosion of Information Integrity: The hoax demonstrated how quickly fabricated claims can achieve viral velocity in politically charged environments, outpacing fact-checking and official denials. The combination of a beloved brand, a polarizing issue, and a specific dollar amount created perfect conditions for misinformation spread.

Exploitation of Tragedy: The deaths of Renée Good and Alex Pretti sparked legitimate grief and activism. The Buc-ee's hoax exploited this tragedy to spread misinformation for unclear motives—whether clout-chasing, political sabotage, or simple trolling.

Key Findings
  • The Claim is Fabricated: No evidence exists in public records, FEC filings, government contracts, or corporate disclosures that Buc-ee's or Arch Aplin donated to ICE.
  • Patient Zero Identified: Instagram accounts @floridafoos and @mexicanworldstarfoo originated the claim without sources or evidence.
  • Massive Viral Spread: One post reached 283,000+ likes; another 90,000+ likes; spread across Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, X.
  • Official Denial: Buc-ee's General Counsel Jeff Nadalo categorically denied the claim multiple times.
  • Real Donation Confused: Buc-ee's donated $1 million to Texas Hill Country flood relief (August 2025), not ICE.

  • Real Political Donations Exist: Aplin donated $575,000 to Greg Abbott, $250,000 to Dan Patrick, nearly 100 political donations over 30 years—but ZERO to ICE.
