Executive Summary

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost of Chicago, elected May 8, 2025 as the first American pontiff in the 2,000-year history of the Catholic Church — has become the single most impersonated and misinformation-targeted religious figure in digital history. Within the first year of his pontificate, Snopes alone documented at least 18 discrete false claims requiring formal fact-checks [1], with a separate collection of nine fabricated quotations warranting their own dedicated investigation [2]. The primary vectors of attack are coordinated AI-generated content farms — many operating from Vietnam — that deploy text-to-speech narration, deepfake video technology, and GPT-generated blog posts to fabricate papal statements, manufacture political conflicts, and profit from advertising revenue generated by emotional engagement [3][4].

The apex of this second, politically motivated track arrived on April 12, 2026, when President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that Pope Leo "thinks it's OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon" — a claim rated "Pants on Fire" by PolitiFact and flatly contradicted by the Pope's documented statements calling nuclear weapons a threat to "our shared humanity" [7][20]. USCCB President Archbishop Paul S. Coakley responded: "I am disheartened that the President chose to write such disparaging words about the Holy Father. Pope Leo is not his rival" [21].

The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication has described the situation as a crisis of unprecedented scale, receiving "dozens of reports every day about fake accounts that use the pope's image and voice in a very realistic way, increasingly using artificial intelligence to make the pope say words he never uttered" [8]. Pope Leo himself, in his January 9, 2026 address to diplomatic corps, warned that "Language is becoming more and more a weapon with which to deceive, or to strike and offend opponents" [19] — two months before the disinformation campaign against him would peak.

1. The Disinformation Supernova: Scale of the Campaign

No religious leader in modern history has been targeted by disinformation at the scale and velocity directed at Pope Leo XIV. Within days of his May 8, 2025 election [17], the first AI deepfake channels appeared on YouTube. By May 2026 — just twelve months into his pontificate — Snopes had investigated at least 18 discrete false claims [1], nine fabricated papal quotations [2], and the Vatican's communications office acknowledged it was impossible to publicly refute each fabrication given the sheer volume [9].

The campaign operates on two distinct tracks. The first is a commercial clickbait operation targeting Catholic audiences with AI-generated narratives designed to drive traffic to advertising-laden blog sites [5]. The second, more dangerous track involves the deliberate weaponization of Leo's political profile — his opposition to U.S. military strikes on Iran and his vocal criticism of immigration enforcement made him a high-value target for state-adjacent actors and domestic political operatives [6].

Leo's unique vulnerability stems from several structural factors: as the first American pope, he occupies an unprecedented intersection of U.S. domestic politics and global religious authority. He amassed 18 million X followers in a single day after his election, creating enormous reach for any content — real or fabricated — attached to his name. His documented opposition to the Iran war puts him in direct tension with the current U.S. administration, creating authentic news hooks that fake content exploits. And the Catholic information ecosystem has limited rapid-response infrastructure compared to mainstream political fact-checking, creating a lag that disinformation networks systematically exploit.

2. Who Is Pope Leo XIV? The Target Behind the Hoaxes

Robert Francis Prevost was born September 14, 1955, at Mercy Hospital in the Bronzeville neighborhood of Chicago, Illinois. His father Louis Marius Prevost was a WWII Navy veteran and later school superintendent; his mother Mildred Agnes Prevost was a librarian from a mixed-race Black Creole Louisiana family. He became an Augustinian friar in 1977, was ordained a priest in 1982, and earned a Doctor of Canon Law from the Pontifical University of St. Thomas Aquinas in Rome in 1987 [18]. He spent decades as a missionary in Peru before being named Prefect of the Dicastery for Bishops in 2023 and was elected on the fourth ballot on May 8, 2025.

Understanding his background is essential to understanding why disinformation networks target him. An American pope who has worked with migrants and indigenous communities for decades, who leads the world's largest religious institution, and who has placed himself in direct moral opposition to the current U.S. administration is a uniquely potent target: any fabricated quote attributed to him will be believed by some on both sides of every culture-war divide. His authenticity — his actual willingness to speak plainly about war, nuclear weapons, and the poor — makes fabricated "authentic" quotes doubly effective [6].

4. Trump's Most Dangerous False Claim: The Iran Nuclear Lie

On April 12, 2026, President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social: "I don't want a Pope who thinks it's OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon." He subsequently doubled down: "Will someone please tell Pope Leo that Iran has killed at least 42,000 innocent, completely unarmed, protesters in the last two months, and that for Iran to have a Nuclear Bomb is absolutely unacceptable." The White House, when asked by CNN fact-checker Daniel Dale for evidence of any papal statement supporting Iranian nuclear weapons, produced none — citing only Trump's original post [11][20].

The claim is not merely misleading — it is the direct opposite of the Pope's documented position. Pope Leo XIV stated in a March 5, 2026 video message: "Lord, enlighten the leaders of the nations, so they may have the courage to abandon projects of death, halt the arms race. May the nuclear threat never again dictate the future of humanity." [7] The Holy See is a signatory to the UN Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons. PolitiFact rated Trump's claim "Pants on Fire" — its most severe rating, reserved for claims that are not only false but "ridiculous" [20]. CNN, PBS NewsHour, Poynter, and KRDO simultaneously published fact-checks within hours of the Truth Social post [11][12].

USCCB President Archbishop Paul S. Coakley issued a formal statement the same day: "I am disheartened that the President chose to write such disparaging words about the Holy Father. Pope Leo is not his rival." He characterized the Pope as "the Vicar of Christ who speaks from the truth of the Gospel and for the care of souls" — not a political opponent [21]. Aboard the papal plane to Algeria on April 13, Pope Leo replied directly: "I have no fear of neither the Trump administration nor of speaking out loudly about the Gospel's message" [22]. A Newsweek poll found 48% of American Catholics believed it was appropriate for the Pope to criticize the president's policies; only 21% supported Trump criticizing Leo [29].

5. Anatomy of Three Specific Hoaxes

The Fake "Cor Unum" Reform Document

The most theologically impactful hoax was a viral video falsely claiming Leo had issued 15 sweeping reforms via a document titled Cor Unum ("One Heart"). Posted July 19, 2025, the video exceeded 300,000 YouTube views. It opened: "Right now, as you are watching this, millions of Catholics..." and proceeded to enumerate fabricated reforms including ending priestly celibacy, replacing confession with communal penance, establishing a "Senate of All People" including atheists, and abolishing the title "Vicar of Christ" [13].

What made this hoax particularly effective: the fake reforms targeted real Catholic fault lines — celibacy, women's ordination, interfaith dialogue — making them plausible enough to spread within communities that follow doctrinal debates. The document name Cor Unum is itself the name of a real Vatican dicastery, lending false institutional credibility. No Vatican source, episcopal conference, or reputable Catholic journalist corroborated any claim. Snopes confirmed no such document existed [1].

The Burkina Faso Deepfake: 1 Million Views in 9 Days

Uploaded to YouTube on May 17, 2025 — just nine days after Leo's election — a 36-minute video falsely depicted the newly elected pontiff delivering a speech of support to Ibrahim Traoré, the military president of Burkina Faso who came to power in a 2022 coup. It spread via the Facebook page "Ghanaba News" on June 6, 2025, and accumulated over one million views before YouTube terminated the account [8][25].

Full Fact's forensic analysis identified three technical indicators of AI fabrication [25]: (1) the Pope pronounces Traoré's name differently in successive sentences — a telltale artifact of AI voice synthesis patching phonemes; (2) deepfake researcher Dr. Dominic Lees noted the mouth region was blurry: "Artificial intelligence finds it very difficult to generate a natural look in the teeth so often leaves this blurry and out-of-focus"; (3) background music was overlaid — Lees noted "No official video of a speech by a Pope has ever been given a music backing" and that music "frequently" masks poor voice cloning quality. The source footage was authentic video from Leo's May 12, 2025 journalist audience; the Vatican's published English transcript of that meeting confirmed he "did not mention Africa."

The Karoline Leavitt/CNN Fabrication: Physical Alibi Proves the Hoax

On April 22, 2026, Vietnam-based Facebook account "Lil chase" claimed Pope Leo appeared live on CNN to read White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt's résumé on air. Lead Stories identified three layers of impossibility [26]: (1) On April 22, Pope Leo was in Equatorial Guinea completing the final leg of his 11-day Africa apostolic journey — official Vatican travel documentation confirms he was physically on another continent [24]; (2) no record exists on CNN's website of any such interview; (3) the "Lil chase" account's Meta transparency data confirmed Vietnam management and showed multiple similar fabrications targeting other American political figures simultaneously.

6. Why Now? The Trump-Leo Relationship Arc and Platform Accountability

The Trump-Leo feud developed in escalating stages. Trump attended Leo's inaugural Mass on May 18, 2025 — the first sitting U.S. president to attend a papal inauguration. By January 2026, Leo issued sharp criticism of Trump's immigration enforcement. By February and March 2026, his moral warnings about the Iran war placed him in direct public opposition to the administration. On April 7, Leo publicly criticized Trump's threat to "wipe out" Iranian civilization as "truly unacceptable." Five days later, Trump's Truth Social attack followed [27][28].

Leo told Italian journalists on March 16 that journalism must "guard against the risk of becoming propaganda," "verify the news so as not to become a mouthpiece for those in power," and report war "through the eyes of the victims, so as not to transform it into a video game" [28]. On May 1, he appointed three new U.S. bishops, each a vocal critic of the Trump administration — a structural rather than rhetorical escalation.

Platform responses have been inadequate. YouTube terminated the Burkina Faso deepfake account after one million views, and has removed fabricated "Pope Leo XIV Sermons" channels. But the Vatican's Dicastery for Communication describes the enforcement response as playing "whack-a-mole" — new channels appear faster than existing ones can be reported [8]. Meta's ad transparency tools exposed the Vietnamese origins of the spam networks but did not automatically enforce removal [5]. No public record exists of X, TikTok, or Instagram taking platform-level policy action specifically targeting Leo XIV disinformation.

The Vatican is fighting back proactively. Pope Leo designated the theme for the 60th World Day of Social Communications as "Preserving Human Voices and Faces" — a direct reference to AI's threat to authentic human communication. He has proposed a global Media and AI Literacy (MAIL) curriculum for schools emphasizing source verification, watermark detection, and critical thinking [30]. In February 2026, he told priests not to use AI to write sermons: "To give a true homily is to share faith, and AI will never be able to share faith." [23]

7. Where the Hoaxes Spread: Platform Distribution

Facebook is the dominant origin platform for Pope Leo XIV disinformation, accounting for an estimated 40% of all documented hoaxes. This reflects the operational model of Vietnamese spam networks, which use Facebook's massive Catholic community groups and comment sections to seed AI-generated content before it migrates to YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram. YouTube serves as the primary platform for video deepfakes (25% of documented cases), while X amplified head-of-state level misinformation — most critically, Trump's Truth Social attacks which were immediately shared on X by millions of followers [6].

The broader pattern of AI deepfakes targeting Catholic leaders extends beyond Pope Leo. Bishop Robert E. Barron (Winona-Rochester), Cardinal Marc Ouellet, Father Mike Schmitz, and Father Rafael Capó have all been victimized by fabricated content. The structural vulnerability is clear: religious institutions rely on the moral authority of named individuals — popes, bishops, priests — to communicate with communities. This makes them particularly susceptible to deepfakes, which weaponize exactly that named personal authority [31].

8. Conclusion: What This Means for Religious Institutions in the AI Age

The Structural Risk

Religious institutions that depend on the personal moral authority of named leaders face a systemic vulnerability to deepfake technology that political organizations — with their press teams and rapid-response infrastructure — are better equipped to counter. The Vatican has acknowledged that given the volume of fake material, "it is impossible to publicly refute each and every one of them." [9]

Pope Leo XIV's case represents something genuinely new: a religious leader who warned in January 2026 that "Language is becoming more and more a weapon with which to deceive" [19] was, within three months, facing an unprecedented industrial-scale campaign deploying exactly that weapon against him. The Vatican's response — the MAIL curriculum, the "Preserving Human Voices and Faces" communications theme, the guidance to priests on AI sermons — represents the most comprehensive institutional response to AI-generated religious disinformation yet attempted [30].

But the fundamental asymmetry remains: generating AI-fabricated content costs nearly nothing; debunking each fabrication requires time, expertise, and credible authority. At 18+ documented hoaxes in twelve months, with the Vatican unable to rebut them all and platforms removing them only after they've already reached millions, the disinformation infrastructure targeting Pope Leo XIV is outpacing the fact-checking ecosystem designed to counter it. The question is no longer whether AI will be weaponized against religious institutions — it already has been. The question is whether those institutions can build literacy and enforcement infrastructure fast enough to matter.

As Leo himself advised in 2026: "Educate ourselves and others about how to use AI intentionally" and "protect our image — our face and our voice — to prevent them from being used in the creation of harmful content." [31]
