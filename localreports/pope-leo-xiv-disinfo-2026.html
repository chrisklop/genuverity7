Pope Leo XIV, elected May 8, 2025 as the first American pope, has been targeted by a multi-vector AI disinformation campaign unprecedented in scope for any religious figure. Within 12 months: 18+ distinct fabricated rumors documented by Snopes; a TikTok deepfake sermon accumulated 32.9 million views; a Burkina Faso junta deepfake exceeded 1 million views before removal; and a Vietnam-based ad-revenue farm manufactured fake papal quotes at industrial scale. Executive Summary

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost on September 14, 1955, in Chicago, elected as the 267th pope on May 8, 2025 — became the most misinformation-targeted religious figure in the digital age within weeks of his election. His profile was uniquely exploitable: as both the first American pope and a fluent English speaker, his words carried direct, unfiltered resonance in the American media ecosystem at a moment of explosive tension with the Trump administration. Within 12 months, Snopes documented at least 18 distinct rumors, and researchers counted dozens of AI-generated deepfake channels on YouTube and TikTok accumulating tens of millions of views. [1][2][3]

The campaign's architecture reveals a convergence of ideologically opposed actors using the same toolkit. Vietnam-based content farms manufacture Trump-baiting fake papal quotes to generate advertising revenue. [4][5] Pro-junta actors in West Africa deploy 36-minute deepfake videos of Leo endorsing military rulers. [8] Domestic U.S. partisan accounts circulate fabricated quotes confirming their preferred narrative. The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication has described an "exponential proliferation" of fake content, acknowledging "it is impossible to publicly refute each and every one of them." [9]

Pope Leo XIV has made combating AI disinformation a central pillar of his papacy. He chose the name Leo partly as a response to what he sees as a new industrial revolution driven by artificial intelligence. [10] His World Communications Day message warned that AI can "fabricate parallel 'realities,' usurping our faces and voices." [16] The bitter irony: the pope most focused on combating AI-generated falsehoods has himself become their most prolific victim.

1. The Scale: 18+ Debunked Rumors in Under 12 Months

No pope in history has been targeted by documented disinformation at the scale and velocity directed at Leo XIV. By April 2026 — less than twelve months after his election — Snopes had published fact-checks on 18 separate fabricated claims and maintained a dedicated Leo XIV rumors collection page. [1] This is unprecedented for any single religious figure within a single year.

The rumors spanned an extraordinary range: inflammatory fake quotes about Trump, false health claims (hospitalization hoaxes), fabricated 3 a.m. livestreams warning of political threats, AI-generated video deepfakes endorsing foreign military leaders, and false accusations that Leo had endorsed Iran's nuclear program. [6][7]

The total documented harm extends far beyond the 18 Snopes cases. AFP's investigation identified 26 dedicated YouTube channels posting AI-generated Leo XIV content; 16 were terminated, 6 were demonetized. [3] On TikTok, AI-generated content reached over 42 million views across documented incidents. [2] The Vatican confirmed that new fake accounts appear "as quickly as others are removed." [9]

2. Who Is Pope Leo XIV? The Target Profile

Robert Francis Prevost was born September 14, 1955, in Chicago. He joined the Order of Saint Augustine, earned a doctorate in canon law from the Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas in Rome, and spent decades as a missionary in Peru — becoming a Peruvian citizen. Pope Francis appointed him Prefect of the Dicastery for Bishops in 2023 — one of the most powerful Vatican positions. He was elected as the 267th pope on May 8, 2025, on the fourth ballot of the conclave's second day. [10]

Several characteristics made Leo XIV uniquely vulnerable:

  • Native English fluency: Previous popes communicated through translation layers. Leo speaks English natively, placing him as a direct participant in American political discourse without intermediaries.
  • Political salience: His known positions on immigration, war, and human dignity made him a natural foil for the Trump administration — and therefore a high-value target for fabricated inflammatory quotes.
  • Theological authority as exploit: Word on Fire described AI-generated papal speech as "fabricating a false Magisterium — a shadow papacy of machines." [12] Papal statements carry unique moral weight unavailable to most public figures.
  • Global audience: His dual American/Peruvian identity made fabricated content weaponizable simultaneously across multiple countries, languages, and political contexts.

He chose the papal name "Leo" deliberately — to echo Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum defense of workers against industrial exploitation, and as a response to the challenges of a "new industrial revolution" driven by AI. [10] The name was a statement of intent. The disinformation campaign was the immediate test.

4. Forensic Analysis: The Fabrication Playbook

The disinformation campaign began within hours of Leo's election. On May 8, 2025 — the day of his election — a Threads user (@atheistadamx) posted a fake meme claiming Leo had embraced being "woke." The user later admitted: "The fact that so many of you wanted this to be true shows how far Catholicism is from real progress. Yes, I made up these words, with the help of my AI friend." [1]

Within two weeks, a separate fake quote — "You cannot follow both Christ and the cruelty of kings" — began circulating. Snopes confirmed no evidence Leo ever said it, with the earliest posting traced to May 14, 2025. [7] By May 23, the first major video deepfake appeared: a 36-minute AI-generated video falsely depicting Leo addressing Burkina Faso's military leader Ibrahim Traoré, using "morphing" lip-sync technology on authentic papal footage. [8]

Key AI Detection Markers

  • GPTZero confirmation: AI authorship "with high certainty" on blog posts containing fake Leo quotes [4]
  • Cyrillic substitution: Vietnam blogs replaced visually identical Latin letters with Cyrillic equivalents to evade automated detection [5]
  • Music masking: Deepfake audio used music soundtracks to conceal voice-cloning artifacts — identified by Dr. Dominic Lees, University of Reading [19]
  • Pronunciation inconsistency: AI voice model mispronounced "Traoré" differently in consecutive sentences — impossible in human speech [19]
  • Blurred mouth rendering: Lip-sync AI produces a smeared appearance in the teeth/mouth area — a documented artifact of current video synthesis models [19]
  • Absent room ambience: Authentic papal recordings have characteristic spatial audio; the deepfake audio lacks environmental sound [19]

5. Evidence Deep-Dive: The Burkina Faso Deepfake & TikTok's Labeling Failure

The Burkina Faso Deepfake: Four Technical Failures

The 36-minute video depicting Leo addressing Ibrahim Traoré is the most technically sophisticated attack in this campaign. The video repurposed authentic footage from Leo's May 12, 2025 journalist audience and applied digital "morphing" to synchronize his lip movements with an AI-generated English voiceover. In the fabricated dialogue, Leo is made to say: "I have read your words not once, but many times, and each reading has been deeper than the last, because in your voice I have heard not only the anger of a president, but the righteous cry of a continent long wounded by the twin blades of abandonment and exploitation." [8]

Full Fact's investigation with Dr. Dominic Lees (University of Reading) identified four specific technical failure modes: (1) music masking voice-cloning artifacts; (2) absent room ambience; (3) inconsistent "Traoré" pronunciation across consecutive sentences; and (4) blurred mouth rendering in the lip-sync zone. [19] Despite these markers and the Vatican publicly confirming the video was fake, it surpassed 1 million views. The video included a buried disclaimer — "This video is a work of fiction inspired by the life of Ibrahim Traoré" — that failed to prevent viral spread.

TikTok's 32.9 Million View Labeling Failure

The single most-viewed piece of fake Leo XIV content was a TikTok AI-labeled sermon video accumulating 32.9 million views. The video carried TikTok's required AI disclosure label — placed at the bottom of the screen in a format that proved "largely ineffective" at preventing viewer deception. [2] No video on Leo XIV's official Instagram has exceeded 6 million views — meaning this single fake item reached more than 5.5 times the audience of any authentic papal video.

A separate Spanish-language deepfake on TikTok depicting Leo preaching about "supportive women" reached 9.6 million views. [2] TikTok's self-reporting disclosure model — requiring creators to opt in to labeling AI content — creates a structural gap: bad actors have no incentive to self-identify. Platform-side detection for AI-generated video deepfakes of real people remains inadequate at scale.

6. Contemporary Context: Leo vs. Trump & the Vatican's AI Policy Architecture

The Leo–Trump Feud as Disinformation Accelerant

The genuine conflict between Pope Leo XIV and the Trump administration has provided fertile ground for fabricated escalations. The trigger was Operation Epic Fury — U.S.-Israeli airstrikes on Iran launched February 28, 2026. Leo expressed "deep concern" the next day, escalated in March, and on April 7 called Trump's threat to "destroy Iranian civilization" as "truly unacceptable." [13]

Trump responded on Truth Social calling Leo "WEAK on Crime, and terrible for Foreign Policy" and falsely accused him of endorsing Iran having nuclear weapons. He also posted an AI-generated image of himself as Jesus Christ — directly mocking Leo's moral authority. Leo's response to reporters in April 2026 was unambiguous: "I have no fear of neither the Trump administration nor of speaking out loudly about the message in the Gospel." [13]

This real conflict generated parallel fake escalations. Snopes documented a fabricated quote claiming Leo said Trump "chose the road of darkness, bitterness, and lies" — designed to make the genuine feud appear even more explosive. Disinformation on this topic flows from actors on both sides of the political divide, each seeking a papal endorsement that does not exist.

The Church's AI Policy Architecture: Three Tracks

The Catholic Church has developed its response on three parallel institutional tracks: [16][17][22]

  1. Doctrinal — "Antiqua et Nova" (January 2025): Established the theological foundation: AI systems "operate largely through pattern recognition and lack the creative, spiritual and moral dimensions of human thought." Explicitly warned AI "presents a serious risk of generating manipulated content and false information."
  2. Governance — Vatican AI Framework: One of the world's first state-level AI governance frameworks, requiring systems to be ethical, transparent, and human-centered; explicitly prohibiting AI used to "manipulate people, discriminate or threaten security." [22]
  3. Operational — Dicastery for Communication: Under Prefect Dr. Paolo Ruffini, a fake-content reporting system processes "dozens of reports every day." Public authentication protocol: all authentic papal statements are on vatican.va in ten languages. [9][18]

Leo's January 24, 2026 World Communications Day message articulated the core issue: "By simulating human voices and faces, wisdom and knowledge, consciousness and responsibility, empathy and friendship, the systems known as artificial intelligence not only interfere with information ecosystems, but also encroach upon the deepest level of communication, that of human relationships." [16] He also called for a new initiative — Media and Artificial Intelligence Literacy (MAIL) — to be introduced into educational systems globally. [21]

Why Religious Authority Is a High-Value Target

Pope Leo XIV is not the only major religious figure targeted by AI disinformation — but he is the most extensively targeted. Steven Umbrello (Institute for Ethics and Emerging Technologies) identified the structural logic: "Deepfakes aim straight at that credibility. They can make it appear that a pastor endorsed something he never endorsed." [20] Religious authority is exploitable because it is built on trust rather than institutional verification, statements carry unique moral weight, and congregants worldwide often lack access to primary sources.

OSV News documented a parallel pattern across Catholic bishops: multiple fabricated videos depicting clergy "confronting ICE agents" — with identical scripts appearing "word for word" across videos featuring different simulated bishops and congregations, showing industrialized production of fake religious authority content. [20]

8. Conclusion: The First Pope to Fight Deepfakes — and Their Most Prominent Victim

Pope Leo XIV's situation represents a paradox unique in the history of disinformation: the world leader most focused on combating AI-generated falsehoods has simultaneously become the single most impersonated religious figure in the AI deepfake era. The Vatican's institutional response — doctrinal framework, governance architecture, communications protocols — is arguably the most comprehensive response to AI disinformation mounted by any single institution. Yet the campaign continues, with new fake accounts appearing as quickly as platforms remove old ones. [9]

The structural lesson is clear: AI-generated disinformation targeting high-authority figures has outpaced both institutional response capacity and platform enforcement. AFP's investigation identifying 26 fake channels represents a one-time audit, not an ongoing capacity. TikTok's 32.9 million-view disclosure failure demonstrates that labeling requirements without algorithmic detection and enforced placement are insufficient. [3]

Pope Leo XIV has stated that "people must not die because of fake news" and has called on journalists to be "the barrier to protect the world from disinformation." [11] For readers: if a papal statement is not at vatican.va, it should be treated as unverified by default.
