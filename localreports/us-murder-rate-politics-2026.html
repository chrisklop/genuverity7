TL;DR

VERDICT: CONTEXT REQUIRED

Both the “Red State Murder Problem” and “Blue City Murder Problem” narratives contain partial truths but fundamentally mislead. Red states have maintained higher aggregate homicide rates than blue states every year since 2000—a gap that persists even after removing their largest cities. But 27 of the 30 highest-murder-rate cities have Democratic mayors. When demographic and economic controls are applied, the partisan correlation effectively disappears. Poverty, inequality, social mobility, and institutional trust predict homicide far more reliably than which party controls government. Both narratives weaponize incomplete data for electoral advantage while obscuring the structural causes of American violence.

Executive Summary

The United States occupies a paradoxical position in global homicide statistics: a G7 nation with a murder rate three to six times higher than its economic peers. At 6.38 per 100,000 in 2022, the U.S. rate dwarfs Japan (0.23), Italy (0.55), Germany (0.91), and the United Kingdom (0.99). This disparity has fueled a domestic political war in which each side claims the other’s governance causes murder. Third Way’s analysis of CDC data shows red states averaged 33% higher murder rates than blue states in 2021–2022—a gap persisting since 2000 that survives even the removal of large cities from red state calculations. The Heritage Foundation counters that 27 of 30 highest-murder cities have Democratic mayors, blaming “rogue prosecutors” and progressive criminal justice policies. The Manhattan Institute resolves this tension: when controlling for demographics, urbanization, and income, partisan affiliation shows no statistically significant correlation with homicide. The actual predictors are socioeconomic—a one-standard-deviation increase in social mobility reduces gun homicide by 25%, while neighborhood poverty increases it by 26%. Meanwhile, crime-related disinformation—amplified by generative AI, foreign actors, and partisan media—ensures the public perceives its environment as far more dangerous than the data supports. As of 2024, the national homicide rate has returned to the pre-pandemic baseline of 5.0 per 100,000, with 71% of major cities reporting year-over-year declines.

The American Paradox: Global Homicide Context

The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime defines intentional homicide as the “unlawful death inflicted upon a person with the intent to cause death or serious injury,” a standard rooted in the International Classification of Crime for Statistical Purposes (ICCS). This definition encompasses murder, honor killings, fatal serious assaults, and femicide while excluding deaths directly related to war or armed conflict. [1]

Global homicide rates have declined from 7.6 per 100,000 in 2004 to approximately 5.61 per 100,000 in 2022. But this aggregate masks extreme regional variation. The Americas report the highest regional rate at 14.37 per 100,000, driven by organized crime, inequality, and firearms availability. Europe (2.10) and Asia (2.04) represent the lowest risk. [1] [2]

Within this hierarchy, the United States occupies a position of significant concern among developed nations. While Japan maintains a rate of 0.23 and Italy 0.55, the U.S. rate of 6.38 per 100,000 in 2022 places it closer to the developing-world average than to its economic peers. This disparity is not random—the UNODC identifies organized crime, high inequality, and firearms proliferation as primary drivers of violence in the Americas, factors that are mediated in the U.S. through a decentralized political system where crime control policies vary dramatically between states. The 2024 Contraction: National Homicide Trends

Tracking lethal violence in the United States relies on two primary systems with different methodologies: the FBI’s Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) program, which collects voluntary reports from local law enforcement, and the CDC’s National Vital Statistics System (NVSS), which uses mandatory death certificates from state registries. Analysts often prefer CDC data for state-level comparisons because it captures a more complete picture, while FBI data provides more granular detail on crime circumstances. [3] [13]

The national picture is one of dramatic volatility followed by recovery. Homicide rates surged during 2020–2021, peaking at 6.8 per 100,000—the highest in decades. This spike has been attributed to the compounding stresses of the COVID-19 pandemic: economic disruption, social isolation, disruption of community-based violence intervention programs, and strained police-community relations. [6]

By the end of 2024, the national rate had dropped to an estimated 5.0 per 100,000, representing a full return to pre-pandemic baselines. Early 2025 data from the Council on Criminal Justice indicates a further 21% decrease in homicides across a sample of major cities, suggesting the downward trajectory is accelerating. [6]

The Red State Murder Gap: A 22-Year Pattern

The “Red State Murder Problem” was popularized by the centrist think tank Third Way, which analyzed CDC data to compare homicide rates in states won by Donald Trump in 2020 against those won by Joe Biden. Their findings are striking: the average murder rate in red states was 33% higher than in blue states in both 2021 and 2022. This is not a recent anomaly—Third Way’s historical review found red state murder rates have exceeded blue state rates every single year from 2000 to 2022. [3] [7]

The persistence of this gap suggests that state-level policy environments—firearm regulations, social service funding, economic strategies—play a meaningful role. Blue states spent approximately 33% more per capita on policing ($453.67) than red states ($341.37) in 2021. Red states more frequently feature higher poverty rates and lower levels of what researchers call “institutional social capital”—citizens’ trust in the institutions that govern their lives. [3] [4]

The “Remove the Blue Cities” Experiment

The most common rebuttal to the state-level data is that these high red state rates are driven solely by large, Democratic-led cities. To test this, Third Way performed a deliberately conservative experiment: they removed murders occurring in the county containing the largest city from 22 of 25 red states, while leaving blue state data completely untouched. [3]

Even with this “extra help” given exclusively to red state data, the red state murder rate remained 20% higher than the full blue state rate in 2021 and 16% higher in 2022. After removing Jackson, Mississippi’s largest city, the state still maintained one of the nation’s highest homicide rates. Alabama actually jumped to the number one position in 2022 after its largest city was removed from the calculation. This demonstrates that the violence in red states extends well beyond urban cores into suburban and rural areas. [3]

Methodological Caveat

Third Way classifies states as “red” or “blue” based on 2020 presidential vote. This binary ignores swing states, split governments, and the fact that policy implementation occurs at multiple levels. A state’s governor, legislature, and local officials may represent different parties. Treating an entire state’s homicide rate as a product of one party’s governance oversimplifies a deeply layered reality.

The Blue City Rebuttal: Municipal-Level Analysis

The Heritage Foundation and other conservative analysts point to the municipal level as the more honest unit of analysis. Their argument: 27 of the 30 U.S. cities with the highest murder rates are governed by Democratic mayors. In this framing, the “Blue City Murder Problem” is the primary driver of national crime statistics, not state-level governance. [5] [9]

Heritage emphasizes the role of “progressive” or “rogue” prosecutors who have allegedly implemented policies favoring criminals: the elimination of cash bail, refusal to prosecute certain felony categories, and deprioritization of quality-of-life offenses. This narrative gained particular traction during the 2022–2024 election cycles and was submitted as formal congressional testimony. [10] [11]

However, the 2024 municipal data complicates this narrative considerably. Of 24 major cities tracked by Rochester Institute of Technology researchers, 70.8% experienced decreases in homicide rates—many of them dramatic. New Orleans saw a 32.4% decline, Detroit 18.3%, Washington D.C. 30.7%, Oakland 30.9%, Hartford 38.9%, and Greensboro a staggering 43.1%. All of these cities remain under Democratic leadership. [12]

The Correlation Trap

The Heritage Foundation’s point about Democratic mayors is structurally misleading for a simple reason: nearly all large American cities have Democratic mayors. As of 2024, only two of the 30 largest U.S. cities have Republican mayors. Attributing crime to Democratic governance is functionally equivalent to attributing it to “being a large city.” By this logic, you could also correlate high murder rates with having professional sports teams, public transit systems, or tall buildings.

Beyond Party Labels: What Actually Predicts Murder

The Manhattan Institute offers the most intellectually rigorous resolution of the red-blue debate. Their researchers found that when models are adjusted for basic demographic and economic characteristics—age distribution, racial demographics, urbanization levels, and per capita income—the correlation between political party and homicide rate effectively disappears. There is no statistically significant relationship between a county’s Trump vote share and its homicide rate once these controls are applied. [14] [15]

This implies that partisan affiliation serves as a proxy for a suite of underlying socioeconomic factors. States and counties that vote Republican often feature different demographic profiles—different poverty rates, urbanization patterns, age distributions, and racial compositions—compared to those that vote Democratic. The politics don’t cause the murders; both the politics and the murders are downstream of deeper structural conditions.

A peer-reviewed academic study using negative binomial regression found that “Social Determinants of Health” provide a far more predictive framework for violence than partisan labels: [8]

These findings explain both narratives simultaneously. Red states tend to have higher poverty rates and lower educational attainment—known risk factors for homicide. Blue states, while often having higher urbanization, may mitigate violence through higher social spending and more restrictive firearm legislation. The actual story is not about which party governs, but about the structural conditions—poverty, inequality, social isolation, and institutional distrust—that predict violence regardless of who holds office. The Ecological Fallacy

Both narratives commit the “ecological fallacy”—the error of drawing conclusions about individuals or local processes from aggregate data. A state’s murder rate tells you nothing about whether any specific policy caused any specific murder. A city’s Democratic mayor tells you nothing about whether that city’s prosecutors are “soft on crime.” The scale of analysis determines the conclusion: state-level data favors the Democratic narrative; city-level data favors the Republican one. Both are technically correct at their chosen scale. Neither is the full picture.

The Information Crisis: Crime Disinformation in 2024–2025

The analytical challenge of understanding American homicide is compounded by systematic disinformation. Strategic disinformation—the intentional use of false narratives to manipulate perceptions—has become a weapon in the “politics of division,” often deployed to exaggerate crime levels for electoral or financial gain. [16]

Multiple vectors drive this crisis. Advances in generative AI enable photo-realistic images and synthetic videos, making it increasingly difficult to distinguish real from fabricated crime reporting. When political identities become highly polarized, individuals more readily accept false information that confirms existing biases about “red” vs. “blue” jurisdiction safety. Declining trust in traditional media has created a vacuum where unverified social media content is treated as authoritative. And foreign actors—notably Russian influence operations—have deliberately disseminated fake content to highlight American domestic “chaos.” [17] [20]

Gallup polling consistently shows that Americans believe crime is “getting worse” nationally even during periods of significant decline. Key Conclusions: What the Data Actually Shows

Bottom Line

The Global Disparity: The U.S. homicide rate, while declining, remains 3–28x higher than its G7 peers—driven by firearms proliferation and structural inequality, not partisan governance. [1]

The Political Scale Trick: Red states have higher aggregate murder rates. Blue cities have higher per capita urban rates. Both are true. Neither is causal. The scale of analysis determines the conclusion. [3] [5]

The Actual Predictors: Poverty, inequality, social mobility, and institutional trust predict homicide far better than party affiliation. When these are controlled, partisan correlation vanishes. [8] [14]

The Data Divide: Voluntary FBI reporting vs. mandatory CDC data creates opportunities for narrative manipulation. Analysts should prioritize CDC data for comparative state metrics. [3]

The Disinformation Threat: Crime-related disinformation—fueled by AI, foreign actors, and partisan media—ensures Americans perceive escalating danger during a period of historic decline. [16] [17]

The 2024 return to pre-pandemic homicide levels is a significant milestone. But achieving the safety levels observed in Europe and Asia requires addressing the structural conditions—poverty, inequality, firearms access, and institutional distrust—that produce American violence in the first place. Simply blaming “red states” or “blue cities” is not analysis. It is campaign advertising. [22]
