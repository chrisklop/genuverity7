TL;DR

GOVERNMENT MISINFORMATION

DHS created and heavily promoted a public-facing website with hundreds of documented errors, falsely portraying people arrested for minor offenses (traffic violations, marijuana possession) as dangerous criminals, then blamed a "glitch" only after media scrutiny. Part of a broader pattern of false official statements contradicted by video evidence and independent data showing 73.6% of ICE detainees have no criminal conviction.

Executive Summary

On February 19, 2026, CNN published an exclusive investigation revealing that the Department of Homeland Security admitted its website showcasing the "worst of the worst" arrested immigrants was rife with errors.[1] The website, launched December 8, 2025 at wow.dhs.gov, listed approximately 25,000 people along with criminal charges—but a CNN analysis found that hundreds were incorrectly described, with many listed only for minor offenses like traffic violations, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry, not the violent crimes the site purported to showcase.[1][2]

DHS acknowledged the errors only after CNN's inquiry, attributing them to a "glitch" affecting "about 5 percent of the entries."[3] However, the site had been heavily promoted on social media by DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, the White House, and federal officials as evidence justifying aggressive immigration enforcement operations—including Operation Metro Surge in Minneapolis, which deployed approximately 3,000 federal agents.[14] Independent data contradicts the administration's core claims: according to TRAC Immigration, 73.6% of the 68,289 people in ICE detention had no criminal conviction.[4] The Cato Institute found that only 5% had violent criminal convictions.[5]

A Government Database Built on False Premises

The Department of Homeland Security launched wow.dhs.gov on December 8, 2025, billing it as a transparency initiative that would allow Americans to "find criminal illegal aliens removed from your community."[10] The website displayed names of arrested individuals, arrest locations by city and state, criminal charges or convictions, countries of origin, and mugshot photos in some cases.

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, the White House, and DHS social media accounts heavily promoted the site alongside enforcement announcements. Despite being available for only 24 days in 2025, the site became the 7th most-viewed page on DHS.gov for the entire year.[23] DHS expanded the database multiple times: adding 5,000 entries on December 18, improving state search features on January 22, and adding another 5,000 entries on February 5—bringing the total to approximately 25,000 listed individuals.[11]

The administration used the website to justify large-scale enforcement deployments, administrative warrant policies allowing warrantless home entries, refugee detention and rescreening policies, and claims that 70% of ICE arrests involved "criminal illegal aliens." President Trump wrote on Truth Social: "Show the numbers, names, and faces of the violent criminals, and show them NOW."[1]

Hundreds of Errors Exposed by Media Investigation

On February 19, 2026, CNN published an analysis revealing that hundreds of people listed on the DHS website were incorrectly characterized. Many were listed for single charges of traffic offenses, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry—not the violent crimes the "Worst of the Worst" branding implied.[1][2] After receiving CNN's questions, a DHS spokesperson admitted that "the charges against hundreds of immigrants listed on the website were described incorrectly by the agency."[1]

DHS attributed the errors to a "glitch" affecting "about 5% of the entries," claiming many listed for traffic offenses actually had "additional crimes."[3] However, 5% of 25,000 entries equals approximately 1,250 people falsely characterized—and DHS provided no technical explanation for how a "glitch" could systematically misrepresent charges. The agency claimed the "glitch" was "resolved" within one day of CNN's inquiry, an implausibly short timeline for complex database systems requiring data validation.[3]

Former acting ICE director John Sandweg noted that "the vast majority of so-called criminal aliens are individuals charged with or convicted of traffic offenses, DUIs and immigration-related offenses"—context conspicuously absent from DHS promotional materials.[1] Jason Houser, former ICE chief of staff during the Biden administration, stated bluntly: "It just shows that this is about the political debate. It's not about actually arresting the most convicted criminals."[15]

Geographic Manipulation: Prison Towns as "Arrest Locations"

CNN's analysis revealed another deceptive pattern: cities with the highest arrest numbers were small towns with large federal detention facilities.[13] The city representing the most arrests on the DHS website was Conroe, Texas—population approximately 114,000—which is home to the Joe Corley Processing Center, a privately owned detention facility that ICE uses to house immigrants. Similar patterns appeared in Eden, Texas; Lompoc, California; and Yazoo City, Mississippi—all small towns with federal detention centers.

This indicates DHS counted routine transfers from federal prison custody as new "arrests" of community threats, inflating the narrative that dangerous criminals were "lurking in your neighborhood." The geographic manipulation allowed DHS to claim high arrest numbers in specific communities while concealing that these "arrests" were actually administrative transfers of people already in federal custody—sometimes for years.

Minnesota Officials Provide Counter-Evidence

Minnesota Corrections Commissioner Paul Schnell accused DHS of "pure propaganda" and presented video evidence refuting DHS claims about Operation Metro Surge arrests.[6][7] While DHS claimed it "arrested" people released into communities by Minnesota Department of Corrections, Schnell showed videos of coordinated, routine transfers to ICE custody—not "arrests."

Some people on DHS lists were transferred to ICE years ago—as far back as the 1990s. Many listed were never in Minnesota prisons or had no criminal record in Minnesota.[7] Governor Tim Walz similarly accused DHS of inflating numbers by taking credit for pre-existing state custody transfers that occurred before Operation Metro Surge even began.

Schnell stated: "This is no longer a simple misunderstanding. At best, DHS fundamentally misunderstands Minnesota's correctional system. At worst, it is pure propaganda, numbers released without evidence to stoke fear rather than inform the public."[15]

Independent Data Contradicts "Worst of the Worst" Narrative

Multiple independent analyses directly contradict DHS's public messaging that it was targeting "the worst of the worst." TRAC Immigration data current through February 7, 2026 shows that of 68,289 people in ICE detention, 50,259 (73.6%) had no criminal conviction and only 18,030 (26.4%) had any criminal conviction.[4] This percentage has increased from 70.4% in June 2025, indicating enforcement is becoming less focused on convicted criminals over time.

The Cato Institute, analyzing leaked DHS data from October 1, 2025 to the present, found that 73% of people booked into ICE custody had no criminal conviction, only 8% had violent or property crime convictions, and only 5% had violent criminal convictions.[5] When the Cato Institute published this analysis, DHS called the report's main chart "made up" with "no legitimate data behind it"—yet the raw data came from DHS itself.[16]

Between September 21, 2025 and January 7, 2026, ICE detention increased by 11,296 people. Of that increase, only 902 were convicted criminals (8%), 2,273 had pending criminal charges (20%), and 8,121 had no criminal convictions or pending charges (72%).[12] This means 92% of ICE detention growth came from people with no criminal convictions—the exact opposite of the "worst of the worst" narrative.[24]

Evidence Deep-Dive: Pattern of False Official Statements

The website errors are part of a documented pattern of DHS making false claims, then retracting or being contradicted by evidence. On January 24, 2026, Alex Pretti—a U.S. citizen—was shot and killed by federal agents in Minneapolis. Officials immediately called him a "domestic terrorist" who "brandished" a gun and "wanted to massacre law enforcement."[8][9]

Multiple bystander videos showed Pretti's hands holding only his mobile phone when shots were fired—no brandishing of a weapon. Footage captured an officer removing a handgun from Pretti's waist area, then moving away—after which the first shot was fired.[8] Ten shots total were fired, including rounds after Pretti was on the ground motionless.[9] Video analysis showed the slide of Pretti's firearm did not move to the rear, indicating it was not fired.[25]

Former DHS officials warned this pattern erodes public trust. John Sandweg, former acting ICE director during the Obama administration: "Public trust is everything to these agencies—once destroyed, it affects all their work."[15]

Government Accountability Office Findings

The website errors exist within a broader context of ICE data integrity problems documented by the U.S. Government Accountability Office. In July 2024, the GAO found that ICE understates the total number of people it detains in its public reports.[17] ICE excludes people who were first booked into certain temporary facilities before being transferred to immigration detention facilities—an exclusion that amounts to tens of thousands of people.

During calendar year 2022, ICE did not count among its total detained population 203,350 individuals—roughly 42% of people detained—because they initially were held in temporary ICE or CBP facilities.[18] ICE claimed it did not count these individuals because it did not detain them for very long. However, the GAO investigation found that approximately 70% of individuals not counted in the tally were eventually detained by ICE for weeks, and in some instances, even years.[18]

The GAO recommended ICE publicly report complete detention data and explain methodology. DHS did not concur, stating ICE already reports sufficient information. The GAO continues to believe ICE should report complete and transparent information.[17] A subsequent 2025 GAO review of ICE facility inspections found continued deficiencies in data quality and reporting transparency.[19]

Congressional Oversight Battles

In February 2026, a federal court granted emergency relief to 13 members of Congress, ordering DHS and ICE to restore unannounced congressional oversight of ICE detention facilities.[20] After a court had preliminarily stayed a DHS policy requiring members of Congress to provide prior notice of oversight visits to ICE facilities in December, DHS Secretary Kristi Noem secretly reinstated the requirement through a previously undisclosed memorandum.

On February 3, 2026, Representative Robert Garcia and Senator Richard Blumenthal held a bicameral public forum to receive testimony on the violent tactics and disproportionate use of force by agents of the Department of Homeland Security.[21] Senate Democrats demanded Secretary Noem testify "without delay" following fatal shootings by federal immigration officials in Minneapolis. Noem has failed to appear before the Senate Homeland Security Committee for its annual hearing since her confirmation, despite testifying before a House panel.

The Washington Post reported that DHS is targeting Americans with administrative subpoenas—unlike judicial subpoenas approved by a judge, administrative subpoenas are issued by federal agencies, allowing investigators to seek information about individuals from tech and phone companies without a judge's oversight.[22]

Contemporary Context: Why This Matters Now

The DHS website errors represent more than a technical failure—they exemplify a broader pattern of government misinformation used to justify aggressive enforcement policies. Hundreds of people were publicly characterized as "worst of the worst" criminals when they had only minor charges, with their names, photos, and locations searchable online. This false characterization had real human consequences.

The website was used to justify Operation Metro Surge deploying 3,000 federal agents to Minnesota, warrantless home entry policies, refugee detention policies, and other controversial enforcement actions. Policy justification based on false data undermines democratic accountability and informed public debate.

Former DHS officials warn that repeated false statements—about Alex Pretti, Renee Good, Marimar Martinez, and the website errors—undermine public trust in law enforcement. John Sandweg: "Public trust is everything to these agencies—once destroyed, it affects all their work."[15]

The pattern of errors suggests the problem may be rooted in how DHS defines "criminal aliens" rather than a one-time technical glitch. DHS inflates numbers by counting pending charges (not convictions) as "criminals" and claiming this "doesn't even include foreign fugitives, terrorists, and gang members who lack a rap sheet in the U.S."[16]

Despite GAO recommendations, court orders, and congressional pressure, DHS has resisted transparency measures and independent oversight. The website remains online with continued daily updates, but DHS has not published a corrected list of affected entries, technical explanation of what caused the errors, independent audit of remaining data accuracy, or methodology for validating criminal charge descriptions.

Conclusion

The Department of Homeland Security's admission that its "Worst of the Worst" website contained hundreds of errors falsely characterizing people with minor offenses as dangerous criminals represents a significant breach of public trust. DHS only acknowledged the errors after media investigation, offered an implausible "glitch" explanation with no technical details, and claimed it was resolved in one day.

Independent data contradicts the core premise of the website: 73.6% of ICE detainees have no criminal conviction, only 5% have violent convictions, and 92% of detention growth since September 2025 came from people with no criminal record. Minnesota officials provided video evidence refuting DHS claims about Operation Metro Surge arrests, showing routine custody transfers DHS later claimed as enforcement "arrests."

The website errors are part of a documented pattern of false official statements contradicted by video evidence in the Alex Pretti, Renee Good, and Marimar Martinez cases. Former DHS officials warn this pattern undermines public trust in law enforcement and affects all agency work. The immediate question for DHS is narrow: what exactly went wrong and how was the correction validated. For the public, the larger question remains structural: whether a database marketed as proof of precision targeting can earn credibility when the underlying data presentation proves fragile.
