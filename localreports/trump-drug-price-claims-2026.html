While his administration negotiated discounted cash prices for 43 brand-name drugs through TrumpRx.gov, there is no evidence this led to broad decreases in drug prices. Executive Summary

President Donald Trump has repeatedly claimed ahead of his February 24, 2026 State of the Union address that Americans are "now paying the lowest price anywhere in the world for drugs" thanks to his administration's negotiations with pharmaceutical companies. [1] This claim is false and misleading on multiple levels.

While the Trump administration did launch TrumpRx.gov on February 5, 2026, offering discounted cash prices for 43 brand-name drugs from five manufacturers, independent fact-checkers and health policy experts found no evidence that these voluntary deals have led to broad decreases in drug prices. [2] The median list price increase for hundreds of brand-name drugs in early 2026 was 4% — exactly the same as 2025, according to drug pricing research firm 46brooklyn. [8]

All 16 pharmaceutical companies that signed "Most Favored Nation" (MFN) pricing deals with the Trump administration still raised prices on 872 brand-name drugs in January 2026, including cancer treatments, heart failure drugs, and COVID vaccines. [2]

The claim that Americans now pay the "lowest prices in the world" is directly contradicted by comprehensive international data. According to RAND Corporation analysis of 2022 data published in 2024, U.S. prescription drug prices are 2.78 times higher than other developed nations on average, and 4.22 times higher for brand-name drugs specifically. [4] Trump has also made mathematically impossible claims about cutting drug prices by "400, 500, and even 600 percent" or "1,200 to 1,500 percent," which fact-checkers note would require drug companies to pay patients to take medications. [5]

The Claim: "World's Lowest Prices"

The core claim originated from President Trump himself in a January 27, 2026 speech in Iowa, where he stated: "We now are paying the lowest price anywhere in the world for drugs." [1] This claim was formalized and amplified through the February 5, 2026 launch of TrumpRx.gov, a government website that prominently features the tagline "the world's lowest prices on prescription drugs."

The White House framed this as the culmination of Trump's May 12, 2025 Executive Order on "Delivering Most-Favored-Nation Prescription Drug Pricing to American Patients," which directed HHS to establish MFN price targets matching the lowest prices charged in OECD countries. [19] Following a September 29, 2025 compliance deadline with threats of tariffs and regulatory action, 16 pharmaceutical companies signed voluntary agreements to lower prices on select drugs for Medicaid and cash-paying customers.

The claim escalated over time, with Trump making increasingly exaggerated percentage claims. He stated he would cut drug prices by "1,200, 1,300, 1,400, 1,500%" and "400, 500, and even 600 percent." [5] These mathematical impossibilities — cutting a price by more than 100% would mean negative costs — suggest either fundamental misunderstanding of price reduction math or deliberate exaggeration for political effect.

What the Data Actually Shows

The claim that Americans now pay the "lowest prices in the world" is directly contradicted by comprehensive international data. RAND Corporation's 2024 analysis of 2022 data found U.S. drug prices are 2.78× higher than 33 OECD comparison countries on average. For brand-name drugs specifically, U.S. prices are 4.22× higher. [4]

The U.S. represents 62% of global pharmaceutical sales but only 24% of volume consumed — proof Americans are paying far more per unit. [4] According to Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker, Americans spent $1,126 per person on prescription drugs in 2019 compared to an average of $552 in comparable countries — roughly double. [6]

Trump's claim implies widespread drug price reductions. The evidence shows the opposite. Drug companies raised prices on 872 brand-name drugs in January 2026, with a median increase of 4% — identical to 2025. [8] Every pharmaceutical company that signed an MFN deal with Trump still increased list prices on at least some drugs in January 2026. [2]

Rena Conti, health economist at Boston University, concluded the negotiations "don't appear to have translated into actual savings for people at the pharmacy counter" except in rare cases. [1]

The TrumpRx Reality: When Generics Are Cheaper

The TrumpRx.gov website claims to offer "the world's lowest prices," but independent analysis revealed at least 18 of the 43 brand-name drugs on TrumpRx have cheaper generic alternatives available via GoodRx or Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs. [3]

For example, the antidepressant Pristiq costs $200/month on TrumpRx but only $16.65/month for the generic version on Cost Plus Drugs. A patient buying the brand-name version via TrumpRx would spend $2,401.20 a year, compared to just $320.88 for the generic using GoodRx or Cost Plus — a savings of over $2,000 annually by avoiding TrumpRx. [12]

Similarly, Colestid (cholesterol) costs $67.20/month on TrumpRx versus $21.70/month for the generic version. [3]

Many TrumpRx prices are powered by GoodRx's existing pricing infrastructure, with identical or near-identical prices for brand-name drugs that were already available. [3] A White House spokesperson acknowledged that "it's possible cheaper alternatives are available for some products listed on TrumpRx," but defended the site's value as providing the lowest-cost option for branded products specifically.

The Mathematical Impossibility of Trump's Percentage Claims

Math Reality Check

Cutting a $100 drug price by 100% = $0. Cutting by 500% would = -$400 (drug company pays you $400 to take the medication).

Trump's claims of cutting prices by "400-600%" or "1,200-1,500%" are mathematically incoherent. Even if Trump's policy does produce a big decline in drug prices, he can't actually cut the price of any product by 500% to 1,500%. If he magically got the companies to reduce the prices of all of their drugs to $0, that would be a 100% cut. [5]

Geoffrey Joyce, Director of Health Policy at USC Schaeffer Center, called it "total fiction," noting that "[A 500% reduction] would amount to drug companies paying customers, rather than the other way around." [5]

Timothy McBride, Professor at Washington University School of Public Health, stated: "Just not logical. A 500% price reduction would mean that a drug that now costs $100 would cost negative $400. I know of no drug company in the world that would give a person $400 to buy a drug that they are now pricing at $100." [5]

Even Fox News host John Roberts fact-checked Trump on air: "If you cut it by 4-, 5-, 600 percent, the drug companies are actually paying you to take their product." [5]

Trump appears to be confusing the percentage by which U.S. prices exceed foreign prices (e.g., 520% more for Zepbound in U.S. vs. Europe) with the percentage by which prices would be cut — two completely different calculations.

Who Actually Benefits from TrumpRx

The impact of TrumpRx is far narrower than Trump's universal-sounding claims suggest. TrumpRx discounts only apply to cash-paying patients. Purchases don't count toward insurance deductibles or out-of-pocket maximums. [9]

Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, and VA beneficiaries are explicitly excluded from TrumpRx cash-pay pricing. [9] Only 43 drugs from 5 manufacturers are covered as of February 2026, compared to thousands of prescription drugs available in the U.S.

Yunan Ji, health policy expert at Georgetown McDonough School of Business, concluded: "The prices are absolutely cheaper than list prices, but only for cash patients or patients whose insurance does not sufficiently cover these drugs. For a small share of the market, it will deliver meaningful savings, but it is not going to change the whole drug pricing landscape for most Americans." [9]

There are rare genuine wins: GLP-1 weight loss drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Zepbound) and fertility medications saw meaningful reductions for uninsured patients, as these are often not covered by insurance to begin with. [18]

The Biden IRA Connection: The Real Price Reductions

Trump's claims omit crucial context: the most significant drug price reductions in 2026 came from the Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), not Trump's MFN deals.

The first 10 drugs negotiated under the IRA (passed in 2022 under Biden) saw price discounts of 38-79% off list prices, with $6 billion in projected Medicare savings in 2026 plus $1.5 billion in patient out-of-pocket savings. [10] Specific IRA price cuts included Januvia (-79%), Eliquis (-43%), Jardiance (-44%), and Fiasp insulin (-75%) — all taking effect in January 2026 due to IRA negotiations. [10]

There's political irony here: Trump's administration is implementing a program created by Democrats that Republicans opposed. KFF noted both parties can claim credit: Trump for delivering lower prices, Democrats for building the program. [7]

When comparing actual prices, Biden's IRA Medicare negotiations delivered lower costs than Trump's MFN deals on key drugs. Eliquis, a blood thinner taken by millions, costs $231/month under IRA negotiations vs. $346/month on TrumpRx — a $115/month difference favoring the statutory IRA approach. [14]

The Human Cost: Patient Testimonials

Critical Impact

Despite Trump's claims of "world's lowest prices," medication rationing in America reached crisis levels in 2025-2026. More than 4 in 10 Americans (42%) were prescribed a medication they couldn't afford, while 30% cut back on food or clothing to pay for prescriptions. Nearly 1 in 4 Americans report difficulty affording medications. [15]

Medicare patients report seeing no relief at the pharmacy counter despite announced price reductions. One patient stated: "I am on Eliquis and I will be paying MORE for it in 2026 than I did in 2025!" [15]

Another patient reported: "I can't afford my meds because my rent went up and cost of food. My Eliquis doubled in out-of-pocket price" in 2026. [15]

A third patient questioned: "My Xarelto 90-day supply cost $1,980.99 in 2025, and when I refilled in January 2026, it is the exact same price. When does this reduction take effect?" [15]

National statistics reveal the depth of the crisis:

  • 1 in 4 Americans report difficulty affording medications [15]
  • 30% of patients have skipped or rationed doses due to cost [15]
  • 1 in 5 U.S. adults did not fill a prescription because of price [15]
  • 1.3 million insulin users (17% of all insulin users) rationed insulin for cost-related reasons in 2021 [15]

Lower-income respondents (earning less than $50,000/year) and individuals with disabilities are significantly more likely to report rationing medication. [15]

Medicare Part D Cost Crisis

The Congressional Budget Office revealed in a February 2026 analysis that Medicare Part D spending is surging far beyond projections, threatening to add $500 billion in unexpected costs over the next decade. [11]

Recent bids from private insurance plans anticipate a 35% increase in annual per-enrollee costs in 2026 — seven times higher than the 5% increase CBO had projected. [11] This massive discrepancy stems from insurer overhead costs revised upward from 6.5% to 11.4% of net benefit costs, specialty drug spending surges, and tariff uncertainty creating instability for insurers.

From 2017 to 2022, annual growth in projected per-enrollee costs for Part D plans ranged from a 5% reduction to a 4% increase. Since then, projections have skyrocketed: 20% in 2024, 42% in 2025, and 35% in 2026. [11]

The Inflation Reduction Act's out-of-pocket cap provides real relief for high-spending Medicare beneficiaries, with a $2,100 annual out-of-pocket maximum in 2026, up from $2,000 in 2025. [20] An estimated 3.2 million Americans are expected to benefit from the cap in 2025, rising to 4 million by 2029.

State of the Union Context and Political Dimensions

President Trump will deliver his 2026 State of the Union address on February 24, 2026 at 9 PM ET to a joint session of Congress. [21] Trump indicated he plans to focus heavily on the economy, including drug pricing as a major policy achievement.

FactCheck.org published "Trump Misleads on Drug Pricing Deals" on February 18, 2026 — six days before the SOTU — specifically to address expected claims. [1] Key pre-emptive findings included no evidence Trump's deals have led to broad decreases in drug prices, and that the median list price increase for brand-name drugs in 2026 was 4% — identical to 2025.

Consumer advocacy organization Public Citizen filed a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) lawsuit against HHS and the Department of Commerce for failing to provide documents related to Trump's MFN drug pricing policy. [16] The administration refused to provide copies of MFN agreements between government agencies and pharmaceutical companies, with Pfizer stating the agreement was "confidential."

During quarterly earnings calls, Big Pharma's MFN deals with Trump "barely came up," according to Axios analysis — "yet another sign that the agreements were mutually beneficial for both parties." [13] The deals included three-year tariff exemptions and commitments to avoid regulatory penalties, creating quid-pro-quo arrangements beyond pure price concessions.

Congressional Action: PBM Reform

On February 3, 2026, Congress passed and President Trump signed the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026, ending a partial government shutdown and including landmark PBM (Pharmacy Benefit Manager) reform that will reshape drug pricing infrastructure. [17]

Key reforms effective in 2028 include:

  • Medicare Part D plans must compensate PBMs solely through flat, bona fide service fees that reflect fair-market value, eliminating rebate-linked compensation [17]
  • PBMs must remit 100% of rebates, fees, alternative discounts, and other remuneration from manufacturers to plan clients on a quarterly basis [17]
  • PBMs must provide employer health plans with detailed, semiannual reports on net drug spending, rebates, and "spread pricing" arrangements [17]
  • "Any willing pharmacy" provision: starting in 2028, prescription drug plan sponsors must allow any pharmacy meeting standard contract terms to participate in their networks [17]

The Congressional Budget Office projects the legislation will reduce the federal deficit by $2.1 billion over 10 years. [17] The PBM reform passed with bipartisan support, representing rare common ground on healthcare between Republicans and Democrats.

Senate Democrats outlined a drug pricing agenda in February 2026 to expand Medicare drug price negotiation to 30 drugs per year starting in 2026 (could save $500-550 billion over 10 years), extend negotiated prices to Medicaid and commercial insurance plans (potentially doubling savings to $1 trillion over 10 years), and prohibit drug companies from charging more in the U.S. than abroad (93% public support). [22]

Conclusion

President Trump's claim that Americans are "now paying the lowest price anywhere in the world for drugs" is false and contradicted by comprehensive evidence. While TrumpRx.gov offers discounts on 43 brand-name drugs for cash-paying patients, U.S. drug prices remain 2.78× higher than peer nations on average, and all 16 companies with MFN deals raised prices on 872 drugs in January 2026.

The most significant 2026 drug price reductions came from the Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act, not Trump's voluntary MFN deals. At least 18 drugs on TrumpRx have cheaper generic alternatives available elsewhere, and TrumpRx purchases don't count toward insurance deductibles.

Trump's mathematically impossible claims of cutting prices by "400-600%" or "1,200-1,500%" reveal either fundamental misunderstanding or deliberate exaggeration. Meanwhile, medication rationing in America continues at crisis levels, with 42% of Americans prescribed medications they can't afford.

The gap between Trump's rhetoric and pharmacy counter reality underscores the need for transparent, statutory drug pricing reforms rather than confidential voluntary deals that leave the fundamental pricing structure intact.
