Verdict: MISLEADING & UNVERIFIED

DHS inflated voluntary departure figures by 4x-10x

The Department of Homeland Security claimed 1.6-2.2 million undocumented immigrants "self-deported" in 2025 based on flawed Census survey data with a ±830,000 margin of error. Independent experts estimate actual voluntary departures at 210,000-405,000 — roughly 1/5 to 1/8 of DHS claims. The administration misused inappropriate survey data, ignored Census Bureau warnings, conflated multiple categories (deportations, deaths, status changes), and misinterpreted fear-driven survey non-response as mass exodus. Zero methodology was released, NPR data requests went unanswered, and verified CBP Home app participation reached only 100,000 — 4.5% of claimed total.

Executive Summary

The Department of Homeland Security claimed that between 1.6 million and 2.2 million undocumented immigrants "voluntarily self-deported" from the United States between January and December 2025.[1][2][3] DHS Secretary Kristi Noem announced on August 14, 2025: "According to the Bureau of Labor and Statistics, we have 1.6 million illegal aliens that have gone home voluntarily."[4] This figure was later revised to 1.9 million in September and 2.2 million by December 2025.[5]

However, independent immigration experts, fact-checkers, and research institutions have found these claims to be vastly overstated and fundamentally unverifiable.[6][7][8] The Center for Migration Studies called the statement "a self-serving fantasy," describing it as based on a flawed interpretation of population data and estimating that a more realistic number was around 1/10th of the claimed self-deportations.[9] The Brookings Institution estimated around 310,000 deported and only 210,000 to 405,000 voluntary departures due to enforcement activity — far below DHS's claims.[10]

The core methodological problem: DHS relied on an analysis by the Center for Immigration Studies (CIS) of the Census Bureau's Current Population Survey (CPS), which has a margin of error of ±830,000 people for the foreign-born population — nearly as large as the claimed decline itself.[4] The Census Bureau explicitly warns against using the CPS to estimate changes in immigrant populations, stating that apparent declines "can also reflect survey non-response, coverage error or the impact of population controls."[11] Multiple experts concluded that the reported drop primarily reflects immigrants' fear-driven reluctance to respond to government surveys during an intense enforcement campaign, not actual departures from the United States.[12][13][14]

The Claim: 1.6 Million "Went Home Voluntarily"

On August 14, 2025, DHS Secretary Kristi Noem issued a press release titled "Secretary Noem Announces 1.6 Million Illegal Aliens Have Left the U.S."[16] Five days later, at a White House event, she expanded: "According to the Bureau of Labor and Statistics, we have 1.6 million illegal aliens that have gone home voluntarily. They left at the encouragement of President Trump with a chance to return legally."[4]

The claim originated from a blog post published two days earlier by the Center for Immigration Studies (CIS), a restrictionist think tank.[15] CIS researcher Steven Camarota analyzed the Census Bureau's Current Population Survey (CPS) and estimated a 1.6 million decline in the unauthorized immigrant population from January to July 2025.

DHS presented this third-party estimate as established fact and used it to claim historic success of the Trump administration's enforcement policies. Over the following six months, DHS systematically escalated the claim:

  • August 2025: 1.6 million self-deportations
  • September 2025: "Over 2 million illegal aliens" left the U.S. (1.6M self-deported + 527K formally deported)[5]
  • December 2025: 1.9 million self-deportations + 622,000 formal deportations = 2.5 million total[2]
  • January 2026: 2.2 million self-deportations claimed[3]

The escalation from 1.6 million to 2.2 million represents a 37.5% increase in claimed self-deportations — yet DHS released no new data, no methodology, and no verification to support the increase. NPR repeatedly requested supporting data from DHS and received no response.[6]

The Data Source Problem: Census Bureau Warnings Ignored

The Census Bureau explicitly cautions against using the Current Population Survey (CPS) to estimate foreign-born population changes.[26][27] The Bureau's official guidance states:

"While the change in the foreign-born population in a survey over time can imply emigration, it can also reflect survey non-response, coverage error or the impact of population controls."[11]

The CPS surveys approximately 60,000 households monthly, compared to 3.4+ million for the American Community Survey (ACS).[4] This small sample size creates a margin of error of ±830,000 for foreign-born population estimates in July 2025 — nearly as large as the claimed 1.6 million decline.

Robert Warren of the Center for Migration Studies explained: "Researchers generally rely on the bureau's more robust American Community Survey. That's because the Current Population Survey's sample size has a large margin of error."[4]

The Census Bureau reported that the United States experienced an unprecedented shift toward negative net migration in 2025: "Currently, the estimates of NIM are trending toward negative net migration. If those trends continue, it would be the first time the United States has seen net negative migration in more than 50 years."[27] However, the Bureau emphasized that this decline reflects multiple confounding factors beyond actual emigration.

The Methodology Critique: Survey Non-Response, Not Mass Exodus

The St. Louis Federal Reserve published a comprehensive analysis showing that the CPS population decline mostly reflects decreased immigrant survey participation, not actual emigration.[11]

CPS response rates declined from 90.3% in January 2013 to 67.1% in July 2025.[11] More critically, non-naturalized immigrant response rates dropped 16.6% from January to November 2025 — roughly 10 percentage points more than native-born and naturalized citizens.[11]

Julia Gelatt of the Migration Policy Institute stated: "I see the 1.6 million as a pretty shaky estimate, and one that could be explained away by fewer immigrants feeling comfortable answering a government survey."[17]

A Kaiser Family Foundation/NY Times survey found 41% of all immigrants (including legal residents and citizens) worried they or a family member could be detained or deported, and 14% said they or a family member had avoided going to church or on family outings in 2025 because of concerns about drawing attention to immigration status.[9]

Jeffrey Passel of Pew Research Center noted: "For [20]25, all we have is CPS data and that points to a decline from a January peak of immigrants. How big the decline is could be affected by the decrease in response rates."[17]

Category Conflation: Deportations, Deaths, and Status Changes

Even the original researcher behind the 1.6 million figure acknowledged it does not represent purely voluntary self-deportations.

Steven Camarota of CIS told PolitiFact that the "overwhelming majority" of the 1.6 million would have left on their own, but admitted the number "also includes immigrants who were deported, died, left voluntarily, and those whose status changed, such as by getting asylum."[4]

This directly contradicts DHS's characterization of the figure as representing purely "self-deportations." The 1.6 million estimate conflates:

  • Formal ICE/CBP deportations (~239,000 as of June 30, 2025)[4]
  • Deaths of undocumented immigrants
  • Status changes (asylum grants, legal residency, etc.)
  • Actual voluntary departures
  • Survey non-response (immigrants who are still in the U.S. but declined to participate in government surveys)

The CIS report itself acknowledged: "Immigrants in the U.S. illegally might be more reluctant to complete the government's Current Population Survey or identify themselves as foreign-born."[35] It also noted: "Administrative data needed to estimate the unauthorized immigrant population is unavailable, further increasing the uncertainty of our estimate."[35]

Austin Kocher, a researcher at the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse (TRAC), wrote: "DHS Claims 622,000 Deportations, Here's Why That Number Might Be Meaningless."[25] Kocher documented systematic opacity in DHS data reporting.

Evidence Deep-Dive: Labor Markets, Court Statistics, and Economic Impact

Labor Market Evidence

If 1.6-2.2 million workers actually left the United States, labor markets should show dramatic disruption. Independent economic analysis found minimal evidence of such an exodus.

The number of foreign-born workers in the labor force fell by 1.6 million (about 5%) from March 2025 through mid-2025.[37] However, employment expanded by only 584,000 in 2025 — the weakest annual pace since 2020 and second-weakest since the 2009 financial crisis.[38] A whopping 84% of those gains occurred in the first four months of the year, before immigration enforcement intensified.[39]

University of Michigan economist Betsey Stevenson noted: "Looking at the last three months, we're creating fewer jobs by quite a lot. Instead of something more like 180,000 [jobs per month], we're creating something closer to 35,000 jobs per month."[37]

The Minneapolis Federal Reserve found that around half of the decline in monthly employment payroll growth is attributable to declining net immigration, with estimates ranging from 40-60%.[40] This suggests workforce reductions in the range of several hundred thousand, not 1.6-2.2 million.

Border Crossing Statistics Show Declining Arrivals, Not Mass Departures

CBP border encounter data for FY 2025-2026 shows unprecedented declines in new arrivals, but does not support claims of mass outflows:

  • October 2025: 30,561 total nationwide encounters — lowest start to a fiscal year ever recorded, 29% lower than previous record low[41]
  • November 2025: 30,367 encounters, bringing combined Oct-Nov total to 60,940 — 28% lower than previous two-month low[42]
  • FY 2025 Full Year: Southwest Border apprehensions totaled 237,538 — the lowest level since 1970[43]

These statistics demonstrate a collapse in new arrivals, not evidence of mass departures. CBP does not track voluntary departures or emigration from the U.S. interior.[44]

Immigration Court Voluntary Departure Statistics

Out of 57,531 completed cases in December 2025, immigration judges issued 38,215 removal orders and 7,359 voluntary departure grants.[51] For full FY 2026 through December 2025, judges issued 149,706 total deportation orders.[51]

Approximately 40,000 court-ordered voluntary departures occurred in 2025, compared to around 10,000 in 2024 — a 4x increase.[52] However, this represents only 1.8% of DHS's claimed 2.2 million self-deportations.

Attorneys argue that restricted bond access and low asylum success rates create hopelessness, with individuals prioritizing immediate freedom from custody over long-term pursuit of legal residency.[52] Asylum grant rates plummeted from over 50% monthly in 2022-2024 to only 29% by December 2025.[52]

Contemporary Context: Midterm Messaging and Historical Precedent

2026 Midterm Election Messaging

The DHS self-deportation claims have become a cornerstone of Republican campaign messaging heading into the November 2026 midterm elections. DHS reports it has deported more than 675,000 migrants since Trump's return to office and claims some 2.2 million others have "self-deported."[54]

Senator Katie Britt (R-AL) stated: "President Trump is not going to back away from the mission, the mission that American people said they wanted him to complete, and that is securing our border and making sure that we actually do interior enforcement."[55]

After federal immigration agents fatally shot Alex Pretti, a Minneapolis nurse, on January 24, 2026, Senate Democrats refused to pass the annual DHS funding bill without significant accountability reforms.[56] This triggered a partial DHS shutdown in February 2026, though ICE and CBP continued enforcement operations.[57]

Rep. Delia Ramirez (D-IL) stated it was vital that Democrats "begin building a case against the agency ahead of the midterm elections."[58]

The White House directed DHS to investigate alleged voter fraud by naturalized citizens, escalating concerns among federal prosecutors about frightening voters from going to the polls in upcoming midterms.[59]

Economic Impact Projections

The Brookings Institution found that immigration policy under Trump's second term will reduce 2025 GDP growth by between 0.1 and 0.4 percentage points, or by $30 to $110 billion.[10] Reduced immigration will weaken consumer spending by an estimated $60-$110 billion combined over 2025-2026.[10]

The Peterson Institute for International Economics found that deporting 1.3 million immigrants would raise prices by 1.5% by 2028, while deporting 8.3 million immigrants would raise prices by 9.1%.[62] PIIE estimates it would cost 44,000 U.S.-born workers their jobs for every half million immigrants removed from the labor force.[63]

Historical Precedent: Operation Wetback

No previous administration has claimed self-deportation numbers on the scale of the Trump administration's 2025-2026 claims. The closest historical parallel is Operation Wetback (1954).[65][66]

Operation Wetback was a program of massive apprehensions and deportations aimed at undocumented Mexican nationals, carried out by the U.S. Border Patrol under President Eisenhower.[67] A key aspect was using fear to induce self-deportation — hundreds of thousands of mostly Mexican undocumented immigrants were forcibly removed or self-deported during the campaign.[67]

Alexander Aviña, associate professor of history at Arizona State University, noted: "There is this element of spectacle that both administrations use. They try to induce fear and terror in immigrant populations to get them to self-deport, and that's something they tried to do with Operation Wetback."[67]

The historical record on Operation Wetback's scale is heavily contested. Officially, the Eisenhower administration claimed it carried out more than 1 million "returns."[68] However, historian Kelly Lytle Hernandez argues the number of actual deportees was drastically lower — likely closer to 300,000.[70] Historians believe many people were deported more than once, inflating the count.[68]

Doris Meissner, former INS Commissioner, stated: "Operation Wetback was lawless; it was arbitrary; it was based on a lot of xenophobia, and it resulted in sizable large-scale violations of people's rights, including the forced deportation of U.S. citizens."[71]

CBP Home App (Verified Self-Deportation Program)

The government launched the CBP Home app with cash incentives ($1,000, later raised to $3,000) to encourage voluntary departures.[18] Actual participation:

  • April 2025: ~2,500 registrations[19]
  • Mid-2025: ~13,000 participants over six months[20]
  • January 2026: "Nearly 100,000 users"[18]

Even taking the highest figure, the actual verified self-deportation program served only 100,000 people — 1/16th of DHS's claimed 1.6 million self-deportations and 4.5% of the claimed 2.2 million.

Systemic Data Quality Problems: GAO Findings on ICE Reporting

The Government Accountability Office (GAO) identified systematic data quality problems with ICE deportation reporting in a comprehensive audit published in 2024.[28]

GAO found that ICE systematically understates the total number of people it detains in its public reports. ICE excludes people who were first booked into certain temporary facilities before being transferred to immigration detention facilities — an exclusion amounting to tens of thousands of people.[28]

In FY 2022 alone, ICE's reporting excluded detentions of 136,706 individuals. ICE does not fully explain its rationale for these exclusions or its methodology.[28]

GAO concluded: "As a result, Congress and the public may not have a complete understanding of how to interpret the data."[28] GAO also found that DHS and ICE lack clear performance goals and measures for immigration detention facility inspection programs.[29]

Deficiencies related to ICE's 14 core detention standards — including environmental health and safety, medical care, and food service — accounted for approximately 74% of the total deficiencies identified across all inspections reviewed.[29]

Congressional Testimony: Secretary Noem's Contradictions

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem testified before Congress multiple times in 2025-2026, making escalating claims about deportation numbers while contradicting herself on key facts.

On May 20, 2025, Noem testified before the Senate Homeland Security Committee that the Trump administration's FY 2026 budget request included over $175 billion for DHS "to implement the President's mass removal campaign."[30] She claimed ICE arrested a "105% increase" in suspected gang members compared to FY 2024, and a "700% increase" in known or suspected terrorists.[30]

In a contentious hearing on December 11, 2025, Rep. Seth Magaziner (D-RI) asked Noem directly if DHS had deported any military veterans. Noem stated the department "have not deported U.S. citizens or military veterans."[31]

Congressman Seth Moulton publicly released a letter from Secretary Noem dated September 2, 2025, which directly contradicted this testimony. The letter stated: "Regarding your question on the number of veterans that have been removed since January 20, 2025, ICE has removed eight veterans."[31]

Rep. Magaziner also introduced via Zoom a Purple Heart recipient named Sae Joon Park, who was shot twice while serving with the U.S. Army in Panama in 1989, and whom Magaziner said was deported to Korea by the Trump administration.[32]

According to internal ICE data obtained by journalists, most people arrested by immigration agents in 2025 did not have criminal records — contradicting the administration's claims of targeting only dangerous criminals.[34]

Conclusion: A Self-Serving Fantasy Built on Flawed Data

The Department of Homeland Security's claim that 1.6-2.2 million undocumented immigrants "voluntarily self-deported" in 2025 represents one of the most significant instances of government statistical manipulation in recent immigration enforcement history. The claim fails basic methodological scrutiny on every level:

  1. Inappropriate data source — Used Census survey data explicitly flagged as unsuitable for measuring immigrant population changes
  2. Massive margin of error — ±830,000 for a claimed 1.6M decline
  3. Ignored confounding factors — Survey non-response among frightened immigrants misinterpreted as emigration
  4. Category conflation — Mixed deportations, deaths, status changes, and voluntary departures into single figure
  5. Zero verification — No methodology released, no independent audit, no response to data requests
  6. Implausible scale — Claimed exodus would require 200,000/month departures with no observed economic impact
  7. Contradicted by own program — Actual CBP Home app participation was 4.5% of claimed total

Independent experts across the political spectrum — from the Migration Policy Institute to the Brookings Institution to the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse — agree actual voluntary departures were likely 200,000-405,000, or roughly one-fifth to one-eighth of DHS's claims.

The Peterson Institute for International Economics called the 2.2 million figure "implausibly large and uncorroborated," arguing it would "imply absurd gyrations in the labor market."[13]

The real story: Aggressive enforcement drove immigrants "deeper into the shadows" rather than out of the country, reducing their willingness to respond to government surveys. DHS then misinterpreted this fear-driven survey decline as proof of its policy success, creating inflated statistics to justify continued aggressive enforcement — a self-reinforcing cycle of manufactured evidence and escalating claims.

As the St. Louis Federal Reserve concluded: "The question is whether the decline in surveyed immigrants indeed means an actual drop in net immigration or simply fewer immigrants choosing to participate in the survey. The CPS population estimate showing a 1.86-million decline in the number of immigrants mostly reflects a decrease in the participation of immigrants in the CPS."[11]
