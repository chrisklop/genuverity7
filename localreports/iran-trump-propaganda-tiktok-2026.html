The cost barrier to state-level information warfare has effectively collapsed.

07. Hollywood as a Weapon

Both sides weaponize cultural production — but in different ways.

The US Approach

The White House directly appropriated 15+ Hollywood franchises without permission, splicing fictional heroism with real bombing footage. This technique borrows the emotional resonance of beloved characters (Iron Man, Maximus) and transfers it onto military operations. Ben Stiller called it what it is: “a propaganda machine” [3][4]. The irony, as critics noted, is that several films used actually satirize or critique the violence being celebrated: Tropic Thunder is a war satire, Breaking Bad’s Walter White is a villain, and Kylo Ren is a dark-side antagonist [1].

The Iran Approach

Rather than stealing existing IP, Iran created original content in a recognizable style (Lego animation) that functions as a parallel entertainment product. It tells a story — heroes and villains, cause and effect, triumph and defeat — using visual language familiar to global internet audiences. The Lego format itself is a strategic choice: it appears innocent and playful while delivering deeply political messaging.

The Copyright Dimension

Wikipedia now documents the systematic pattern under “Use of copyrighted works by the second Trump administration” [26]. The Isaac Hayes estate won a court injunction in 2024. Ben Stiller publicly demanded removal. The Pokémon Company formally objected. Yet the pattern continues — each new unauthorized use tests the boundary between political speech and copyright infringement in a grey zone no court has definitively resolved for a sitting president.

08. Platform Accountability — TikTok’s Role

TikTok occupies a paradoxical position: the platform is blocked in Iran, yet Iranian state-affiliated content circulates freely to global audiences. This creates an asymmetry where Iran can project propaganda through TikTok without its own citizens having access to counter-narratives.

TikTok’s enforcement record shows awareness but incomplete solutions:

  • February 2024: 65 Iran-backed accounts removed [20]
  • May 2024: Iran-based UK-targeting network dismantled [20]
  • October 2024: 26,964 network accounts removed (all countries) [19]
  • January 2025: 3,743 accounts from previously disrupted networks removed [19]

The challenge: when official White House content and IRGC-linked propaganda are both posted to TikTok and re-amplified by third-party commentators, platform moderation faces an impossible question — which state’s propaganda should be labeled? The current answer appears to be: neither, as long as it drives engagement.
