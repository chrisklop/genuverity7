TL;DR — VERDICT: FALSE

No congressional hearing ever took place. The videos are entirely fabricated.

A YouTube channel called “Homeless People” used spliced footage from unrelated real hearings and a synthetic AI voiceover to falsely claim Rep. Thomas Massie exposed Stephen Miller funneling $890 million through Cayman Islands shell companies. A second video used the same template to fabricate Rep. Ted Lieu exposing a $6.2 billion offshore transfer. Both were debunked by Snopes on April 4 and April 20, 2026. Miller’s documented net worth — drawn from OGE financial disclosures — is approximately $1.8M–$4.8M total, a universe wholly incompatible with the fabricated billions. The operation is consistent with a 26-channel AI content farm identified by DFRLab as having accumulated nearly 2 billion views using the same production methods.

Executive Summary

In late March 2026, a viral AI-generated YouTube video falsely claimed that Republican U.S. Rep. Thomas Massie of Kentucky had exposed White House Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller for funneling $890 million through Cayman Islands shell companies during a congressional hearing. [1] The video — uploaded March 22, 2026, by a YouTube channel called “Homeless People” — was constructed using footage spliced from an unrelated September 2025 congressional hearing and a March 5, 2026 White House briefing, with a synthetic AI voiceover narrating fabricated accusations. [2] Snopes rated the claim FALSE on April 4, 2026, citing the absence of any credible news reporting, Massie’s non-membership on the House Oversight Committee, AI voiceover glitches, and mismatch between the closed captioning and the audio. [2]

A virtually identical hoax emerged in April 2026: the same channel published a video claiming Democratic Rep. Ted Lieu had confronted Miller at a congressional hearing over a $6.2 billion wire transfer to the Cayman Islands. [3] That video recycled footage from the January 22, 2026 House Judiciary hearing where Lieu questioned Special Counsel Jack Smith about federal criminal investigations into President Trump — a hearing with no connection to Miller or offshore finance. [8] The existence of two nearly identical hoaxes — with different congressional figures but the same target, the same fictional dollar amounts, and the same production style — confirms a systematized AI content farm operation, not isolated organic disinformation. [7]

Section 1: The Claim — What the Viral Video Said

The primary hoax video, titled “WHO Got Paid?: Massie’s Bank Records Question Miller Refused to Answer for 101 Seconds,” made several specific and sensational claims: that Rep. Thomas Massie had obtained Stephen Miller’s bank records, that those records showed $890 million transferred through Cayman Islands shell companies, that Miller refused to answer Massie’s questions for a full 101 seconds during a House Oversight Committee hearing, and that the confrontation was being suppressed by mainstream media. [1]

The video’s title formula — a dramatic unresolved allegation paired with a precise time figure (“101 Seconds”) — is a deliberate authenticity signal. Some versions of the claim inflated the figure further, to $2.7 billion. [2] The narrative used emotionally charged, non-journalistic language consistent with AI-generated content designed to provoke outrage rather than inform. The World Economic Forum’s 2026 analysis of AI disinformation noted that algorithms “reward engagement, often financially,” causing “outrage [to deliver] more quickly before fact-checking occurs.” [11]

A Facebook post amplifying the video appeared two days after upload, on March 24, 2026, and ad-revenue-driven blogs republished the story with display advertising alongside it. [2] If the story were true, journalists at AP or Reuters would have reported it widely. That was not the case — no credible outlet published any account of the alleged confrontation. [4]

Section 2: The Patient Zero — “Homeless People” YouTube Channel

Snopes investigated the origin channel and determined it operates under the name “Homeless People” on YouTube. The channel “uses artificial intelligence tools to create inspiring or shocking stories” about public figures, with no verifiable editorial operation behind it. [2] The channel’s content strategy is not accidental — it follows a documented AI content farm playbook: select a high-profile, politically polarizing target; use real congressional footage as visual scaffolding; layer AI narration over the footage making fabricated criminal accusations; deploy the video on YouTube’s monetized ad network; and seed it through Facebook and ad-revenue blogs for maximum amplification before platform moderation can act.

The “Homeless People” channel is not an isolated bad actor. The DFRLab identified a 26-channel YouTube network in March 2026 operating with the same production methodology across war coverage — 1,965,892 collective subscribers, 1.8 billion lifetime views, and five channels featuring AI-generated news anchors positioned behind digital news desks. [7] As of March 23, 2026, 23 of those 26 channels remained active and were serving advertisements, confirming that enforcement does not automatically precede monetization. [15]

The vidIQ platform, which tracks YouTube policy enforcement, confirmed that despite YouTube “clarifying its ‘inauthentic content’ policy in July 2025 to explicitly target channels that churn out templated, low-effort videos at scale,” channels in AI content farm niches continued to earn ad revenue — with CPM rates of $0.50–$2.50, meaning even modest view counts generate income. [18] That economic incentive explains the systematic, repeatable nature of the operation.

Section 3: How the Hoax Was Built

The Massie/$890M video was assembled from two real footage sources stitched together beneath an AI voiceover: an unrelated September 2025 congressional hearing, and a March 5, 2026 White House briefing in which Miller appeared. Neither had any connection to Massie, offshore finance, or Cayman Islands shell companies. [2] Over these real visuals, a text-to-speech AI system narrated the fabricated confrontation.

The Massie committee error is particularly revealing. Massie’s actual committees for the 119th Congress are Transportation & Infrastructure (subcommittees: Aviation; Highways and Transit) and Judiciary (subcommittees: Courts, Intellectual Property, Artificial Intelligence, and the Internet; and The Constitution and Limited Government). He was removed from the Rules Committee in January 2025 after voting against Speaker Johnson’s reelection. The Select Subcommittee on the Weaponization of the Federal Government was a 118th Congress body and is not among his current 119th Congress assignments. [21] [22] The House Oversight and Accountability Committee does not appear in his assignments — a fact verifiable in under 30 seconds on massie.house.gov. The AI system generating the script hallucinated a plausible-sounding but incorrect institutional detail and no human editor caught it before publication.

This failure mode is a specific vulnerability of AI content farm production. Copyleaks AI Detector achieves up to 99.12% accuracy on raw AI text, but accuracy drops to 0% when content is humanized or edited. [23] The hybrid approach of pairing real video with AI audio creates a detection gap that automated tools struggle to close — which is precisely why the primary forensic markers here are behavioral and contextual rather than algorithmic.

Section 4: The Parallel Hoax — Ted Lieu’s $6.2B Variant

The Lieu/$6.2B variant followed the same production template as the Massie video, but recycled entirely different source footage: the January 22, 2026 House Judiciary Committee hearing titled “Oversight of the Office of Special Counsel Jack Smith.” [19] That hearing was convened to examine Jack Smith’s investigations and prosecutions of President Donald Trump. The sole witness was former Special Counsel Jack Smith. Rep. Ted Lieu participated in his actual committee role — as a member of the House Judiciary Committee — and told reporters afterward: “I’m thrilled and frankly stunned House Republicans called Jack Smith to testify, because Jack Smith is reminding the American people of the criminal that Donald Trump is.” [20]

The hearing lasted approximately five hours and was extensively covered by AP, Reuters, NPR, CNN, NBC News, and CBS News. [8] There is no version of this hearing in which Lieu questioned anyone about Stephen Miller, offshore bank accounts, or DHS wire transfers. The AI content farm repurposed the visual credibility of a genuine, widely-covered event as a wrapper for a completely fabricated narration.

In the fabricated Lieu version, the narrator spelled out “W-H-O” letter-by-letter at the 40-second mark — a text-to-speech artifact characteristic of systems that treat “WHO” as an initialism (the World Health Organization) rather than a pronoun. [3] The fabricated narration claimed Lieu confronted Miller with a “$6.2 billion wire from a DHS emergency operations account to a corporate entity registered in the Cayman Islands” — a claim that is structurally incoherent: DHS emergency operations accounts are not mechanisms for private offshore wire transfers, and Miller holds no authority over DHS financing. [24]

The Lieu variant circulated at multiple YouTube URLs simultaneously, including /watch?v=typSYC02Gv4, /watch?v=ftNowXMzXAE, /watch?v=njEBNFFN1Tw, and /watch?v=yWRVTCWv3qU, accumulating hundreds of thousands of views before debunking. [3] Running two versions — one featuring a Republican congressman, one a Democrat — doubled the potential audience reach and made the claim appear to come from multiple ideological directions simultaneously.

Snopes debunked the Lieu variant on April 20, 2026, explicitly linking it to the same “Homeless People” YouTube channel responsible for the Massie version and confirming the two hoaxes as products of the same systematized operation. [3]

Section 5: Evidence Deep-Dive — DFRLab Network & Miller’s Actual Financial Profile

The fabricated scenario requires Miller to function as something like a Treasury official or shadow banker controlling hundreds of millions in offshore funds. His actual documented financial profile is the antithesis of this. According to ProPublica’s Trump Team Financial Disclosures, which draw directly from Miller’s OGE Form 278e filing, his income consists of: $509,000 salary/bonus from America First Legal; $202,000 from consulting services via Rushmore Ventures Inc.; and a government salary of $195,200 as White House Deputy Chief of Staff. [16] His reported assets include publicly traded stocks held in ranges (Palantir Technologies, CrowdStrike Holdings, Intel, U.S. Treasury Bonds — each valued at $100,000–$250,000) and a child brokerage account. Total disclosed assets: $1.8M–$4.8M. [16]

NOTUS reported separately, drawing from America First Legal’s IRS Form 990 tax filing, that Miller received a $75,000 bonus and a $100,000 “adjustment” payment before joining the Trump administration, bringing his 2024 total compensation to $567,000+, more than double his 2023 total of $266,600. Ethics experts cited in the NOTUS article questioned whether the payments constituted “golden parachutes” tied to entering government service. [17] Nothing in this documented financial history is consistent with $890 million or $6.2 billion in Cayman Islands holdings.

The DFRLab’s March 23, 2026 investigation into the broader AI YouTube content farm ecosystem documented a 26-channel network that had collectively accumulated nearly 2 billion views, with 1,965,892 subscribers across channels. [7] [15] Five channels featured AI-generated news anchors “positioned behind digital news desks.” The operation spread fabricated reports of destruction in Mykolaiv, Ukraine; strikes on Polish military infrastructure in Rzeszów; an alleged U.S. capture of Nicolas Maduro; and invented Armenian-Azerbaijani diplomatic ruptures. Video descriptions on at least one channel contained residual LLM response artifacts — “visible framing text that had not been edited out before publication, indicating minimal human oversight.” [7]

The DFRLab also observed that the network “maintains stylistic consistency between factual and fabricated segments” to “reduce friction for viewers attempting to distinguish between accurate reporting and invented narratives.” [15] This is precisely the technique employed in the Miller/Massie/Lieu videos: genuine congressional footage creates a visual context of authenticity that carries the viewer past the point at which the fabricated audio narration begins.

Section 6: Contemporary Context — Regulatory Vacuum, YouTube Policy Gap, Miller’s Actual Role

Miller’s documented role as White House Deputy Chief of Staff for Policy and Homeland Security Adviser involves daily 10 a.m. conference calls with agency officials to demand implementation updates, with influence spanning “immigration to homeland security to law enforcement… foreign policy, to trade and even to education,” per NBC News reporting with named sources including Steve Bannon, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, and Sen. Lindsey Graham. [24] Bannon described Miller as “the hammer” of the Trump agenda. Defense Secretary Hegseth stated Miller is most likely to call with after-hours emergencies: “It’s 100%.” None of this authority involves managing offshore accounts, wire transfers, or shell company networks.

The Miller/Massie hoax operates in a regulatory environment characterized by significant institutional gaps. The FCC proposed disclosure rules for AI-generated content in political advertisements in July 2024, requiring broadcast stations to ask advertisers whether their ads contain AI-generated content. [25] However, this proposed rule applies only to broadcast radio and television — explicitly not to digital platforms, social media, or YouTube — because the FCC’s statutory authority does not extend to online content. As of 2026, the rule has not been finalized. [25]

The FEC has not issued specific AI regulations and instead evaluates AI-related complaints “on a case-by-case basis” under existing rules prohibiting fraudulent misrepresentation. [26] State-level regulation is fragmented — several states passed AI disclosure requirements for campaign ads in 2024, but these apply to political campaigns, not to independent content farms producing fake news videos for ad revenue. [26] The result is a regulatory vacuum in which the “Homeless People” channel’s operations face no specific federal enforcement mechanism.

YouTube announced the expansion of its likeness detection tool to politicians, government officials, and journalists on March 10, 2026 — twelve days before the Massie/$890M hoax was uploaded. [27] The timing gap is significant: the detection tool requires participants to submit a government ID and video selfie for identity verification before enrollment, creating an opt-in, delayed protection window. YouTube’s enforcement against these videos applied only the lower-tier “altered or synthetic content” label — a disclosure mechanism, not a removal mechanism. Under YouTube’s tiered enforcement system, only videos determined to be “demonstrably malicious deepfakes intended to deceive, defame, or cause harm” qualify for immediate removal. [27] The Snopes debunks on April 4 and April 20, 2026 preceded any documented removal of the primary “Homeless People” channel.

Section 8: Conclusion

The Miller/Massie and Miller/Lieu AI hoaxes are a case study in how AI content farms have industrialized political disinformation. The operation demonstrates three core mechanics working simultaneously: target selection calibrated to pre-existing audience hostility (Miller’s 17% favorability rating ensures a large pool of viewers predisposed to believe the worst [10]); visual anchoring using genuine congressional footage to confer false authenticity before viewers can assess the audio; and monetized amplification through YouTube’s ad network and a blog republication ecosystem that earns revenue at each distribution node. [11] [12]

The two-version strategy — one Republican congressman, one Democrat — is particularly sophisticated. By deploying the same hoax template with figures from both parties, the “Homeless People” channel reached two distinct partisan audiences, made the claim appear independently sourced from multiple ideological directions, and doubled total potential reach. The parallel structure confirms this is not opportunistic disinformation but a deliberately engineered product.

The regulatory environment provides no specific deterrent. The FCC’s proposed AI disclosure rules cover only broadcast media, not YouTube. The FEC has no AI-specific rules for non-campaign content. YouTube’s enforcement response was a disclosure label rather than removal. [25] [26] [27] As PBS NewsHour and the Associated Press noted in April 2026: “Misinformation created with AI is being shared with unprecedented speed from an endless number of sources.” [13]

The X post from Snopes’ official account put it plainly: “Rep. Thomas Massie didn’t ‘expose’ Stephen Miller for funneling $890 million into offshore accounts through the Cayman Islands, as posts claimed. The false rumor originated from unreliable accounts.” [14] The challenge for audiences, platforms, and regulators is that by the time any of those reliable voices spoke, the videos had already accumulated hundreds of thousands of views and seeded the allegation into the social media ecosystem where corrections rarely reach the audiences who saw the original fabrication.
