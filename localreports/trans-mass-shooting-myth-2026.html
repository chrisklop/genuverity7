TL;DR

VERDICT: FALSE

Transgender individuals account for less than 1% of all mass shooters across every major database, while comprising approximately 1% of the U.S. population. Cisgender men commit 95-97.5% of all mass shootings. Trans people are statistically underrepresented as perpetrators and are actually 4 times more likely to be victims of violence. Since 2013, 399+ transgender people have been murdered in the United States, 70% by firearms.

Executive Summary

The claim that transgender people commit mass shootings at disproportionate rates is demonstrably false and represents one of the most persistent and harmful pieces of misinformation in contemporary American discourse. According to the Violence Prevention Project at Hamline University, of 201 mass shootings recorded in the United States between 1966 and 2025, 196 were committed by cisgender men (97.5%), 4 by cisgender women (2%), and only 1 (0.5%) by a transgender person — the March 2023 Nashville Covenant School shooting. [1] [2] The Gun Violence Archive counted 5,748 mass shootings between January 2013 and September 2025, with only 5 confirmed transgender shooters (0.09%). [3] For comparison, approximately 0.8-1.3% of the U.S. adult population identifies as transgender, meaning transgender individuals are statistically underrepresented among mass shooters, not overrepresented. [6] [7]

The Claim: An "Epidemic" of Transgender Mass Shooters

Following the February 10, 2026 shooting in Tumbler Ridge, Canada, and a domestic violence incident in Rhode Island days later, social media exploded with claims of an "epidemic of transgender violence." Elon Musk amplified the narrative on X/Twitter, Russia Today promoted it internationally, and Canadian politician Tara Armstrong tweeted about a pattern of trans shooters. [9] [14]

This wasn't the first time. After the March 2023 Nashville school shooting, Donald Trump Jr. declared a "clear epidemic of trans mass shooters." Following the May 2022 Uvalde shooting, Rep. Paul Gosar (R-AZ) tweeted that the shooter was a "transexual leftist illegal alien" — wrong on all counts. After multiple 2024 shootings, right-wing accounts immediately blamed transgender perpetrators before facts were established. [10] [11]

The pattern is consistent: immediate misattribution on platforms like 4chan, amplification by influencers and politicians before authorities release information, viral charts with fabricated data, and legislative weaponization citing "safety concerns" about transgender violence. The claims persist even after being thoroughly debunked by every major fact-checking organization and contradict all available data. [13]

What the Data Actually Shows

Four major databases track mass shootings in the United States, each using slightly different definitions. All reach the same conclusion: transgender people are vastly underrepresented as mass shooters.

Violence Prevention Project (Hamline University): Funded by the National Institute of Justice, this database covers 201 mass shooters from 1966 to 2025, tracking over 195 variables including demographics and life histories. Of 201 mass shooters, 196 were cisgender men (97.5%), 4 were cisgender women (2%), and only 1 was transgender (0.5%) — the Nashville 2023 shooter. [4] [5]

Gun Violence Archive: Using a broader definition (4+ shot, killed or injured), the GVA documented 5,748 mass shootings between January 2013 and September 2025. Founder Mark Bryant confirmed only 5 confirmed transgender shooters (0.09%), possibly 8 if unconfirmed cases are included. That's less than one-tenth of one percent. [2]

FBI Active Shooter Data (2023): Of 48 active shooters in 2023, 47 were cisgender male and 1 was transgender (Nashville). [3]

U.S. Secret Service National Threat Assessment Center: Analyzing mass public attacks from 2016-2020, the Secret Service found 93% of attackers were male. The report does not specifically highlight transgender identity as a risk factor, instead emphasizing behavioral warning signs and grievances. [15]

The statistical reality: Approximately 0.8-1.3% of the U.S. adult population identifies as transgender, representing roughly 2.1-2.8 million people. [6] [7] If transgender people committed mass shootings at the same rate as their population share, we would expect approximately 2 trans shooters in the Violence Prevention Project database (1% of 201) and 57 in the Gun Violence Archive (1% of 5,748). The actual numbers are 1 (0.5%) and 5 (0.09%), respectively — meaning transgender individuals are underrepresented by 50% to 91% depending on the database.

As Ohio State University professor Laura Dugan stated: "The risk of transgender people committing shootings is just not a concern, and transgender people are actually underrepresented as mass shooters." [3]

Patient Zero: How the Myth Took Root

While isolated misattributions occurred earlier, the modern false narrative crystallized around two pivotal events that established a playbook for misinformation.

Uvalde, Texas (May 24, 2022): Within hours of the Robb Elementary School shooting that killed 19 children and 2 teachers, 4chan users posted photos of an unrelated transgender woman with false claims she was the shooter. Despite authorities having already identified the shooter as Salvador Ramos, an 18-year-old cisgender male, the lie spread rapidly. Rep. Paul Gosar tweeted the shooter was a "transexual leftist illegal alien named Salvatore Ramos" — wrong on all counts. Candace Owens told her 3 million Twitter followers about "cross-dressing" photos. Alex Jones echoed the claims on Infowars. [10] [11]

The innocent trans woman whose photos were stolen faced death threats for days. Even 4chan users mocked Gosar for falling for "obvious click bait," with one writing: "It was a lie, spread by this board." Yet the template was established: flood the information space with false trans attribution immediately after shootings, before facts emerge, knowing corrections never fully catch up to the viral spread. [11]

Nashville, Tennessee (March 27, 2023): When Aiden Hale, a 28-year-old transgender man, killed three children and three adults at The Covenant School before being killed by police, it became the first mass shooting in the Violence Prevention Project's database with a confirmed transgender perpetrator. Media initially reported Hale had a "manifesto," a characterization that defined coverage for two years. [12]

In reality, as the Nashville Metro Police Department's April 2025 final report concluded: "In this case, a manifesto didn't exist." Hale left no single document explaining motives, but rather 16 notebooks with over 1,000 pages of rambling writings expressing suicidal ideation and desire for notoriety. Investigators determined Hale was motivated by wanting to die in a place with happy memories (the school Hale attended as a child) and to achieve fame. The attack was not motivated by race, not motivated by a grudge, and targeted Christians because Hale believed "their faith would make them meek and afraid," ensuring instant media attention. [12]

Yet conservative media and politicians seized on this single case to construct a pattern where none existed, amplifying it into claims of a "trans terrorism" epidemic.

The Misinformation Ecosystem: From 4chan to Congress in Hours

The false narrative spreads through a coordinated, multi-phase ecosystem that exploits the speed of social media and the slow pace of fact-checking.

Phase 1: Immediate Misattribution (Hours 0-24) — Anonymous users on 4chan, 8kun, and extremist forums post photos of random trans people with false shooter claims before authorities release confirmed information. As Joe Ondrak of Logically AI explained: "Social media trolls pump out disinformation before a shooter has been identified because they know that our news cycle moves so quickly now that a lot of the attention will just evaporate by the time the truth surfaces." [13]

Phase 2: Amplification by Influencers (Hours 12-72) — Right-wing accounts with millions of followers share unverified claims. Politicians legitimize misinformation through official channels. Foreign propaganda outlets amplify divisive narratives. Tech CEOs use platform ownership to boost false claims. [13]

Phase 3: Viral Charts and Memes (Days-Weeks) — Fabricated data visualizations claiming trans people are "highest per capita" mass shooters circulate widely. These charts ignore base rates: with only 1-2 trans-identified shooters out of thousands of incidents, any non-zero number appears inflated when calculated "per capita" for a 1% population. The Gun Violence Archive confirmed it never published data supporting these viral charts. [2]

Phase 4: Legislative Weaponization (Months-Years) — Following the Nashville shooting, the U.S. Department of Justice considered policies to ban transgender people from owning guns based on the false premise they're more prone to mass shootings. Over 500 anti-trans bills were introduced in 2023 alone, with many citing "safety concerns" rooted in violence misinformation. [16]

As University of Buffalo professor Yotam Ophir warned: "If you persuade conservatives that LGBTQ people are inherently flawed, that they are violent, that they are a risk to society, then any legislation against them will be justified." [3]

The Real Epidemic: Violence AGAINST Transgender People

While false claims paint transgender people as threats, the actual data shows they are overwhelmingly victims, not perpetrators.

Human Rights Campaign tracking (2013-2025): At least 399 transgender and gender-nonconforming people have been killed in the United States since 2013. 70% were murdered by firearms (258 lives lost). 84.4% were people of color. 73.7% were Black trans women specifically. 74.7% were under age 35. [8]

FBI Hate Crime Data (2024): Anti-transgender crimes accounted for 378 of 521 reported gender identity hate crimes in 2024, up 55% since 2020. LGBTQ people were the third-most targeted group, falling just behind hate crimes motivated by race/ethnicity and religion. [20]

However, the FBI data massively undercounts actual violence. Between January 2013 and October 2024, the FBI recorded only 16 fatal hate crimes against trans people, while the Human Rights Campaign tracked 372 deaths over the same period. This discrepancy exists because not all jurisdictions report to the FBI, legal definitions of "hate crime" vary, and only 42% of transgender individuals have ever reported being a victim of hate crime to police due to lack of trust in law enforcement. [20]

UCLA Williams Institute research: Transgender individuals are more than four times more likely to be victimized than cisgender people. [3]

As one researcher summarized: "Trans people are substantially more likely to be victims than perpetrators of violence." The misinformation creates a deadly feedback loop: false claims about trans violence justify legislative attacks, which increase vulnerability to actual violence against trans people. [8]

Why This Matters Now: The Mental Health Crisis

The false narrative about transgender violence has real, measurable consequences for the mental health and safety of transgender youth.

In September 2024, researchers at The Trevor Project published a landmark study in Nature Human Behaviour establishing the first causal link between anti-transgender state laws and suicide attempts among trans youth. The study surveyed more than 60,000 trans and nonbinary young people ages 13-24 from 2018 to 2022. [17] [18]

Key findings: Anti-trans laws caused an increase in suicide attempts by an estimated 7% to 72%. The highest increase — 7% to 72% — was among participants younger than age 18. Across the full sample ages 13-24, an increase of 38% to 44% was observed. Youth of color reported even higher rates, facing both transphobia and racism. [17]

Ronita Nath, co-author and Vice President of Research at The Trevor Project, stated: "State-level anti-transgender laws caused — not associated with, not linked to — we can say very confidently, they caused up to a 72% increase in number of past-year suicide attempts among trans and nonbinary young people." [17]

The study found that the mere introduction of anti-trans legislation did not have a noticeable impact. It was the passage of those bills into laws that fueled suicide attempts. In 2024 alone, politicians introduced 658 anti-trans bills — triple the record set the year before. In the two years following the study's research period (2023-2024), statehouses weighed 1,197 anti-transgender bills, of which 129 became law. [16]

Trevor Project 2024 National Survey: Representing more than 18,000 LGBTQ+ young people ages 13-24 across all 50 states, the survey found that 39% of LGBTQ+ young people seriously considered attempting suicide in the past year. Among transgender and nonbinary youth specifically, that number was 46%. 90% said their well-being was negatively impacted due to recent politics. 45% of transgender and nonbinary young people reported they or their family have considered moving to a different state because of anti-LGBTQ+ politics. [24]

As one clinician explained: "I'm seeing more of my patients who are being closeted, more of them who are just avoiding sports altogether. I really don't think we can overstate the negative impact that the rhetoric has, even when we're in places that the laws aren't directly impacting us." [24]

The Psychology Behind the Myth: Base Rate Neglect and Availability Heuristic

Two well-studied psychological phenomena explain why people dramatically overestimate the frequency of transgender mass shooters, even when confronted with contradictory data.

Base Rate Neglect: This cognitive bias occurs when individuals ignore or underweight the base rate (statistical reality) of an event in favor of more specific, but potentially less reliable, information. When a rare event like a mass shooting is associated with a minority group like transgender people, people tend to ignore the actual base rate (trans people commit less than 0.1% of mass shootings while comprising approximately 1% of the population) and instead focus on dramatic individual cases. [25]

The Availability Heuristic: This refers to the tendency to judge the likelihood of an event based on how easily examples come to mind. When vivid or dramatic instances are readily available in memory — such as media coverage of the Nashville shooting — they can disproportionately influence perception of reality, leading people to overestimate the frequency or severity of certain events. [25]

As Dr. James Densley, co-founder of the Violence Prevention Project, explained, this is "base rate neglect" — "even a couple of incidents in a short window can make something feel like a pattern even when it's not." [9]

The scapegoating pattern: Densley also noted the "long and ugly history" of associating entire marginalized groups with mass violence based on statistically negligible cases, emphasizing that such scapegoating "said more about the politics of the moment than about any actual causal relationship." This tactic has been used throughout history against Jewish people, immigrants, Muslims, and other minorities. [9]

The false claims about transgender mass shooters fit a well-documented pattern of moral panic and minority scapegoating. Trans people are blamed for a number of often contradictory harms, including corrupting children, threatening free speech, violence against women, and both dismantling and reinforcing problematic gender norms. In the last five years, more anti-trans legislation has been proposed in the United States than in the previous 200 years combined. [23]

The Heritage Foundation's "TIVE" Proposal: Designating Trans Advocacy as Terrorism

On September 19, 2025, the Heritage Foundation and its affiliate the Oversight Project released a four-page policy memo urging the FBI to designate transgender activism as a new category of domestic terrorism called "Transgender Ideology-Inspired Violent Extremism" (TIVE). [19]

The memo defined "TIVE" to include anyone who believes violence is justified against opponents of "transgender ideology" — but also anyone who argues that stripping away transgender rights constitutes violence or an existential threat to transgender people. By this logic, nearly every transgender rights activist or organization would fall under the label of extremist merely for pointing out the tangible harm that comes from losing rights. [19]

The Heritage Foundation claimed that "TIVE has played a role in the majority of mass shootings at schools," in flagrant disregard for statistics demonstrating that the common thread underlying most school shootings is that they are committed by cisgender men. A Wired investigation examined the claim and found it to be false, describing it as "misleading by design, arbitrary." [19]

Alejandra Caraballo, a Harvard law instructor and trans legal expert, stated: "Heritage Foundation has released an absolutely insane policy proposal to label all trans people as domestic terrorists. It uses completely made up instances of terrorism and made up statistics but facts don't matter to them." [19]

Critics drew parallels to the FBI's COINTELPRO program, which targeted civil rights leaders, anti-war protestors, and LGBTQ+ activists during the mid-20th century, warning that such surveillance risks repeating patterns of government overreach and suppression of marginalized groups. As of February 2026, the FBI has not enacted this policy, though Heritage's influence inside the Trump administration is significant. [19]

Conclusion: Facts vs. Fear

The claim that transgender people are more likely to commit mass shootings is not supported by any credible data source. Across four major databases spanning 1966 to 2025, transgender individuals account for 0.09% to 0.5% of mass shooters while comprising approximately 1% of the U.S. population — making them statistically underrepresented as perpetrators by 50% to 91% depending on the database.

The overwhelming pattern is clear: cisgender men commit 95-97.5% of all mass shootings. Transgender people, meanwhile, are four times more likely to be victims of crime than cisgender people, with 399+ transgender and gender-nonconforming people murdered in the United States since 2013.

Yet the false narrative persists, amplified by politicians, right-wing media, and social media algorithms that prioritize engagement over accuracy. The consequences are measurable: anti-trans legislation has surged from 615 bills in 2023 to 1,022 in 2025, with each new law causing a documented 7-72% increase in suicide attempts among trans youth.

This is not an accident. The misinformation serves multiple purposes: deflecting from gun control debates, justifying discriminatory legislation, and scapegoating a vulnerable minority for societal problems. As University of Buffalo professor Yotam Ophir warned: "If you persuade conservatives that LGBTQ people are inherently flawed, that they are violent, that they are a risk to society, then any legislation against them will be justified."

The data is unambiguous. The harm is real. And the truth matters — not just for accuracy, but for the lives at stake.
