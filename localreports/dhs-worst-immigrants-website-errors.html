TL;DR

Misleading

DHS's "Worst of the Worst" immigrant database (wow.dhs.gov) was found by CNN to contain hundreds of entries listing incorrect crimes — including individuals arrested only for minor traffic offenses or marijuana possession alongside violent felons. DHS blamed a "glitch" affecting just 5% of entries, while independent TRAC and Cato Institute data show 73.6% of ICE detainees had no criminal conviction at all. The Minneapolis ICE shooting case resulted in federal agents being investigated for perjury after video evidence contradicted their sworn testimony, and the claimed 1.6–2 million self-deportations have been called "vastly overstated and based on conjecture" by independent researchers.

Executive Summary

The Department of Homeland Security launched wow.dhs.gov on December 8, 2025, as a public database of approximately 25,000 arrested immigrants branded the "worst of the worst." Secretary Kristi Noem, the White House, and DHS communications staff actively promoted the site to justify aggressive enforcement operations. For 73 days, mainstream and right-wing media reproduced DHS framing without independent verification. A CNN investigation published February 19, 2026 forced DHS to admit the database was "rife with errors," with hundreds of individuals listed under incorrect crimes. DHS's public response was not contrition — it was mockery: the agency added 5,000 more entries and sarcastically thanked CNN for publicizing the site.

The website errors are one data point within a broader, documented pattern of false DHS enforcement claims. As of February 7, 2026, TRAC data showed 73.6% of ICE detainees had no criminal conviction whatsoever — directly contradicting Noem's January 18, 2026 claim that "70% of them have committed or have charges against them on violent crimes." The Cato Institute found only 5% of detainees had violent crime convictions. DHS called the Cato report "made up" before FOIA data obtained directly from ICE confirmed Cato's numbers. Administration claims of 1.6–2 million self-deportations have received no data support from DHS despite repeated press requests; the Center for Migration Studies calls the figures "vastly overstated and based on conjecture."

The pattern extends to field operations. On January 14, 2026, ICE agents shot Venezuelan immigrant Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis in Minneapolis. DHS immediately characterized the event as a violent ambush; Noem called it "attempted murder of federal law enforcement." Video evidence showed Sosa-Celis was shot while closing his front door. Charges were dismissed with prejudice on February 13, 2026. A federal perjury probe was opened against the agents. A peer-reviewed CU Boulder study published February 18, 2026 found daily ICE arrests had tripled while the percentage with criminal records fell to a near-historic low of 30%, with community arrests doubling to nearly half of all arrests. The Brennan Center documented that ICE detention capacity grew 91% in a single year — fueled primarily by immigrants with no criminal records.

1. The WOW Database: What It Is and How It Was Built

DHS launched wow.dhs.gov on December 8, 2025 with an initial list of 10,000 arrested immigrants. The official DHS press release described the site as letting visitors "search through some of the hundreds of thousands of criminal illegal aliens who have been arrested across all 50 states." [14] Secretary Noem personally promoted the site on social media from its first day of operation, and the White House amplified it as evidence of tough enforcement.

The site's framing was inflammatory by design. DHS press releases used language like "ICE Law Enforcement Risk Their Lives to Arrest Worst of the Worst Criminal Illegal Alien Pedophiles, Sex Criminals, and Armed Robbers." The database grew steadily: approximately 15,000 entries by mid-January 2026, 25,000 by early February, and beyond 30,000 after CNN's investigation published. On January 22, 2026, DHS added state-by-state navigation to the site — expanding its reach 26 days before the investigation revealed it was rife with errors. [16]

The database listed individuals alongside the crimes DHS claimed they were arrested for or convicted of. Crucially, DHS did not employ any consistent methodology for determining what crime to display. The result: some individuals were shown their most recent or most serious crime; others were shown minor charges that obscured more serious histories; still others had incorrect crimes listed altogether. The site functioned as a public-facing propaganda tool, not a verified criminal records database.

A Common Dreams analysis found that the total WOW entries (approximately 25,000) represent less than 5% of all ICE arrests since the start of the Trump administration — meaning by DHS's own framing, over 95% of those arrested were not classified as "worst of the worst." [22] The site's geographic distribution was also skewed: jurisdictions with the highest arrest numbers often contained large federal prisons, meaning DHS was taking credit for people already incarcerated — not dangerous individuals lurking in communities.

2. The Errors CNN Found

CNN's exclusive investigation, published February 19, 2026, found the WOW database fell into distinct categories of misrepresentation. [1] The first problem: hundreds of individuals were described as convicted of or arrested for serious crimes when the available records showed only minor offenses — traffic violations, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry (a federal felony, but not a violent crime). These were labeled "worst of the worst" alongside individuals with genuine violent crime histories.

The second problem was geographic inflation. Jurisdictions with the highest WOW arrest numbers often contained large federal prisons, creating the false impression that dangerous individuals had been lurking in those communities. DHS was claiming credit for people already incarcerated. The Minnesota listing included 487 individuals in total, with 60 in Rochester alone — a city that hosts a federal medical center and corrections facility. [2]

DHS's official response to CNN was a single sentence: "This is a glitch on the WOW website the impacted about 5% of the entries." The spokesperson offered no explanation of what type of technical failure causes a database to list incorrect human crimes, nor did DHS address the broader issue of minor offenses appearing in a "worst of the worst" designation. DHS claimed the glitch was resolved on February 18 — the day before the story published. [3]

Minnesota Corrections Commissioner Paul Schnell stated publicly: "The inconsistency and lack of clarity in Homeland Security data raises important questions about the level of federal resources being applied to the 'worst of the worst' targets in Minnesota." [3]

The following day — February 20 — DHS responded to the accountability reporting not with corrections but with mockery. The agency published a press release titled: "To Award the Mainstream Media for Raising Awareness About DHS's Worst of the Worst Website, DHS Drops Another 5,000 Criminals." The database expanded beyond 30,000 entries. [15]

3. The Minneapolis ICE Shooting: What Really Happened

On January 14, 2026, ICE agents shot Venezuelan immigrant Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis in the leg during Operation Metro Surge in Minneapolis. DHS immediately released a statement characterizing the incident as an attack on law enforcement. Secretary Noem posted on social media calling it "an attempted murder of federal law enforcement," describing agents as having been "ambushed and attacked" with shovels and broom handles. [32]

Sosa-Celis and co-defendant Alfredo Alejandro Aljorna were charged with felony assault on a federal officer — a charge carrying up to 20 years in prison. At a January 21, 2026 court hearing, an ICE officer provided sworn testimony describing the attack. Both defendants and three eyewitnesses contradicted that testimony. Video evidence was subsequently obtained. [10] [20]

On February 12–13, 2026, a joint ICE-DOJ review of video evidence concluded that the sworn testimony of two separate agents "appears to have made untruthful statements." U.S. Attorney Daniel N. Rosen filed a motion to dismiss, stating that newly discovered evidence was "materially inconsistent with the allegations against them" in both the criminal complaint and testimony given at the January 21 hearing. U.S. District Court Judge Paul A. Magnuson dismissed the charges with prejudice on February 13 — legally preventing the charges from being refiled. [19]

Defense attorneys stated "the charges against them were based on lies by an ICE agent who recklessly shot into their home through a closed door." [21] NPR reported that DHS confirmed agents had appeared to make untruthful statements and that the two agents were placed on administrative leave, with ICE's acting director stating they could face termination and criminal prosecution. A federal perjury probe was opened concurrently. [9]

The Minneapolis case was not isolated. Reporting documented at least five ICE shooting incidents in 2025–2026 where video evidence directly contradicted initial official accounts — including the fatal shooting of American citizen Renee Nicole Good on January 7, 2026. [11] In the first three weeks of 2026 alone, there were 6 deaths in ICE custody and 2 fatal ICE shootings. [33] [34]

4. "70% Violent Criminals" — Noem's Claim vs. the Data

On January 18, 2026, DHS Secretary Kristi Noem stated: "70% of them have committed or have charges against them on violent crimes." The claim circulated widely and was used to justify mass detention policy. [12]

TRAC data published February 7, 2026 told a starkly different story: as of that date, 50,259 out of 68,289 people held in ICE detention — 73.6% — had no criminal conviction. [4] The Cato Institute found that of ICE detainees from October to November 2025, only 5% had been convicted of violent crime. [5]

When the Cato Institute published these findings, DHS called the report "made up" with "no legitimate data behind it." Cato then obtained FOIA data directly from ICE, which confirmed that 71% of ICE arrests from October 1–15, 2025 had no criminal convictions — substantiating Cato's original figures and directly contradicting DHS's denial. DHS never issued a correction. [6]

Five independent fact-checking organizations examined Noem's claim. PolitiFact (January 23, 2026) found the claim relied on conflating convictions with pending charges — a legally significant distinction: "Someone with a pending charge who is not convicted is not usually called a 'criminal' in our criminal system." [25] FactCheck.org found that in Trump's first 10 months, only 37% of ICE arrests involved people with criminal convictions — down from 52% under Biden's final year. [26] The New York Times found only 7% of detained immigrants from January 20 through October 15 had violent convictions. [12]

Even taken charitably — counting pending charges alongside convictions — Wisconsin Watch found only 52% combined, still well below Noem's 70% figure, with only 29% having actual convictions. The consistent conclusion across five fact-checkers: the claim is false.

5. The 1.6 Million Self-Deportation Myth

DHS and the White House claimed repeatedly that between 1.6 million and 2.5 million undocumented immigrants had "left the U.S." under the Trump administration — including a large number who had "self-deported" voluntarily. The figures were promoted in press conferences, social media posts, and official DHS releases. [7]

NPR requested data from DHS to support the 1.6 million self-deportation claim "repeatedly" and received no response. [8] The Center for Migration Studies analyzed the claim and concluded the figures are "vastly overstated and based on conjecture, not facts." Critically, the 1.6–2 million estimate appears to derive from a CIS chart whose own authors included "prominent caveats explaining that the apparent evidence of out-migration might be a statistical artifact" — meaning DHS cited a source that explicitly cautioned against interpreting the data as proof of voluntary departures. [7]

The Deportation Data Project — a joint initiative of UC Berkeley Law School and UCLA School of Law — obtained actual ICE removal records through federal FOIA litigation (CILP v. ICE). Their February 17, 2026 data update found actual deportations through mid-October 2025 likely numbered fewer than 300,000, while DHS was simultaneously claiming 2.5 million total departures. The researchers themselves cautioned the data "likely contain significant errors," but even in the worst-case reading, the verified figure is a fraction of DHS claims. [17] [18]

The Deportation Data Project's comprehensive nine-month analysis confirmed: daily ICE arrests tripled under Trump's second term, but actual removals did not match the inflated numbers. DHS had stopped publishing in-depth monthly enforcement data as of early 2025, forcing independent researchers to obtain records through litigation rather than voluntary government disclosure. [36]

6. Evidence Deep-Dive: FOIA Data, CU Boulder, and the Brennan Center

A peer-reviewed study by CU Boulder labor economist Chloe East, published February 18, 2026, analyzed federal FOIA data on all ICE arrests from October 2015 through October 2025. The findings are stark: daily ICE arrests nearly tripled under Trump's second term, rising from 304 per day in Biden's final year to 821 per day in Trump's first year. The percentage of ICE arrests involving people with a criminal record simultaneously fell to approximately 30% — a near-historic low compared to 52% under Biden, 70% in Trump's first-term first year, and 79% under Obama's final year. [23]

Notably, the CU Boulder study found that nearly half of all ICE arrests in 2025 were community arrests — meaning agents apprehended people in neighborhoods, homes, and workplaces rather than at jails or after criminal proceedings. Community arrests had accounted for only 19% of arrests in 2024 under Biden. Democratic-leaning jurisdictions saw disproportionate spikes, suggesting political rather than criminal enforcement priorities. [23]

The Brennan Center for Justice documented the financial scale of the expansion: between October 1, 2024 and June 16, 2025, ICE arrested approximately 204,000 people, of whom approximately 65% (133,000 individuals) had no criminal record at all. Congress allocated $75 billion to ICE over four years, with $28.7 billion available in FY 2025 — exceeding the entire Department of Justice budget request for the federal prison system ($11.25 billion), which houses 155,000 people. [24]

The detention capacity grew at extraordinary speed: by end of November 2025, ICE was using 104 more detention facilities than at the start of the year — a 91% increase — adding tent camps with documented poor conditions. ICE detention on any given day increased over 75% in one year, from roughly 40,000 to 70,000+. The American Immigration Council documented that 2025 was the deadliest year on record for ICE detention, with 2026 on pace to exceed it: at least 6 deaths in ICE custody in the first three weeks of 2026 alone. [28]

Austin Kocher's Substack analysis of TRAC data found that 92% of ICE detention growth from September 21, 2025 to January 7, 2026 was driven by immigrants with no criminal convictions. Of those detained, 72% had no convictions and only 8% had any actual convictions. Kocher concluded: "The available data do not support" the administration's claim that enforcement was driven by national security necessity. [13]

8. Congressional Oversight, the Impeachment Resolution, and the Cannibal Story

The Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee held an oversight hearing on February 12, 2026, with ICE Acting Director Todd Lyons, CBP Commissioner Rodney Scott, and USCIS Director Joseph Edlow testifying alongside Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison and Corrections Commissioner Schnell. DHS officials declined to directly address the Minneapolis agents' false testimony under oath, and the hearing addressed DHS's concurrent decision to cut the Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties — an internal watchdog — as ICE enforcement expanded. [29]

Secretary Noem attempted to require 7 days' advance notice before members of Congress could inspect immigration detention facilities. A federal judge blocked this policy twice, finding it conflicted with Congress's constitutional oversight authority. [30] By February 2026, a bipartisan impeachment resolution (H.Res.996) sponsored by Rep. Robin Kelly (D-IL) had attracted support from over 180 House Democrats. The charges included obstruction of congressional oversight of detention facilities, personal use of her DHS position, circumvention of federal contracting law, and repeated violations of immigration law and constitutional rights. [31]

On February 23, 2026, The Intercept published an investigation into a story Noem had repeatedly told publicly — including alongside President Trump — about ICE detaining and deporting a cannibal who "began to eat himself" on a deportation flight. Three federal law enforcement officials, including one from within DHS itself, told The Intercept the story was "completely false." DHS's explanation: Noem was told the story by an air marshal on a deportation flight and promoted it publicly without verification. No records of the alleged incident could be located. [27]

The pattern across Noem's false claims is consistent: the "70% violent criminals" claim (Jan 18), the "2 million self-deportations" claim (ongoing), the false characterization of the Minneapolis shooting (Jan 14), and the fabricated cannibal story (multiple occasions including alongside the President) — three of these four were publicly contradicted within weeks by data, video evidence, or federal law enforcement officials from within DHS itself. [32]

The Transparency Comparison

Under Obama and Biden, DHS published regular monthly enforcement statistics through the Office of Homeland Security Statistics. Obama's administration oversaw approximately 3.1 million formal removals across two terms with publicly available monthly data. Under Trump's second term, DHS stopped publishing in-depth monthly data as of early 2025, forcing independent researchers to obtain records through FOIA litigation rather than voluntary disclosure. The Deportation Data Project obtained records only through a federal lawsuit (CILP v. ICE). Despite halting voluntary disclosure, the administration simultaneously claimed record-high removal numbers that FOIA data could not confirm. [35] [36]

10. Conclusion: Propaganda Infrastructure, Not Accountability

The wow.dhs.gov database was not a transparency tool that happened to contain errors. It was propaganda infrastructure — designed to publicly stigmatize immigrants and justify mass enforcement regardless of individual criminal histories. The "glitch" explanation is insufficient: a technical error does not cause a law enforcement database to list human beings under the wrong crimes, and it does not explain why individuals arrested for single traffic violations appeared alongside violent felons under the label "worst of the worst."

The broader pattern confirms this assessment. Secretary Noem's "70% violent criminals" claim was false by every independent measure. The 1.6–2.5 million self-deportation claim lacked any data support. The Minneapolis shooting narrative was fabricated by sworn law enforcement testimony later contradicted by video evidence. The "cannibal deportee" story was, per three federal law enforcement sources, "completely false." All four claims were publicly promoted by the Secretary of Homeland Security, the White House, or both — and all four were subsequently debunked.

The structural asymmetry is key to understanding how this operates: false claims require only a press release or a social media post to spread. Corrections require FOIA litigation, peer-reviewed research, federal court proceedings, and investigative journalism. DHS stopped voluntarily publishing monthly enforcement data in 2025, forcing transparency through lawsuit. When independent researchers published FOIA-obtained data showing DHS claims were false, DHS called the data "made up." When it turned out the data was accurate, DHS never corrected the record.

Verdict: Misleading

The DHS "Worst of the Worst" website contained hundreds of factual errors that DHS admitted only after CNN's investigation forced the issue. DHS's broader enforcement statistics — the "70% violent criminals" claim, the "2 million self-deportations" figure, and the Minneapolis shooting narrative — have each been independently debunked by data, video evidence, or federal court proceedings. The pattern represents a systemic, government-originated campaign of disinformation about immigration enforcement, propagated through official channels for 73+ days before corrections were possible.
