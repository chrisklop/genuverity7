TL;DR

CONFIRMED COORDINATED INFLUENCE OPERATION

Within 48 hours of U.S.-Israeli strikes killing Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei on February 28, 2026, IRGC-linked networks generated 91,000 "Epstein Fury" mentions from 60,000+ accounts — exploiting a pre-seeded conspiracy that Trump launched the war to bury the Epstein scandal. The operation ran on two simultaneous tracks: Iran's formal state media (PressTV, Tasnim, Mehr) published 18 verified false military claims in a single week, a 260% acceleration; and a covert network of 62 IRGC-affiliated fake persona accounts posted 59,403 original posts on X alone. A Zeteo/Data for Progress poll on March 10 found 52% of likely American voters — and 66% of under-45 voters — now believe the war was launched at least in part to cover up Trump's Epstein connections. The operation accumulated 145 million views in under two weeks. TikTok accounted for 72% of total campaign views.

Executive Summary

On February 28, 2026, hours after U.S. and Israeli strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, pro-Iran influence networks launched what researchers are calling the most sophisticated coordinated disinformation operation in the conflict's short history. The campaign exploited a pre-existing conspiracy theory — that Trump launched "Operation Epic Fury" to bury the Epstein files — and turbocharged it across X, Instagram, TikTok, and Telegram using AI-generated content, fake "news" account networks, and Iranian state media amplification. Within 48 hours, the phrase "Epstein Fury" had been mentioned over 91,000 times by more than 60,000 unique accounts, according to the ADL's Center on Extremism. [1] A network of at least 15 anonymous X accounts with blue checkmarks — including "HDX News" and "GPX News" — served as primary distribution nodes, with a single fabricated video accumulating 6.8 million views before X suspended the account. [2]

The operation ran on two parallel tracks. The first was Iran's formal state-media infrastructure: PressTV, Tasnim, Mehr News, and IRIB TV1 flooded international feeds with fabricated battlefield claims. The second was a covert IRGC-affiliated network of fake personas documented by Clemson University's Media Forensics Hub: 62 accounts masquerading as Scottish independence supporters, Irish nationalists, and Latina women from Texas and Venezuela, which generated 59,403 original posts on X alone. [4] The strategy, per Institute for Strategic Dialogue researcher Bret Schafer: "You come for the Epstein content, and you stay for the propaganda." The operation represents the convergence of three distinct disinformation actors — Iran's formal media machine, the IRGC's covert influence infrastructure, and commercial engagement-farmers — all reinforcing the same set of narratives. Total reach: 145 million views in under two weeks. [16]

Section 1: 91,000 Posts in 48 Hours — How the Campaign Launched

The convergence of the Epstein narrative with the Iran war began at precisely 4:00 PM ET on February 28, 2026 — within hours of the first U.S.-Israeli strikes. The ADL Center on Extremism documented 35,000 mentions of "Operation Epstein Fury" and 28,000 unique authors within that single afternoon. [1] This speed was not organic. The rapidity of deployment suggests pre-positioning: IRGC-linked content networks had been cultivating the Epstein disinformation environment for weeks prior, monitoring the rolling release of Jeffrey Epstein files by the Trump Justice Department and preparing to weaponize them the moment military action began.

By 48 hours out, "Epstein Fury" had generated 91,000 total mentions from more than 60,000 unique accounts — a figure verified by ADL and independently confirmed by The Washington Post and Reuters. The ADL documented convergence across "white nationalists, influencers, anti-war voices, and Islamist-aligned groups" around the same core messaging: that Trump launched the war as a cover-up. The operation exploited an information environment already primed by the Epstein file releases and a population already conditioned to distrust institutions.

The campaign's architectural logic was a three-part narrative chain: (1) Trump released partial Epstein files, raising questions about his own documented connections; (2) Trump launched "Operation Epic Fury" days later; (3) therefore, the war was launched to bury the scandal. Iranian state media formalized this as the "Epstein class" or "Epstein regime" framing — portraying Trump, Netanyahu, and Western elites as members of a criminal network using military violence to distract from their crimes. [3]

Section 2: Iranian State Media's 18 Verified Lies

Iran's formal state media published 18 war-related claims documented as false by NewsGuard in the first week after the February 28 strikes — compared to just 5 in the preceding two weeks, a 260% acceleration. [3] The fabrications were operationally specific and targeted American military credibility: Mehr News Agency reported four Iranian ballistic missiles struck the USS Abraham Lincoln. CENTCOM denied it: "The Abraham Lincoln was not hit. The missiles didn't even come close." The Lincoln continued operations. Tasnim News, citing an IRGC spokesperson, claimed 650 U.S. troops killed or wounded in the first two days. CENTCOM confirmed 6 U.S. service members killed in total — an inflation factor of 10,833%.

Tasnim News Agency and PressTV are not independent media outlets. Both are OFAC-designated instruments of the IRGC under the U.S. Treasury's Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list. [19] OFAC documentation confirms Tasnim's founding by two IRGC commanders — Majid Gholizadeh and Hamidreza Moghadam Far — who "continue to serve as Tasnim executives and exert control over Tasnim on behalf of the IRGC." These are legally designated state propaganda instruments, not news organizations operating under journalistic standards.

Section 3: The IRGC Covert Network — 62 Fake Personas, 59,403 Posts

Clemson University's Media Forensics Hub published its March 11 report — "From Texas to Tehran" — tracing the IRGC-affiliated account network's origins to December 2023, with accounts building credibility in niche communities until February 2026, when they simultaneously pivoted to the war. [4] The 62 accounts masqueraded as Scottish independence supporters, Irish nationalists, and Latina women from Texas and Venezuela. They were not blunt propaganda vehicles — they were carefully cultivated community members who had spent years building trust before their activation.

Clemson's Darren Linvill described the accounts as "trolls, in the 2016 Russian sense of the word, trying to appear to be real people, in order to integrate into very specific communities and influence those communities online." On X alone, the network created 59,403 original posts. This "dormant persona" playbook mirrors techniques Clemson had previously documented in a 2024 Iranian network promoting Scottish independence narratives — the current operation is broader and more multilingual, spanning English, Spanish, Irish, and Farsi content across three platforms: X, Instagram, and Bluesky.

The IRGC infrastructure behind the disinformation operation is legally documented, not inferred. OFAC-designated Tasnim News was founded by IRGC commanders and formally designated as an IRGC instrument by the U.S. Treasury in September 2023, alongside Fars News and PressTV. [19] Iran's IRGC Deputy Head of Public Relations Ali Mohammad Naeini publicly acknowledged before this conflict that Iran's propaganda architecture was insufficient — he explicitly cited failures in the PS752 Ukrainian plane downing (2020) and the Mahsa Amini morality police controversy (2022) as cases where "we did not have a proper media operation," and warned: "Without timely and effective media operations, we will face problems." The March 2026 operation represents the infrastructure performing exactly the function Naeini was calling for. [19]

The most widely circulated fabricated artifact was a two-minute AI-generated Lego animation titled "Narrative of Victory," produced by Iran's Revayat-e Fath Institute and aired on Iranian state television on March 11, depicting Lego-figure Trump, Netanyahu, and Satan reviewing a folder labeled "Jeffrey Epstein File" before Trump launches missiles at an Iranian classroom. The video's inclusion of the Burj al-Arab and the U.S. Embassy in Riyadh signals a goal beyond domestic Iranian audiences: it implicitly threatens Gulf states and their civilian infrastructure, targeting those governments' political calculus about the conflict. [22]

The fake news account network operated on a parallel track. The "HDX News" and "GPX News" accounts on X — both holding paid blue verification badges — posted a fabricated video depicting Trump and Epstein together. The caption read: "This video is not fake. These pedophile perverts started a war so that this wouldn't be talked about." HDX News alone accumulated 6.8 million views before X suspended the account following a Washington Post inquiry; GPX News accumulated 4.7 million views on the same synthetic content. [2]

Gizmodo's analysis identified the central strategic puzzle of this content: authentic Trump-Epstein materials already exist in the public record. Real photographs and documented interactions are available. The use of AI fabrication was operationally unnecessary for factual persuasion. This implies the operation's actual goal was not convincing skeptics — it was intensifying the emotional certainty of already-primed audiences. Turning passive concern about Epstein into active conviction about a cover-up, and that conviction into voting-booth-level political belief. [21]

The synthesized HDX News footage depicted not just Trump and Epstein but also Sean "Diddy" Combs, Bill Gates, and Elon Musk — targeting a broader "elite pedophile network" conspiracy cluster. Nine of the 15 network accounts held X Blue verification, creating a visual credibility signal that cost approximately $8/month per account. [21] A separate viral false claim — that Trump named the operation "Epic Fury" specifically so that "Trump ep" would autocomplete to "Trump Epic Fury" rather than "Trump Epstein" in Google searches — accumulated 1.7 million views despite being demonstrably false. [9]

Section 5: The Bot Blackout — Forensic Proof of Centralized Command

Key Evidence: 16-Day Synchronized Silence

When Israel's military strikes hit on February 28, Iran implemented a near-total internet blackout. Within hours, a network of 1,332 fake profiles — operating since May 2025 in UK political communities — went completely silent, simultaneously, for 16 days. This synchronized silence is considered the clearest structural proof of centralized IRGC command-and-control over a globally distributed disinformation infrastructure. [17]

Cyabra's investigation documented a specific sub-network of 1,332 fake profiles that had been operating since May 2025, focused on UK political discourse — Scottish independence, Brexit, UK institutional collapse. Key metrics before the blackout: 224 million views generated across the network's operational lifetime; 26% of all accounts discussing Scottish independence on X were fake, far above normal baseline rates. The network operated using engagement clusters, linguistic patterns, recycled content, and coordinated hashtag use including #FreeScotland and #BrexitBetrayal. [17]

Cyabra CEO Dan Brahmy stated the internet blackout "exposed the entire operation" by revealing its state-level coordination. When the accounts returned after Iranian communications were partially restored, their content had shifted from UK political discourse to overtly pro-Iran war messaging, framing Iran as a model of "unity and resistance" — confirming what network analysis already suggested. [18]

The IRGC-affiliated fake Scottish and Latina persona networks documented by Clemson and Cyabra's UK-targeting network represent the same operational playbook at different geographic scales — "dormant credibility" accounts built in local political communities, activated for war propaganda when needed. The Clemson analysis confirmed behavioral and narrative overlaps with Iran-linked campaigns from April 2024, indicating what Cyabra called "a recurring and evolving information warfare strategy." [16]

Platform distribution across the campaign was heavily weighted toward TikTok. Cyabra's primary investigation found TikTok accounted for 72% of total campaign views — over 7 million engagements — with X, Facebook, and Instagram making up the remainder. The coordination signature was traceable: identical videos across platforms, synchronized posting windows, and fixed hashtag clusters pointing to centralized content production. [16]

X's own response was limited. On March 3, X Head of Product Nikita Bier announced a new AI content policy — 90-day suspension from Creator Revenue Sharing for undisclosed AI-generated war videos, permanent on a second violation — but the policy applied only to monetized accounts. Bier himself had identified "a guy in Pakistan managing 31 accounts posting AI war videos — all hacked and usernames changed on Feb 27 to 'Iran War Monitor.'" His characterization: "In nearly all cases, the users posting fake content were people looking to game monetization." [10] Researchers described the policy as ineffective: "The feeds I monitor are still flooded with AI-generated content about the war."

Section 6: The Poll — 52% of Voters Believe It, 66% of Under-45s Accept It as Fact

A Zeteo/Data for Progress poll published March 10 found that a majority of likely American voters — 52% to 40% — believe Trump launched the Iran war at least in part to cover up his Epstein connections, with 8% unsure. [5] The poll was conducted March 6–8, 2026, sampling 1,272 likely voters on a national web panel. The partisan breakdown: Democrats 81–14 agreeing; Republicans 25% agreeing; and — the operationally significant figure — voters under 45 at 66%, approaching near-consensus.

The under-45 figure is the most significant. It demonstrates that the Epstein-Iran narrative has achieved near-consensus among younger voters regardless of party affiliation — consistent with TikTok being the primary amplification platform at 72% of total campaign views. Young voters in America are predominantly consuming content about this conflict on a platform where 72% of the disinformation views accumulated, and they have formed beliefs accordingly. [16]

The 25% of Republicans agreeing with the narrative represents a structural problem for democratic accountability. These are members of the party that controls the presidency, expressing belief that their own president launched a war as a personal cover-up. If the premise of the conflict is widely believed to be fraudulent — even partially, even by a quarter of the president's own partisan base — legitimate oversight of the war becomes operationally difficult. Opposition from across the political spectrum on the same factually false basis creates a coalition built on fabrication. [5]

Clemson's Darren Linvill assessed the network's explicit goal as attempting to "redirect its resources toward propaganda around the war, trying to make the war more painful for the United States" — with the objective of shortening the conflict by eroding American political will. The poll represents the most direct available evidence that this objective is producing measurable results.

Section 7: The Geopolitical Architecture — Iran-China-Russia Pact, PRISONBREAK, and Counter-Operations

Iran's unprecedented disinformation acceleration after February 28 reflects not just military strategy but a structural crisis in the regime's information apparatus. The killing of Khamenei removed the single figure whose authority could coordinate the regime's media response from the top. What followed was a decentralized activation of pre-positioned assets: Iran's formal media machine, the Revayat-e Fath propaganda unit, the covert persona networks in Scotland and Texas, and the monetized amplification ecosystem — all running simultaneously but without unified strategic direction.

The geopolitical backdrop: the Iran-China-Russia trilateral strategic pact was signed on January 29, 2026 — just 30 days before the strikes. [23] While not a mutual defense treaty, the pact included intelligence cooperation provisions. Russian intelligence was providing Iran with "precise locations of U.S. warships and aircraft." China had spent years shifting Iranian military navigation from GPS to China's encrypted BeiDou-3 constellation. Whether information warfare coordination was also part of the agreement has not been confirmed in open sources — but tactical alignment in the cyber domain is documented: the pro-Russian hacktivist group NoName057(16) and Iranian Cyber Islamic Resistance ran a joint #OpIsrael DDoS campaign starting March 2, targeting Israeli defense entities including Elbit Systems. [13]

Counter-operations also ran simultaneously. The Citizen Lab's PRISONBREAK investigation (Report No. 189, October 2025) documented an Israeli government-linked network of 50+ AI-enabled X accounts coordinating pro-regime-change narratives — created in 2023, active primarily from January 2025 onward. [6] Two days into the 2025 June conflict, PRISONBREAK accounts urged Iranians to withdraw money from ATMs, claiming the regime was "stealing their savings" — backed by AI-generated videos depicting long ATM lines. Some accounts impersonated BBC Persian, posting fabricated screenshots suggesting senior Iranian officials were fleeing the country. A separate "Operation Overload" targeted social media algorithms by flooding searches with pro-counter-narrative content. [14]

Citizen Lab researchers assessed "an unidentified agency of the Israeli government, or a sub-contractor working under its close supervision" is conducting PRISONBREAK — assessed after "systematically reviewing alternative explanations." [6] The three-way information environment — pro-Iran IRGC accounts, anti-Iran Israeli-linked accounts, and monetization-farming engagement accounts — creates a situation where no single narrative can be trusted. This tripartite confusion is itself a functional outcome for each actor: when the information environment is opaque enough, all actors benefit from the inability of audiences to verify anything.

Lawfare's analysis documented that Iran "is using artificial intelligence in its cyber activities, operations, and threats," with particular emphasis on generative AI for influence campaigns. Historical context: Iranian-sponsored deepfakes had already disrupted news broadcasts in the UAE, UK, and Canada in December 2023. [12] The March 2026 operation is the same playbook deployed at orders-of-magnitude greater scale, enabled by the cost collapse in AI-generated video production.

Section 8: Who Benefits, Who Is Harmed — Including the Epstein Investigation Itself

The Iranian regime served three simultaneous objectives with this operation. First, domestic legitimation: portraying the conflict as imposed by a depraved Western elite — not resulting from Iran's nuclear weapons program — provided a narrative that could sustain internal support for a conflict the regime did not choose on favorable terms. Second, international positioning: framing Iran as a resistant power facing Zionist-American corruption targeted sympathetic audiences in the Global South, UK political margins, and U.S. anti-war communities. Third, war-shortening: eroding American political will by making the war's premises appear fraudulent to a broad domestic coalition.

The most underanalyzed harm from this operation is the damage done to the Epstein accountability process itself. By attaching AI-fabricated content, antisemitic imagery, and an irresponsible causal claim to the Epstein story, the operation made the real investigation significantly harder to prosecute in public discourse. [21] Real journalists covering the Epstein files must now navigate an information environment in which "Epstein evidence" is associated with IRGC propaganda, blue-checkmark bot networks, and AI fakes. The Iranian operation did not need to suppress the Epstein story — it needed only to contaminate the information environment around it.

The EDMO's characterization of this conflict as "the first AI war" reflects a qualitative shift in disinformation economics. [20] AI-generated video content that would have required significant production budgets in 2020 can now be produced at near-zero marginal cost per item. The engagement-farmer in Pakistan running 31 accounts is the commercial endpoint of this cost collapse: individual actors with no political allegiance, monetizing wartime attention at industrial scale. Platform infrastructure was architecturally mismatched to the problem — designed for case-by-case content review, confronted with an automated content flood. Meta's Oversight Board documented that Meta failed to act on six separate user reports of a single AI-fabricated war video. [15]

Brandon Amacher of the Emerging Tech Policy Lab described the outcome: "We are now currently in an era where you cannot trust information by default on open social media." [7] The attention asymmetry is structural and severe: Cyabra documented 145 million total views in under two weeks; BBC Verify documented 100+ million combined views for the top three AI-generated false videos; NewsGuard documented 21.9 million views for fabricated visuals on X alone. Against these numbers, individual fact-checks — which require search intent, reading comprehension, and trust in the outlet — cannot achieve comparable reach. The information environment is structurally asymmetric against correction, not because fact-checkers aren't trying, but because the production economics of disinformation have been permanently altered by AI.

The Structural Finding

The Epstein Fury campaign is not an anomaly. It is the proof-of-concept for "the first AI war" doctrine: pre-position dormant fake persona networks in high-trust communities, weaponize a real grievance with a fabricated causal link, activate the network simultaneously across platforms at the moment of military action, and measure success not in battles won but in polls shifted. The 52% figure is the campaign's most important statistic — not because it reflects Iranian military success, but because it represents the operational effectiveness of an information warfare strategy that has no battlefield equivalent and no regulatory framework capable of containing it.
