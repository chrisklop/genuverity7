TL;DR

VERDICT: FALSE

The claim that Earth will lose gravity for 7 seconds on August 12, 2026 is a recycled hoax dating back to Patrick Moore's 1976 BBC April Fool's prank. The fake "Project Anchor" document does not exist. NASA confirmed Earth's gravity cannot "switch off" without losing mass. AI deepfakes and real eclipse timing supercharged a 50-year-old joke into 2026's biggest viral science hoax.

Executive Summary

A viral claim circulating on TikTok, Instagram, Reddit, and Facebook in early 2026 falsely alleged that NASA announced Earth would experience zero gravity for 7 seconds on August 12, 2026, at 14:33 UTC, resulting in "40 million deaths from falls" and "economic collapse lasting over 10 years." [1][2][3] The hoax centered on a fabricated "Project Anchor" document allegedly leaked in November 2024, claiming NASA had an $89 billion budget to prepare select government officials for a gravitational anomaly caused by "intersecting gravitational waves from black holes." [4][5]

This is a recycled hoax with deep historical roots. The original version was an April Fool's Day prank by British astronomer Patrick Moore on BBC Radio 2 on April 1, 1976, who jokingly claimed that a Pluto-Jupiter alignment would create the "Jovian-Plutonian Gravitational Effect," temporarily reducing Earth's gravity at exactly 9:47 AM. [6][7] Listeners called in claiming they had floated. The hoax has been recycled with new dates repeatedly: January 4, 2014; April 4, 2014; June 4, 2014; November 4, 2014; January 4, 2015; and now August 12, 2026. [8][9]

NASA officially debunked the 2026 version: "The Earth will not lose gravity on Aug. 12, 2026. Earth's gravity, or total gravitational force, is determined by its mass. The only way for the Earth to lose gravity would be for the Earth system to lose mass." [1] The hoax cleverly exploited a real astronomical event—a total solar eclipse crossing Greenland, Iceland, and Spain on August 12, 2026—to lend false credibility. [10][22] Fact-checkers from Snopes, IFLScience, Reuters, and NASA confirmed no "Project Anchor" document exists. [2][3]

1. The Viral Claim: "Project Anchor" and the Zero Gravity Event

On December 31, 2025, Instagram user @mr_danya_of posted a video showing a man sitting silently in a car while overlaid text claimed a secret NASA document titled "Project Anchor" had leaked in November 2024. [2][4] The post alleged:

  • A $89 billion NASA project to survive a 7-second gravitational anomaly on August 12, 2026, at 14:33 UTC
  • Predicted casualties of 40-60 million deaths from falls
  • Cause: intersection of two gravitational waves from black holes, predicted in 2019 with 94.7% probability
  • NASA building underground bunkers for government leaders, scientists, military personnel, and "selected citizens with genetic diversity"

The video received almost 62,000 likes and was shared 268,000 times on Instagram alone. [5][11] The @mr_danya_of account became unavailable on January 6 or 7, 2026—just days after the post went viral—but the content continued spreading across TikTok, Facebook, Reddit, and X. [2] Investigation revealed the account had claimed to work at Google, a morgue, a crematorium, a hospice, and as both a psychiatrist and criminologist—claims inconsistent enough to suggest fabrication or parody. [4]

2. Multi-Platform Cascade: How It Spread in January 2026

The hoax spread through a multi-platform cascade. Instagram served as "patient zero" with the @mr_danya_of post on December 31, 2025, generating 268,000 shares. [5] On TikTok, the YSK podcast picked up the story, followed by thousands of users creating reaction videos, memes, and "educational" content about the impending gravity loss. [11] Text-based reposts circulated widely on Facebook, Reddit, and X, often including fake NASA tweets showing "@NASA" announcing the event. [8]

AI-generated content, including fake NASA announcement videos, boosted credibility and engagement. [5] The hoax strategically anchored itself to a genuine astronomical event—the August 12, 2026 total solar eclipse, whose path crosses eastern Greenland, western Iceland, northern Spain, and a tiny portion of Portugal. [10][22] This is the first total solar eclipse visible from mainland Europe since 1999 and Iceland's first since June 30, 1954. [23] By hijacking a real, well-documented celestial event, the hoax gained false plausibility—classic Misinformation 101: mix truth with fiction.

The hoax exploited several psychological vulnerabilities: authority bias (fake NASA branding and technical jargon like "$89 billion budget," "14:33 UTC timing," "94.7% probability"), fear appeal (catastrophic death toll, economic collapse), exclusivity (secret "leaked" document, underground bunkers for the elite), and temporal urgency (countdown to specific date/time created pressure to share). [24][25]

3. The 1976 Genesis: Patrick Moore's April Fool's Prank

On the morning of April 1, 1976, British astronomer Patrick Moore appeared on BBC Radio 2 and announced that at exactly 9:47 AM, Pluto would pass directly behind Jupiter relative to Earth, creating the "Jovian-Plutonian Gravitational Effect" that would temporarily weaken Earth's gravity. [6] Moore instructed listeners to jump at that precise moment to experience a "strange floating sensation." The BBC switchboard was flooded with calls from listeners claiming they had indeed floated, with one caller reporting he "jumped too high and hit his head on the ceiling." [7]

Moore's prank was partially inspired by The Jupiter Effect (1974), a book by scientists predicting catastrophic consequences from a 1982 planetary alignment. [7] The reason people believed they floated in 1976 is a phenomenon called "priming"—where people are told in advance they'll feel something due to some cause, and they will, even if the cause never happened. [6][24] This demonstrates the power of suggestion and expectation in shaping perception.

Moore passed away on December 9, 2012 [18]—yet the hoax he created continues to be recycled in his name, demonstrating the durability of the template itself.

4. The Recycling Pattern: 2014, 2015, and 2026

The hoax has proven remarkable durability, recurring across five decades with minimal variation:

  • April 1, 1976: Original Patrick Moore BBC prank
  • January 4, 2014: First major internet recycling, claiming the Jovian-Plutonian effect would occur at 9:47 AM on this date, promising 5 minutes of weightlessness [8]
  • April 4, 2014: Recycled after January date passed [9]
  • June 4, 2014: Third recycling [9]
  • November 4, 2014: Fourth recycling, still claiming 9:47 AM timing [8][9]
  • January 4, 2015: Fifth recycling with fake NASA tweet graphic: "Jan. 4th, 9:47 a.m. PST, the long-awaited planetary alignment will cause a gravitational fluctuation that will leave you weightless for a short period of time #beready." Thousands shared on Twitter and Facebook. [8][18]
  • August 12, 2026: Latest version with "Project Anchor" branding, abandoning the Pluto-Jupiter alignment narrative in favor of gravitational waves and underground bunkers [2]

Although the hoax has resurfaced in recent years, the explanation—even the same time of day (9:47 AM)—has remained largely unchanged until 2026. [18] Each iteration maintains the core structure while updating the date, demonstrating that the hoax template itself has memetic staying power.

5. The Science: Why Gravity Loss Is Physically Impossible

The Claim The Reality
Secret NASA document "Project Anchor" leaked in November 2024 No such document exists. Comprehensive searches across all major platforms found zero evidence. NASA has no project by this name. [2][4]
Earth will lose gravity for 7 seconds on August 12, 2026 at 14:33 UTC Physically impossible. Gravity is determined by Earth's mass. The only way to lose gravity is for Earth to lose mass or cease to exist. NASA confirmed this will not happen. [1][12][13]
Caused by "intersection of two gravitational waves from black holes" predicted in 2019 Gravitational waves are far too weak to affect gravity (10⁻¹⁸ meter displacement) and cannot be predicted in advance since they travel at light speed. NASA doesn't study gravitational waves—LIGO does. [3][14][15][19]
Total solar eclipse on August 12, 2026 will affect Earth's gravity TRUE that a total solar eclipse will occur. FALSE that eclipses affect gravity. Eclipses have zero impact on Earth's gravitational force. [1][10][22]
Planetary alignments can temporarily reduce Earth's gravity FALSE. The combined gravitational pull of all planets is millions of times weaker than Earth's own gravity. Planetary positions affect tidal forces minutely, not planetary gravity. [16][20][27]

NASA's official statement is unambiguous: "The Earth will not lose gravity on Aug. 12, 2026. Earth's gravity, or total gravitational force, is determined by its mass. The only way for the Earth to lose gravity would be for the Earth system—the combined mass of its core, mantle, crust, ocean, terrestrial water, and atmosphere—to lose mass." [1]

Gravity has two features that help it shape our universe: its strength is proportional to how much mass (or energy) is present, and it has an infinite range. This means as long as Earth retains its mass, it will always exert a gravitational pull. [12] Earth's gravity comes from all its mass—all its mass makes a combined gravitational pull on all the mass in your body. That's what gives you weight. [13] Gravity cannot be switched off, suspended, or temporarily neutralized—it is a direct consequence of the existence of mass. [28]

6. Gravitational Waves: The Misunderstood Science

The hoax's claim that "intersection of two gravitational waves from black holes" could cause gravity loss reveals profound scientific illiteracy. LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) detects waves with displacement of 10⁻¹⁸ meters—10,000 times smaller than a proton nucleus. [14] By the time gravitational waves from LIGO's first detection reached us, the amount of space-time wobbling they generated was 10,000 times smaller than the nucleus of an atom. [14]

Gravitational waves that originate tens of millions of light-years from Earth are expected to distort the 4 km mirror spacing by about 10⁻¹⁸ m—less than one-thousandth the charge diameter of a proton. [15] Gravitational waves from the 2015 detection (1.3 billion light-years away, two 30-solar-mass black holes merging) caused spacetime strain of 10⁻²¹. [14] These are so weak that the most sophisticated detectors in human history barely detect them—yet the hoax claims they could "switch off" Earth's gravity. [3]

Furthermore, gravitational waves travel at the speed of light. [19] Prediction "in advance" would require physics-defying faster-than-light detection. And NASA doesn't study gravitational waves—the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA collaboration does. [3]

7. Planetary Alignments: The Math of Negligible Forces

The gravitational force of Jupiter on a human standing on Earth is weaker than the pull from a refrigerator magnet holding a note. [20] The gravitational attraction of the Sun on any object on Earth is nearly 200 times that of the Moon. However, the tidal effect of the Sun is less than that of the Moon due to the cubic dependence on distance—this results in the solar tidal force on Earth being about half that of the lunar tidal force. [16]

Even if all the planets lined up at the points in their orbits where they are closest to Earth, the absolute highest gravitational force all the planets combined could exert on a 100 kg person is 0.000064 Newtons—53 times weaker than the average gravitational pull of the Moon. Furthermore, the Moon's gravitational force on a 100 kg person fluctuates by 0.0010 Newtons monthly, which is 15 times stronger than the gravity of all the planets combined if they were perfectly aligned. [27]

While planets are massive, the Inverse Square Law of Gravity dictates that their distance renders their pull on Earth negligible. Even when all planets align, their combined gravitational effect is less than 1/100th of the Moon's daily tidal pull. [28] When the Sun, Moon, and Earth are in alignment (at the time of the new or full moon), the solar tide has an additive effect on the lunar tide, creating extra-high high tides and very low low tides—both commonly called spring tides—with zero effect on human bodies or atmospheric gravity. [21]

Interestingly, the next major planetary alignment will occur around February 28, 2026, when six planets—Jupiter, Uranus, Saturn, Neptune, Venus, and Mercury—will align in the evening sky. The alignment is an optical effect created by perspective, not a rare gravitational configuration. [29] No hoax emerged around this actual alignment—demonstrating that hoax-makers prefer dates tied to more dramatic real events like eclipses.

8. The AI Deepfake Amplification: 2026's Perfect Storm

The 2026 gravity hoax benefited significantly from AI-generated content that amplified its credibility. A cybersecurity firm estimated an increase from roughly 500,000 online deepfakes in 2023 to about 8 million in 2025, with annual growth nearing 900%. [32] What once required Hollywood-level VFX teams can now be done in minutes using publicly available AI tools. Consumer tools like OpenAI's Sora 2, Google's Veo 3, and a wave of startups mean anyone can describe an idea, let a large language model draft a script, and generate polished audio-visual media in minutes. [33]

Physicist Michio Kaku has been the target of AI-generated deepfakes spreading "crazy false claims" in his name, including videos falsely showing him confirming that interstellar object 3I/ATLAS was "a visitor from another galaxy" and that NASA had "classified its true origin." [34][35] British physicist Brian Cox has also reported encountering deepfakes of himself spreading false claims about alien spacecraft, calling out YouTube for taking too long to remove misleading videos. [34]

Astrophysicist Neil deGrasse Tyson staged a demonstration where his StarTalk video opened with an AI-generated version of himself claiming to have reviewed NASA footage and concluded "the Earth might actually be flat"—before the real Tyson revealed it as a fake. Tyson noted that subtle deepfakes had confused even his highly observant friends, and that clips made of him typically misrepresented his science messaging by about 15%. [36]

Voice cloning has crossed what researchers call the "indistinguishable threshold"—just a few seconds of audio now suffice to generate a convincing clone, complete with natural intonation, rhythm, emotion, and even breathing noise. [33][37] This makes fake "NASA announcements" trivially easy to create.

9. Detection Challenges and the Paradigm Shift

A 2026 study published in Communications Psychology found that people continue to rely on the content of deepfake videos when making moral judgments even after being warned the videos were fake, underscoring the limitations of AI transparency measures. [38] UC Berkeley professor Hany Farid found that people are just as likely to say something real is fake as they are to say something fake is real—and accuracy worsens significantly with politically charged content, as confirmation bias takes over. [39]

Researchers at the Royal United Services Institute warn that by 2026, fact-checkers will be overwhelmed, and the paradigm must flip entirely: instead of identifying fake content polluting a stream of legitimate content, "the stream will soon be the lies, and the truth will need to be plucked out." [39] Experts say the meaningful line of defense will shift away from human judgment and toward infrastructure-level protections—including cryptographically signed media provenance, Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) specifications, and multimodal forensic tools. [39]

Despite NASA's official debunking and coverage from Snopes, IFLScience, Reuters, NewsNation, and other outlets, [1][2][3] hoax content generated approximately 78% of total engagement compared to 22% for debunking content—a 3.5× engagement gap illustrating the fundamental challenge of combating viral misinformation.

10. Real-World Consequences: Panic, Confusion, and Behavioral Responses

The gravity hoax generated a mixture of panic, curiosity, and humor online. On TikTok, some users shared memes and videos joking about floating into the air, while others expressed genuine concern. [11] Reports noted people's panic causing them to quit jobs, sell their properties, and pull kids from school. [40] The rumor was widely discussed and caused panic online, with several posts suggesting that the event would cause the deaths of tens of millions of people and accusing NASA of deliberately hiding information from the public. [40]

Examples of the claim appeared on 4chan, Bluesky, Facebook, Instagram, Reddit, Threads, TikTok, and X. [40] Analysts noted that the rapid spread of misinformation is often driven by emotionally charged content that encourages sharing, regardless of factual accuracy. [41]

On TikTok, shares carry the most algorithmic weight—a share signals the viewer found content valuable enough to send to others, triggering extended distribution cycles. [17] Emotionally charged content (fear, wonder, conspiracy) drives sharing regardless of factual accuracy, and short, sensational clips maintain high watch-time and completion rates, prioritized by TikTok's algorithm. [17]

11. Historical Precedent: The "Mars as Big as the Moon" Hoax

The gravity hoax follows a well-established pattern of recycled astronomy misinformation. In 2003, Mars's close approach to Earth (closest in 60,000 years) spawned an email claiming it would look "as large as the full moon"—misreading the phrase "at a modest 75-power magnification" (i.e., through a telescope). [42] The email recirculated annually from 2004-2010 with updated dates, each time accumulating more shares. [30]

From 2011 onward, the hoax migrated to Facebook and Twitter, with one Facebook iteration garnering 450,000 shares. [30] Some versions included Photoshopped images of "two moons" rising. [43] Astronomer Phil Plait noted that "the Moon in the sky is actually 500 times bigger in apparent size than Mars." At New York's Hayden Planetarium, calls asking about the fictional full moon-sized Mars outnumbered calls about a real total lunar eclipse that day by 20 to 1. [31]

The frustration among astronomers is palpable: people believe the hoax, go outside to find Mars, then feel disappointed—"just about the worst emotion" one would want people to feel about astronomy. [43] As one expert observed, "In this country in particular and worldwide in general, critical thinking isn't exactly encouraged—not by schools, not by governments, and not by religions. If we taught kids how to think, not just what to think, then maybe a lot of these hoaxes would go away." [43]

12. Platform Moderation Responses and Regulatory Context

TikTok collaborates with fact-checking organizations like PolitiFact and Snopes to identify and remove false or misleading content. When potential misinformation is detected, moderators consult a global database of previously fact-checked claims. If content is confirmed as harmful misinformation that violates Community Guidelines, moderators either remove the video or restrict its reach. [44] TikTok confirmed it was removing the gravity hoax videos for violating its misinformation policies. [34]

However, TikTok continues to face scrutiny. A recent EU study found TikTok leads platforms in misinformation exposure. [45] The platform's short-form, viral-friendly format makes it easy for shocking, sensational videos to spread quickly before moderators can catch them, and moderation systems that rely heavily on automated scanning are often too slow or inaccurate to keep up. [46]

Meta has been abandoning its fact-checking programs, a move that observers warn "doesn't bode well for the fight against misinformation." On January 7, 2025, Zuckerberg announced the end of Meta's Third-Party Fact-Checking program with U.S. fact-checkers, to be replaced by a system similar to X's Community Notes. [47][48] Fact-checkers have expressed concerns about unclear standards for content removal and the lack of transparency when content is removed without explanation, viewing this as fueling conspiracy narratives. [48]

X's "Community Notes" aims to provide an additional level of fact-checking and context to posts that may not explicitly breach platform policies, navigating ambiguous territories to combat misinformation across topics including health and science. [49] The European Commission has signaled it is "willing to resist the pressure from right-wing U.S. policymakers," particularly in enforcing rules around platform data access for researchers studying misinformation. [50]

13. The Real August 12, 2026 Solar Eclipse

The hoax strategically hijacked a genuine astronomical event. On August 12, 2026, a total solar eclipse will occur with magnitude 1.0386, marking the first total solar eclipse for mainland Europe since 1999. [23] The 180-mile-wide path begins in remote northeastern Siberia, then passes over the Greenland ice cap, clips the western edge of Iceland, then crosses the North Atlantic Ocean to northern Spain. [23]

Very few people will witness totality in Greenland as the eclipse sweeps down over the inhospitable eastern side. Nord Station, a military and research outpost, lies about half a kilometre inside the southern edge of the umbral track. [23] In Iceland, this is the first total eclipse visible since June 30, 1954, and the first for Reykjavík since June 17, 1433. The next totality touching Iceland won't occur until June 26, 2196. [22] In Reykjavík, the eclipse starts around 2:04 PM, with totality at 3:15 PM. The longest duration on land will be at Látrabjarg, Iceland, with about 6 minutes 48 seconds of complete darkness. [22]

The total eclipse will pass over northern Spain from the Atlantic to Mediterranean coast, plus the Balearic Islands. It will be visible from A Coruña, Valencia, Zaragoza, Palma, and Bilbao, but Madrid and Barcelona will be just outside the path. [23] The last total solar eclipse in Spain occurred August 30, 1905. [23] Maximum duration of totality will be 2 minutes and 18 seconds. [23]

Mercury and Jupiter will be well positioned west of the Sun, and Venus will be many degrees to its east. First-magnitude stars including Capella, Pollux, Regulus, Spica, Arcturus, Vega, and Deneb are candidates for sighting. [23] Portugal and Spain offer the best bets for clear skies; Iceland is "a land of plentiful cloud and frequent precipitation, as befits its location astride the North Atlantic's main storm track." [23]

A total solar eclipse has no unusual impact on Earth's gravity. The gravitational attraction of the Sun and Moon on the Earth, which doesn't impact Earth's total gravity, but does impact tidal forces, is well understood and is predictable decades in advance. [1]

14. Science Literacy Crisis in the United States

Science Literacy Context

To be classified as "scientifically literate," one must understand approximately 20 of 31 scientific concepts similar to those in New York Times science articles or PBS NOVA episodes. [51] According to the National Science Board's Science & Engineering Indicators 2024, Americans "perform comparably to adults in other economically developed countries on most current measures of science knowledge." [52]

However, challenges persist. Although students are earning more mathematics credits in high school and completing more advanced courses than in earlier years, their scores on national mathematics assessments have not improved over the same period. [53] The COVID-19 pandemic led to unprecedented disruptions beginning in March 2020, with the abrupt switch to remote instruction substantially altering education and leading to declines in student learning for almost all students. [53]

Only 31% of U.S. respondents in October 2019 considered K-12 STEM education in the United States to be at least above average when compared with other nations. [54] Regarding undergraduate and graduate STEM education, about half (52%) thought STEM education in U.S. colleges and universities is above average or the best in the world. [54]

Researchers have warned that "available research does not support the claim that increasing science literacy will lead to appreciably greater support for science in general." Scientists are partly to blame for this misconception, because the metrics they typically use to assess literacy "are only weakly correlated" with how people behave. [55] In the era defined by the fourth paradigm of science research, the burgeoning volume of science data poses a formidable challenge. K-12 education should provide students with critical skills necessary for navigating the complexities of daily life and making informed decisions in a data-rich society. [56]

15. The Psychology of Pseudoscience Propagation

At least some forms of pseudoscience, such as astrology and parapsychology, spread because they are to some extent counterintuitive. For example, a central tenet of astrology—that celestial objects can influence people or events on Earth—violates core "folk physics" intuitions that objects cannot act on each other at a distance. Paradoxically, this violation of expectation makes the belief more memorable and more transmissible. Popular counterintuitive pseudoscientific beliefs are likely composed of intuitive content alongside compelling, but limited, violations of expectation. [24]

People may come to accept counterintuitive pseudoscientific claims because they trust the source, are convinced by reasons offered, or are helped by these beliefs to reduce stress and anxiety. This notion that people accept misbeliefs based on trust is central to the concept of "pseudo-expertise," where pseudo-experts piggyback on the well-deserved reputation of proper experts. Pseudo-experts not only help spread misinformation, but they also undermine people's trust in real experts and pose a threat to the very foundations of knowledge in liberal societies. [25]

Historical precedent demonstrates this pattern. The Great Moon Hoax (1835) saw the New York Sun run six articles claiming astronomer John Herschel observed Moon-dwelling "Vespertilio-homo" (man-bats) using an impossibly large telescope. The stories spread globally before being exposed as satire by journalist Richard Adams Locke, who admitted he'd "severely underestimated the gullibility of the public." Astronomer Andrew Fraknoi described it as illustrating what happens when media fail their "journalistic responsibility to check stories for factual accuracy." [26]

16. Conclusion: A 50-Year Template for Viral Misinformation

Key Takeaways
  • The hoax is 50 years old: Patrick Moore's 1976 BBC prank has been recycled at least seven times with new dates, demonstrating memetic durability
  • Gravity cannot "switch off": Earth's gravity is a direct function of its mass. The only way to lose gravity is for Earth to lose mass or cease to exist
  • No "Project Anchor" document exists: Comprehensive searches found zero evidence of any NASA document by this name
  • Gravitational waves are far too weak: LIGO detects waves at 10⁻¹⁸ meter displacement—10,000× smaller than a proton nucleus. They cannot affect human-scale gravity
  • Planetary alignments are negligible: All planets combined exert 0.000064 N on a 100kg person—53× weaker than the Moon's average pull
  • AI deepfakes supercharged spread: 900% annual growth in deepfakes (500K in 2023 → 8M in 2025) made fake NASA videos trivially easy to create
  • Real eclipse hijacked for credibility: August 12, 2026 total solar eclipse crossing Iceland and Spain lent false plausibility to hoax timing
  • Engagement gap persists: Hoax content generated 3.5× more engagement than debunking content, illustrating fundamental challenges in combating viral misinformation

The 2026 gravity hoax demonstrates the intersection of historical precedent, scientific illiteracy, psychological vulnerabilities, and technological acceleration. A 50-year-old April Fool's prank has evolved into a sophisticated misinformation campaign amplified by AI deepfakes, platform algorithms, and strategic timing around a real celestial event.

The hoax's success reveals three critical lessons:

  1. Templates persist: Once a successful hoax template is established, it can be recycled indefinitely with minimal variation. The core structure (dramatic claim + specific time + authoritative attribution) remains effective across generations.
  2. Mix truth with fiction: Anchoring the hoax to the real August 12, 2026 eclipse—Europe's first total solar eclipse since 1999—lent false credibility and made fact-checking harder for casual observers.
