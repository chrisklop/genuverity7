TL;DR

CONTEXT: The Architecture of Modern Conservative Media

Rupert Murdoch built the most influential media empire in history—controlling newspapers, television, and streaming across Australia, the UK, and the United States. His outlets have shaped election outcomes, promoted climate denial for decades, and paid $787 million to settle defamation claims over 2020 election lies. At 93, he stepped down as Fox chairman in 2023, but his legacy—partisan media as a political weapon—remains.

Executive Summary

From inheriting a single Adelaide newspaper in 1952 to commanding a media empire worth over $20 billion, Rupert Murdoch transformed global media and politics. His holdings include Fox Corporation (Fox News, Fox Sports), News Corp (Wall Street Journal, The Sun, The Australian), and stakes in streaming platforms. His newspapers have backed winning candidates in nearly every UK election since 1979. His cable network became the most-watched in America. Yet scandals followed: the phone hacking crisis that shuttered News of the World, and the $787M Dominion settlement exposing Fox's knowing broadcast of election lies. [4]

Australian Origins: The Adelaide Inheritance (1952-1968)

Keith Rupert Murdoch was born March 11, 1931, in Melbourne, Australia. His father, Sir Keith Murdoch, was a prominent war correspondent and newspaper executive. When Keith died in 1952, the younger Murdoch inherited The News, a struggling Adelaide afternoon paper—and a burning ambition. [9]

Within a decade, Murdoch had acquired newspapers across Australia:

  • 1960: Purchased The Daily Mirror (Sydney)
  • 1964: Founded The Australian—the country's first national newspaper
  • 1967: Acquired The Herald (Melbourne)

His tabloid formula—sensationalism, scandal, and political controversy—proved devastatingly effective. But Australia was too small for his ambitions.

UK Expansion: Fleet Street and Political Kingmaking (1969-1989)

In 1969, Murdoch bought News of the World and The Sun—beginning his transformation of British media and politics. The Sun pioneered tabloid journalism with aggressive headlines, Page Three girls, and unabashed Conservative backing. [8]

In 1981, he acquired The Times and The Sunday Times—prestigious broadsheets that gave his empire credibility. Margaret Thatcher's government approved the purchase despite concerns about media concentration.

The Kingmaker Pattern

The Sun has backed the winning party in every UK general election since 1979—except 1992 (when it still backed the winner) and 2017 (hung parliament). From Thatcher to Blair to Johnson, Murdoch's endorsement became a virtual requirement for power. [8]

In 1989, Murdoch launched Sky Television, later merged with BSB to form BSkyB—revolutionizing British satellite broadcasting and sports coverage.

The Phone Hacking Scandal: News of the World's Demise

The British empire unraveled spectacularly in 2011. The phone hacking scandal exposed systematic illegal activity at Murdoch papers, including:

  • Hacking voicemails of murdered schoolgirl Milly Dowler
  • Targeting 7/7 bombing victims' families
  • Intercepting communications of royals, celebrities, and politicians

The revelations led to: [1]

  • Closure of News of the World (168 years of publication ended July 2011)
  • The Leveson Inquiry into press ethics
  • Criminal convictions for editors and executives
  • Over $500 million in legal settlements to victims

Rupert Murdoch testified before Parliament, calling it "the most humble day of my life." His son James resigned from multiple positions. [3]

Fox News, launched in 1996 with Roger Ailes, became the dominant force in American cable news. By 2002, it surpassed CNN in ratings. Key characteristics: [5]

  • Opinion-driven primetime: O'Reilly, Hannity, Carlson commanded massive audiences
  • "Fair and Balanced" branding: Positioned as alternative to "liberal mainstream media"
  • Republican alignment: Near-total support for GOP candidates and policies
  • Climate skepticism: Systematic questioning of climate science

Climate Denial: A Decades-Long Campaign

Research published in Nature Climate Change documented Murdoch outlets' systematic promotion of climate skepticism. [7]

The pattern spans all three continents:

  • Australia: The Australian and Sky News Australia consistently feature climate skeptics
  • UK: The Sun and Times have published climate-skeptic opinion pieces
  • US: Fox News hosts regularly question climate science; Tucker Carlson called it "a religion"
Australian Bushfire Coverage

During the 2019-2020 Australian bushfires, Murdoch outlets promoted the false narrative that arson, not climate change, was primarily responsible. Analysis found The Australian published 45 articles in one week suggesting arson as the main cause—contradicting official findings that less than 1% of fires were deliberately lit.

The Dominion Reckoning: $787 Million in Lies

Fox News' coverage of the 2020 election led to the largest defamation settlement in American media history. [6]

Dominion Voting Systems sued Fox for broadcasting false claims that their machines were rigged against Trump. Discovery revealed: [4]

  • Murdoch privately called election fraud claims "really crazy stuff"
  • Fox hosts texted each other that they knew claims were false
  • Network continued broadcasting lies to retain viewers fleeing to Newsmax
  • Murdoch acknowledged in deposition he could have stopped the coverage

In April 2023, Fox agreed to pay $787.5 million—without admitting wrongdoing but with the truth exposed in court filings. A separate Smartmatic lawsuit seeking $2.7 billion remains pending.

Political Influence Across Three Continents

Murdoch's political influence has shaped democratic outcomes worldwide:

United Kingdom:

  • Every prime minister from Thatcher to Cameron cultivated Murdoch relationships
  • Tony Blair flew to Australia in 1995 to address News Corp executives before his election
  • The Sun's "It's The Sun Wot Won It" headline after 1992 Conservative victory
  • Brexit advocacy across Murdoch papers preceded 2016 referendum

United States:

  • Fox News became unofficial Trump administration media arm (2017-2021)
  • Multiple Fox personalities joined Trump White House
  • New York Post amplified Hunter Biden laptop story before 2020 election

Australia:

  • Former Prime Minister Kevin Rudd called Murdoch a "cancer on democracy"
  • 2021 parliamentary inquiry examined Murdoch media dominance
  • News Corp controls ~70% of Australian print circulation

Departure and Legacy (2023-Present)

In September 2023, Murdoch announced he would step down as chairman of both Fox Corporation and News Corp, passing control to son Lachlan Murdoch. At 92, he assumed the title of "Chairman Emeritus." [10]

In a letter to staff, he wrote: "For my entire professional life, I have been engaged in the contest of ideas... Our companies are in robust health."

His departure came amid:

  • The $787M Dominion settlement (largest in media history)
  • Pending $2.7B Smartmatic lawsuit
  • Tucker Carlson's firing after text messages revealed in litigation
  • Fox News ratings dominance despite controversies
The Transnational Conservative Media Ecosystem

Murdoch's legacy is not any single outlet but the architecture of modern partisan media. Before Fox News, American cable news attempted objectivity. Before The Sun, British tabloids didn't openly function as party organs. His model—entertainment-first news, opinion-as-news, audience-as-tribe—has been copied worldwide. The ecosystem he built continues operating under Lachlan Murdoch, with Fox News remaining America's most-watched cable network.
