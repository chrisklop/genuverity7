A 2023 Harvard-led analysis published in Science confirmed that ExxonMobil's internal models predicted global warming with 72% accuracy from the 1970s onward — while the company's public communications systematically promoted doubt.

The national security consequences are now measurable. Only 47 of 500+ installations had completed resilience master planning. Naval Station Norfolk faces 280 tidal flood events annually by 2050, up from 9 today. Section 1: The $15 Billion Reality

The scope of climate damage to U.S. military readiness is no longer a projected risk — it is an accounting problem. A February 2026 Government Accountability Office report (GAO-26-107786) found that the Defense Department's own estimate puts extreme weather damage to military installations at at least $15 billion over the past decade. Of that figure, $8.5 billion was absorbed directly by military installation budgets, straining funds otherwise earmarked for modernization and operational readiness. Congress has passed over $4 billion in emergency supplemental assistance for major disasters — money that is additive to, not a replacement for, the installation budget strain. [19]

The GAO finding exposed a secondary crisis: the DoD only began systematic tracking of extreme weather costs in 2024. All prior damage figures are estimates, meaning the $15 billion may significantly undercount actual losses. GAO Director Kristy Williams noted that "the scope of DoD's data collection excludes potentially billions of dollars in damage" from natural disasters including seismic events. [19] Former Senior Climate Advisor to the U.S. Army Will Rogers, writing in Breaking Defense in February 2026, confirmed the $8.5 billion absorption figure from firsthand institutional experience. [23]

As of spring 2025, only 47 of 500+ military installations had completed resilience master planning requirements mandated by Congress — with approximately 130 more in progress. The DoD manages approximately 300,000 buildings valued at $590 billion, and at least 30 installations already face elevated sea-level rise risk. [8] Three feet of sea-level rise — a conservative mid-century projection — would threaten 128 coastal bases valued at $100 billion. [7] Despite canceling 91 climate studies and banning "climate" from planning documents, the Navy simultaneously raised piers at Norfolk at a cost of $60 million each — physical evidence that climate adaptation is operationally necessary regardless of how it is labeled. [26]

Section 2: Patient Zero — 1980

The organized campaign to suppress climate science traces to a specific, documented origin. Archival research published in Policy Sciences and confirmed by Boston University found that the American Petroleum Institute was distributing false and misleading information about climate change to Congress as early as 1980 — nearly a decade before the Global Climate Coalition was formed, which had previously been considered the origin point of organized industry denial. [15]

The industry's internal knowledge predates even the 1980 disinformation campaign. In 1959, nuclear scientist Edward Teller explained at an American Petroleum Institute symposium that CO2 emissions from burning oil would melt ice caps and raise sea levels. In 1977, ExxonMobil senior scientist James Black briefed the company's management committee: "there is general scientific agreement that the most likely manner in which mankind is influencing the global climate is through carbon dioxide release from the burning of fossil fuels." [5] A 2023 Harvard-led analysis published in Science confirmed that ExxonMobil's internal models predicted global warming with an average skill score of 72% — while the company's public communications simultaneously promoted uncertainty. [2]

In 1989, ExxonMobil, Mobil, Chevron, BP, Shell, and other fossil fuel companies co-founded the Global Climate Coalition (GCC) explicitly to oppose emissions reductions and cast doubt on climate science. The GCC operated as the primary organized denial vehicle through the 1990s. When it dissolved in 2002 as the science became undeniable, the strategy evolved — pivoting from outright denial to delay tactics, greenwashing, false solution promotion, and what the 2024 congressional investigation called "doublespeak." [4]

A 2024 joint bicameral staff report by Senator Sheldon Whitehouse and Representative Jamie Raskin documented that BP's own internal presentations contained "8+ statements contradicting its public ad campaigns." ExxonMobil's 2025 Climate Report again failed independent scientific review, per the Union of Concerned Scientists. Shell funded $25 million in University of California–Berkeley research while lobbying against climate regulations. [1] [3]

Section 3: The Military Installations Under Threat

Naval Station Norfolk is the world's largest naval base and among the most climate-vulnerable pieces of military infrastructure on earth. Sea-level rise in Norfolk runs at 5.6 millimeters per year — twice the global average — due to ocean currents and active land subsidence. Today, tidal flooding affects the area 9 times per year on average. Under intermediate sea-level rise projections, by 2050 that figure reaches 280 flood events per year. [9]

Tyndall Air Force Base in Florida was effectively destroyed by Hurricane Michael in October 2018. The Category 5 storm brought 9–14 feet of storm surge and winds of 140–161 mph, damaging or destroying 95% of facilities on the installation. F-22 Raptors that could not be evacuated were damaged. The initial $3 billion rebuild estimate was revised upward to a confirmed $5 billion. The Air Force Installation and Mission Support Center (AFIMSC) is using the rebuild — approximately 15% complete as of early 2025, with the bulk of projects expected by mid-2027 and full completion expected by 2029 — as a template for climate-resilient base design across all installations. All new construction is built to withstand minimum 165 mph winds. [20]

Andersen Air Force Base in Guam — the U.S. military's critical hub for Indo-Pacific Command operations — sustained approximately $10 billion in damage from Typhoon Mawar in 2023. [10] Camp Lejeune in North Carolina required $3.6 billion in recovery funding after Hurricane Florence in 2018. Offutt Air Force Base in Nebraska — headquarters of U.S. Strategic Command (STRATCOM), the command responsible for the nation's nuclear arsenal — was inundated by Missouri River flooding in March 2019 as a direct consequence of extreme precipitation. [23] More than 10,000 troops have been sidelined by heat-related illnesses since 2018, with heat exhaustion rates among service members rising 52% between 2020 and 2024. [10]

The incident received limited public attention because it occurred during a period when the DoD was still permitted to use the words "climate change." [23]

Section 4: The "New Denial" — Attacking Solutions, Not Science

Climate denial has undergone a structural transformation. Research tracked by the Center for Countering Digital Hate (CCDH) found that "New Denial" claims — those attacking climate solutions (renewable energy, electric vehicles, emissions policy) rather than the underlying science — now constitute 70% of all climate denial content on YouTube, up from 35% six years prior. [16] This pivot is strategically sophisticated: attacking solutions rather than settled science is harder to debunk because it operates in the legitimately contested space of policy tradeoffs rather than empirical fact.

The disinformation infrastructure operates through overlapping layers. The fossil fuel industry channels money through 501(c)(4) nonprofits not required to disclose donors, shell companies, and political action committees. The Epoch Times generated $1.5 million in advertising revenue despite violating Google's climate denial content policies. [16] The Heritage Foundation, Heartland Institute, and Cato Institute receive significant fossil fuel funding and produce congressional testimony and media commentary designed to manufacture uncertainty. BP budgeted $2.5–$4.5 million for "hard persuasion" tactics targeting Washington state specifically. [4] FirstEnergy paid $61 million in bribes to Ohio House Speaker Larry Householder to secure a $1.3 billion ratepayer bailout; Alabama Power funneled $900,000+ to "news" websites to generate favorable coverage. [17]

Generative AI has lowered the cost of producing misleading content to near zero, enabling industrial-scale output of climate denial material. Meta discontinued its U.S. fact-checking program in 2025. Platform enforcement of climate misinformation policies remains inconsistent. The structural advantages of disinformation — cheap to produce, simple to consume, politically pre-sorted — have only widened as accuracy-oriented journalism becomes more expensive and politically risky in fossil-fuel-dependent markets. [16]

Section 5: Evidence Deep-Dive — The Hegseth Memo

On March 20, 2025, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth signed a memorandum directing termination of over $580 million in programs, contracts, and grants deemed misaligned with DoD priorities. The memo is a primary source document, available directly from the Defense Department's official servers. [22]

The memo's key operational directives:

  • All mission statements and military planning documents must be reviewed to ensure removal of "references to climate change and related subjects"
  • DoD contracted for a word-search of all outside contracts for the term "climate" to zero out all related work
  • Climate-specific cuts included: research into climate-linked instability in Africa, Naval ship decarbonization programs, and the Biden-era climate resilience portal
  • 91 studies were canceled, saving a claimed $30 million — while the military simultaneously spent $60 million per pier to raise Navy infrastructure at Norfolk from rising seas
  • Navy Secretary John Phelan separately ended the Navy's Climate Action Plan

The memo contains a legally and operationally significant internal contradiction. While banning all references to climate change from planning, the same document directs installations to continue "assessing weather-related impacts on operations" and "mitigating weather-related risks." This effectively bans the scientific framework — climate science, climate models, climate risk assessment — that military planners need to execute what the memo still requires them to do. [22] [26]

Context for the scale of the reversal: the classified 2008 National Intelligence Assessment concluded that climate "will have wide-ranging implications for US national security interests over the next 20 years." Its lead author, Dr. Thomas Fingar, former Deputy Director of National Intelligence, stated of the document: "There's no secret stuff in here… Just good analysts working with publicly available information and applying good methodological tradecraft." The document remains classified as of 2026, 17 years after its completion. [14]

Section 6: Foreign Amplification — Russia, China, and Climate Disinformation as Geopolitical Weapon

Climate disinformation has metastasized beyond fossil fuel industry funding into foreign adversary information operations. Russia exploits climate disasters as force multipliers for geopolitical destabilization, using four documented narrative frames: Climate Denialism; "Securitization" (framing fossil fuel dependence as a security strength rather than a vulnerability); climate action as Western Neocolonialism; and Green Parties as foreign-political-interference vectors targeting European democracies. [27]

After the Maui wildfires killed more than 100 people in 2023, Russian state media suggested U.S. aid to Ukraine had left Hawaii vulnerable to the disaster. Chinese state media went further, falsely attributing the deaths to an experimental U.S. weather control weapon. After major floods struck Spain, Poland, and the United States in late 2024, Russian state media ran coordinated campaigns explicitly claiming that Western support for Ukraine had left nations unable to respond to disasters. [12]

Polish military intelligence confirmed in a February 2025 report that Russia had been systematically intensifying climate disinformation campaigns since the invasion of Ukraine, specifically targeting EU climate policy instruments including the Emissions Trading Scheme, the European Green Deal, and the Fit for 55 legislative package. The China-Russia information environment report published by the Center for European Policy Analysis (CEPA) in 2025 documented growing convergence in foreign information manipulation operations, with climate denial serving as a shared narrative aligned with both countries' fossil fuel economic interests. [27]

Russia does not need to manufacture climate denial content — it amplifies existing domestic narratives generated by industry-funded American think tanks and media outlets, making attribution to a foreign source difficult and deniable. Former DHS Secretary Michael Chertoff, a George W. Bush appointee, warned in March 2026 that Americans are "less able to protect" themselves when relying on false information from adversaries or dysfunctional platforms, noting that "reliable information is a requirement for tackling any risk — whether hurricanes or security threats." [11]

Section 7: The Legal Battlefield

The accountability reckoning for six decades of climate disinformation is now playing out in American courtrooms — and has drawn an extraordinary federal government intervention on behalf of fossil fuel companies.

In May 2025, the State of Hawaii filed suit in state court against seven groups of affiliated fossil fuel companies and the American Petroleum Institute for climate deception, failure to warn, public nuisance, trespass, negligence, and civil aiding and abetting. The lawsuit was filed directly after the U.S. Department of Justice filed a preemptive suit in federal court seeking to block Hawaii's action before it was even filed. Columbia Law's Michael Gerrard, founder of the Sabin Center for Climate Change Law, called the DOJ maneuver unprecedented: "I've never heard of the federal government — or anyone — filing a lawsuit seeking an injunction to block someone from filing a lawsuit against a third party." [21]

On April 30, 2025, the DOJ also filed suit against Michigan to block state climate damages claims, and separately against New York and Vermont challenging their climate superfund laws. The legal basis: that state climate actions violate the Clean Air Act (preemption), constitutional due process, and the federal foreign affairs power. [25] The April 8, 2025 executive order "Protecting American Energy from State Overreach" provided the framework for the DOJ actions — making explicit the connection between industry disinformation and executive branch policy capture.

In February 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court agreed to hear the Boulder, Colorado climate accountability case — Boulder v. Suncor/ExxonMobil — with oral arguments expected during the October 2026 term. The Court will decide whether federal law shields fossil fuel companies from state and local damages claims for climate change-related harms. At stake: lawsuits filed by 11 states (California, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Maine, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, Rhode Island, Vermont) plus dozens of cities, counties, and tribes. Every state supreme court that has reviewed climate deception claims to date has allowed them to proceed. The SCOTUS case is the most consequential legal proceeding directly targeting the financial accountability of the climate disinformation campaign. [24]

Section 8: Contemporary Context — The Institutional Silencing

The 2025 Annual Threat Assessment released by DNI Tulsi Gabbard in March 2025 was the first ATA since 2009 to contain no mention of climate change — a historic departure from a 16-year bipartisan consensus in intelligence community threat assessment. Every prior ATA, under both Republican and Democratic administrations, had included climate as a threat driver. [6] [13]

The chilling effect on military expert opinion is documented and consequential. America's top military officers sat in "stony silence" when President Trump addressed them at Quantico on climate — those who spoke their minds risked being stripped of rank. Active military leaders cannot publicly contradict the commander-in-chief; the climate readiness warning has therefore shifted entirely to retired generals, admirals, and former officials. Senator Tammy Duckworth's March 11, 2025 letter to Secretary Hegseth warned that ending DoD climate programs "threatens national security, puts American lives at risk, and wastes billions of taxpayer dollars." [28]

The institutional silence itself is a victory condition for disinformation: the people with the most direct knowledge of climate's operational impact on military readiness cannot publicly say so. Former Army Climate Advisor Will Rogers explicitly called on Congress to mandate climate planning in defense legislation, independent of executive branch direction — noting that relying on a politically captured executive branch to voluntarily conduct climate risk assessment is no longer a viable strategy. [23]

The precedent being set is measurable: in 2019, the White House Legislative Affairs office edited Dr. Rod Schoonover's Congressional climate testimony, removing five pages on scientific baselines. National Security Council notes from that period were attributed to climate-science skeptic William Happer. The 2025 pattern is not aberrational — it is the institutionalization of a strategy that the fossil fuel industry spent decades and hundreds of millions of dollars making politically viable.

Section 9: Conclusion — Sixty-Seven Years of Foreseeable Harm

The chain of evidence is unusually complete. In 1959, fossil fuel executives were told that their products would melt ice caps and raise seas. By 1977, ExxonMobil's own scientists had modeled the warming trajectory with documented accuracy. By 1980, the American Petroleum Institute was distributing false information to Congress. By 1989, a formal industry coalition existed specifically to obstruct climate policy. By 2002, the coalition disbanded — not because it had succeeded in disproving climate science, but because the science had become too solid for the old arguments to work. By 2024, a joint bicameral congressional investigation had documented 60+ years of systematic deception using the industry's own internal documents. And by March 2025, the cumulative effect of that deception had helped produce a Defense Secretary who declared the Department of Defense "does not do climate change crap" — while simultaneously ordering installations to assess "weather-related impacts."

The downstream consequences are no longer theoretical. The GAO has confirmed $15 billion in military installation damage. The world's largest naval base faces 280 flood events per year within 25 years. The headquarters of U.S. Strategic Command was inundated by floodwater. The nation's F-22 fleet was exposed to a Category 5 hurricane. More than 10,000 troops have been sidelined by heat. And the intelligence community's annual threat assessment — the document that tells the United States what to prepare for — no longer mentions the threat at all.

Meanwhile, the legal accountability reckoning for this campaign is approaching a constitutional inflection point. The Supreme Court's October 2026 decision in Boulder v. Suncor will determine whether decades of documented industry deception can result in financial liability — or whether federal preemption arguments effectively immunize fossil fuel companies from any accountability for the harm their deliberate disinformation campaign caused. The outcome will either begin unwinding the damage or confirm that in America, the most consequential disinformation campaigns remain profitable and beyond legal reach.
