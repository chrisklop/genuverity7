TL;DR

HISTORICAL CONTEXT: America's First Modern Propaganda Apparatus

Created one week after the U.S. entered WWI, the Committee on Public Information mobilized 75,000 volunteer speakers, produced 100+ million pamphlets, and commissioned iconic war posters. The CPI's techniques—fear appeals, enemy demonization, and manufactured consent—were later privatized by Edward Bernays into the modern public relations industry. Its partnership with the Espionage and Sedition Acts created a template for wartime suppression of dissent still debated today.

Executive Summary

On April 13, 1917—exactly one week after Congress declared war on Germany—President Woodrow Wilson created the Committee on Public Information via Executive Order 2594. Headed by journalist George Creel, the CPI became the most ambitious propaganda operation in American history. It employed over 150,000 people at its peak, saturated the nation with pro-war messaging, and established the theoretical and practical foundations for both government information warfare and commercial public relations. [1]

Creation and Structure

Wilson's choice of George Creel—a muckraking journalist from Kansas City—was deliberate. Creel had edited campaign materials for Wilson's 1916 re-election and understood the power of mass communication. The President appointed him chairman, joined by the Secretaries of State, War, and Navy (Robert Lansing, Newton Baker, and Josephus Daniels). [7]

Creel organized the CPI into 21 divisions, each targeting a specific medium or audience:

  • Division of News: Released 6,000+ press releases (more than any government agency in history to that point)
  • Division of Pictorial Publicity: Commissioned 1,400+ poster designs from leading artists
  • Division of Films: Produced feature films like Pershing's Crusaders and America's Answer
  • Division of Four Minute Men: Coordinated 75,000 volunteer speakers nationwide
  • Foreign Section: Operated in over 30 countries

The Four Minute Men: 7.5 Million Speeches

The CPI's most innovative program was the Four Minute Men—a network of volunteer speakers who delivered brief patriotic talks during the four-minute reel changes at movie theaters. Named for the Revolutionary War minutemen, the program grew from a pilot in Chicago to a national phenomenon. [2]

By war's end, 75,000 speakers had delivered 7.5 million speeches to an estimated 314 million listeners—in a nation of 103 million people. Each speaker received weekly bulletins with approved talking points on topics like Liberty Bonds, food conservation, and the German threat. [6]

Four Minute Men Bulletin (1918)

"Ladies and Gentlemen: I have just received the information that there is a German spy among us—a German spy watching us. He is around, here somewhere, reporting on you and on me—sending reports about you and about me to Berlin and telling the Huns just what we TALK about..."

The Poster Campaign

The Division of Pictorial Publicity, led by illustrator Charles Dana Gibson, enlisted America's top artists to create what became the most iconic propaganda images in U.S. history. [3]

Key posters included:

  • "I Want YOU for U.S. Army" by James Montgomery Flagg (4 million copies printed)
  • "Destroy This Mad Brute" depicting Germany as a gorilla kidnapping Liberty
  • "Beat Back the Hun with Liberty Bonds" showing a blood-soaked German soldier
  • "Food Will Win the War" promoting rationing

The Library of Congress holds over 1,900 WWI propaganda posters from this campaign, many still studied as masterpieces of persuasive design.

Anti-German Campaigns and Hate Propaganda

The CPI's messaging deliberately stoked anti-German hysteria. Germans became "Huns"—a term evoking barbarism—and were portrayed as rapists, baby-killers, and subhuman monsters. This propaganda had real consequences: [4]

  • Sauerkraut was renamed "liberty cabbage"
  • Hamburgers became "liberty sandwiches"
  • German shepherds were rebranded "Alsatians"
  • German-language newspapers were shut down nationwide
  • Robert Prager, a German immigrant, was lynched by a mob in Illinois (1918)

The CPI distributed pamphlets with titles like "German War Practices" and "The German Whisper" warning citizens that German spies lurked everywhere.

Suppression of Dissent: The Legal Apparatus

The CPI operated in tandem with two of the most repressive laws in American history. The Espionage Act of 1917 criminalized interference with military operations or recruitment, while the Sedition Act of 1918 made it illegal to "utter, print, write, or publish any disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language" about the U.S. government, Constitution, or military. [5]

Over 2,000 prosecutions occurred under these acts, including:

  • Eugene V. Debs, Socialist Party leader, sentenced to 10 years for an anti-war speech
  • Charles Schenck, whose case produced Justice Holmes' famous "clear and present danger" test
  • Dozens of editors, publishers, and labor organizers imprisoned

In Schenck v. United States (1919), the Supreme Court unanimously upheld these convictions, ruling that free speech could be curtailed during wartime. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote: "The most stringent protection of free speech would not protect a man in falsely shouting fire in a theatre." [10]

Legacy: Edward Bernays and Modern PR

The CPI's most enduring impact came through its alumni. Edward Bernays—Sigmund Freud's nephew—worked in the CPI's Foreign Press Bureau and later became known as the "Father of Public Relations." [9]

Bernays recognized that wartime propaganda techniques could be applied commercially. His 1928 book Propaganda argued:

Edward Bernays, Propaganda (1928)

"The conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is an important element in democratic society. Those who manipulate this unseen mechanism of society constitute an invisible government which is the true ruling power."

Bernays applied CPI-developed techniques to campaigns including:

  • "Torches of Freedom" (1929): Made women's smoking socially acceptable
  • Bacon and eggs: Created the "All-American breakfast" for the pork industry
  • United Fruit Company (1954): Helped orchestrate the CIA-backed coup in Guatemala

Dissolution and Historical Assessment

The CPI was abolished on August 21, 1919, less than a year after the Armistice. Congress refused to appropriate funds for peacetime propaganda, and the agency's records were transferred to the National Archives. [8]

Creel defended his work in How We Advertised America (1920), arguing that the CPI engaged in "expression" rather than "repression." He maintained that the agency never censored information directly but instead flooded the zone with positive messaging. Critics then and now dispute this characterization.

The CPI established templates still in use today:

  • Message discipline: Centralized talking points distributed to thousands of speakers
  • Multi-channel saturation: Coordinated campaigns across all media
  • Enemy demonization: Dehumanizing language to build public support for war
  • Manufactured consent: Using psychological techniques to shape public opinion
Key Takeaways
  • Created April 13, 1917—one week after U.S. entered WWI
  • 75,000 Four Minute Men delivered 7.5 million speeches
  • 100+ million pamphlets distributed to American households
  • 1,400+ poster designs including iconic "I Want YOU"
  • Anti-German hysteria led to lynchings and cultural purges
  • 2,000+ prosecutions under Espionage and Sedition Acts
  • Edward Bernays privatized CPI techniques into modern PR
  • Dissolved August 1919—legacy shapes propaganda today
Historical Significance

The Committee on Public Information represented the first time the U.S. government mobilized the full apparatus of mass communication for persuasion. Its success demonstrated that public opinion could be manufactured at scale—a lesson applied by both democracies and dictatorships in the century since. The CPI's methods influenced Nazi propaganda minister Joseph Goebbels, who studied American WWI techniques, and continue to inform government communications, political campaigns, and corporate PR to this day.
