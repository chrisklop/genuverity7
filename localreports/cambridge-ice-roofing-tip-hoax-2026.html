The Viral Claim: What the Livestream Said

On March 23, 2026, six roofing workers were arrested by ICE agents at a property on Bayly Road in Cambridge, Maryland. Bryan Polanco — a Dominican national with U.S. permanent residency who was working alongside the detained men — livestreamed roughly 30 minutes of the encounter from atop the roof on Facebook. [2]

In the livestream and in subsequent statements to Spanish-language media, Polanco accused the homeowner of calling ICE on his colleagues to avoid paying them. He alleged the job was nearly complete and that the workers were owed approximately $10,000 for three days of labor. His framing — workers arrested immediately after finishing a job, allegedly by the very person who hired them — was emotionally compelling and structurally simple. It fit a readily available narrative about immigration enforcement being weaponized for wage theft.

The video spread explosively. Posts spread across TikTok, X, and Facebook, reaching millions of views and naming Karen Trevino as the woman responsible. Trevino's home address and contact details were shared widely, and she began receiving threatening messages within hours. [3] By March 26, some outlets were speculating whether Trevino could face felony charges for aiding an ICE operation. [18]

ICE's Official Denial: Pre-Planned Targeted Operation

On approximately March 26, 2026, ICE issued a formal statement dismantling the central allegation: "This was a targeted enforcement operation, not a tip from a caller." [4]

ICE confirmed that several of the six arrested workers had pre-existing final orders of removal — outstanding federal deportation orders issued by an immigration judge, the existence of which constitutes prior intelligence that ICE uses to plan targeted operations. One worker had a prior conviction for illegal reentry under 8 U.S.C. § 1326, a felony offense. [5] These facts indicate the operation was intelligence-driven, planned before the roofing job began, and was not reactive to any phone call made on March 23.

Cambridge city officials confirmed they had no advance knowledge of the ICE operation — consistent with Maryland's February 2026 law banning local law enforcement from entering 287(g) partnership agreements with ICE. Under that legal framework, there was no formal channel through which a homeowner's tip could have been acted upon by local police and relayed to ICE for same-day response. [16]

ICE's Eastern Shore enforcement pattern further contextualizes the Cambridge arrests. A prior ICE press release documented a 22-arrest targeted operation across the Eastern Shore — demonstrating that ICE regularly conducts planned multi-arrest operations in this region, independent of individual complaints. [17]

The Physical Evidence: Only 15% of the Roof Was Removed

Key Physical Contradiction

Cambridge police investigators confirmed that approximately 15% of the old roof had been removed at the time of the ICE operation — not 90% or "essentially complete" as stated in viral posts. The property owner had a valid permit, issued well before the arrest date, further establishing this was an ongoing multi-day job. [12] [13]

The "completed job" narrative was the emotional core of the viral claim — without it, the wage-theft motive collapses entirely. If only 15% of the roof was removed, there was no completed work to withhold payment for. Shore News Network independently confirmed Cambridge PD's finding, reporting that the department stated the viral story about the woman calling ICE after the job was done is false. [13]

The existence of a valid roofing permit also establishes that the property owner (Trevino's father, not Karen Trevino herself) had engaged the workers through an official channel — undermining any theory of a scheme to defraud from the outset.

Karen Trevino: The Misidentified Woman

Karen Trevino was identified in social media posts as the woman visible in Polanco's livestream. She has publicly and categorically denied making any call to ICE. Her denial includes three structurally significant facts: [3]

  • She does not own the property. The property is owned by her father. She is not the party who contracted the roofing job.
  • She was not present during the arrests. Her appearance in Polanco's livestream was incidental — she arrived during or after the ICE operation, not before it.
  • She made no call to ICE. "They are accusing me of calling ICE when I didn't," she told The Baltimore Sun. "I am not a racist. I do not hate immigrants. I am a Latina myself."

Despite these facts, Trevino received over 1,342 threatening messages within days, including explicit threats to her physical safety ("don't sleep at home peacefully"). [3] Her personal information — home address, phone number, workplace — was circulated alongside calls for accountability that, in practice, amounted to a targeted harassment campaign against a private individual who had no role in the ICE operation.

Snopes confirmed, as of April 2, 2026, that Maryland court records show no arrests or charges filed against Karen Trevino or her father in connection with this incident. [7]

The Secondary Hoax: Fake Arrest Video

A second wave of misinformation followed the initial viral claim. A video circulated on Threads that falsely depicted Karen Trevino being arrested — apparently an edited splice using footage from the original Polanco livestream combined with unrelated arrest footage. The video was identified as fabricated and debunked on Threads. [6] [15]

The fabricated arrest video represents a secondary harm cascade: the original claim sparked harassment; the fake arrest video was designed to intensify it by creating the false impression that authorities had concluded Trevino was guilty. Yahoo News fact-checked the fake arrest video directly, confirming it does not show Trevino being arrested. [15]

This two-stage pattern — viral misidentification followed by fabricated evidence of guilt — is consistent with documented harassment campaigns against individuals caught in immigration enforcement viral videos, including the January 2026 Renee Nicole Good / Steven Grove misidentification case documented by Slate, in which an innocent bystander was misidentified in an immigration enforcement video and faced similar harassment before the error was corrected.

Contemporary Context: Maryland's ICE Ban & Enforcement Patterns

The Cambridge incident took place against a significant policy backdrop. In February 2026, Maryland Governor Wes Moore signed legislation formally banning 287(g) agreements — formal partnerships between local law enforcement agencies and ICE. Under this law, local police cannot enter into formal cooperation arrangements with federal immigration enforcement. This structural fact makes it implausible that a homeowner could have called a local tip line and triggered a same-day coordinated ICE arrest of six workers. [16]

ICE's own enforcement data establishes the regional context. A prior press release from ICE documented a targeted operation that arrested 22 individuals across the Maryland Eastern Shore — demonstrating that the agency regularly conducts pre-planned multi-arrest sweeps in Dorchester County and surrounding areas, entirely independent of individual complaints. [17] ICE conducted approximately 1,736 arrests statewide in Maryland in 2025, reflecting a broad enforcement posture that does not depend on reactive tips.

The case also intersects with a well-documented pattern of viral misidentification in immigration enforcement videos. Research by Slate (January 2026) on the Renee Nicole Good / Steven Grove case documented how quickly innocent bystanders become targets when social media posts falsely identify individuals in immigration enforcement footage — often before any verification occurs and long after corrections are issued.

Conclusion: The Real Harm of Immigration Enforcement Misinformation

The Cambridge ICE roofing tip story represents a case where a genuine, documented event — six workers arrested by ICE — became the vehicle for a false narrative that caused serious real-world harm to an uninvolved private citizen. The harm did not require fabrication from scratch: it required only misidentification, assumption of motive, and a social media ecosystem optimized for rapid sharing ahead of verification.

Five distinct information ecosystem failures compound in this case:

  1. Eyewitness credibility mismatch: A physically present witness (Polanco) made a confident factual claim that was wrong — and audiences correctly weight physical presence as an evidence marker.
  2. Narrative fit: The claim fit an existing, emotionally resonant story framework (wage theft + immigration enforcement) that required no new information to feel plausible.
  3. Identification propagation: Once Karen Trevino was named, that identification was reshared faster than any correction could follow.
  4. Secondary fabrication: The fake arrest video was designed to foreclose doubt and intensify action before the primary claim was fully debunked.
  5. Correction asymmetry: ICE's official denial received a fraction of the attention the original viral claim received — this is structurally inherent to social media dynamics, not a correctable editorial failure.
Before Sharing Viral Enforcement Videos

1. Check whether an official agency statement has been issued.
2. A person visible in a video is not necessarily the person who made a decision — presence does not imply agency.
3. Search for the name being shared + "debunked" or "fact check" before amplifying.
4. Secondary videos purporting to show arrests or charges should be treated as unverified until court records confirm.
5. Harassment of an individual identified in a viral claim can cause serious harm even if the identification is later corrected.
