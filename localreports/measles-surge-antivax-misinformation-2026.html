Section 1: The Outbreak by the Numbers

The CDC confirmed 1,136 measles cases in 2026 through February 26 — achieved in just 58 days. By comparison, it took until May 2025 for the prior year to reach the same milestone, and the full-year 2024 total was only 285. The 2026 pace, if it continued, would project to more than 7,000 cases by year's end. [1] [2]

Of the 1,136 confirmed 2026 cases, 90% (1,023) are outbreak-associated, spread across 28 jurisdictions: Arizona, California, Colorado, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Illinois, Kentucky, Maine, Minnesota, Nebraska, New Mexico, New York City, New York State, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, South Dakota, Texas, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin. The geographic breadth underscores that South Carolina is not an isolated pocket — measles is re-establishing community transmission chains across the entire country. [1]

Approximately 94–96% of confirmed 2026 cases occurred in people who were unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated. The proximate cause of the outbreak is therefore unambiguous. [4] Epidemiologist Amy Winter of the University of Georgia stated flatly: "This is 100 percent a reflection of the recent declines in vaccination rates." [2]

The full-year 2025 count was 2,281 cases across 45 jurisdictions — the most since 1992 — with 218 hospitalizations, 3 deaths, and an estimated $244.2 million in economic costs. The 2025 West Texas outbreak, which originated in Gaines County in a community of unvaccinated homeschooled children, killed two children (ages 6 and 8) and one adult — the first measles deaths in the United States since 2015. All were unvaccinated. [12]

Section 2: South Carolina — The Epicenter

The South Carolina Department of Public Health confirmed an outbreak in Spartanburg County beginning October 2, 2025. By late February 2026, the total reached approximately 979 cases — the largest single US measles outbreak since the disease was declared eliminated in 2000. Of those, 95% of patients were unvaccinated, 90% were children, and 26% were preschoolers. [15]

The community conditions that allowed the outbreak to take hold are specific and documented. NPR's February 20, 2026 analysis of Spartanburg identified a structurally vulnerable population: the area is described as "solidly conservative" with a sizeable Slavic immigrant community whose vaccine skepticism was partly rooted in distrust of government authority following COVID-era lockdowns. One charter school in Spartanburg recorded an MMR vaccination rate of just 21% — catastrophically below the 95% herd immunity threshold. Approximately 10% of county students hold religious liberty vaccine exemptions. [16]

State Senator Josh Kimbrell acknowledged that some parents "lacked genuine religious motivations" for their exemptions — they used the religious exemption pathway as a general opt-out, a pattern that recurs in high-exemption communities nationally. The consequence was swift: dozens of students at a middle school were quarantined at once. A teacher at one affected school "spent time in intensive care over the holidays and nearly passed away." [16]

By late February, new case rates had begun declining, and South Carolina DPH officials were cautiously optimistic the outbreak could end sooner than projected. The state activated Mobile Health Units to offer free MMR vaccines to unvaccinated Spartanburg residents. However, the virus had already seeded transmission chains in multiple neighboring states. [3]

Section 4: Social Media Amplification — How Platforms Became a Vector

False claims about the MMR vaccine circulate across Facebook groups, TikTok, Telegram channels, and influencer-driven "tradwife" communities. Research published in PMC found that misinformation was present in 58.8% of measles-rubella vaccine videos on TikTok; 85.7% of content produced by lay individuals contained misinformation. Critically, misinformation content was more frequently liked, shared, and saved than accurate content — meaning the platform's engagement mechanics actively amplify false information. [22]

A study published in Science quantified the structural problem on Facebook: unflagged vaccine-skeptical content had a 46-fold greater impact than misinformation content flagged by fact-checkers. The EU has issued findings that Meta breached EU content moderation rules — but US platforms face no equivalent regulatory pressure. On X (formerly Twitter), under Elon Musk's ownership, Community Notes replaced algorithmic misinformation labels; anti-vaccine content that previously received warning labels now circulates without restriction in many cases. [23]

A 2025 KFF poll of 1,380 US adults found that 25% believed vitamin A "probably or definitely" prevents measles infection; 20% believed the MMR vaccine is more dangerous than measles itself; and 20% believed the MMR vaccine probably or definitely causes autism. The partisan dimension was sharp: 35% of Republicans endorsed the autism link vs. 10% of Democrats. Among parents who accepted at least one false claim, 1 in 4 had skipped or delayed childhood vaccines — more than twice the rate among parents who rejected all claims. [5] [6]

Nature reported in February 2026 that pro-science influencers were going viral on TikTok specifically to counter measles misinformation — a reactive response from the scientific community to an information environment already saturated with false claims. This asymmetry — where misinformation spreads first and corrections chase it — is the structural failure that policy interventions have so far failed to address. [24]

Section 5: Evidence Deep-Dive — Federal Policy Failure

The CDC's "Cost of Doing Business" Statement

On January 20, 2026, Ralph Abraham — Principal Deputy Director of the CDC and former Louisiana Surgeon General — was asked at a media briefing whether he would consider losing the US measles elimination status to be significant. His response: "Not really...You know, it's just the cost of doing business, with our borders being somewhat porous [and] global and international travel."

He characterized potential elimination status loss as an inevitable result of international travel and "personal freedom" of parents who choose not to vaccinate — framing the collapse of a 25-year public health achievement as routine operational friction rather than a policy failure. The statement was condemned by public health professionals across the country. [25]

The federal institutionalization of vaccine misinformation has proceeded through several documented mechanisms:

ACIP Dismantlement: In June 2025, Kennedy fired all 17 voting members of the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices — the independent expert body that had advised on the childhood vaccine schedule for decades — and replaced them with vaccine skeptics. The new ACIP includes Robert W. Malone (physician-scientist turned prominent vaccine skeptic), Martin Kulldorff (Great Barrington Declaration co-author), and Angelina Farella, a pediatrician who appeared on Newsmax in 2021 stating: "You know how you fight a pandemic...You treat it," while recommending steroids and vitamins as COVID alternatives instead of vaccination. The ACIP February 2026 meeting was cancelled entirely. [19]

Schedule Overhaul: On January 5, 2026, the CDC issued a "Decision Memo" restructuring the childhood vaccine schedule to resemble Denmark's, reducing universal vaccine recommendations from 18 diseases to 11 — dropping rotavirus, meningococcal disease, hepatitis A, hepatitis B, influenza, COVID-19, and RSV from universal recommendation. Measles vaccination technically remained on the schedule, but experts warned the broader overhaul would increase confusion and reduce overall uptake. Dr. Jake Scott of Stanford called it "the most significant weakening of childhood vaccine recommendations in modern American history." [7] [8]

CDC Website Alteration: In November 2025, under Kennedy's direction, the CDC's vaccine safety webpage was changed to state: "'Vaccines do not cause autism' is not an evidence-based claim" and suggested that studies supporting an autism-vaccine link "have been ignored by health authorities." This directly contradicts 40+ independent studies involving 5.6 million people across seven countries, and was made by political appointees without input from CDC scientists. The American Medical Association condemned the changes. [10]

Vitamin A Promotion: During the West Texas outbreak in March 2025, Kennedy publicly promoted vitamin A, cod liver oil, clarithromycin, and budesonide as measles treatments — none of which are evidence-based treatments for measles. A 2026 systematic review published in PMC found that vitamin A supplementation provides no benefit on fever duration, hospitalization, or complication rates in measles patients in high-income country settings. The WHO's own recommendation for vitamin A is explicitly limited to children already infected with measles who are vitamin A deficient — primarily applicable in low-income countries with nutritional deficiency. The consequence of Kennedy's promotion: children in Lubbock, Texas were hospitalized with vitamin A toxicity from overdoses their parents administered as a vaccine substitute. [13] [21]

Jay Bhattacharya as Acting CDC Director: On February 18, 2026 — with South Carolina approaching 1,000 cases and the national total exceeding 1,100 — the White House appointed Jay Bhattacharya (NIH Director and Great Barrington Declaration co-author) as Acting CDC Director, giving him simultaneous control of both agencies. At a Senate hearing, Bhattacharya stated: "I think the best way to address the measles epidemic in this country is by vaccinating your children for measles." However, he had co-authored a Nature Medicine commentary arguing that "vaccine mandates contributed to decreased public confidence in routine voluntary immunizations." He is also a named defendant in the 15-state AG lawsuit. [17]

Section 6: The 15-State Lawsuit and Congressional Confrontations

On February 24, 2026, the attorneys general of Arizona, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Maine, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, New Mexico, Oregon, Rhode Island, Wisconsin, and the Governor of Pennsylvania filed a federal lawsuit against RFK Jr., the CDC, HHS, and Jay Bhattacharya. The lawsuit targets three specific acts: [14] [18]

1. The January 5, 2026 Decision Memo that stripped seven vaccines of universally recommended status without scientific review
2. The unlawful dismissal and reconstitution of ACIP with individuals alleged to "lack the scientific qualifications required by ACIP's own charter and the Federal Advisory Committee Act (FACA)"
3. Procedural violations: the Decision Memo "was not based on any new scientific evidence, any recommendation by a lawfully constituted ACIP, or any systematic review of the available data"

Arizona AG Kris Mayes delivered the sharpest summary: "Copying Denmark's vaccine schedule without copying Denmark's health care system doesn't give families more options — it just leaves kids unprotected." [18]

Congressional confrontations preceded the lawsuit. When Kennedy testified before the Senate HELP Committee in May 2025, Senator Chris Murphy (D-CT) stated Kennedy had "consistently been undermining the measles vaccine" and cited Kennedy's claim on the Dr. Phil show that the vaccine "wanes very quickly." After Kennedy declined to give an unambiguous recommendation for the vaccine, Murphy concluded: "The Secretary of Health and Human Services is no longer recommending the measles vaccine." Senator Bernie Sanders added: "You have undermined the vital role vaccines play in preventing disease during the single, largest measles outbreak in 25 years." [20]

Kennedy's own responses were deliberately ambiguous. When asked if he would recommend the MMR vaccine, he stated: "My opinions about vaccines are irrelevant." At a separate appearance, he said he would "probably" vaccinate his own children against measles, but immediately qualified: "My opinions about vaccines are irrelevant." Dr. Yvonne Maldonado of Stanford, reviewing the Decision Memo, stated: "There are no data, no papers, no discussions at all that are cited in this quote-unquote exhaustive search." [8]

This is the second major lawsuit against Kennedy within a year. In July 2025, six medical societies challenged HHS over discontinued COVID-19 vaccine recommendations for pregnant women.

Section 7: Contemporary Context — International Alarm and the Invisible Victims

Americas Region Measles Collapse

On February 4, 2026, PAHO issued a formal epidemiological alert for the entire Americas region. In the first three weeks of 2026 alone, the Americas recorded 1,031 measles cases across 7 countries — a 43-fold increase versus the same period in 2025. The 2025 regional total was 14,891 confirmed cases and 29 deaths — a 32-fold increase from 2024's 466 cases. [26]

Canada's Warning: Canada lost its measles elimination status on November 10, 2025, designated by PAHO after more than 12 months of sustained transmission producing 5,436 cases and 2 deaths. The Americas region collectively also lost elimination status as a result — the first regional status loss since measles elimination was achieved in 2016. Mexico recorded 6,428 cases in 2025 with 24 deaths. [27]

The US Is Next: The PAHO Regional Verification Commission is scheduled to meet April 13, 2026 to evaluate whether the United States and Mexico have also lost elimination status. A CDC scientist told STAT News that the agency was sequencing viruses across jurisdictions to determine whether the US outbreak represents continuous domestic transmission (which would trigger elimination-status loss) or a series of new foreign introductions. But with 1,136 cases in the first 58 days of 2026 and Spartanburg's 979-case outbreak showing community transmission chains concentrated in a single county, the scientific consensus among outside experts is that the US will lose elimination status — a 25-year public health achievement. [25]

MMR Coverage Below Threshold: US kindergarten MMR coverage fell from 95.2% in 2019–2020 to 92.5% in 2024–2025 — leaving approximately 286,000 kindergartners unprotected and crossing below the 95% population immunity threshold required to prevent measles transmission. Idaho's MMR coverage stands at 78.5%; only 10 states plus DC maintained the 95% threshold in 2024–2025. Non-medical exemptions increased from 2.2% to 3.4% of kindergartners nationally between 2019–2020 and 2024–2025. Idaho enacted a 2025 law allowing vaccination exemptions "for any reason." [11]

The Invisible Victims: The populations who bear the largest burden of the anti-vaccine crisis are precisely those who had no role in creating it:

Infants under 12 months cannot yet receive the MMR vaccine — their protection depends entirely on herd immunity. PAHO data shows infants under 1 year have the highest incidence rates of any age group in the 2025–2026 Americas outbreak. Immunocompromised individuals — cancer patients in chemotherapy, transplant recipients, people with certain autoimmune conditions — cannot safely receive the live MMR vaccine. Their only protection is community immunity. As coverage falls below 21% in some school settings, these patients face measles exposure risk they cannot personally mitigate. Children of vaccine-accepting parents in high-exemption communities face elevated risk even when fully vaccinated because the MMR is 97% (not 100%) effective — a vaccinated child in a 21% vaccination-rate school faces materially greater risk than the same child in a 97%+ vaccinated school. [26]

The Global Virus Network — representing virologists from 90+ Centers of Excellence across 40+ countries — issued a formal statement of "deep concern" about the US resurgence and its global implications. PAHO specifically called for heightened surveillance around the 2026 FIFA World Cup, a mass-gathering event at which measles could spread internationally. [28]

Section 9: What Needs to Happen

The measles crisis of 2025–2026 is not a mystery. It is a documented, predicted consequence of declining vaccination rates in the face of organized misinformation — now amplified by the capture of federal health infrastructure. The path back is also not a mystery; it is the same path that achieved elimination in 2000: high vaccination coverage above the 95% herd immunity threshold, strong ACIP-guided schedule recommendations, and accurate public health communication.

The immediate structural requirements are:

Restore ACIP independence. The 15-state lawsuit challenges the legality of the Kennedy-reconstituted ACIP. Courts will rule on whether the dismissal of all 17 expert members violated the Federal Advisory Committee Act. Regardless of legal outcome, restoration of a scientifically qualified ACIP is prerequisite to any credible vaccine policy. The February 2026 ACIP meeting was cancelled entirely; a rescheduled March meeting provides the first opportunity to reverse course.

Restore the CDC's scientific integrity. The alteration of the CDC vaccine safety webpage to imply the autism-vaccine link is unresolved must be reversed. The American Medical Association and American Academy of Pediatrics — which broke publicly with the CDC in early 2026 to maintain the broader pre-Kennedy schedule — provide the clinical guidance that parents now lack from federal sources. [10]

Address platform moderation failure. A 46-fold amplification advantage for unflagged versus flagged misinformation on Facebook represents a structural failure that voluntary fact-checking programs have not solved. The EU regulatory approach — requiring platforms to demonstrate misinformation removal compliance — has no US equivalent. [23]

Tighten exemption policies. Idaho's "for any reason" vaccine exemption law, enacted 2025, and Florida's expanded conscience exemptions are live experiments in what happens when policy actively enables vaccine avoidance. The South Carolina outbreak — concentrated in a community where 10% of students hold religious exemptions — provides the empirical result. [11]

The 2026 FIFA World Cup represents the next major international transmission risk. PAHO has specifically called for heightened surveillance. The PAHO Regional Verification Commission's April 13, 2026 ruling on US elimination status will be the formal milestone — but elimination status is a lagging indicator. The actual loss has been underway for months. The question is no longer whether the US will lose a 25-year public health achievement. The question is how quickly it can be recovered, and at what human cost. [26] [28]

The Bottom Line

Measles was eliminated from the United States in 2000 because vaccines work. The MMR vaccine remains 97% effective after two doses. Every death, every hospitalization, every case of vitamin A toxicity from the 2025–2026 surge was preventable. The misinformation claims driving vaccine refusal have been refuted by more than 40 independent scientific studies involving over 5.6 million people. The unique feature of the 2025–2026 crisis is that the United States federal government — the institution with the greatest authority and responsibility to counter these myths — is now the institution most actively promoting them.
