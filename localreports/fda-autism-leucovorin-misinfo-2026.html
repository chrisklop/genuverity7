TL;DR

MISLEADING — Official government misinformation from the FDA Commissioner

On September 22, 2025, FDA Commissioner Dr. Marty Makary stood at a White House briefing and claimed "hundreds of thousands" of autistic children would benefit from leucovorin — specifically citing 20–50% of kids with severe autism. On March 10, 2026, the FDA finalized a label change approving leucovorin only for a confirmed FOLR1 gene mutation (CFD-FOLR1), affecting approximately 70 children in the United States. The gap is a factor of roughly 3,500. In between: a 71% prescription surge, drug shortages affecting cancer patients, a retracted clinical trial, and the American Academy of Pediatrics formally declining to recommend the drug for autism.

Executive Summary

On September 22, 2025, FDA Commissioner Dr. Marty Makary stood alongside President Trump and HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. at a White House briefing and announced that the FDA would relabel the drug leucovorin "so that it can be available to children with autism," claiming "hundreds of thousands of kids, in my opinion, will benefit." He added that "20 to 50% of kids with autism" might see improvement. The announcement caused leucovorin prescriptions to surge 71% within two and a half months, according to a study published in The Lancet, creating drug shortages and sending families scrambling for a treatment that had not been approved — and was based on evidence so weak that the largest supporting clinical trial would later be retracted. [1][3]

On March 10, 2026 — the day the FDA actually finalized the label change — the reality fell dramatically short of Makary's promises. The FDA approved leucovorin only for cerebral folate transport deficiency caused by a confirmed variant in the FOLR1 gene (CFD-FOLR1), a condition estimated to affect fewer than 1 in 1 million people — approximately 70 children in the entire United States. Senior FDA officials explicitly stated that "right now, we don't have sufficient data to say that we can establish efficacy for autism more broadly," and Dr. George Tidmarsh, who led the FDA's drugs division until his resignation in November 2025, had stated plainly in October 2025: "We're not proposing to approve leucovorin for [people with] the diagnosis of autism." [2][4][5]

Section 1: "Hundreds of Thousands" — The Original Claim

The misinformation originated at the highest level of the FDA itself. On September 22, 2025, Commissioner Makary appeared at a White House briefing alongside President Trump, HHS Secretary RFK Jr., NIH Director Jay Bhattacharya, CMS Administrator Mehmet Oz, and HHS Deputy Secretary Dorothy Fink. Makary announced a planned Federal Register notice to update leucovorin's label and framed it in sweeping terms as an autism breakthrough for "hundreds of thousands of kids." [12]

Trump personally said the label change "gives hope to the many parents with autistic children." The HHS press release issued the same day explicitly stated that "state Medicaid programs will be able to cover leucovorin for the indication of ASD" after the label update — language that implied autism spectrum disorder coverage that was never delivered. [12][27]

The gap between Makary's September claims and the March outcome can be stated simply: Makary said "hundreds of thousands" — a figure that, taken at its conservative lower bound of 250,000, is approximately 3,500 times larger than the roughly 70 children for whom the drug was actually approved. Makary's other claim — that "20 to 50% of kids with severe autism" might see "clinical improvement in studies" — was directly contradicted by the FDA's own findings six months later. [1][11]

Section 2: The Science — Why Leucovorin Works for CFD-FOLR1 but Not General Autism

To understand the magnitude of Makary's overstatement, it is necessary to understand what CFD-FOLR1 actually is — and how different it is from autism. Cerebral folate transport deficiency (CFD-FOLR1) is an autosomal recessive neurological disorder caused by loss-of-function mutations in the FOLR1 gene. The FOLR1 gene produces folate receptor alpha, a protein embedded in cell membranes that transports folate across the choroid plexus and into cerebrospinal fluid. When both copies of FOLR1 are mutated, folate cannot reach the brain even if systemic folate levels are normal. [20][21]

The condition is vanishingly rare. Peer-reviewed prevalence data from NCBI GeneReviews confirms fewer than 1 in 1 million individuals worldwide. As of the most recent published literature, fewer than 50 confirmed cases have been identified globally — with some estimates suggesting more than 35 individuals from 32 families. Applied to the U.S. child population of approximately 73 million, this implies roughly 70 eligible children — exactly the FDA's estimate. [20][13]

Treatment with high-dose oral leucovorin is highly effective for this specific indication: 87% of CFD-FOLR1 patients show clinical improvement, and among oral-leucovorin-only patients, 24 of 27 showed "a range of clinical improvements" per the FDA label. The mechanism is receptor-bypass: leucovorin (5-formyltetrahydrofolate) does not use the FOLR1 receptor — it enters cells via a different pathway. This only helps when the FOLR1 receptor is the problem. [4][15][16]

The critical disconnect: Most autistic children do not have FOLR1 mutations. They have normally functioning folate transport. Giving them leucovorin does not address any underlying mechanism. The "folate receptor autoantibody" theory Makary invoked — that immune antibodies block the FOLR1 receptor in autistic children without a genetic mutation — remains unverified. And crucially, autism-unaffected siblings of autistic children show the same antibody prevalence, which strongly suggests the antibodies are not causally linked to autism at all. [8][22]

Dr. David Mandell of the University of Pennsylvania put it plainly: "We have nothing resembling even moderate evidence that leucovorin is an effective treatment for autism symptoms." [8]

Section 3: The Retracted Trial — When the Evidence Base Collapsed

The scientific basis for Makary's September claims was already collapsing before the March 2026 approval. In January 2026 — while the FDA was still conducting its review — the largest randomized controlled trial on leucovorin and autism was retracted. The trial, published in September 2024 in the European Journal of Pediatrics, involved 77 children and was led by Prateek Kumar Panda at the All India Institute of Medical Sciences. [6][17]

The retraction notice, posted the last week of January 2026, confirmed that the journal's review "confirmed several of the concerns raised with the data and statistical analysis and was unable to replicate the results reported in the article from the dataset provided." Data table numbers "did not add up correctly," statistical analysis errors were identified, and the results could not be reproduced from the dataset provided. Only two of the six authors agreed to retraction; four did not respond. The authors stated they intend to submit a revised manuscript. [17]

Expert reactions were unsparing. Dr. Thomas Challman stated: "The retraction of this paper removes a significant portion of the already weak evidence supporting the value of folinic acid as a treatment for autism." Dr. Dorothy Bishop called the overall research quality "uniformly poor" and characterized the statistics as "all over the place." Dr. Shafali Jeste noted the drug's prominence was "because of the publicity around it, not because we as scientists believe that this is the drug that we should be testing." [6]

The remaining evidence base consists of four small studies, most originating from the same research group. The largest non-retracted trial enrolled 48 children. No large multicenter Phase 3 randomized controlled trial has ever been completed. The American Academy of Pediatrics' October 2025 interim guidance noted explicitly that all studies involve fewer than 100 patients, most originate from "the same group of researchers," and no large Phase 3 trial exists. [22][23]

Section 4: Real-World Harm — 71% Prescription Surge and Cancer Patient Shortages

The authority of the FDA Commissioner's office did not merely spread misinformation — it caused quantifiable harm. A study published in The Lancet by Dr. Jeremy Faust of Brigham and Women's Hospital found that outpatient leucovorin prescriptions for children ages 5–17 were 71% higher than normal in the three months immediately following the White House briefing. New prescriptions doubled in the most acute initial window. [3]

This surge had a direct and concrete downstream effect: cancer patients lost access to a drug they genuinely need. Leucovorin has a decades-long primary use in oncology — it is given alongside the chemotherapy drug 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) to treat colorectal cancer and other malignancies as part of the FOLFOX and FOLFIRI regimens. The demand surge created direct competition with cancer patients for a finite drug supply. [24][25]

By early December 2025, the FDA took the unusual step of issuing a "Dear Provider" letter authorizing emergency imports of leucovorin tablets from Canada and Spain to help ease shortfalls — a rare intervention typically reserved for declared drug shortages. Despite this, CVS Health spokesperson Roslyn Guarino confirmed: "We are seeing supply challenges among certain versions of generic leucovorin." The American Society of Health-Systems Pharmacists (ASHP) shortage list showed most leucovorin manufacturers had placed tablets on allocation or backorder. [24]

The families of autistic children were also harmed by false hope. CNN reported in early March 2026 that parents were scrambling to find the drug amid growing shortages, with some turning to unregulated over-the-counter folate supplements when they couldn't obtain prescriptions. Alycia Halladay of the Autism Science Foundation described the gap between the September rhetoric and the March approval as "1,000% different" and noted: "The bell has been rung, and we've already seen through the data that prescriptions for leucovorin have skyrocketed." [7][24]

The AAP's October 2025 interim guidance identified additional safety risks in the off-label prescribing surge that Makary's announcement provoked. High-dose folinic acid can mask the hematologic signs of vitamin B12 deficiency while allowing neurological damage to continue. Additionally, leucovorin is contraindicated in MTHFS (5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate synthetase) deficiency — a separate metabolic disorder where folinic acid cannot be processed, causing toxic buildup. Children with undiagnosed MTHFS deficiency receiving leucovorin for presumed autism could be actively harmed. [22]

Section 5: Evidence Deep-Dive — Dr. Frye's Conflicts, AAP Findings, and the Trial Landscape

The leucovorin-autism theory traces almost entirely to one researcher: Dr. Richard Frye, a child neurologist now at the Rossignol Medical Center in Phoenix, Arizona. Frye has spent approximately 20 years building the evidence base Makary cited. His 2012 Molecular Psychiatry study reported folate receptor antibodies in 70 of 93 autistic patients tested, providing the statistical basis for the "20–50%" claim that Makary broadcast from the White House. [19]

Frye's conflicts of interest are substantial. He is a consultant to Neuroneeds, which markets the "Spectrum Needs" supplement; he authored a self-published book called The Folate Fix; and his 2006 co-authored case report failed to disclose that the subject was the daughter of lead author Jon Poling, who had filed a vaccine injury compensation claim. His first NIH-supported leucovorin trial was suspended by the FDA in 2015 for compliance violations posing "unreasonable and significant risk." His second NIH-funded multicenter trial remains unpublished after his termination from Phoenix Children's Hospital in 2022. [19]

Frye's own response to the White House announcement was revealing: "For the administration to come through and say there is a treatment for autism is a major turning point. Could they have given some more nuance to it? Sure." On the decades of institutional resistance his research faced: "It is rich that the NIH and Big Pharma want to take over leucovorin when they and the universities have fought my research for 20 years." Frye has also stated: "Several times a year when I used to do the hospital thing, I'd have a child come in that was injured by a vaccine" — placing him squarely in the MAHA ideological ecosystem. [19]

Commissioner Makary himself carries significant financial conflicts. His confirmed interests at the time of his confirmation hearing included: board director at Harrow, an ophthalmic pharmaceuticals company ($40,000 compensation, 2023); chief medical officer at Sesame, a telehealth company offering compounded GLP-1 medications; public advisor to Paragon Health Institute; advisor to Sidecar Health; and chief medical advisor at Nava. The Sesame relationship was flagged by senators at his 56-44 confirmation vote. [18]

The timeline of institutional responses tells the story of a professional scientific community's rejection of the administration's framing. The AAP published interim guidance in October 2025 — less than a month after the White House briefing — explicitly declining to recommend leucovorin for routine autism use and specifically calling out: insufficient patient numbers, concentration of research in one group, absence of independent replication, masking risks for B12 deficiency, and the MTHFS contraindication. JAMA published similar guidance. [22][23]

Section 6: The MAHA Pipeline — A 5-Step Political Playbook

The leucovorin episode is not an isolated scientific error. It is structurally embedded in the broader "Make America Healthy Again" (MAHA) agenda being executed jointly by RFK Jr. and Makary at HHS/FDA. The political logic follows a consistent five-step pattern: [9][10]

The MAHA 5-Step Playbook

Step 1 — Identify a fringe medical theory with enough published literature to provide scientific cover. In this case: leucovorin and folate receptor autoantibodies, backed by Dr. Frye's research.

Step 2 — Elevate a favored researcher whose work supports the theory and who aligns with the MAHA anti-establishment narrative. Dr. Frye fit perfectly, having spent 20 years fighting "NIH and Big Pharma."

Step 3 — Make a dramatic public announcement from the White House to generate hope and political capital among target constituencies: autism parents, anti-establishment health consumers.

Step 4 — Let the FDA process run its course while the political messaging has already done its work. Prescriptions surge. Narrative shifts. The announcement is treated as fact.

Step 5 — Quietly narrow the actual approval to a defensible scientific subset. The press coverage of the retreat is far smaller than the original announcement. The damage is done.

The FDA's own September 22, 2025 press release — titled "FDA Takes Action to Make Treatment Available for Autism Symptoms" — used language that implied far broader coverage than what was ultimately delivered. The original document described "making treatment available for autism symptoms" and implied Medicaid coverage for the ASD indication. Neither was fulfilled. [27]

A career FDA official described the culture under the Kennedy-Makary FDA as: "go find data to support this, go find data to support that" — a direct reversal of the normal evidence-to-approval procedure where evidence is gathered first and regulatory decisions follow. The Genetic Literacy Project, in a February 2026 investigation, quoted former FDA official Henry Miller, who spent 15 years at the agency: "At RFK, Jr.'s FDA, scientific caution and rigor now matter less than political messaging." [9]

GSK's response to receiving the CFD-FOLR1 approval is revealing: the company explicitly stated it "does not plan to manufacture or market Wellcovorin again" despite receiving the approval. No major pharmaceutical manufacturer appears to have meaningfully benefited from the episode — suggesting the approval was driven by political dynamics rather than commercial medical need. [5]

Section 7: Contemporary Context — Who Benefits, Who Is Harmed, and the Institutional Pattern

The leucovorin case represents a distinct and more dangerous form of health misinformation: the government official as patient zero. Standard misinformation models trace false claims from fringe sources through social media amplification toward mainstream credibility. In this case, the Commissioner of the FDA — the world's most trusted drug regulator — was the originating source. This creates a structural problem for fact-checkers: the normal authority hierarchy that allows debunking breaks down when the false claim carries the institutional authority of the FDA itself. [1]

The leucovorin episode fits a broader pattern of institutional decay at the FDA under Kennedy's HHS. The FDA removed its webpage warning against false autism treatment claims — which had listed chlorine dioxide (bleach), raw camel milk, and clay baths as dangerous quackery — in January 2026. The FDA explained the removal as "a routine clean up of dated content at the end of 2025." No autism advocacy group supported the removal. The CDC simultaneously overhauled its vaccine-autism website to suggest previously ignored studies supported a link that decades of research have refuted. [14][26]

Who bears the harm from this episode: Parents of autistic children were misled into pursuing an unproven drug with known side effects. Cancer patients lost access to a standard-of-care drug for colorectal cancer treatment. The ~70 children who genuinely have CFD-FOLR1 — who would legitimately benefit from the drug at an 87% clinical response rate — risk having their legitimate treatment need obscured by the broader controversy. Dr. Richard Frye and the folate supplement ecosystem (Neuroneeds' "Spectrum Needs," his self-published book The Folate Fix) benefit from sustained public interest in folate treatments for autism, regardless of clinical evidence. [19][25]

Makary's broader FDA agenda under the MAHA umbrella is consistent with this pattern: he is simultaneously pushing to eliminate artificial food dyes, crack down on ultra-processed foods, and review 11,000 chemicals in U.S. food that are banned elsewhere. Some of these MAHA priorities carry genuine scientific merit — but the leucovorin episode illustrates how the same apparatus can generate misinformation and concrete patient harm when political messaging is allowed to drive regulatory announcements. [9][10]

Section 8: Conclusion — The Structural Problem of the Official Misinformer

The leucovorin episode is a case study in what happens when the institutional apparatus for building public trust in medicine is turned against that trust. The FDA Commissioner's office exists to translate the outputs of rigorous clinical science into public health guidance. When that same office generates claims that outpace the evidence by a factor of 3,500 — from a White House podium, alongside the President — the resulting harm is qualitatively different from the everyday flow of health misinformation online.

The counterfactual matters: had Makary announced in September 2025 that the FDA was considering a narrow label change for a genetic condition affecting fewer than 100 children in the United States, there would have been no prescription surge, no cancer patient drug shortage, no emergency imports from Canada and Spain, and no families spending money on an unproven treatment with documented side effect risks. The ~70 children with actual CFD-FOLR1 would still have received their genuinely beneficial approval — at an 87% clinical response rate — without the surrounding noise. [4][16]

Instead, Makary chose to announce the maximum possible version of an event that had not yet happened, using numbers that had no scientific basis, to an audience of families desperate for hope. The result was documented in a peer-reviewed journal, quantified at 71% above baseline, and felt at oncology clinics across the country. FactCheck.org — which formally contradicted a sitting FDA Commissioner based on the FDA's own subsequent actions — characterized the episode as "no broad autism approval for leucovorin, despite FDA Commissioner's prior suggestions." [1][3]

The verdict is MISLEADING — not in the ordinary sense of a claim that is merely exaggerated, but in the structural sense of a government official using the authority of his office to make health claims he knew, or should have known, were not supported by the evidence his own agency was reviewing. The 70 children who genuinely have CFD-FOLR1 deserve the treatment that was approved. The 2.5 million autistic children in the United States deserved an FDA Commissioner who told them the truth. [7][13]
