TL;DR

VERDICT: FALSE

The Craigslist ad was a prank created for entertainment by podcasters Joey LaFleur and Logan Quiroz, posted one day before protests began, with no connection to anti-ICE demonstrations [1][2][3]. Despite fact-checks from FactCheck.org, PolitiFact, PBS NewsHour, and Snopes, the false narrative reached 5 million views and contributed to President Trump deploying 2,800 military personnel at a cost of $134 million [5].

Executive Summary

In June 2025, as protests erupted in Los Angeles following aggressive ICE immigration raids, a Craigslist ad promising $6,500-$12,500 per week for "tough, brave men" went viral across social media as supposed evidence that demonstrators were paid actors. The ad, posted on June 5 by podcaster Joey LaFleur for his comedy show "Goofcon1," was completely unrelated to the immigration protests that began the following day. Despite this, screenshots of the ad garnered millions of views and were shared by political influencers and ordinary users alike as proof of a coordinated, funded protest operation.

President Trump amplified the narrative, calling protesters "Paid Insurrectionists!" and "paid troublemakers" without providing evidence, while deploying 2,000 National Guard troops and 700 Marines to Los Angeles at an estimated cost of $134 million. Multiple fact-checkers—including FactCheck.org, PolitiFact, and PBS NewsHour—debunked the claim, rating it False and confirming LaFleur's account that the ad was an unrelated prank. LaFleur himself stated: "I literally had no idea it was ever going to be connected to the riots. It was a really weird coincidence."

This incident exemplifies a well-documented disinformation pattern: the "paid protester" narrative has been repeatedly deployed to delegitimize grassroots activism, from the 2017 Charlottesville rally to the 2020 George Floyd protests. Experts note this tactic functions to dismiss dissent as inauthentic, despite consistent lack of evidence. The case also demonstrates how satire can be weaponized—similar to the 2020 Protestjobs.com incident where a satirical "protesters-for-hire" website was shared over 30,000 times on Facebook as "proof" of paid activism.

1. Origin: The Prank That Went Viral

The Ad Itself

On June 5, 2025, at 1:20 PM PT, Joey LaFleur posted a Craigslist ad in the Los Angeles general labor section. An almost identical ad appeared in Austin at 1:57 PM PT [2][10]. The post sought "extremely tough, brave men for new crew," offering $6,500-$12,500 weekly for unspecified duties. It specified military experience was desired "except for Marines, we don't work with Marines anymore, long story," and sought people with "experience facing trained opposition" [1].

The ad made no mention of protests, demonstrations, or political activity [1].

The Pranksters

Joey LaFleur and Logan Quiroz host "Goofcon1," a prank podcast that streams on YouTube and Patreon [3]. On June 6, they livestreamed Episode 3, calling applicants live and describing it as "the new goof that we have cooked up" [2]. LaFleur later posted on Instagram: "Accidentally goofed the entire nation on the latest @goofcon1" [19].

When contacted by the Associated Press on June 10, LaFleur confirmed the ad was purely for entertainment and expressed surprise at its viral misuse. "I literally had no idea it was ever going to be connected to the riots. It was a really weird coincidence," he told the AP [3]. Snopes independently verified LaFleur's account [4].

Critical Timing

The ad was posted one day before the Los Angeles ICE protests began on June 6, 2025 [2]. This coincidental timing became the foundation for the false narrative linking the two events.

2. Propagation: From Prank to "Proof"

Social Media Amplification

Screenshots of the ad began circulating on June 9, 2025, stripped of context and presented as evidence of paid protesters:

  • X (Twitter): A post asking "Who's paying people $12,500 A WEEK to riot in California?" reached 5 million views [2]
  • X: "CALIFORNIA RIOT IS A FUNDED OPERATION. Destabilizing the Trump administration and the United States in general is the goal" [1]
  • TikTok: A video calling participants "paid agitators" garnered approximately 14,100 views [2]
  • Threads/Facebook: Conservative commentator David J. Harris Jr. shared screenshots with captions like "LA 'protests' Craigslist ad searching for members. Follow the money" [1]

Posts collectively gained tens of thousands of likes, shares, and views across platforms [1].

Political Amplification

President Donald Trump called protesters "Paid Insurrectionists!" and "paid troublemakers" in Truth Social posts and spoke on June 10, 2025, saying "I believe many of them are paid" without providing evidence [1][5]. The White House provided no evidence when asked by fact-checkers [11]. Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard and Sen. Markwayne Mullin echoed similar claims [5].

3. Why It Spread: The "Paid Protester" Archetype

Historical Pattern

The "paid protester" narrative is a recurring delegitimization tactic with deep historical roots:

  • 2017 Charlottesville: Baseless claims that counterprotesters were recruited via Craigslist [9]
  • 2020 George Floyd Protests: False claims of George Soros funding rioters; an AP review found most Minnesota arrestees had local driver's licenses, contradicting "outside agitator" claims [16]
  • 2020 Protestjobs.com: A satirical "protesters-for-hire" website was shared 30,000+ times on Facebook by far-right groups as "proof" of paid activism, despite obvious satire [7][17]

Expert Analysis

Historians and scholars identify this as a delegitimization strategy:

Expert Perspectives

Dr. Danielle Brown (Michigan State): The narrative "falls in line with a pattern of delegitimizing protests that are trying to really shake up what people in power want to be the norm" [12].

Criminologist Ed Maguire: It's "almost like a convenient bogeyman to blame, but often the evidence of outside agitation turns out to be pretty slim or nonexistent" [13].

Historian Angus Johnston: American movements have always combined "orderly and unruly protests"; treating unruliness as proof of conspiracy contradicts "hundreds of years of American history" [14].

First Amendment Scholar Timothy Zick: The paid-protester framing is "baseless" and aims to "diminish and dismiss dissent" [15].

Cognitive Factors

The claim exploited several cognitive vulnerabilities:

  1. Confirmation bias: Aligned with pre-existing beliefs that opposition must be inauthentic
  2. Coincidental timing: Ad posted one day before protests began
  3. Plausibility: Companies like "Crowds on Demand" do exist and charge $100-$300 per participant for small events, lending surface credibility to broader claims [20]
  4. Stripped context: Screenshots removed satire/prank disclaimers

Satire Weaponization

Media experts note that satirical content, when stripped of context, becomes potent disinformation. The DFRLab analyzed the 2020 Protestjobs.com case and found: "While initially achieving its aim of fooling people as satire, it has since moved beyond that and is being used mostly by far-right groups as 'proof' that protests are staged" [7].

4. Evidence: What Actually Happened

The Real Protests

On June 6, 2025, ICE conducted coordinated raids across Los Angeles, targeting businesses in the Fashion District and locations like Home Depot parking lots. A total of 44 individuals were arrested for immigration violations. Protests erupted that evening, with thousands gathering outside the Edward R. Roybal Federal Building.

Grassroots Organization

Investigations found protests were organized through labor unions (including SEIU), faith groups, Signal chat networks, and neighborhood coalitions. Professional organizers participated, which experts note is normal for all protests and does not indicate payment [18].

Federal Response

  • June 7: Trump authorized 2,000 National Guard troops
  • June 9: Additional 2,000 Guard members authorized; 700 Marines deployed
  • Estimated cost: $134 million
  • December 31: National Guard deployment ended after court losses

Legal Challenges

  • July 2, 2025: ACLU filed lawsuit alleging unconstitutional arrests based on race
  • July 11: U.S. District Judge Maame Ewusi-Mensah Frimpong ruled administration likely violated rights, ordered stop to arrests without probable cause
  • September 3: U.S. District Judge Charles Breyer ruled military deployment illegal, violating Posse Comitatus Act
  • Prosecutors failed to secure indictments for most protesters; dropped cases after DHS agents made false statements

Key Takeaway

The Craigslist hoax demonstrates how easily satirical content can be weaponized when stripped of context. Despite universal fact-checker debunking, the false narrative reached millions, influenced presidential policy, and contributed to a $134 million military deployment. This pattern has repeated across major U.S. protests since 2017, with the "paid protester" narrative consistently used to delegitimize grassroots activism—despite zero credible evidence across any case.
