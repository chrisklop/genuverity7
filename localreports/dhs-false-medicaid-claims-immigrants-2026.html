Executive Summary

On February 17, 2026, the Department of Homeland Security posted a statement on its official X account claiming that up to 4 million "illegal" immigrant households would be removed from Medicaid under a new DHS public charge policy. The post reproduced a graphic from the Kaiser Family Foundation (KFF) while fundamentally misrepresenting what the data shows. Georgetown University's Center for Children and Families (CCF) published a point-by-point fact-check the same day, documenting multiple layers of misrepresentation: the KFF data concerns "noncitizen households" — a category that includes US citizens, lawful permanent residents, refugees, and other lawfully present immigrants — not "illegal immigrants" as DHS claimed. Undocumented immigrants have never been eligible for full federal Medicaid coverage under any existing law. [1] [3]

The misrepresentation is compounded by a second layer: the 4 million figure does not represent people who will be "removed" from Medicaid. It represents the upper-bound estimate of people who might voluntarily disenroll due to fear generated by DHS's own public charge rule. DHS's own regulatory filing acknowledged the enrollment impacts "relate predominantly to enrollment decisions made by those who are not subject to the public charge" test. [5] Georgetown CCF noted the DHS post was "nearly identical" to one posted a day earlier by the White Papers Policy Institute, an organization characterized as espousing white supremacist replacement theory beliefs. [20]

The real-world harm is substantial. According to KFF, 13.4 million Medicaid and CHIP enrollees live in noncitizen households, and 5.9 million of them are US citizen children. [4] If projected disenrollment rates of 10–30% materialize, between 600,000 and 1.8 million US citizen children would lose health coverage — not because they are ineligible, but because their parents are too frightened by enforcement rhetoric to maintain enrollment. The claim has also been used to justify the "One Big Beautiful Bill" (H.R. 1), which CBO projects will strip Medicaid and CHIP coverage from 10.9 million people by 2034. [24]

1. The DHS Claim and Its Origin

On February 17, 2026, the DHS official X account posted a claim that "up to 4 million illegal immigrant households" would be removed from Medicaid under a new public charge rule. The post reproduced a data graphic originating from KFF's analysis of the 2023 American Community Survey. [1]

Georgetown CCF's Joan Alker and colleagues documented that the DHS post was "almost identical" to a post published one day earlier — on February 16, 2026 — by the White Papers Policy Institute (WPPI), an X/Twitter-based account that Georgetown CCF characterized as espousing "beliefs associated with White Supremacy, such as replacement theory." The DHS version was described by researchers as "even more inaccurate" than the WPPI original: where WPPI had referenced "non-citizen households," DHS explicitly relabeled the population as "illegal" immigrants. [20]

The underlying data graphic shared by DHS came from KFF's analysis of the 2023 American Community Survey. KFF estimated that approximately 3.5 million Medicaid enrollees and 600,000 CHIP enrollees lived in a household with at least one noncitizen — a combined 4.1 million, which DHS rounded to "4 million" and described as "illegal immigrants." The KFF data has nothing to do with immigration enforcement and does not identify any enrollee as undocumented. [3] [5]

Origin Pipeline

Far-right replacement theory ideology (White Papers Policy Institute, Feb. 16, 2026) → Reproduced with additional inaccuracies by federal agency (DHS X account, Feb. 17, 2026) → National news coverage → House floor debate justifying H.R. 1 Medicaid cuts. The disinformation pipeline ran in under 48 hours from fringe ideology to legislative talking point.

2. Georgetown CCF's Same-Day Debunk: Three Layers of Misrepresentation

Georgetown CCF published its fact-check the same day as the DHS post — February 17, 2026, with an expanded version on February 26. The researchers identified three structurally distinct layers of misrepresentation embedded in a single claim. [1]

Layer 1 — Wrong Population Label. "Noncitizen household" under the ACS definition is any household in which at least one member is not a US citizen. This category explicitly includes: US citizen children with a noncitizen parent, lawful permanent residents, refugees, asylees, DACA recipients, TPS holders, people on work visas, and mixed-status families with no undocumented member. Georgetown CCF stated directly: "The graphic clearly states it pertains to 'non-citizen households,' which include mixed-status households with U.S. citizens and lawfully present individuals with no undocumented members." [1] Of the 13.4 million enrollees in noncitizen households, 5.9 million are US citizen children. [4]

Layer 2 — Wrong Mechanism. DHS framed the 4 million as people who "will be removed" from Medicaid. In reality, the 4 million is the upper-bound estimate of people who might voluntarily disenroll due to fear of the public charge rule. These are eligible people who choose to leave a program they qualify for because they are frightened — not people removed because they were ineligible. The distinction is the difference between enforcement and fear-induced behavior. [4] [5]

Layer 3 — Circular Logic / Manufactured Pretext. Most analytically damning: DHS's own regulatory language in the November 2025 Federal Register Notice acknowledged the disenrollment would "relate predominantly to enrollment decisions made by those who are not subject to the public charge" test. [5] The people who would leave Medicaid are US citizens and lawfully present immigrants disenrolling voluntarily because the enforcement environment is too threatening. DHS created the fear, then cited the resulting fear-driven disenrollment as evidence of a Medicaid "illegal immigrant" problem it invented. Georgetown CCF's Joan Alker called this out explicitly: the administration was citing the consequences of its own fear-creation as proof that a non-existent problem existed.

3. What the Data Actually Shows: ACS Methodology and Real Population Composition

The KFF analysis relied on the 2023 American Community Survey. The ACS does not include a direct measure of whether a noncitizen has legal status. KFF uses a methodology drawing on SHADAC analysis and recommendations from Van Hook et al., using the Survey of Income and Program Participation to develop a predictive model for immigration status. This means the 4.1 million figure is a household-level estimate — counting any household with at least one noncitizen member — not a count of undocumented individuals enrolled in Medicaid. [3]

Federal law under the 1996 Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (PRWORA) explicitly bars undocumented immigrants from federally funded Medicaid, CHIP, and Medicare. The only exception is Emergency Medicaid, which reimburses providers for emergency care and represents less than 0.4% of total Medicaid spending in FY2023. [3] [16] Pew Research found that as of January 2025, 9.2% of Medicaid enrollees are foreign-born noncitizens — predominantly lawfully present immigrants, not undocumented people — and 84.2% were born in the United States. The KFF-DHS methodology gap is particularly notable: DHS used a household inference methodology projecting only 460,000 total disenrollments. KFF's direct ACS analysis projected 1.3 to 4.0 million disenrollments depending on rate (10–30%). DHS's own figure was 3–10 times lower than KFF's independent estimate — while DHS paradoxically used KFF's own data graphic to support its post. [4]

4. The Emergency Medicaid Reality: 0.4% of Spending

The fiscal argument embedded in the DHS claim collapses on contact with the actual numbers. Emergency Medicaid — the only Medicaid access available to undocumented immigrants under existing law — cost $3.8 billion in FY2023. That represents 0.4% of total Medicaid spending for that year. CBO data from FY2017 through FY2023 shows Emergency Medicaid for noncitizens totaled $27 billion — less than 1% of total Medicaid spending across the entire period. [16]

Much of Emergency Medicaid spending goes toward labor and delivery costs — pregnant women in labor presenting at emergency rooms, an access point that cannot be legally denied under EMTALA regardless of immigration status. The DHS framing implies that eliminating "illegal immigrant" Medicaid access would generate massive savings. In reality, the maximum theoretical savings from eliminating all noncitizen Medicaid costs (including the approximately 6% of enrollees who are lawfully present noncitizens eligible for Medicaid) would be roughly 6% of total spending. [3]

The H.R. 1 Medicaid cuts total $911 billion over 10 years — approximately 97% of the program's 10-year baseline. The program being cut is 250 times larger than the "problem" being cited to justify the cuts. [24] The ACLU summarized the arithmetic directly: "H.R. 1 is the biggest cut to Medicaid in history — slashing approximately $1 trillion, which will result in at least 12 million people being removed from the program... The politicians who supported H.R. 1 took health care away from millions, while giving $170 billion in taxpayer dollars to an immigration police force larger than most of the world's militaries." [23]

5. The Chilling Effect Is Real: Historical Proof and 2025 Survey Data

DHS's claim rests on projections of voluntary disenrollment. The existence of such chilling effects is not speculative — it is historically documented and currently measurable. The 2019–2020 public charge rule provides direct precedent. Migration Policy Institute analysis found that during the first three years of the first Trump administration, participation in TANF, SNAP, and Medicaid declined twice as fast among noncitizens as among citizens. [22]

The American Hospital Association — a major hospital industry trade group, not an immigrant advocacy organization — filed formal comments on December 19, 2025 warning that the proposed DHS rule would produce exactly these harms. The AHA cited the documented 2016–2019 impact: "Previous changes to the public charge rule resulted in disenrollment from health care programs, including 8% of children living in citizen-only households who disenrolled from Medicaid and CHIP." Among citizen children with a noncitizen parent, the decline was even steeper according to KFF chilling effects research. [19] [27]

The 2025 environment is demonstrably more severe. The KFF/New York Times 2025 Survey of Immigrants (n=1,805, nationally representative, field dates August–October 2025) found: [21]

  • 29% of immigrant adults skipped or postponed healthcare in the past 12 months — up from 22% in 2023
  • 14% avoided seeking medical care since January 2025 specifically due to immigration concerns
  • 48% of likely undocumented immigrants avoided medical care since January 2025
  • 11% stopped participating in assistance programs since January 2025 due to immigration-related fears
  • 40% of immigrant adults overall experienced negative health impacts (stress, anxiety, sleep or eating problems, worsening chronic conditions)
  • 77% of likely undocumented immigrants reported these negative health impacts
  • 18% of immigrant parents report their child's well-being has been negatively impacted since January 2025
  • 51% of immigrant adults are concerned their health provider may share their information with ICE or CBP

KFF survey data from early 2026 showed 11% of immigrant adults stopped participating in assistance programs since January 2025 due to immigration fears; the rate was 20% among those in noncitizen households. [7] One in four US children has an immigrant parent. The policy's primary measurable effect is frightening American citizens out of their own health coverage. [27]

6. The CMS-ICE Data Sharing Regime and the H.R. 1 Legislative Context

The February 2026 DHS disinformation post did not exist in isolation. By February 2026, a parallel data-sharing infrastructure had been built that gave the chilling effect institutional teeth. In summer 2025, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) entered into a formal data-sharing agreement with ICE — a direct reversal of prior explicit assurances to the public, applicants, providers, and states that Medicaid data would never be used for immigration enforcement. [17]

The CMS-ICE data agreement, established July 2025 and formally noticed in the Federal Register on November 25, 2025, enables ICE access to Medicaid enrollee data including name, address, Medicaid ID, SSN, date of birth, sex, phone number, ethnicity, race, banking information, and IP addresses. The stated purpose is to "identify and locate aliens in the United States." [25]

California filed suit and was joined by 21 other states. A judge issued a preliminary injunction limiting data sharing to six data fields and only for individuals not lawfully present in the 22 plaintiff states. However, Georgetown CCF warned that the T-MSIS system cannot isolate undocumented immigrants from lawfully present immigrants in the same eligibility category — meaning data sharing likely captures lawfully present immigrants in violation of the court order. KFF Health News documented in February 2026 that hospitals and states faced a direct legal dilemma: whether to warn immigrant patients that their Medicaid enrollment data had been shared with ICE (potentially considered "obstruction") or stay silent (breaching duty of care). Patients were avoiding hospitals. Healthcare workers were uncertain about their legal obligations. [26]

The DHS February 2026 post was at least the fifth distinct episode of major disinformation about immigrant Medicaid access within a single year, all following the same consistent pattern: mischaracterizing the population in KFF or government data from "noncitizens" or "noncitizen households" to "illegal immigrants." Georgetown CCF documented four prior episodes: May 2025, June 4, 2025, June 10, 2025, and October 2, 2025. [11]

7. H.R. 1 and Who Really Loses: The Political Economy of the Fabrication

H.R. 1 — the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — passed the House 218–214 and the Senate 51–50 (with Vice President Vance casting the tie-breaking vote), and was signed by President Trump on July 4, 2025. It contains the largest cuts to Medicaid in the program's 60-year history. CBO projections show $911 billion in reduced federal Medicaid spending over 10 years and 10.9 million more people uninsured in 2034 compared to baseline — 7.5 million from Medicaid provisions alone. [24]

The DHS "4 million illegal immigrants" claim provided political cover by framing cuts affecting primarily US citizens, disabled individuals, veterans, and low-income working families as immigration enforcement. The population that actually loses Medicaid coverage under H.R. 1 breaks down as: US citizens (the overwhelming majority of the 10.9 million projected to lose coverage); lawfully present immigrants — LPRs, refugees, domestic violence survivors, and other protected populations — approximately 1.4 million per KFF; and undocumented immigrants, who were already ineligible for full Medicaid and are minimally impacted by the cuts. [12]

The DHS fabrication also served to obscure a structural fiscal trade-off: H.R. 1 simultaneously allocated $170 billion to ICE and immigration detention expansion while cutting Medicaid for millions of eligible Americans. As the ACLU documented: the politicians who supported H.R. 1 funded an immigration police force larger than most of the world's militaries by taking healthcare from millions of their own constituents. [23]

State-level consequences are also measurable. As of April 2025 (pre-OBBBA enactment), 37 states plus DC covered lawfully residing immigrant children without a five-year waiting period in Medicaid and CHIP — a protection directly threatened by H.R. 1. States using state-only funds to cover immigrants face indirect pressure as federal matching funds are reduced. Twenty-two states sued the Trump administration over the CMS-ICE Medicaid data sharing. [9]

5.9 million US citizen children in noncitizen households whose coverage is destabilized by the chilling effect — eligible for Medicaid but may disenroll when parents are afraid. 1.4 million lawfully present immigrants — legal residents, refugees, domestic violence survivors — losing coverage directly under H.R. 1. 10.9 million total projected to lose Medicaid/CHIP by 2034 under CBO's H.R. 1 estimate, the overwhelming majority US citizens. Hospitals and emergency rooms absorbing increased uncompensated care as previously insured patients arrive uninsured and sicker than they would have been with preventive coverage. The AHA warned this increases wait times and reduces bed capacity for all patients, regardless of immigration status. [19]

8. Verdict and Forensic Assessment

Verdict: FALSE

The DHS claim that "up to 4 million illegal immigrant households" would be removed from Medicaid is demonstrably false across three independent dimensions. The population label is wrong: the data describes noncitizen households, not illegal immigrant households — millions of the affected people are US citizens. The mechanism is wrong: the 4 million represents voluntary disenrollment driven by fear, not enforcement removal of ineligible enrollees. And the premise is wrong: undocumented immigrants have been legally barred from federal Medicaid since 1996.

This is not a misinterpretation or a matter of framing. The KFF data graphic reproduced by DHS visibly stated "noncitizen households." DHS knowingly relabeled it. The post was sourced from — and was less accurate than the original of — a far-right organization espousing replacement theory. DHS's own regulatory filing contradicted the claim in its own footnotes. The fabrication was then used on the floor of Congress to justify the largest Medicaid cut in the program's history.
