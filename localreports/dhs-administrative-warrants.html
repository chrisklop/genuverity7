TL;DR

MISLEADING: ICE claims administrative warrants authorize forcible home entry—every federal court to rule on the question says no.

A secret May 2025 ICE memo reverses decades of policy, contradicting the Fourth Amendment, Supreme Court precedent (Payton v. New York), ICE's own training materials, and bipartisan legal consensus. Administrative warrants (Form I-205) are signed by ICE officers—not judges—and authorize arrests in public, not home entry. Federal judges in Minnesota and California have already ruled such entries unconstitutional. More than 170 U.S. citizens were wrongfully detained in 2025 under this policy.

Executive Summary

In February 2026, constitutional alarm erupted over whether ICE agents armed with "administrative warrants" can legally force their way into private homes. The controversy centers on a May 12, 2025 internal ICE memorandum, leaked via whistleblower complaint in January 2026, that reversed decades of DHS policy by asserting that Form I-205 administrative warrants—signed by ICE officers, not judges—authorize forcible home entry [1].

Constitutional scholars, federal judges, civil liberties organizations, and even six former DHS general counsels (both Republican and Democrat) emphatically say no: only judicial warrants signed by a neutral magistrate authorize home entry under the Fourth Amendment [3][4]. U.S. District Judge Jeffrey Bryan ruled on January 17, 2026 that ICE's warrantless home entry in Minneapolis violated the Fourth Amendment, ordering the immediate release of Garrison Gibson, a Liberian national legally residing in the U.S. under supervised release [5].

The 1980 Supreme Court precedent Payton v. New York established that "the physical entry of the home is the chief evil against which the wording of the Fourth Amendment is directed," requiring a warrant from a neutral magistrate except in exigent circumstances [6]. Yet DHS General Counsel James Percival defended the policy, claiming "deep state actors" had wrongly restrained ICE for decades [7]. This triggered an unprecedented rebuke from the six former DHS counsels: "It is not the so-called deep state that has restrained ICE from entering homes using only administrative warrants. It is the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution" [4].

1. The Claim: Administrative Warrants Authorize Home Entry

On January 22, 2026, the Associated Press broke the story of a secret ICE memo that had been circulating internally since May 12, 2025. Authored by Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons, the memo asserted that DHS Office of General Counsel had "recently determined that the U.S. Constitution, the Immigration and Nationality Act, and the immigration regulations do not prohibit relying on administrative warrants for this purpose"—specifically, forcible home entry to arrest individuals with final removal orders [1].

The memo was kept secret. Employees were reportedly expected to read it and return it to supervisors rather than retain copies—a secrecy that six former DHS general counsels said "speaks volumes about the weakness of its arguments" [4]. Two anonymous ICE employees filed a whistleblower complaint through Whistleblower Aid in January 2026, exposing the policy [10].

On February 4, 2026, DHS doubled down. A press release titled "DHS Sets the Record Straight on Administrative Warrants" claimed that "illegal aliens aren't entitled to the same Fourth Amendment protections as U.S. citizens" [7]. DHS General Counsel James Percival published a Wall Street Journal op-ed arguing that the so-called "deep state" had improperly restrained ICE for decades and that undocumented immigrants lack full Fourth Amendment protections.

2. What Administrative Warrants Actually Are

Administrative warrants come in two primary forms used by ICE: Form I-200 (authorizing arrest of anyone agents believe is present in violation of federal immigration law) and Form I-205 (targeting non-citizens who already have a final removal order issued by an immigration judge) [8].

Critically, both are signed by ICE officers—not judges. A Congressional Research Service analysis notes that an administrative warrant "does not confer authority to enter a home or private area" [8].

Judicial warrants, by contrast, are signed by a neutral federal magistrate or judge after a finding of probable cause. Only judicial warrants satisfy the Fourth Amendment's requirement for home entry.

The distinction isn't academic. As Stanford Law Professor Orin Kerr explained: "It allows ICE to basically decide itself to go into people's homes... The government's not allowed to come into your home—unless you give them permission—without a warrant" [2].

3. The Secret Memo: How ICE Reversed Decades of Policy

For decades, DHS policy required judicial warrants for home entry. A 2007 DHS document explicitly stated: "A warrant of removal is administrative in nature and does not grant the same authority to enter dwellings as a judicially approved search or arrest warrant" [9].

ICE's own written training materials—used in the Basic Immigration Enforcement Training Program (BIETP)—state in bold and underlined text: "a warrant of removal/deportation does NOT alone authorize a 4th amendment search of any kind" [9].

Yet the May 2025 memo reversed this guidance without providing legal rationale. According to the whistleblower complaint, the memo was "rolled out in a secretive manner in which some agents were verbally briefed while others were allowed to view it but not keep a copy" [10]. New ICE recruits are reportedly being told verbally to ignore the written training materials and follow the memo instead.

The Brennan Center for Justice characterized the move as "an attempt to circumvent constitutional requirements through executive fiat" [3].

4. What Courts Have Ruled: Unanimous Rejection

Court & Year Ruling Key Quote
Payton v. New York (1980, SCOTUS) Admin warrants insufficient "The Fourth Amendment has drawn a firm line at the entrance to the house"
Kidd v. Mayorkas (2024, CA) Admin warrants insufficient DHS carved out Central District of CA from policy after ruling
U.S. v. Gibson (2026, MN) Fourth Amendment violation "Forcibly entered... without his consent and without a judicial warrant"

The Supreme Court established the controlling precedent in Payton v. New York (1980): "In terms that apply equally to seizures of property and to seizures of persons, the Fourth Amendment has drawn a firm line at the entrance to the house. Absent exigent circumstances, that threshold may not reasonably be crossed without a warrant" [6].

The Court emphasized that warrants must be issued by "neutral and detached magistrates" to satisfy the Fourth Amendment—a requirement administrative warrants signed by ICE officers do not meet.

Legal scholars are in unanimous agreement. Emmanuel Mauleón of the University of Minnesota Law School stated: "This has been long-standing Fourth Amendment interpretation. There has been no U.S. Supreme Court case ever that has found that an administrative warrant meets this bar" [14].

David Schultz of the University of St. Thomas added: "There really is no debate on this one among legal scholars that an administrative warrant gives you no authority to enter houses at all" [14].

5. The Minneapolis Raid: Garrison Gibson's Story

On January 11, 2026, the Associated Press witnessed ICE officers in heavy tactical gear use a battering ram to force their way into the home of Garrison Gibson, a 37-year-old Liberian national who fled Liberia's civil war as a child [20]. His wife, Teyana Gibson Brown, and their 9-year-old child were inside at the time.

In a sworn declaration, Gibson stated that agents arrived in the early morning while his family slept. He refused to open the door and repeatedly demanded to see a judicial warrant. His wife filmed the encounter. Armed agents holding rifles stood in the doorway, and one agent repeatedly claimed "we're getting the papers" in response to demands for a warrant [21].

Without showing a warrant, five to six agents moved in "as if they were entering a war zone." Only after Gibson was handcuffed did agents show his wife documentation—which the court later confirmed was an administrative warrant, not a judicial warrant.

Gibson had been living in the U.S. under an order of supervision, checking in regularly with immigration authorities. Days before his arrest, he had complied with a check-in requirement at the very office where agents had been staging enforcement raids.

Judge Bryan's Ruling: On January 17, 2026, U.S. District Judge Jeffrey Bryan found that ICE agents violated Gibson's Fourth Amendment rights when they "forcibly entered Garrison G.'s home without his consent and without a judicial warrant." Judge Bryan ordered Gibson's immediate release [5].

ICE's Defiance: One day after the judge ordered Gibson's immediate release, ICE agents took him back into custody when he appeared for a routine immigration check-in. According to Gibson's attorney, the lead officer said the decision came from a higher authority and was "above my pay grade," and that the order came from the White House [20].

6. Scale of Constitutional Violations: 170+ Citizens Detained, 96 Court Orders Violated

According to a ProPublica investigation, more than 170 U.S. citizens were detained or arrested by ICE and other DHS agencies in just the first nine months of 2025 [22]. The majority of cases involved citizens of Latino descent, who were questioned about their citizenship status during raids or routine stops. Many were held without being charged, denied access to lawyers, or detained alongside undocumented relatives.

Mistaken identity, language barriers, racial profiling, and flawed databases all contribute to wrongful detentions. ICE runs fingerprints through error-ridden databases, and without further investigation, issues detainers—resulting in the detention of scores of U.S. citizens.

Court Order Violations

Federal Judge Patrick Schiltz found that ICE violated at least 96 court orders in January 2026 alone—an "extraordinary" number that he noted means "ICE has likely violated more court orders in January 2026 than some federal agencies have violated in their entire existence."

As of late January 2026, there were nearly 600 habeas corpus petitions filed in federal court challenging wrongful detentions—more petitions than had been filed in the preceding year combined.

Beyond the Gibson case, advocates and attorneys have documented multiple cases of ICE agents breaking down doors without judicial warrants in 2026 [25]:

  • Hmong American Citizen (Jan 18, 2026, Minneapolis): ICE officers broke into the home of a Hmong American citizen without presenting any warrant. The man whose home was searched was neither present nor the target of the action.
  • Multiple Minneapolis Residences: Federal lawsuits document that ICE agents deployed pepper spray toward neighbors who gathered outside during raids, used battering rams on front doors while warning that "children were inside," and entered homes despite repeated demands for judicial warrants.
  • Broader Pattern: Civil rights organizations filed lawsuits documenting that in cities targeted by ICE operations, "agents breaking down people's doors to arrest them without a warrant" has become a documented enforcement tactic.

7. Detention System Crisis: Record Deaths and Overcrowding

The ICE detention system reached unprecedented levels in 2026, with approximately 73,000 individuals held in custody as of January 16, 2026—the highest number in the agency's 23-year history, representing an 84% increase from January 2025 [15].

More people died in ICE detention in 2025 than in the last four years combined. At least 31 deaths were reported in 2025—the deadliest year on record. January 2026 alone saw 6 additional deaths in ICE custody, plus 2 fatal shootings by ICE agents [16]. The rising fatalities are attributed to acute overcrowding, abysmal detention conditions, medical neglect, soaring mental distress, and gun violence.

The Trump administration dramatically changed the profile of who is being arrested by increasing the use of "at-large" arrests in American communities by 600% [17]. According to the Prison Policy Initiative, 73.6% of detainees have no criminal convictions whatsoever.

By November 2025, ICE was using 104 more facilities for immigration detention than at the start of the year—a 91% increase, ranging from small county jails to previously closed state prisons to newly constructed tent facilities on military bases [24].

8. Congressional Response and Bipartisan Legal Opposition

On January 21, 2026, Senator Richard Blumenthal (D-CT), Ranking Member of the Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations, sent formal letters to DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons, and the Director of the Federal Law Enforcement Training Centers demanding a full explanation for the secret policy [19].

During a Senate hearing, Blumenthal confronted Acting ICE Director Lyons directly, arguing that case law "does not abrogate the Fourth Amendment" and "does not say that ICE agents or CBP agents can simply bash down doors and barge into people's homes, terrorize their children, detain and arrest people without a judicial warrant."

Blumenthal stated publicly: "I won't vote for any funding for DHS, the Department of Homeland Security, or ICE or the Customs and Border Patrol, unless these demands are met."

Bipartisan Legal Consensus

Six former DHS general counsels who served in both Republican and Democratic administrations—Stevan Bunnell, Gus Coldebella, Ivan Fong, Kara Lynum, Jonathan Meyer, and John Mitnick—wrote in The New York Times:

"It is not the so-called deep state that has restrained ICE from entering homes using only administrative warrants. It is the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution—and the lawyers who took an oath to support and defend it." [4]

They added: "The job of the general counsel is not to provide convenient legal cover or constitutional workarounds. It is to provide sound legal analysis and advice, even when that advice may be inconvenient for the administration."

Multiple lawsuits challenging the policy are now in federal court, including one filed by the Greater Boston Latino Network and Brazilian Worker Center against DHS Secretary Kristi Noem and Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons [13].

9. Weaponization of Administrative Tools: Subpoenas to Silence Critics

DHS has also weaponized administrative subpoenas—which, like administrative warrants, require no judicial approval—to silence critics. In February 2026, reports surfaced that DHS has issued hundreds of administrative subpoenas to tech companies like Google, Meta, Reddit, and Discord demanding the identities of anonymous social media accounts that criticize or track ICE operations [11].

Unlike traditional warrants, administrative subpoenas do not require approval from a judge before they are issued. Instead of seeking court authorization first, DHS can sign and send the demands directly to tech companies [12].

Privacy advocates warn this creates a chilling effect on First Amendment-protected speech and allows the government to unmask anonymous critics without meaningful judicial oversight.

10. Know Your Rights: What to Do if ICE Comes to Your Door

Critical Rights Guidance

If immigration agents want to enter your home, you have the right to:

  • Ask if they have a warrant signed by a judge. If they say they have a warrant, ask them to slip it under the door or hold it up to a window.
  • Refuse to open the door if they do not have a signed court-issued warrant.
  • Know that immigration agents can lie to get into your home—they may say they are investigating a crime or need to "talk or take a quick look around." Do NOT let them in.
  • Clearly state: "I do not consent to your entry or any searches. I am exercising my right to remain silent, and I will not talk without a lawyer present."

Critical Distinction: A document with "DHS Form I-200" or "DHS Form I-205" on it is NOT a valid judicial warrant. A valid judicial warrant will have "U.S. District Court" or the name of a state court at the top, and will list your name, the name of someone who lives in your home, or your home address [18].

The ACLU acknowledged the internal ICE memo from May 2025 directing agents to use force to enter a residence with only an administrative warrant, but the ACLU stated it stands by its recommendations: administrative warrants are not signed by a judge and do not authorize home entry [18].

Immigration attorney Rosanna Berardi stated that the ICE memo "represents a fundamental Fourth Amendment challenge and another chapter of the Trump Administration ignoring long-established legal precedence and acting like the legislative branch."

11. Tom Homan's Defense and Executive Branch Overreach

Tom Homan, appointed as "Border Czar" in the Trump administration, has publicly defended the use of administrative warrants for home entry. In a February 15, 2026 Face the Nation interview, when asked what's wrong with obtaining a judicial warrant to enter private property, Homan responded: "That's not what the federal law requires" [23].

He argued that Congress wrote the Immigration Nationality Act giving power to administrative warrants, and said: "If Congress wants that change, then Congress can legislate. But right now, ICE is acting within the framework of federal statutes enacted by Congress and signed by a president."

When pressed about whether he had a "change of heart" on judicial warrants, Homan distanced himself from the legal determination, attributing it to DOJ guidance rather than taking personal responsibility for the policy change, despite the fact that the memo was signed by Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons and implemented under Homan's oversight as Border Czar.

Legal scholars view this as executive branch overreach—attempting to reinterpret constitutional requirements without congressional authorization or judicial approval.

Conclusion: A Constitutional Crisis Hiding in Plain Sight

The administrative warrant controversy represents more than a technical legal dispute—it's a fundamental test of whether the executive branch can unilaterally reinterpret constitutional protections without judicial or legislative approval.

The legal consensus is clear: administrative warrants do NOT authorize home entry under the Fourth Amendment. Every federal court to rule on the question has held this position. ICE's own training materials acknowledge this. Six former DHS general counsels from both parties agree. Legal scholars are unanimous.

Yet the policy remains in effect, with ICE agents conducting warrantless home entries across the country, federal judges being openly defied, and more than 170 U.S. citizens wrongfully detained in 2025 alone.

The implications extend beyond immigration enforcement. If the executive branch can redefine what counts as a valid warrant for home entry, it threatens Fourth Amendment protections for all Americans—not just immigrants.

As the six former DHS counsels wrote: "You can't wish away the Fourth Amendment" [4].
