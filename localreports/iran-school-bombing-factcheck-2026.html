Section 2: What Happened at Shajareh Tayyebeh School

The Shajareh Tayyebeh elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan Province, southern Iran, was a girls' school serving approximately 300 students aged 7–12. Between 2013 and 2016, the school had been physically separated from the adjacent IRGC naval base with a new perimeter wall and independent entrance. By 2017, satellite imagery showed civilian markings including soccer pitch lines clearly demarcating the school from the military facility. [3]

At approximately 10:23–10:45 AM local time on February 28, 2026 — the first day of Operation Epic Fury — a Tomahawk cruise missile struck the school compound during peak classroom hours. The two-story building collapsed. Iranian authorities and the Minab mayor confirmed a triple-tap pattern: a second missile hit the school prayer room, where the principal had moved surviving students after the first impact, and a third strike hit the compound shortly after. The second and third strikes killed those who had sheltered. [1] [17]

The death toll, per the Iranian Red Crescent's confirmed figure cited by Amnesty International, stands at 168 killed: 110+ schoolchildren, 26 teachers, and 4 parents. At least 95 more were injured and transported to Hazrat Abolfazl Hospital, approximately 2 km from the school. Iranian state media reported 175–180 killed; the Al Jazeera aggregate tracker places the figure at 175+ as of March 17. [10] [28]

Iran held a mass funeral for the victims on March 3, 2026, attended by tens of thousands of mourners. The Iranian government announced plans to convert the school site into a permanent museum and memorial. Al Jazeera's coverage of the mass funeral was among the most-watched international news broadcasts of the conflict. [17]

The strike occurred within a 22-minute window in which CENTCOM launched multiple Tomahawk strikes across southern Iran. Chairman of the Joint Chiefs Dan Caine confirmed on March 2 that US Navy vessels fired Tomahawks in southern Iran on February 28. The Minab IRGC naval base — adjacent to the school — was on the US target list for Operation Epic Fury. The school was not meant to be targeted, but the DIA intelligence used to generate targeting coordinates had not been updated since before the school was separated from the base. [2]

Section 3: The Physical Evidence — Tomahawk ID, Fragments, and 8 Experts

The physical evidence chain in this case is unusually strong for a wartime disinformation incident. The evidence was assembled through open-source intelligence, independent expert analysis, and satellite imagery — all pointing to the same weapon and the same operator.

Video Footage: Footage filmed from a nearby construction site and released by Mehr News Agency shows a subsonic cruise missile diving steeply in terminal phase — consistent with the Tomahawk's known flight profile. Bellingcat's Head of Research, Carlos Gonzales, led the geolocation effort, manually comparing video elements — trees, buildings, walls, power poles and cables — to satellite imagery to confirm the video was captured in the school's direction. Shadow analysis confirmed the time of day matched the known strike window. Gonzales found "no irregularities" suggesting the footage was AI-generated or altered. [4]

Eight Independent Munitions Experts: TIME Magazine assembled eight independent weapons experts who reviewed the video and fragment photos and unanimously confirmed the missile as an American UGM-109 Tomahawk land-attack cruise missile. John Gilbert of the Center for Arms Control and Non-Proliferation stated: "The video taken on February 28, 2026, conclusively shows a Tomahawk cruise missile diving almost vertically into the area of the medical clinic adjacent to an IRGC facility." Wes Bryant, a USAF Special Operations targeting expert, called the incident a "failure in fundamental targeting doctrine and standards." An unnamed US government official told The Intercept it represented "colossal negligence." [5] [6]

Missile Fragments: Photos from the bomb site showing Tomahawk airframe fragments were confirmed by CNN and independently verified by Snopes. The distinctive fin and fuselage sections visible in the images are consistent with UGM-109 Tomahawk components. [18] [19]

Satellite Imagery: Planet Labs imagery obtained by NPR shows at least eight precision strike points across the school compound, consistent with precision air-delivered munitions. The same Planet Labs imagery compared against 2013–2017 historical imagery reveals the school was clearly demarcated from the adjacent IRGC base by 2016, with soccer pitch markings visible. NPR reported: "Analysis of high-resolution satellite imagery captured on March 4 confirms that explosive weapons detonated in at least eight points." [3]

Corroborating Bellingcat Investigation: On March 11, Bellingcat published a second investigation — "Tracing Tomahawks: US Missiles Bound for Iran Spotted Over Iraq" — independently corroborating the February 28 strike pattern by geolocating Tomahawk cruise missiles transiting Iraqi and Kurdish airspace en route to Iranian targets. This investigation confirmed the flight paths of missiles that struck the Minab area. [30]

Section 4: The AI Targeting Failure — Maven Smart System and the Outdated DIA Data

The Minab school strike raises a critical question beyond who fired the missile: what role did artificial intelligence play in generating the targeting coordinates that sent a Tomahawk into a school full of children?

The Maven Smart System — Scale of AI Targeting in Operation Epic Fury

Multiple reporting outlets confirmed that Anthropic's Claude AI model, embedded within Palantir's Maven Smart System, was used for targeting analysis during Operation Epic Fury. In the first 24 hours of operations (February 28, 2026), the US struck more than 1,000 targets — each processed through Maven's pipeline with GPS coordinates, weapons recommendations, and automated legal justifications. Over the first three weeks, between 5,500 and 6,000 total targets were struck. Maven replaced eight or nine separate military intelligence systems, compressing kill chain decisions from hours to minutes. [26] [27]

On March 12, Rep. Jason Crow (D-CO), Rep. Yassamin Ansari (D-AZ), and Rep. Sara Jacobs (D-CA) led a letter signed by 120 total Democratic House members to Defense Secretary Hegseth, demanding answers by March 20, 2026. The letter asked verbatim: "Was artificial intelligence, including the use of Maven Smart System, used to identify the Shajareh Tayyebeh school as a target? If so, did a human verify the accuracy of this target?" [21] [22]

CENTCOM Commander Adm. Brad Cooper confirmed the use of "advanced artificial intelligence tools" during the operation, stating: "Humans will always make final decisions on what to shoot and what not to shoot and when to shoot, but advanced AI tools can turn processes that used to take hours and sometimes even days into seconds." This statement underscores the Minab problem: when AI compresses targeting cycles from hours to seconds, human review time compresses proportionally — and outdated source data is deployed at machine speed. [13]

As The Conversation noted in a peer-written analysis, "the tech doesn't lessen the need for human judgment in war" — specifically because AI targeting systems are only as accurate as their input data. Outdated DIA intelligence fed into Maven produces outdated target coordinates: AI does not correct for stale data, it accelerates the deployment of whatever data it receives. Whether Claude-Maven generated the specific coordinates for the Minab school compound — and whether the school was already flagged as civilian in any data layer the system had access to — has not been publicly confirmed as of publication. [25]

The Anthropic-Pentagon relationship adds further complexity. On March 1, 2026 — the day after the war began — the Trump administration designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security" and ordered federal agencies to phase out Claude use within six months. This followed CEO Dario Amodei's refusal to drop two contractual restrictions: prohibiting use for mass domestic surveillance of Americans and for fully autonomous weapons. CENTCOM used Claude for the Iran strikes regardless, as the six-month phase-out clock had not yet run. [26]

Section 7: Legal Analysis — Three IHL Principles and the Accountability Gap

New Lines Institute analyst Susanna Kelley published a legal analysis concluding the Minab strike violated three core principles of International Humanitarian Law:

Three IHL Violations — New Lines Institute Analysis

1. Distinction: The strike "failed to differentiate between the civilian school and adjacent military installations, despite satellite imagery showing the school's independent civilian status since 2016." Kelley noted that strikes on the same block reportedly bypassed a medical clinic, demonstrating targeting precision was available but not applied to spare the school.

2. Proportionality: Over 175 civilian deaths — mostly children aged 7–12 — "with no corresponding military advantage constitutes excessive force."

3. Precaution: "Attackers did not exercise sufficient due diligence to identify the school as civilian, though publicly available information clearly established its character."

Kelley's conclusion: "The attack was manifestly disproportionate, as it caused extensive civilian casualties without yielding any concrete or direct military advantage." [23]

The Prosecution Problem: Prof. Marko Milanovic — Professor of Public International Law at the University of Reading and Special Adviser on Cyber-enabled Crimes at the ICC Office of the Prosecutor — published a widely-cited legal commentary at EJIL: Talk arguing that AI involvement does not create fundamentally new accountability gaps. He wrote: "The 'decisions' or 'actions' of current AI systems are simply the consequences of the actions of those humans [who programmed and deployed them]." [24]

Milanovic argues that under Rome Statute Article 32(1), when military officers "honestly but negligently misidentify targets," mistakes of fact can negate criminal intent, making prosecution difficult regardless of AI involvement. His conclusion: "Systematic, patterned targeting of civilians remains prosecutable regardless of AI use. Isolated incidents — even causing mass casualties — typically remain unprosecutable under existing standards." This suggests that even with ICC jurisdiction established over Iran, a single school strike — however catastrophic — may not meet the war crimes prosecution threshold absent evidence of intent. Accountability would most likely require domestic US action (a court-martial or congressional investigation), which experts view as politically unlikely under the current administration. [24]

Historical Precedent: Al Jazeera drew parallels to the 1999 Chinese embassy bombing in Belgrade — where NATO used outdated maps to strike a building that had been rezoned — and the 1991 Amiriyah bunker strike in Iraq. Both resulted in limited accountability despite mass civilian casualties. In both cases, the US government acknowledged the error, paid some compensation, and faced no criminal prosecution. [17]

Section 9: The Information War — US Media vs. International Coverage

The Minab school strike exposed a significant divergence between US domestic media coverage and international reporting. The gap itself became part of the story.

US right-wing media initially amplified Trump's March 7 claim as exoneration, framing it as evidence that Iran was reckless in its own territory. The false claim that Iran had confessed — rated FALSE by PolitiFact — spread to millions of users before the debunking reached comparable scale. This mirrors a well-documented pattern in wartime disinformation: the false claim spreads at machine speed, the correction travels at human speed. [15]

International media — Al Jazeera, BBC, Euronews, CBC — led with the physical evidence from the first days after the strike and treated the Tomahawk identification as established fact by March 8. US mainstream media was more cautious, often framing the story as "disputed" even as the evidence asymmetry was becoming overwhelming. The Washington Post was notable for leading US coverage with the physical evidence rather than the competing claims narrative. [1] [2]

The propagation path of the disinformation followed a documented White House amplification structure: Trump's claim at Dover AFB → White House social media → right-wing media amplification → social media virality → the false IRGC "confession" as a secondary mutation. The correction path was slower and less emotionally engaging: expert consensus → fact-check articles → preliminary Pentagon investigation leaks. By the time Hegseth acknowledged the investigation on March 13, the false claim had already reached hundreds of millions of people. [9] [11]

The Pentagon photographer ban — implemented precisely as investigation findings were published — is a case study in information environment management during wartime. By blocking visual documentation of Hegseth's briefings, the Pentagon reduced the spread of accountability imagery while the verbal briefings themselves continued. Press freedom advocates have called this a structural suppression mechanism that amplifies false narratives by depriving the public of evidence that would counter them. [33]

Conclusion: The Verdict Is Not in Dispute

Final Verdict: FALSE — Disinformation

Trump's claim that Iran bombed its own school is definitively false. The evidence — video forensics, missile fragments, satellite imagery, eight independent munitions experts, and the US military's own preliminary investigation — all identify the weapon as an American UGM-109 Tomahawk cruise missile. Iran possesses no Tomahawk missiles. The strike was caused by CENTCOM's use of outdated DIA targeting data that had not been updated since the school was separated from the adjacent IRGC base between 2013 and 2016.

Trump was reportedly told by his own intelligence community that his initial claim was incorrect. He made the public statement anyway on March 7. When contradicted by weapons experts, he invented a second false claim — that Iran possesses Tomahawk missiles. When his own military's investigation found the US responsible, he said "I don't know about it." Defense Secretary Hegseth ordered a full investigation on March 13 — tacitly acknowledging US responsibility. This is the definitional pattern of presidential disinformation: a false claim advanced with authority, maintained in the face of contradicting evidence, and withdrawn through silence rather than correction.

168 people died at the Shajareh Tayyebeh school on February 28, 2026. At least 110 of them were children aged 7 to 12. They are not a disputed data point. They are the cost of a targeting system that ran on decade-old data at machine speed, and of a political response that chose narrative over accountability.
