Executive Summary

The High-frequency Active Auroral Research Program (HAARP) in Alaska has become one of the internet's most persistent conspiracy theories, blamed for everything from hurricanes to earthquakes to "weather warfare." Following the devastating Los Angeles wildfires in early 2025, viral posts on X and TikTok again accused HAARP of deliberately causing extreme weather events.

The facts: HAARP is a legitimate ionospheric research facility now operated by the University of Alaska Fairbanks. Its radio transmitters study a layer of the atmosphere 60-200 miles above Earth's surface—far too high to affect weather, which occurs in the troposphere (0-10 miles up). The facility's maximum output of 3.6 megawatts is roughly equivalent to a small AM radio station and is billions of times weaker than the energy in a single thunderstorm.

Claim-by-Claim Analysis

"HAARP can control the weather and create hurricanes" FALSE

HAARP operates in the ionosphere, 60-200 miles above Earth. Weather forms in the troposphere, the lowest 6-10 miles. According to NOAA, hurricanes require warm ocean waters, atmospheric moisture, and rotating wind patterns—conditions that exist only in the troposphere. HAARP's radio waves cannot reach or influence this layer. The energy required to create a hurricane is approximately 10^18 watts; HAARP's maximum output is 3.6 × 10^6 watts—a difference of 12 orders of magnitude.

"HAARP caused the 2025 LA wildfires through weather manipulation" FALSE

This claim went viral on X following the January 2025 fires. However, Reuters Fact Check confirmed that HAARP cannot influence surface weather conditions. The LA fires were caused by documented factors: prolonged drought, low humidity, Santa Ana winds, and vegetation conditions—all tracked by National Weather Service forecasts days in advance.

"HAARP can trigger earthquakes" FALSE

Following the 2023 Morocco earthquake, this claim resurfaced. FactCheck.org investigated and found no scientific mechanism by which radio waves directed at the ionosphere could trigger seismic activity. Earthquakes result from tectonic plate movements miles below Earth's surface. USGS seismologists confirmed the Morocco quake was a natural event consistent with the region's known fault lines.

"HAARP is a secret military weapon" MISLEADING

HAARP was initially funded by the U.S. military (Air Force, Navy, DARPA) from 1993-2014, which fueled conspiracy theories. However, it was never classified as a weapon. Since 2015, it has been operated by the University of Alaska Fairbanks as a civilian research facility. The site is open for public tours and regularly hosts scientists from universities worldwide. Research findings are published in peer-reviewed journals.

What HAARP Actually Does

The Science

HAARP uses high-frequency radio waves to temporarily excite a small portion of the ionosphere for scientific study. This allows researchers to understand how the ionosphere affects radio communications, GPS signals, and satellite transmissions.

According to the American Physical Society, HAARP's research has practical applications: improving long-distance radio communication, understanding space weather impacts on satellites, and studying the aurora borealis. The facility's Ionospheric Research Instrument (IRI) consists of 180 antennas spread over 33 acres.

Atmospheric Layers: Where HAARP Operates vs Where Weather Forms

As illustrated above, there's a fundamental disconnect in the conspiracy theory: HAARP's effects are limited to the ionosphere (60+ miles up), while all weather phenomena occur in the troposphere (0-10 miles). These layers don't interact in ways that would allow HAARP to influence surface conditions.

How the Conspiracy Spread

1993
HAARP construction begins in Gakona, Alaska with military funding. Early conspiracy theories emerge linking it to mind control and weather warfare.
2002
Nick Begich publishes "Angels Don't Play This HAARP," popularizing unfounded claims about the facility's capabilities.
2010
Venezuelan leader Hugo Chávez claims HAARP caused the Haiti earthquake—a claim debunked by seismologists.
2015
U.S. Air Force transfers HAARP to University of Alaska Fairbanks for civilian research. Conspiracy theories persist.
2023
Morocco earthquake triggers new wave of HAARP claims; FactCheck.org debunks.
2025
LA wildfires spark viral HAARP claims on X and TikTok despite no scientific basis.

2025 Viral Spread Pattern

HAARP Mentions on Social Media Following LA Fires (Jan 2025)

Analysis of social media data shows HAARP conspiracy posts spiked within 24 hours of the LA fire outbreak, following a pattern seen after other natural disasters. Posts containing "HAARP" and "LA fires" reached millions of views before fact-checks could effectively counter them, demonstrating the challenge of real-time misinformation.

Conclusion

HAARP conspiracy theories persist because they offer simple explanations for frightening events and tap into distrust of government institutions. However, the scientific reality is clear: HAARP is an ionospheric research facility that operates too high in the atmosphere and with too little power to influence weather or trigger earthquakes.

Every major claim about HAARP's weather-control capabilities has been debunked by atmospheric scientists, fact-checkers, and the facility's own publicly available research. The 2025 resurgence of these claims following the LA wildfires follows the same pattern seen after every major natural disaster—exploiting tragedy to spread unfounded theories.
