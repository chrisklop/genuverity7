## Executive Summary

On February 28, 2026, the first day of the US-Israel war on Iran, a Tomahawk cruise missile struck the Shajareh Tayyebeh elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan Province, southern Iran, during peak classroom hours. The two-story girls' school collapsed, killing between 165 and 180 people — the vast majority of them schoolgirls aged 7 to 12, along with 26 teachers and several parents. It was the single deadliest civilian strike of the conflict.[1][2][3] Video footage released by Iranian state media and geolocated by Bellingcat and BBC Verify confirmed the munition as an American UGM-109 Tomahawk land-attack cruise missile — a weapon confirmed by eight independent munitions experts and possessed exclusively by the United States and a small number of treaty allies, none of which were involved in the Iran strikes.[4][5][6]

Despite this evidence, President Trump told reporters on March 7 that the strike was "done by Iran," adding that Iran was "very inaccurate, as you know, with their munitions."[7] Trump subsequently argued that "numerous other nations have Tomahawks" — a claim rated false by PBS NewsHour and leading weapons analysts who confirmed Iran possesses no Tomahawk missiles.[8] A preliminary US military investigation, reported by The New York Times and confirmed by two US officials, determined that CENTCOM officers used outdated Defense Intelligence Agency targeting data that still classified the school building — which had been separated from the adjacent IRGC naval base between 2013 and 2016 — as a legitimate military target.[9][10] Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced a full investigation on March 13, 2026, in what The Washington Post described as a tacit acknowledgment of US responsibility.[11]

Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights, UNESCO, and Senator Sheldon Whitehouse have all called for accountability. Over 120 Democratic members of Congress sent a letter to Hegseth asking whether the Maven Smart System — Palantir's AI targeting platform confirmed to be in use during Operation Epic Fury — played a role in identifying the school as a target, and whether a human verified the AI's output.[12][13] CENTCOM Commander Adm. Brad Cooper confirmed the use of "advanced artificial intelligence tools" in the operation, while insisting humans retain final targeting authority.[14]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin of the Disinformation

The false narrative that Iran bombed its own school originated with a briefing paper prepared for President Trump. According to The New Republic, citing intelligence sources, Trump's initial belief was based on a preliminary report that the CIA subsequently told him was incorrect.[15] Despite being corrected by the intelligence community, Trump made the false claim publicly on March 7, 2026, at Dover Air Force Base — the same day he attended the dignified transfer of US soldiers killed in Kuwait.

Trump's claim rested on two specific assertions:
1. That Iran's munitions "have no accuracy whatsoever" (implying the school strike looked like an Iranian-style accident)
2. That Tomahawk missiles are widely owned ("numerous other nations have Tomahawks")

Both assertions are factually wrong. The Tomahawk is not an inaccurate weapon — it is a precision-guided cruise missile. And beyond the US, only Japan, the United Kingdom, Australia, and the Netherlands operate Tomahawks; Iran possesses none.[8][16]

### Propagation Path

- **March 7**: Trump claims at Dover AFB that Iran bombed the school; the clip goes viral on X (formerly Twitter) and right-wing media immediately amplifies it as exoneration of US forces
- **March 8**: Washington Post publishes video showing Tomahawk striking near the school; Bellingcat and BBC Verify complete geolocation
- **March 9**: A separate false claim circulates on social media that the IRGC admitted to bombing the school — rated **FALSE** by PolitiFact, which found no such statement was ever made[17]
- **March 11**: New York Times reports preliminary Pentagon findings confirm US responsibility; Trump responds "I don't know about it"
- **March 13**: Hegseth orders full investigation, tacitly acknowledging US role
- **March 16**: Amnesty International formally concludes US was responsible

### Why It Spread

Trump's claim spread because: (1) it came with presidential authority; (2) it aligned with a pre-existing narrative that Iran is reckless and inaccurate; (3) the US and Israel both initially denied involvement, creating a vacuum of official accountability; and (4) algorithmic amplification on social media rewarded emotionally compelling counter-narratives that absolved the US. The false IRGC "confession" claim exploited information warfare dynamics, spreading to millions of users before PolitiFact's debunking.

### Physical Evidence

The physical evidence chain is unusually strong:

1. **Video footage**: Filmed from a nearby construction site and released by Mehr News Agency. Geolocated by Bellingcat and BBC Verify to the school compound. Shows a subsonic cruise missile diving steeply — consistent with a Tomahawk's terminal-phase profile.[4]

2. **Munitions experts**: Eight independent experts confirmed the missile as an American UGM-109 Tomahawk, including John Gilbert (Center for Arms Control and Non-Proliferation), Sam Lair (James Martin Center for Nonproliferation Studies), and Wes Bryant (USAF Special Operations targeting expert).[5][6]

3. **Missile fragments**: Photos from the bomb site show Tomahawk airframe fragments, confirmed by CNN and Snopes.[18][19]

4. **Satellite imagery**: Planet Labs imagery obtained by multiple outlets shows at least eight precision strike points across the school compound, consistent with precision air-delivered munitions. Imagery from 2013–2017 reveals the school was clearly demarcated from the adjacent IRGC base by 2016, including visible soccer pitch markings.[3][20]

5. **US military admission**: CENTCOM's own records showed Tomahawk launches targeting the Minab area on February 28. Chairman of the Joint Chiefs Dan Caine confirmed Tomahawk strikes in southern Iran on that date.[21]

6. **Double-tap strike**: Iranian authorities reported a "double-tap" pattern — a second missile struck the school prayer room where the principal had moved students after the first hit, killing those who had sheltered.[22]

Under **Article 12(3) of the Rome Statute**, any state — even non-parties — may lodge a declaration accepting ICC jurisdiction over crimes committed on its territory from a specific date. Iran is not a Rome Statute signatory but can utilize this provision.

The US-based Democracy for the Arab World Now (DAWN) organization separately urged Iran to make a formal Article 12(3) declaration to enable ICC jurisdiction over the conflict. Iran's Red Crescent also independently urged the ICC to examine alleged strikes on civilian sites as possible war crimes.

A critical complication: the United States is not an ICC member and has previously invoked the American Servicemembers' Protection Act (ASPA, "The Hague Invasion Act") which authorizes military force to free any American detained by the ICC. This means even with ICC jurisdiction established, prosecuting US personnel faces severe structural barriers.

## Evidence Deep-Dive — Pass B

### B1. The Anthropic/Claude-Maven AI System — What Actually Happened

Multiple reporting outlets (Washington Post, NBC News, CBS News, Bloomberg, Responsible Statecraft) confirmed that **Anthropic's Claude AI model**, embedded within Palantir's **Maven Smart System**, was used for targeting analysis during Operation Epic Fury. The scale is staggering:

- In the **first 24 hours** of operations (February 28, 2026), the Claude-Maven system generated approximately **1,000 prioritized targets**, each with precise GPS coordinates, weapons recommendations, and automated legal justifications for strikes
- Over the first three weeks, between **5,500 and 6,000 total targets** were struck
- Maven replaced "eight or nine" separate military intelligence systems, compressing kill chain decisions from hours to minutes

**How Claude functioned**: Claude served as a natural-language interface for military planners — operators could query intelligence databases and receive tactical summaries in plain English. Per Anthropic's public framing, Claude "does not directly offer targeting recommendations" but functions as a "decision support system." Critics called this distinction meaningless given the scale of AI-generated target lists.

**The Anthropic-Pentagon Dispute**: On March 1, 2026 — the day *after* the war began — the Trump administration **designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security"** and ordered all federal agencies to phase out Claude use within six months. This followed CEO Dario Amodei's refusal to drop two contractual restrictions: prohibiting use for mass domestic surveillance of Americans and for fully autonomous weapons. CENTCOM used Claude for the Iran strikes anyway, as the six-month phase-out clock had not yet run.

**Key unanswered question**: Whether Claude-Maven generated the targeting coordinates for the Minab school compound — and whether the school was already flagged as a civilian site in any data layer the system had access to — has not been publicly confirmed.

## Contemporary Context & Political Dimensions — Pass C

### C1. Legal Analysis: Three IHL Principles Violated

**New Lines Institute** analyst **Susanna Kelley** published a legal analysis concluding the Minab strike violated three core principles of International Humanitarian Law:

1. **Distinction**: The strike "failed to differentiate between the civilian school and adjacent military installations, despite satellite imagery showing the school's independent civilian status since 2016." Kelley noted that strikes on the same block reportedly bypassed a medical clinic, demonstrating targeting precision was available but not applied to spare the school.

2. **Proportionality**: Over 175 civilian deaths — mostly children aged 7–12 — "with no corresponding military advantage constitutes excessive force."

3. **Precaution**: "Attackers did not exercise sufficient due diligence to identify the school as civilian, though publicly available information clearly established its character."

Kelley's key quote:
> "The attack was manifestly disproportionate, as it caused extensive civilian casualties without yielding any concrete or direct military advantage."

### C2. EJIL: Talk — AI, Accountability Gaps, and the Prosecution Problem

**Prof. Marko Milanovic** — Professor of Public International Law at the University of Reading and **Special Adviser on Cyber-enabled Crimes at the ICC Office of the Prosecutor** — published a widely-cited legal commentary at EJIL: Talk arguing that AI involvement does not create fundamentally new accountability gaps:

> "The 'decisions' or 'actions' of current AI systems are simply the consequences of the actions of those humans."

Milanovic argues that under Rome Statute Article 32(1), when military officers "honestly but negligently misidentify targets" (as occurred at Minab), mistakes of fact can negate criminal intent, making prosecution difficult regardless of AI involvement. His conclusion:

> "Systematic, patterned targeting of civilians remains prosecutable regardless of AI use. Isolated incidents — even causing mass casualties — typically remain unprosecutable under existing standards."

This is significant because it suggests that even with ICC jurisdiction established over Iran, a single school strike — however catastrophic — may not meet the threshold for war crimes prosecution under current law, absent evidence of intent. Accountability would most likely require domestic US action (a court-martial or congressional investigation), which experts view as politically unlikely under the current administration.

**Source**: EJIL: Talk (European Journal of International Law blog)
