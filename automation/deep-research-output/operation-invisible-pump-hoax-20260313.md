## Executive Summary

A fabricated claim began circulating on Facebook on March 10, 2026 — the same week the U.S.-Israel war on Iran sent gas prices surging past $3.50 per gallon nationally. The post, attributed to Facebook user "Zeke Sky" promoting his Substack newsletter "The Weekly Rot," claimed that the White House had announced "Operation Invisible Pump" and that President Trump had signed an executive order "mandating the immediate removal of all digital gas price displays nationwide." The post further claimed Trump declared the signs were "displays of election interference." None of this is true. No executive order by that name, or with that purpose, exists in any official government record. [1][2]

The hoax spread because it exploited a genuine and acute public anxiety: gas prices that had risen nearly 70 cents per gallon in under two weeks following the February 28 U.S.-Israeli strike on Iran. [3] Crude oil spiked from approximately $71 per barrel pre-conflict to over $100 per barrel within days, as Iran's threatened closure of the Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint through which roughly 20% of global oil supply flows — sent markets into panic mode. [4][5] The International Energy Agency characterized the disruption as "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." [6] In this environment, claims that the government was hiding price increases resonated powerfully with an angry public. [7]

The hoax also drew fuel from a credibility gap Trump himself created. At his February 2026 State of the Union address, Trump falsely claimed gas was "below $2.30 a gallon in most states" when the actual national average was $2.975. [8] After the Iran war began, Trump abruptly pivoted from taking credit for low prices to calling high prices "a small price to pay" for military victory. [9] That whiplash — from bragging to minimizing within weeks — primed audiences to believe almost anything about gas price manipulation. The "Operation Invisible Pump" hoax landed directly into that credibility vacuum.

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin of the Hoax

The claim was first posted to Facebook by a user identified as "Zeke Sky," described on his own website as a multi-instrumental musician who also publishes a Substack newsletter called "The Weekly Rot." The post was placed at the bottom of a longer piece promoting the newsletter, suggesting the fabricated "breaking news" angle was a content-driving tactic rather than an earnest belief. The original Facebook post read:

> "BREAKING: The White House has announced 'Operation Invisible Pump.' President Trump signed an Executive Order this morning mandating the immediate removal of all digital gas price displays nationwide. Trump stated the signs were 'displays of election interference.'"

The post carried no supporting links, no White House press release citation, no Federal Register number — none of the markers an authentic executive order announcement would carry.

### Why the Hoax Found an Audience

Three converging factors made the claim credible to a significant slice of the public:

**1. Real price pain.** Gas prices jumped 51 cents per gallon in a single week in early March 2026, a shock that hit working-class and fixed-income consumers hardest. [3] Alma Newell, a 52-year-old consumer, told Al Jazeera: "The prices have a big impact because I'm not working right now. Food and rent are already very expensive." [10] When prices rise sharply with little warning, conspiracy-flavored explanations — government manipulation, hidden orders — feel more intuitive than commodity market explanations.

**2. Trump's credibility collapse on gas prices.** Trump campaigned on "drill, baby, drill" and promised to "cut energy prices in half within 12 months." [11] At the February 2026 State of the Union he claimed gas was below $2.30 in most states — false by roughly 65 cents. [8] Then within weeks of the Iran war's start, his messaging flipped entirely: surging prices were now "a small price to pay." [9] This pattern — promise, exaggerate, pivot — trained audiences to distrust official gas price narratives, making unofficial ones more appealing.

**3. A bipartisan cognitive bias.** Research shows Americans across the political spectrum tend to believe the president secretly controls gas prices when that serves their political priors. [12] During Biden's term, right-wing viral posts claimed Biden was hiding true prices; during Trump's Iran war, the same psychological template resurfaced for left-leaning audiences.

### How It Spread

The claim spread primarily through Facebook shares in the days after March 10. The framing mimicked a breaking news flash, complete with ALL CAPS headline formatting. The absurdity of the specific claim — removing all digital price signs as "election interference" — was extreme enough to be memorable and shareable as outrage content, while being just plausible enough (given Trump's history of unusual executive actions) to avoid immediate dismissal.

### The Debunking

Snopes fact-checkers confirmed the claim was false through three parallel verification steps:
- A WhiteHouse.gov search for "Operation Invisible Pump" returned no results
- A Federal Register search returned no matching executive order
- No Associated Press, Reuters, or major wire service carried any reporting on such an order

Snopes noted that if Trump had signed this executive order, it would have been "officially documented" in multiple government databases and immediately covered by the wire services that monitor White House actions continuously. [1]

### A4. Gas Price Sign Regulation — State Authority, Not Federal

Research into the legal infrastructure governing gas price signs confirms the core debunking point: no president has ever had statutory authority to mandate or prohibit gas station price displays nationwide because this power rests entirely with state governments through weights-and-measures law.

Key findings from state-level research:
- **Michigan:** The Michigan Weights and Measures Law requires all petroleum product price advertising to be accurate and not deceptive. Digital and roadside signs must display prices to the tenth of a cent. Enforced by the Michigan Department of Agriculture and Rural Development.
- **New Hampshire:** State law requires stations to do at least two of three things: post price on each fuel dispenser, display a unit price indicator, or post a roadside sign visible from the street.
- **Maryland:** State law requires posting "the lowest price for a whole measurement unit of regular and mid-grade gasoline sold on the premises."
- **California:** Has among the most detailed requirements, with precise rules on lettering size, sign placement, and lighting.
- **New York City:** It is illegal to sell gasoline without posting accurate price-per-gallon information.

The pattern is consistent: gas price sign requirements are a "patchwork of state laws," not federal mandates. An executive order attempting to remove these signs nationwide would conflict with each state's weights-and-measures statutes and would almost certainly be immediately enjoined by federal courts as an intrusion on state regulatory authority.

### B2. Strait of Hormuz Shipping Insurance Collapse — The Real Supply Shock

The Strait of Hormuz disruption was not merely a diplomatic threat — it caused a near-total collapse of marine insurance coverage, physically blocking oil shipments:

- **Insurance cancellations:** Major P&I clubs — Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, the London P&I Club, and the American Club — announced cancellation of war risk cover effective March 5, 2026.
- **Premium spike:** War risk premiums rose from ~0.2% of ship value per voyage to as high as 1% — a 5x increase in days. For a $100M tanker, a single voyage's war-risk premium jumped from ~$200,000 to ~$1 million.
- **Traffic collapse:** Tanker traffic through the Strait dropped approximately 70-80% as ships anchored outside the strait rather than risk the transit without coverage. Over 150 ships waited at anchor outside the chokepoint.
- **VLCC freight rates:** Benchmark freight rates for Very Large Crude Carriers (VLCCs) hit an all-time high of **$423,736 per day** — up more than 94% from Friday's close.
- **Scale of disruption:** Roughly one-third of seaborne crude oil trade moves through the Strait, alongside 19% of global LNG flows and 14% of global refined product trade.

This insurance-driven shutdown — rather than a physical blockade — was the primary mechanism by which the closure translated into global price shocks within days.

### B4. Trump's Exact Quotes — Two Separate Pivots

Research reveals Trump used two distinct minimizing formulations, not one:

1. **"Small price to pay"** — Posted on Truth Social (Sunday, March 8-9, 2026): "Short term oil prices, which will drop rapidly when the destruction of the Iran nuclear threat is over, is a very small price to pay for U.S.A., and World, Safety and Peace. ONLY FOOLS WOULD THINK DIFFERENTLY!"

### B5. Facebook's Handling of the Hoax

Research found no evidence that Facebook applied a misinformation label to the "Operation Invisible Pump" post or removed it. The post remained visible and shareable. This is consistent with Meta's policy shift in early 2025, when the company announced it was ending its third-party fact-checking program in the U.S. in favor of a "Community Notes" model — changes that significantly reduced the speed and consistency of misinformation labeling on the platform. The Snopes fact-check (published March 13) was the primary public debunking mechanism, not platform enforcement.

### C2. History of Fabricated Executive Orders as a Disinformation Tactic

"Operation Invisible Pump" is not an isolated incident. Fabricated executive orders are a recurring disinformation template that exploits the authority and specificity of the presidential order format:

**Obama-era precedents (documented by Snopes and FactCheck.org):**
- **"Obama Bans Pledge of Allegiance" (2016):** A satirical site published a fake "Executive Order" banning the Pledge of Allegiance in schools nationwide. The post received over 2 million Facebook shares. The real EO 13738 was an amendment to a labor-law order about federal contractors.
- **"Obama Bans Fake News Outlets" (2016):** A fabricated executive order claimed Obama criminalized fake news websites following the election. Entirely invented.
- **"Trump Bans Facebook" (April 2017):** A fake executive order claiming Trump would ban Facebook nationwide, traced to a Macedonian fake-news operation. Debunked by PolitiFact.

The template is consistent across all these hoaxes:
1. Exploit a real political anxiety (patriotism, election integrity, gas prices)
2. Attribute a sweeping, unilateral action to the president
3. Use official-sounding formatting ("BREAKING," "Executive Order," specific directives)
4. Omit any verifiable official record citations
5. Rely on the difficulty of disproving a negative ("prove there's no order")

"Operation Invisible Pump" follows this template precisely — and like its predecessors, it spread primarily on Facebook with no authoritative citation.

- Snopes — "Did Obama Sign Executive Order Banning the Pledge of Allegiance?": https://www.snopes.com/fact-check/pledge-of-allegiance-ban/
- Snopes — "In Wake of Election, Obama Passes Executive Order Banning All Fake News Outlets": https://www.snopes.com/fact-check/in-wake-of-election-obama-passes-executive-order-banning-all-fake-news-outlets/

### C3. Gas Price Misinformation as a Pattern — Not an Anomaly

Gas prices generate disinformation predictably and consistently during price spikes. Snopes documented this pattern as early as 2022 (during the Russia-Ukraine spike) and it has recurred in each major price shock since. Several structural factors make gas prices uniquely susceptible:

1. **Presidential accountability misconception:** A significant share of Americans across the political spectrum believe presidents directly control gas prices, despite this being economically false. (NACS research, documented in original report)
2. **Daily visible reminder:** Gas price signs are among the few prices Americans see every day while driving — making price shocks maximally visceral and emotionally activating.
3. **Complex causation:** The true causes of price changes (commodity markets, geopolitics, refinery capacity, seasonal blend switching) are harder to communicate than a simple blame narrative.
4. **Partisan amplification:** During Republican administrations, left-leaning audiences are more susceptible to "the government is hiding true prices" narratives; during Democratic administrations, the mirror dynamic occurs. The hoax crossed partisan lines because of the Iran war's polarizing nature.

The "Operation Invisible Pump" hoax is the latest iteration of a disinformation genre that has accompanied every major gas price spike since at least 2008.

**Source:** Snopes — "4 Tips For Avoiding Online Misinfo About Gas Prices" (2022): https://www.snopes.com/news/2022/06/08/gas-prices-misinformation/
