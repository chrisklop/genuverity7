## Executive Summary

A report released on April 1, 2026 by the Digital News Association (DNA) and coordinated by investigative journalist Jeffrey Scott Shapiro revealed that the Russian government trained more than 1,000 content creators, journalists, and social media influencers to disseminate disinformation across Latin American countries. The report, presented in the United States, identified eight primary target countries — Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Colombia, Cuba, Mexico, Nicaragua, and Venezuela — and found that the training was closely linked to RT en Español, the Spanish-language arm of the Russian state media apparatus. The broader ecosystem also includes approximately 200 Spanish-speaking content creators based in Moscow who serve as coordination nodes between the Kremlin's propaganda infrastructure and Latin American audiences.[1][2][3]

The program is not an isolated event but rather the latest escalation of a years-long, well-funded Kremlin influence campaign across the Western Hemisphere. Russian-aligned entities — including the Social Design Agency (SDA), Structura, and the Institute for Internet Development — have operated overlapping information-manipulation networks targeting at least 13 Latin American nations since at least 2014, with a marked intensification following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.[4][5] The campaign employs a documented "narrative laundering" strategy: pro-Kremlin content is created in Russia, localized by curated regional editorial staff, and published through Latin American media outlets to appear organic, making detection and attribution substantially more difficult.[6]

The credibility of the core claim is reinforced by corroborating U.S. State Department findings, a U.S. Department of Justice disruption action against SDA, a Global Engagement Center (GEC) special report on Brazil's Nova Resistência, and independent investigative journalism documenting a ~$280,000 covert propaganda operation in Argentina in 2024. While Russia has denied all such accusations and no comprehensive list of trained individuals has been publicly released, the analytical methodology — AI-assisted pattern detection of content sharing coordination — is consistent with techniques used in previous validated influence-operation investigations.[7][8]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The immediate origin of the April 2026 reporting is the Digital News Association's "Russian Propaganda Alert," a monitoring initiative overseen by Jeffrey Scott Shapiro, a former senior adviser and director at the U.S. Office of Cuba Broadcasting (2017–2021). Shapiro's team used an AI-assisted platform to identify content exhibiting "components of the Russian state-sponsored media apparatus" — patterns of coordinated posting, narrative alignment, and amplification by accounts linked to RT en Español and Sputnik Mundo.

The underlying infrastructure, however, has a much longer origin. The U.S. State Department documented the campaign architecture in November 2023, identifying SDA (led by Ilya Gambashidze), Structura (led by CEO Nikolay Tupikin), and the Institute for Internet Development as three Russian "influence-for-hire" firms operating under direction of the Russian Presidential Administration. The State Department found that these entities coordinated "on the development of an information manipulation campaign targeting Latin America that aims to promote Russia's strategic interests in the region at the expense of other countries by overtly and covertly coopting local media and influencers to spread disinformation."

### Propagation

The April 2026 report spread first through Spanish-language media in Latin America — notably Infobae (Argentina), BioBioChile and Emol (Chile), Efecto Cocuyo (Venezuela), Correo del Caroní (Venezuela), Valoraanalitik (Colombia), and Vanguardia (Colombia). English-language pickup included Colombia One and Cartel Watch, with social amplification via Threads. The story was amplified further by local radio coverage, including Colombia's Caracol Radio — which notably misattributed the DNA report to Forbes Colombia before corrections.

The propagation pattern reflects the regional sensitivity to the topic: Latin American outlets in the directly named countries led coverage, while U.S. and international outlets provided secondary amplification.

### Why It Spread

Several factors explain rapid regional amplification:
1. **Specificity of named countries** — eight nations were explicitly cited, generating immediate local news hooks.
2. **Concrete numbers** — the "1,000+" figure is distinctive and easily quotable.
3. **Institutional credibility framing** — the story was presented as a U.S.-based report, lending it perceived authority in countries where U.S. intelligence assessments are taken seriously by mainstream media.
4. **Pre-existing anxiety** — years of prior reporting on RT/Sputnik influence in the region primed audiences to find the claim credible.
