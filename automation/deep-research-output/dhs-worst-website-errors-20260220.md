## Executive Summary

On February 19, 2026, CNN published an exclusive investigation revealing that the Department of Homeland Security admitted its website showcasing the "worst of the worst" arrested immigrants was rife with errors.[1] The website, launched December 8, 2025 at wow.dhs.gov, listed approximately 25,000 people along with criminal charges—but a CNN analysis found that hundreds were incorrectly described, with many listed only for minor offenses like traffic violations, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry, not the violent crimes the site purported to showcase.[1][2]

DHS acknowledged the errors only after CNN's inquiry, attributing them to a "glitch" affecting "about 5 percent of the entries."[3] However, the site had been heavily promoted on social media by DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, the White House, and federal officials as evidence justifying aggressive immigration enforcement operations—including Operation Metro Surge in Minneapolis, which deployed approximately 3,000 federal agents.[4][5] The website's errors are part of a broader pattern: DHS officials made demonstrably false claims about Alex Pretti, a U.S. citizen killed by federal agents in Minneapolis on January 24, 2026, calling him a "domestic terrorist" who "brandished" a weapon—claims contradicted by multiple videos showing he never drew his holstered firearm.[6][7]

Independent data contradicts the administration's core claims. According to TRAC Immigration data current through February 7, 2026, **73.6% of the 68,289 people in ICE detention had no criminal conviction**—up from 70.4% in June 2025.[8] The Cato Institute, analyzing leaked DHS data, found that of people booked into ICE custody since October 1, 2025, 73% had no criminal conviction and only 5% had violent criminal convictions.[9] Minnesota officials accused DHS of inflating arrest statistics by taking credit for routine transfers from state custody, with Corrections Commissioner Paul Schnell calling it "pure propaganda" and providing video evidence of coordinated transfers DHS later claimed as enforcement "arrests."[10][11]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin: Government-Created Propaganda Tool

The "Worst of the Worst" (WOW) website was created by DHS and announced on **December 8, 2025**, as a searchable public database of people arrested during Trump administration immigration enforcement operations.[12] DHS framed it as a transparency initiative, claiming it would allow Americans to "find criminal illegal aliens removed from your community."[12] The initial launch featured thousands of entries, which DHS expanded on December 18 (+5,000 entries), January 22 (search improvements), and February 5 (+5,000 more entries), bringing the total to approximately 25,000 listed individuals.[13][14]

The website displayed:
- Names of arrested individuals
- Arrest locations (city/state)
- Criminal charges or convictions
- Country of origin
- Mugshot photos in some cases

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, the White House, and DHS official social media accounts heavily promoted the site, posting it alongside enforcement announcements and Operation Metro Surge updates.[1][15] The site became the **7th most-viewed page on DHS.gov in all of 2025** despite being available for only 24 days that year.[16]

### Propagation: Official Amplification & Media Reach

The website was promoted through:

1. **Official DHS press releases** with headlines like "Most Transparent Administration in American History Adds 5,000 More Criminal Illegal Alien to Public Website"[14]
2. **Social media campaigns** by Kristi Noem, Stephen Miller, and White House accounts showing mugshots and highlighting "worst of the worst" arrests[1][15]
3. **Operation Metro Surge publicity** where DHS claimed to have arrested 4,000+ "criminals" in Minnesota, using the website as supporting evidence[17]
4. **Millions of pageviews**: DHS.gov traffic increased 68.49% in 2025, reaching 102 million pageviews[16]

The White House and DHS used the site to justify:
- Large-scale enforcement deployments (3,000 agents to Minnesota alone)[4]
- Administrative warrant policies allowing warrantless home entries[18]
- Refugee detention and rescreening policies[19]
- Claims that 70% of ICE arrests involved "criminal illegal aliens"[20]

### Why It Spread: Official Authority + Fear-Based Messaging

The website gained traction because:

1. **Government authority**: Official .gov domain with DHS branding created presumption of accuracy
2. **Searchable by location**: Allowed users to see "criminals in your community," triggering local concern
3. **Timing with enforcement surge**: Coincided with Operation Metro Surge and other high-visibility raids
4. **Political messaging**: Aligned with administration narrative about "invasion" and public safety threats
5. **Media amplification**: Initial uncritical coverage treated DHS data as factual before errors emerged

The administration's claims were rarely challenged in early coverage. As former acting ICE director John Sandweg noted, "The vast majority of so-called criminal aliens are individuals charged with or convicted of traffic offenses, DUIs and immigration-related offenses"—but this context was absent from DHS promotional materials.[1]

### Evidence of Systemic Deception

**1. The "Glitch" Explanation Falls Apart**

DHS claimed a "glitch" caused errors affecting "about 5% of the entries," saying many listed for traffic offenses actually had "additional crimes."[3] But:
- DHS provided no technical explanation for how a "glitch" could systematically misrepresent charges
- The 5% figure (1,250 of 25,000) still represents **hundreds of people** falsely characterized
- DHS only acknowledged errors after CNN inquiry—not proactively
- The "glitch" was allegedly "resolved" within one day[3]—implausible for complex data systems

**2. Geographic Manipulation: Prison Towns as "Arrest Locations"**

CNN analysis revealed that cities with the highest arrest numbers were **small towns with large federal detention facilities**:[21]

| City | Population | ICE Facility | Arrests Listed |
|------|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Conroe, TX | ~114,000 | Joe Corley Processing Center (private) | Most arrests nationally |
| Eden, TX | Small | Federal detention center | Disproportionately high |
| Lompoc, CA | Small | Federal detention center | Disproportionately high |
| Yazoo City, MS | Small | Federal detention center | Disproportionately high |

This indicates DHS counted **routine transfers from federal prison custody** as new "arrests" of community threats—inflating the narrative that dangerous criminals were "lurking in your neighborhood."[21]

**3. Minnesota Officials Provide Counter-Evidence**

Minnesota Corrections Commissioner **Paul Schnell** accused DHS of "pure propaganda" and presented video evidence refuting DHS claims:[10][11]

- DHS claimed it "arrested" people released into communities by Minnesota DOC
- Schnell showed videos of **coordinated, routine transfers** to ICE custody—not "arrests"
- Some people on DHS lists were transferred to ICE **years ago** (as far back as the 1990s)[11]
- Many listed were **never in Minnesota prisons** or had no criminal record in Minnesota[11]
- Governor Tim Walz similarly accused DHS of "inflating" numbers by taking credit for pre-existing state custody transfers[22]

Schnell stated: "This is no longer a simple misunderstanding. At best, DHS fundamentally misunderstands Minnesota's correctional system. At worst, it is pure propaganda, numbers released without evidence to stoke fear rather than inform the public."[23]

**4. Contradictory Detention Data**

Independent analysis contradicts DHS's "worst of the worst" framing:

**TRAC Immigration (as of Feb 7, 2026):**[8]
- Total ICE detention: 68,289 people
- **No criminal conviction: 50,259 (73.6%)**
- With criminal conviction: 18,030 (26.4%)

**Cato Institute (analyzing leaked DHS data, Oct 1, 2025–present):**[9]
- **73% had no criminal conviction**
- **Only 5% had violent criminal convictions**
- Only 8% had violent or property crime convictions
- Majority of convicts had vice, immigration, or traffic convictions

**Detention growth analysis:**[24]
- Between Sept 21, 2025 and Jan 7, 2026: ICE detention increased by 11,296 people
- Of that increase: **only 902 were convicted criminals** (8%)
- **92% of detention growth came from people with no criminal convictions**

This data directly contradicts DHS's public messaging that it was targeting "the worst of the worst."

**5. Pattern of False Official Statements**

The website errors are part of a documented pattern of DHS making false claims, then retracting or being contradicted by evidence:

It's not about actually arresting the most convicted criminals."[26]

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B: Detailed Analysis)

### The "Glitch" That Wasn't: Technical Implausibility Analysis

DHS attributed the website errors to a "glitch" affecting "about 5% of the entries," claiming it was "resolved" within one day of CNN's inquiry. However, this explanation raises significant technical and procedural questions:

**Technical red flags:**
1. No explanation of what type of "glitch" could systematically mischaracterize charges across hundreds of entries
2. One-day resolution timeline is implausible for complex database systems requiring data validation
3. DHS provided no technical documentation, error logs, or system audits
4. The pattern of errors (systematically listing minor offenses alongside violent crimes) suggests design choice, not random system failure

**Pattern analysis:**
The errors were not random but followed a clear pattern: individuals with single minor charges (traffic violations, marijuana possession, illegal reentry) were placed in a database explicitly titled "Worst of the Worst" alongside people charged with violent crimes. This creates a false equivalence that serves a political narrative rather than reflects a technical malfunction.

**Expert assessment:**
John Sandweg, former acting ICE director during the Obama administration, stated: "The vast majority of so-called criminal aliens are individuals charged with or convicted of traffic offenses, DUIs and immigration-related offenses." When asked about the scale of violent criminal threats as described by the Trump administration, Sandweg said: "That population is not out there. It's just not there."

### Alex Pretti Shooting: Frame-by-Frame Video Analysis

Bellingcat, CBS News, CNN, and The Washington Post conducted independent frame-by-frame analyses of multiple videos showing the January 24, 2026 shooting. Key timeline:

**9:01:12 AM** — Officer in grey jacket approaches the scuffle between federal agents and Alex Pretti. The officer's hands are empty.

**9:01:13 AM** — Grey jacket officer reaches into the melee and removes a handgun from Pretti's waistband holster. The gun matches the description of Pretti's legally carried Sig Sauer P320 (white pistol grip, black frame, brown slide, red dot sight).

**9:01:14 AM** — Grey jacket officer emerges from the scrum holding the firearm. Pretti's hands are visible on the ground in front of him, not near his holster.

**9:01:14 AM (1 second later)** — An agent in a black beanie, who appears to have line of sight on the gun being removed, begins to draw his own weapon. Someone shouts "gun" or "he's got a gun."

**9:01:15 AM** — First shot fired, followed by nine more shots in approximately six seconds.

**Analysis of subsequent shots:** After Pretti collapses onto the ground following the first shots, agents back away. A second agent then fires at least one additional shot. Shots five through ten were all fired at Pretti's motionless body on the ground.

**Gun discharge analysis:** An alternate angle appears to show that the slide of Pretti's firearm does not move to the rear, which would indicate that it was not fired. There is no evidence that Pretti's gun was fired during the incident.

**9:02:28 AM (74 seconds after first shot)** — The grey jacket officer is heard saying, "I got the gun. I got the gun," walking toward officers surrounding Pretti.

**Critical finding:** It is unclear from the videos whether the officer who took the weapon from Pretti immediately communicated to other officers that he was removing it, which may explain why shots were fired immediately after the gun was removed from Pretti's person.

## Contemporary Context (Pass C: Political Dimensions & Current State)

### The Broader Immigration Enforcement Narrative

The "Worst of the Worst" website serves as a cornerstone of the Trump administration's immigration enforcement messaging in 2026. DHS frames the site as "highlighting the worst of worst criminal aliens arrested by ICE," claiming that "under Secretary Noem's leadership... DHS and ICE are fulfilling President Trump's promise and carrying out mass deportations — starting with the worst of the worst."

However, expert analysis contradicts this framing. An internal DHS document obtained by CBS News found that **less than 14% of nearly 400,000 people arrested by ICE during President Trump's first year back in office had charges or convictions for violent criminal offenses**. The same reporting said ICE made roughly 393,000 arrests between January 21, 2025 and January 31, 2026, and that **nearly 40% of arrestees had no criminal record and were accused only of civil immigration violations**.

The Cato Institute reported that when they published analysis showing 73% of ICE detainees had no criminal conviction, DHS called the report's main chart "made up" with "no legitimate data behind it" — yet the raw data came from DHS itself. DHS maintains that "nearly 70% of ICE arrests are of illegal aliens charged or convicted of a crime in the U.S.," inflating numbers by including pending charges (not convictions) and claiming this "doesn't even include foreign fugitives, terrorists, and gang members who lack a rap sheet in the U.S."

### Who Benefits Politically?

The website and broader enforcement narrative serve clear political objectives:

1. **Justifying massive enforcement operations:** The website was used to justify Operation Metro Surge (3,000 agents to Minnesota), administrative warrant policies allowing warrantless home entries, and refugee detention/rescreening policies.

2. **Maintaining base support:** The website became the 7th most-viewed page on DHS.gov in all of 2025 despite being available only 24 days that year, indicating strong engagement from supporters seeking validation of the "invasion" narrative.

3. **Deflecting criticism:** By framing enforcement as targeting "the worst of the worst," the administration creates a rhetorical shield against criticism of broader enforcement that disproportionately affects people with no criminal record.

4. **Meeting White House demands for publicity:** President Trump wrote on Truth Social: "Show the numbers, names, and faces of the violent criminals, and show them NOW" — creating direct pressure on DHS to boost public-relations profile.

However, this strategy faces diminishing returns as of early 2026:

### Eroding Public Support

**Gallup Poll (February 2026):** Americans' evaluation of Trump's work on immigration is mostly negative, with **35% approving** and **62% disapproving** (45% strongly disapproving). Views are sharply partisan: 85% of Republicans approve, compared with 28% of independents and just 2% of Democrats.

**Pew Research Center (Jan 20-26, 2026):** Only **37% of Americans approve** of how Trump is handling his job as president — the lowest rating of his second term. Support for his policy plans dropped from 35% supporting "all or most" last year to **27%** in January 2026, with 52% supporting "few or none."

**Immigration attitudes shift:** Just months after Trump returned to office amid a wave of anti-immigration sentiment, the share of U.S. adults saying immigration is "a good thing" for the country jumped substantially — including among Republicans. About **79% of Americans now say immigration is "a good thing,"** up from 64% a year ago and a high point in the nearly 25-year trend.

**Support for deportations declines:** Support for deporting all undocumented immigrants has fallen to **38%**, down from 47% last year when it was a Trump campaign promise.

**Gender gap emerges:** Among women, Trump's support on immigration has fallen to 35% in the latest Reuters/Ipsos survey. Male voters' support, which stayed close to 50% throughout 2025, has dropped to 41%.

**ICE approval ratings plummet:** Brookings cited polling data showing a sharp reversal in ICE approval ratings, dropping from +16 in early 2025 to -14 by November, attributed to public reaction to mass deportations.

### Pending Lawsuits and Congressional Investigations

**Congressional oversight battles:**
- February 2026: Federal court granted emergency relief to 13 members of Congress, ordering DHS and ICE to restore unannounced congressional oversight of ICE detention facilities after Secretary Noem secretly reinstated prior-notice requirements through a previously undisclosed memorandum
- The court's ruling restored members' ability to enter detention facilities in real time, speak with detainees, and investigate conditions including overcrowding, shackling, denial of medical care, and lack of access to counsel

**Congressional hearings:**
- February 3, 2026: Bicameral public forum on DHS use of violence in immigration enforcement, led by Rep. Robert Garcia (D-CA) and Sen. Richard Blumenthal (D-CT)
- Senate Democrats demanded Secretary Noem testify "without delay" following fatal shootings by federal immigration officials in Minneapolis
- Noem has failed to appear before the Senate Homeland Security Committee for its annual hearing since her confirmation, despite testifying before a House panel

**Lawsuits documented:**
- Reuters review (February 2026): Federal courts ruled in over 4,400 cases that ICE under the Trump administration detained immigrants illegally, with rulings from over 400 judges, mostly involving denial of bond
- ACLU Southern California: Coalition lawsuit over alleged unlawful stops, arrests, and inhumane detention conditions, claiming constitutional violations targeting "brown communities"
- ACLU Connecticut: Federal lawsuit over DHS system errors terminating international student visa statuses
- Privacy cases: DHS forced to withdraw subpoena for Google to provide personal information about a man who criticized immigration enforcement

**GAO recommendations disputed:**
- July 2024: GAO recommended ICE publicly report complete detention data and explain methodology
- DHS did not concur, stating ICE already reports sufficient information
- GAO continues to believe ICE should report complete and transparent information

### Current State of WOW.DHS.GOV

**Website status (as of February 2026):** The website remains online and active at wow.dhs.gov, with continued updates:

- January 22, 2026: DHS announced state-search feature (wow.dhs.gov/[state name])

The second shutdown affected only DHS.

**Continued promotional use:** DHS continues publishing daily or near-daily press releases under the WOW.DHS.GOV banner, reporting ICE arrests with headlines like:
- "ICE Arrests More Criminal Illegal Alien Pedophiles, Rapists, and Violent Assailants"

### Why This Matters Now

The DHS website errors represent more than a technical failure — they exemplify a broader pattern of government misinformation used to justify aggressive enforcement policies:

1. **Erosion of government credibility:** Former DHS officials warn that repeated false statements (Alex Pretti, Renee Good, Marimar Martinez, website errors) undermine public trust in law enforcement

2. **Real human consequences:** Hundreds of people were publicly characterized as "worst of the worst" criminals when they had only minor charges, with their names, photos, and locations searchable online

3. **Policy justification based on false data:** The website was used to justify Operation Metro Surge (3,000 federal agents), warrantless home entries, refugee detention policies, and other controversial enforcement actions

4. **Systematic vs. isolated errors:** The pattern (traffic violations listed alongside violent crimes) suggests the problem may be rooted in how DHS defines "criminal aliens" rather than a one-time technical glitch

5. **Accountability gap:** Despite GAO recommendations, court orders, and congressional pressure, DHS has resisted transparency measures and independent oversight

As one analysis concluded: "The immediate question for DHS is narrow: what exactly went wrong with the 'Worst of the Worst' site and how the agency validated the correction.
