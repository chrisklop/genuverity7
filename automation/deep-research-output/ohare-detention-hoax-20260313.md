## Executive Summary

On March 8, 2026, Sundas "Sunny" Naqvi, a 28-year-old U.S. citizen born in Evanston and residing in Skokie, Illinois, became the center of a viral story claiming she was detained by federal immigration officials for approximately 30 to 48 hours at O'Hare International Airport following a flight from Istanbul, then transferred to an ICE facility in Broadview, Illinois, and subsequently to Dodge County Jail in Wisconsin. [1][2] The story spread rapidly on social media, amplified by Cook County Commissioner Kevin Morrison at a press conference outside the Broadview facility, amid a charged political environment over Trump administration immigration enforcement. [3][6] Major outlets including NBC Chicago, ABC7, the Chicago Sun-Times, and Block Club Chicago ran initial reports framing the account largely as stated by Naqvi's family and attorney. [4][5]

The official record substantially contradicts the viral narrative. The Department of Homeland Security released surveillance stills showing Naqvi entering secondary inspection at O'Hare at 10:46 a.m. on March 5 and exiting at 11:42 a.m. — a span of 56 minutes — and stated her total time within CBP processing from arrival at 10:21 a.m. was under 90 minutes. [7][8] The Dodge County Sheriff's Office reported no booking, detention, or release record for Naqvi. [9] SAP SE, the German software company Naqvi's now-deleted LinkedIn profile listed as her employer, confirmed she "has never worked for the company" and that none of its employees were detained at O'Hare. [10] The identities of the five alleged co-detainees have never been independently verified. [11] Against this backdrop, a Cook County court record shows Naqvi pleaded guilty in 2022 to filing a false police report alleging sexual assault in 2019, completing two years of probation in 2024. [12]

The case is genuinely complex in one respect: Naqvi's sister produced phone location tracking data showing the device at O'Hare for more than 24 hours, at the Broadview ICE facility for three to four hours on Friday, and near 216 W. Center Road in Juneau, Wisconsin — adjacent to Dodge County detention facilities — at 2:20 a.m. Saturday. [13] Phone location data tracks a device, not a person, and the Dodge County investigation remains open. The weight of official evidence — three independent government bodies finding no detention record, an employer flatly denying the employment claim, and timestamped surveillance footage showing a 56-minute secondary inspection — indicates the core claims as stated are not credible. The story illustrates how politically polarized the immigration enforcement environment has become, with both DHS and anti-enforcement advocates routinely publishing unverified or selectively framed narratives on social media. [14]

## Forensic Analysis

### The Claim

Beginning March 8, 2026, family members, attorney Robert Held, and Cook County Commissioner Kevin Morrison publicly stated that Sundas "Sunny" Naqvi was:

- Detained at O'Hare International Airport by CBP/ICE upon arrival from Istanbul on March 5
- Held at O'Hare overnight — approximately 30 hours
- Transferred to the Broadview ICE processing facility in Illinois
- Further transferred to Dodge County Jail in Juneau, Wisconsin
- Released around 5 a.m. on Saturday, March 7
- Detained alongside five colleagues (two U.S. citizens, three green card holders), all allegedly SAP employees
- Targeted due to "curious travel history" — the group had recently traveled to Bulgaria and Austria

The claim evolved across media cycles, with reported detention duration escalating from "nearly 30 hours" to "43 hours" to "nearly 48 hours" depending on outlet. [1][2][4] Attorney Held alleged Naqvi was released with a dead phone and no transportation. No formal complaint or legal filing against the government has been identified.

### The Surveillance Evidence

On March 11, 2026, DHS released two surveillance still images from O'Hare purporting to show Naqvi. The timestamps show:

- **10:46 a.m., March 5** — Woman with red suitcase entering the secondary inspection area
- **11:42 a.m., March 5** — Woman exiting secondary inspection pushing a luggage cart

The elapsed time between entry and exit: **56 minutes.** [7][8] DHS additionally stated Naqvi entered the CBP area at 10:21 a.m., putting her total processing time — including the secondary inspection — at approximately 81 minutes. A CBP spokesperson stated: "Ms. Naqvi departed CBP within 90 minutes of her arrival to the United States. Ms. Naqvi was not taken into custody or transferred to ICE for detention." [3]

Critics immediately noted the surveillance images contain visibly blurred areas near the top of each frame, just below the timestamp. Cook County Commissioner Morrison alleged the photos were "clearly doctored" and demanded DHS "release all of the relevant video at O'Hare Airport at the days and times in question." [3][8] DHS did not respond to requests from WSAW for the unedited footage. Blurring in surveillance screengrab releases is a standard redaction practice (e.g., obscuring camera identifiers or operational labels), but no independent verification of the images has been completed as of this writing.

### The Corroborating Denials

Three independent institutions — in addition to DHS — found no evidence supporting Naqvi's detention account:

**1. Dodge County Sheriff's Office (Wisconsin)**
Sheriff Dale Schmidt stated his department had "no record of the individual referenced ever being booked, detained, or released from the Dodge County Jail." The investigation was described as "ongoing." [9]

**2. SAP SE (Employer)**
A company spokesperson stated: "Sunny (Sundas) Naqvi is not and was never an SAP employee. None of the ICE detainees from the flight were SAP employees." Naqvi's LinkedIn profile, which had listed SAP as her employer, was deleted after this story broke. [10][11]

**3. Cook County / Broadview Facility**
While Cook County ICE facility records have not been publicly released, no government entity has confirmed her presence there, and the Broadview facility's standard booking logs were cited as showing no record. [9]

### The Phone Location Complication

The strongest piece of evidence on Naqvi's side is phone tracking data. Her sister Sarah Afzal reported that:

- The phone showed O'Hare airport for more than 24 hours on March 5-6
- The phone appeared at the Broadview ICE facility for 3-4 hours on Friday, March 6
- The signal then appeared near 216 W. Center Road, Juneau, Wisconsin — adjacent to Dodge County detention — at 2:20 a.m. Saturday [13]

Several important caveats apply: phone location data tracks devices, not people; location accuracy at the cell-tower level places a device within a radius that can overlap multiple addresses; Broadview detention is a large facility in a densely developed area; and the phone reportedly died during this period, making continuous chain of custody impossible to establish. None of this data has been verified by an independent technical analyst.

### What Actually Happened — Most Probable Account

The most evidentially supported reconstruction is:

1. Naqvi arrived at O'Hare from Istanbul on March 5 and was flagged for secondary inspection, likely based on international travel history to Bulgaria, Austria, and Turkey.
2. She spent approximately 56 minutes in secondary inspection — a routine, if stressful, CBP process that can involve lengthy questioning, luggage examination, and electronic device searches.
3. She departed the CBP area voluntarily around 11:42-11:43 a.m., less than 90 minutes after arrival.
4. The extended detention claims — two states, 43+ hours, colleague transfers — are contradicted by every official record checked and by the employer of record.
5. The phone location data remains unexplained but is insufficient on its own to establish the claimed detention itinerary.

## Research Notes

### What's Confirmed
- Naqvi was referred to secondary inspection at O'Hare on March 5, 2026 — this is not disputed
- The surveillance timestamps show entry at 10:46 a.m. and exit at 11:42 a.m. (56 minutes)
- DHS states total CBP processing was under 90 minutes from arrival at 10:21 a.m.
- Three independent authorities (DHS, Dodge County Sheriff, SAP) have contradicted central elements of the claim
- Naqvi has a documented prior conviction for filing a false police report (2022 guilty plea)
- The five alleged co-detainees have never been named or independently verified
- Naqvi's LinkedIn profile listing SAP was deleted after the employer denial

### What Remains Genuinely Uncertain
- Why phone location data showed the device near Broadview and Dodge County facilities
- Whether the DHS surveillance images were subjected to any editing beyond standard redaction
- Whether secondary inspection lasted exactly 56 minutes or whether she was held elsewhere within the airport prior to 10:46 a.m.
- The full story of what Naqvi did between ~11:43 a.m. March 5 and the morning of March 7

### Contextual Note for Report
This story emerged in a highly charged environment where DHS itself has been documented publishing false or misleading information about detained individuals (NPR, CNN analyses). That context explains why media ran the story quickly and why advocates were credulous. It does not change the evidentiary weight against Naqvi's specific claims, but the report should acknowledge the broader ecosystem of disinformation from all sides.

### Suggested Verdict Framing
- Primary verdict: **MISLEADING** — the viral claim of 30-48 hours of multi-state detention is contradicted by surveillance timestamps, three independent institutional records, and an employer denial
- Qualifying nuance: Secondary inspection did occur and lasted approximately 56 minutes — Naqvi was not simply waved through. But "secondary inspection" is a standard CBP procedure, not the multi-facility detention ordeal described.
- The phone location data is a genuine complication that warrants mention without being treated as dispositive

## Additional Sources (Pass A)

### A1. DHS Official Statement & X Post

The DHS published its formal rebuttal through both a press statement and a post on X (formerly Twitter), with the account @DHSgov writing: "HERE ARE THE RECEIPTS: As we said Sunny Naqvi entered the CBP area at 10:21 am. Surveillance footage from O'Hare CLEARLY shows her entering secondary inspection at 10:46 a.m., and leaving secondary to the public area at 11:42 a.m. Her claims of spending 43 hours in DHS custody [are blatantly false]." [17] The use of social media as the primary venue for DHS's counter-narrative is itself a documented part of DHS's broader 2026 strategy of posting directly on X to dispute media reports — a practice scholars have called "unprecedented" in government communications. [18]

WMTV 15 News submitted a formal request for the underlying O'Hare surveillance footage to verify the DHS timeline. DHS declined and referred the outlet back to its statement. [19] This refusal to release the full video — while publishing two still frames — remains the single legitimate evidentiary gap in the DHS account.

### A2. Cook County Sheriff's Office / Broadview

The CBS Chicago report added an important fourth institutional denial that was not clearly separated in earlier coverage: the Cook County Sheriff's Office, which operates the Broadview ICE processing facility, confirmed it "searched the Broadview ICE facility but found that the individual in question was not present there." [20] This is distinct from the Dodge County denial and means four separate government bodies — DHS/CBP, Cook County Sheriff (Broadview), Dodge County Sheriff, and the Chicago Department of Aviation — found no record of Naqvi's presence.

### A3. Chicago Department of Aviation Denial

The Chicago Department of Aviation (CDA) — a city agency separate from both the federal CBP and DHS — stated it "did not find any records related to the incident." [21] This is a significant secondary confirmation: CDA manages the airport infrastructure and has independent access to facility logs.

### A4. Dodge County Sheriff: Jail Logs and Investigation Scope

Sheriff Dale Schmidt's full statement, as published by WMTV 15 News on March 10, 2026, stated that "jail logs confirm that no female inmates from the federal government were admitted or released during the timeframe." [19] Schmidt went further when presented with the phone location screenshot: he suggested the image "could have been falsified." He noted that Dodge County Jail has partnered with ICE for over 20 years, meaning the facility would have standard intake procedures that would produce records. He also asked Naqvi to contact his department directly "so we may speak with her regarding the allegation and obtain evidence of the incident from her." As of March 13, 2026, Naqvi had not contacted the sheriff's office. [19]

A detail from the investigation: the closest 24-hour gas station to the Dodge County detention facility declined to release surveillance footage without a subpoena, meaning potential corroborating footage from the surrounding area cannot be independently reviewed. [19]

### A5. CBP Secondary Inspection — Procedural Context

CBP's own published guidance confirms that secondary inspection for U.S. citizens can last from 30 minutes to 6 hours and may include device searches, fingerprints, photographs, and detailed questioning. [22] The 56-minute duration logged in the surveillance footage is consistent with a standard secondary screening — not remarkable by procedure. A key distinction: U.S. citizens, unlike non-citizens, have a right to an attorney during secondary inspection and cannot be denied entry for refusing to provide a device passcode. CBP's statement that Naqvi was referred to secondary "based on law enforcement checks and a baggage exam" is procedurally standard language.

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### B1. The Surveillance Stills — Forensic Detail

DHS released exactly two still frames, not continuous video. The images contain visibly blurred areas near the top of each frame, directly below the timestamp — an area where surveillance systems typically display camera identifiers, zone codes, or operator labels. Morrison's allegation that the blurring indicates "doctoring" is not technically supported: redacting camera or zone identifiers before public release is standard operational security practice for law enforcement. However, DHS's refusal to release the full video recording to news organizations requesting it — specifically WMTV 15 and the Chicago Tribune — prevents independent timestamp verification. [7][11] This is a genuine evidentiary gap, even if it falls far short of the "doctored" allegation.

The DHS X post cited above [17] was the first public release of the images, posted on March 11, 2026, with the caption "HERE ARE THE RECEIPTS." The framing is combative and political, consistent with DHS's broader social media strategy in 2026 — which itself has been criticized by NPR and other outlets for presenting selective narratives. [14]

### B2. SAP Denial — Scope and Timing

SAP SE's denial was categorical and broader than often reported: the spokesperson stated not only that Naqvi was never a SAP employee, but that "None of the ICE detainees from the flight were SAP employees." [10] This means all six alleged co-detainees' identities as SAP employees are disputed, not just Naqvi's. Naqvi's LinkedIn profile had listed her as a "Senior Solution Architect" for SAP SE before it was deleted after March 10. [11] Neither Naqvi, her sister Sarah Afzal, nor Commissioner Morrison provided the co-workers' names to any press outlet seeking verification. [11]

The claim evolved across the news cycle: early reports called them "SAP employees traveling from a work trip"; later versions said they were returning from a "canceled work trip." The trip's destination was variously reported as India (American Bazaar, Block Club) and Istanbul-routed with Bulgaria/Austria travel history (DHS's stated basis for the secondary referral).

### B3. The False Police Report — Full Criminal History Context

The false police report conviction is part of a longer documented pattern at the University of Illinois. According to reporting from the News-Gazette (Champaign-Urbana) and court records from 2019-2022:

1. **Spring 2019**: Naqvi, then a UIUC student, filed a sexual harassment complaint against Professor Joe Petry, alleging a grades-for-sex arrangement. The university launched an investigation.
2. **May 2019**: Naqvi and her roommate William Farrell were charged with intimidation and aggravated unlawful restraint (Class 3 felonies) after allegedly coercing Naqvi's ex-boyfriend with a knife to delete computer files potentially relevant to her Petry allegations. Per the charge: "she deleted files from his computer and cellphone while Farrell stood by him with the knife and made threatening statements." [23]
3. **University investigation outcome**: The University of Illinois ultimately found Naqvi's allegations against Petry "not credible" and that "there had been no violation of the University's Sexual Harassment Policy." Petry filed a lawsuit against UI for continuing its investigation against him after Naqvi's arrest. [24]
4. **2022 plea**: Naqvi pleaded guilty specifically to filing a false police report alleging sexual assault in 2019 — the false report charge, not the felony intimidation charge. She received two years of probation, completed in 2024. [12]
5. **Additional charges**: Separate charges against Naqvi involving a January 2020 incident — alleged residential burglary and criminal damage to property related to a different ex-boyfriend's residence — were reportedly dismissed. [23]

This history is material to credibility assessment. However, the report should note that prior bad acts do not constitute proof that current claims are false — the evidentiary weight comes from the government records, not from character evidence alone.

### B4. GoFundMe / Financial Incentive

No GoFundMe or fundraising campaign linked to the Naqvi story has been identified in any coverage reviewed. This is notable: the absence of a financial component removes one common motivational factor in fabricated viral stories. The HotAir analysis speculated the more likely motivation was personal — the author wrote: "This looks a whole lot like a young woman making up a story to her family to explain where she was for a few days, and she picked a story that was based on what she was hearing about on the news." [15] This theory is speculative and not supported by documentary evidence, but no alternative motivation (financial, political, reputational) has been identified.

### B5. Commissioner Morrison's Political Context

Cook County Commissioner Kevin Morrison is a Democrat who represents District 15 and ran for Congress in 2025. He graduated from Conant High School in 2009 — placing him in the same approximate age cohort as Naqvi — and a Block Club Chicago report confirmed he is "a longtime friend of Naqvi." ### B6. Broadview Facility — Procedural Capacity Detail

The ICE Broadview Processing Center officially holds detainees for up to 12 hours before transfer. In June 2025, ICE changed policy to allow stays of up to 72 hours. [26] The facility has capacity for 236 adults. As of October 2025, WBEZ reported it had "become a de facto detention center, minus the rules and oversight," with ICE using it for longer holds than its official classification permits. This context means that even if Naqvi had been held at Broadview, the facility's own records should show her intake — and Cook County Sheriff found no such record. [20]

## Contemporary Context (Pass C)

### C1. DHS Social Media as Counter-Narrative Tool

The Naqvi case unfolded within a documented pattern of DHS using X (Twitter) as its primary venue for rebutting adverse media coverage of immigration enforcement. A February 2026 NPR investigation documented that DHS and ICE had posted about more than 2,000 people in deportation proceedings, often with photographs of "mostly nonwhite people" and descriptions of criminal histories. [14] The Center for Immigration Studies noted DHS had published a formal "Six Months of Keeping America Safe" rebuttal post pushing back on what it called "media narratives." [27] The DHS.gov website itself published a page titled "DHS Debunks New York Times False Reporting." Scholars quoted by NPR described this approach as "unprecedented" government propaganda.

The significance for the Naqvi case: DHS's combative "HERE ARE THE RECEIPTS" X post is consistent with institutional strategy, not a departure from it. This means the DHS response — while likely accurate in this case based on corroborating evidence — should be evaluated as an adversarial party's communication, not a neutral official record.

### C2. Pattern of Disputed Immigration Claims in 2026

The broader 2026 immigration enforcement information environment features disputed claims flowing from both directions:

**Government misrepresentation documented:**
- NPR's February 2026 investigation found in 1-in-7 cases examined, DHS social media posts highlighted deportees whose most recent convictions were at least 20 years old; in 7 cases, the only criminal history was DUI or disorderly conduct. [14]
- DHS published a rebuttal to New York Times reporting on ICE medical care, calling the allegations "FALSE." [28]
- The Center for Migration Studies maintains a "Correcting the Record" database tracking false or misleading statements on immigration. [29]

**Claimant misrepresentation documented:**
- Snopes investigated a viral post claiming ICE killed 9 people in 2026, finding key misattributions: one death was caused by Border Patrol (CBP), not ICE; the distinction between "shot by ICE" and "died in ICE custody" was systematically blurred. [30]
- Snopes separately compiled "20 claims about videos and images related to ICE" requiring investigation in 2026. [30]

The Naqvi case sits in this environment: a story that confirmed fears held by immigration advocates and was amplified by a politically motivated official before basic verification. The pattern — rapid spread, initial media credulity, belated official denial — has recurred multiple times in 2026.

### C3. Detention Scale as Context for Advocate Credulity

The American Immigration Council's January 2026 report documented that immigration detention had reached "the highest level in U.S. history," with nearly 66,000 people detained by late 2025 — a 75% increase from the 40,000 held when Trump took office in January 2025. The report found a 2,450% increase in people with no criminal record being detained. [31] This unprecedented detention scale explains why advocates and media were primed to believe claims of U.S. citizens being swept up: such incidents, while rare, were occurring. The Wikipedia article tracking "Deportations of U.S. citizens in the second Trump administration" confirms documented, verified cases of U.S. citizens detained or deported in error. [32]

The Naqvi case is not one of those documented real cases — but the real cases created the environment in which the fabricated claim could spread.

### C4. Civil Liberties Organizations' Positions

The Brennan Center for Justice documented that DHS's expanded social media vetting policies in 2026 "threaten free speech." [33] ICE's authority to access social media surveillance was expanded in January 2026 under Executive Order 14161. The ACLU and immigration legal organizations noted that the expanded detention system operated with reduced oversight: ICE was using 104 more facilities by late 2025, a 91% increase, with fewer accountability mechanisms. [31]

None of these civil liberties organizations have publicly vouched for Naqvi's specific claims. The ACLU of Illinois and Morrison's office were quoted in early coverage but have not issued statements defending Naqvi's account after the institutional denials emerged.
