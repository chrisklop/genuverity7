## Executive Summary

On March 23, 2026, Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents arrested six roofing workers at a property on Bayly Road in Cambridge, Maryland. A co-worker named Bryan Polanco, who held permanent U.S. residency and was not detained, livestreamed approximately 30 minutes of the encounter on Facebook from the roof of the property [1]. In the livestream and in subsequent statements to Spanish-language media, Polanco accused the female homeowner of having called ICE on his colleagues to avoid paying them roughly $10,000 for a three-day roofing job [2]. The claim spread explosively across X, TikTok, and Facebook, with posts framing the incident as a calculated act of wage theft weaponized through immigration enforcement. The woman was identified by name as Karen Trevino, and her personal information circulated widely online [3].

ICE directly rebutted the central allegation. In a statement issued around March 26, the agency said: "This was a targeted enforcement operation, not a tip from a caller" [4]. The agency confirmed that several of the six arrested individuals carried final orders of removal — effectively outstanding deportation orders constituting a felony under U.S. immigration law — and that one had a prior conviction for illegal reentry [4]. ICE also noted the workers "refused to comply with lawful orders, taunted officers and attempted to flee" [5]. Karen Trevino denied any involvement, telling The Baltimore Sun: "They are accusing me of calling ICE when I didn't," and reported receiving over 1,342 unread threatening messages, including one that stated "don't sleep at home peacefully" [3]. Cambridge city officials stated they had no prior knowledge of the ICE operation, further undermining the spontaneous-tip narrative [1].

The case illustrates a recurring dynamic in the social media era: a compelling, morally legible narrative — a woman using federal enforcement to cheat workers out of wages — travels faster than official corrections. A secondary fake video falsely depicting Trevino's arrest compounded the harm [6]. No charges have been filed against Trevino or her father (the property owner) as of April 3, 2026, and Maryland court records show no arrests or charges [7]. The episode highlights how viral misidentification can cause real-world danger to individuals while also distorting public understanding of how ICE enforcement operations actually work.

## Forensic Analysis

**Origin of the video:** Bryan Polanco, a Dominican national with permanent U.S. residency working alongside the detained crew for Allied Remodeling, filmed and livestreamed the arrest on Facebook from atop the roof. He is visible in the footage calling out to the detained workers and verbally accusing the homeowner of tipping off agents [2]. The original livestream, approximately 30 minutes long, was later made unavailable but had already been clipped and reshared across platforms.

**How the narrative spread:** Clipped versions of the livestream circulated on X (formerly Twitter), TikTok, and Facebook beginning March 23. The account @LongTimeHistory was among those on X that framed the clip with the allegation that Trevino had called ICE to avoid a $10,000 bill [3]. Inflammatory secondary coverage — including headlines using terms like "Maryland Karen" and "Maryland Karen Faces Felony Shadow" — amplified the story with loaded framing that assumed guilt [8]. A separate fake video was later edited together from unrelated clips to falsely suggest Trevino had been arrested; Threads users flagged it as a fabrication [6].

**Why the narrative took hold:** The story contained a powerful emotional logic — an employer allegedly using the federal immigration apparatus as a wage-theft weapon — that resonated with anxieties about both immigration enforcement and worker exploitation. The visual drama of agents arriving while workers were still on the roof made it seem causally connected to a tip. Polanco's firsthand accusation, delivered live, carried immediate credibility before any official account emerged.

**What ICE actually said:** ICE confirmed six arrests on March 23 at the Bayly Road property in Cambridge, Maryland. The agency described the operation as "targeted" and explicitly stated it was "not a tip from a caller" [4]. The arrested individuals had pre-existing immigration violations — several with final orders of removal and one with a prior illegal reentry conviction — indicating the operation was intelligence-driven, not reactive to a homeowner call [5].

**The workers and the job:** The roofing work was being performed by Allied Remodeling under a recently obtained permit [1]. Cambridge police noted that only about 15% of the roof had actually been removed at the time of the arrest, contradicting social media claims that the job was 90% complete or fully finished [1]. This undermines the "waited until work was done, then called" version of events.

**Trevino's situation:** Karen Trevino told The Baltimore Sun she did not own the property (her father did), was not present at the time, and did not call ICE [3]. She reported receiving a flood of harassment — over 1,342 unread threatening messages — including explicit threats to her personal safety [3]. No charges have been filed against her or her father as of April 3, 2026 [7].

### Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

**The Bryan Polanco Livestream — What It Showed and What It Didn't**

The approximately 30-minute Facebook livestream was recorded by Bryan Polanco from the roof of the Bayly Road property while ICE agents detained his co-workers on the ground below [2]. Polanco, identified by Univision as a Dominican national with permanent U.S. residency, was not detained — his legal status protected him from the enforcement action that took the other six men into custody [7]. The visual logic of the livestream was powerful: a worker filming from above while federal agents surrounded his colleagues below, with Polanco verbally pointing at the homeowner and naming her as the reason for the arrest. That firsthand accusatory framing, delivered live as events unfolded, gave the clip an immediacy and apparent credibility that official statements issued days later could not match in emotional impact.

The livestream's reach was amplified when it was clipped into shorter segments and reshared. The original full-length video was subsequently deleted or made unavailable on Polanco's Facebook account, but the clips had already been ingested by X, TikTok, and YouTube. Univision's reporting on Polanco's account played a key role in spreading the narrative to Spanish-language audiences and was cited by multiple English-language fact-checkers as the primary source for identifying Polanco by name [7].

**The Roofing Job Completion Contradiction**

One of the most forensically significant facts in this case is the roof completion percentage. The social media narrative required a specific sequence: the homeowner waited until the work was substantially finished — described variously as 90% complete or "nearly done" — before calling ICE to avoid payment. Cambridge Police investigators, responding to the scene on Bayly Road, found that only approximately 15% of the old roof had been removed at the time of the ICE operation [1][12][13]. This directly contradicts the narrative's essential premise. A job that is 15% complete cannot be the basis for a "work completed, now avoid paying" scheme. Allied Remodeling held the roofing permit [1], and the permit had been issued the day before police publicly responded to the viral claims [13] — suggesting the job had only recently begun.

**The Secondary Fake Arrest Video**

After the initial viral narrative established that Trevino should face criminal charges, a second wave of misinformation emerged: a fabricated video that appeared to show Trevino herself being arrested. Users on Threads identified and flagged the video as fake, with @lisabornfree posting: "This is fake news, they use a clip from the original video and add it to another video to make it look like Karen Trevino got arrested" [6]. A dedicated fact-check piece confirmed the video does not show Trevino's arrest and was assembled from spliced, unrelated footage [15]. The fake arrest video represents a qualitative escalation in the harassment campaign — moving from social media accusation to fabricated evidence of criminal consequence, and likely contributing to the most threatening messages Trevino received.

**The 1,342+ Threatening Messages**

Karen Trevino documented her harassment in concrete terms. She showed The Baltimore Sun her phone, which displayed 1,342 unread messages [3]. The threats ranged from vague menace to specific physical danger: one message read, "don't sleep at home peacefully after what you did … we gonna find out how many ppl live in yo house no worries" [1][3]. Cambridge Police responded by increasing patrols around her residence [13]. Cambridge Police also expressed concern that "the tenant and others at the residence could be targeted" as a direct result of the viral post [12]. Trevino summarized her situation to The Star Democrat: "I don't own the property. I wasn't there ... I am being harassed and threatened" [1]. No public figure or major platform took meaningful action to suppress the false identification before the harassment reached this scale.

**Legal Status of the Six Arrested Workers**

ICE's public statement provided specifics on the detained individuals that are essential to understanding why this was a pre-planned targeted operation rather than a reactive tip response. The agency stated: "Several have final orders of removal — a felony — and one has been previously convicted of illegal reentry" [4][5]. Final orders of removal are not issued overnight; they represent the conclusion of administrative and often court proceedings that can take years. The presence of multiple individuals with final removal orders at the same worksite strongly supports ICE's characterization of the operation as intelligence-driven. The workers' refusal to comply and attempt to flee during the arrest — noted in ICE's statement — is consistent with individuals who had reason to fear detection, not workers caught by surprise through a neighbor's phone call [5].

**The Felony Charge Speculation**

A separate strand of misinformation involved the claim that Trevino could or would face felony charges — variously framed as obstruction of justice, fraud, or harboring undocumented workers. This theory was amplified by a legal expert quoted (but not named with specific credentials) in Yahoo News UK and syndicated broadly [18]. The Migrant Insider piece ran under the headline "Maryland Karen Faces Felony Shadow After ICE Abducts Six Roofers Just Before $10,000 Payday," treating the felony possibility as near-certain [8]. Snopes found no charges in Maryland court records as of April 2, 2026 [7]. As of April 3, 2026, no charges have been filed [7]. The gap between the speculative legal theory and the actual evidentiary record illustrates how credentialed-sounding commentary can accelerate false narratives without bearing accountability for accuracy.

### Contemporary Context (Pass C)

**Maryland's ICE Enforcement Climate in Early 2026**

The Cambridge incident did not occur in a policy vacuum. In February 2026, Maryland Governor Wes Moore signed legislation banning 287(g) agreements — the legal mechanism by which local law enforcement could formally partner with ICE [16]. This state-level policy change had a direct bearing on this case: it explains why Cambridge Police and Dorchester County officials had no prior knowledge of the operation and did not participate in it [1][14]. Under Maryland law as of March 2026, local police cannot coordinate with ICE for immigration enforcement, meaning the tip-from-a-homeowner-to-local-police version of events was structurally impossible in addition to being factually denied.

ICE nonetheless continued to operate independently on the Eastern Shore. The agency's own press releases document prior "targeted enforcement" operations across multiple Eastern Shore locations — Eden, Easton, Fruitland, Ocean City, Ocean Pines, and Salisbury [17] — establishing a pre-existing operational pattern in the region. ICE made 1,736 arrests in Maryland in 2025, with 1,683 occurring after January 20, 2026 [search result: WUSA9 / Maryland Matters]. This enforcement intensity created a charged atmosphere in which any visible ICE operation attracted immediate, intense public attention.

**The Pattern of Viral Immigration Enforcement Misidentification**

The Cambridge case fits a documented 2026 pattern of viral misidentification in the context of immigration enforcement. The closest structural parallel: following the death of Renee Nicole Good at the hands of an ICE agent, social media users attempted to identify the masked agent using AI tools and the platform's own Grok chatbot. The process produced misidentifications that went viral — one wrongly identified individual, Steven Grove, a gun store owner in Springfield, Missouri, received death threats [Slate, January 2026]. The Cambridge case differs in that the misidentified subject (Trevino) was a bystander accused of a specific act rather than someone confused with an enforcement officer, but the structural dynamic is identical: a compelling post-incident narrative, a named or visible individual, and a harassment campaign that outpaced any correction.

Cambridge Police's decision not to share the original viral post due to "lack of verifiable information" [12] represents a model of institutional restraint that was not replicated by the dozens of media outlets that ran with the worker's unverified accusation. The pattern suggests that the media ecosystem around immigration enforcement stories in 2026 is structurally biased toward rapid amplification of accusatory narratives and structurally slow at issuing corrections.

**Chilling Effects and Witness Deterrence**

The harassment campaign against Trevino has implications beyond her individual case. The American Immigration Council has documented "the chilling effect of Trump's indiscriminate immigration arrests and propaganda," but the Cambridge case illustrates that chilling effects can run in both directions: bystanders and property owners who happen to be present when ICE conducts an enforcement operation now face potential viral misidentification and mass harassment regardless of their actual involvement. Cambridge Police explicitly stated they were concerned Trevino and "others at the residence could be targeted" — treating the online harassment as a physical safety threat requiring increased police presence [12].

A separate documented pattern in 2026 involves federal agents intimidating immigration observers: in one reported incident in Portland, Maine, agents scanned an observer's face and took her license plate, with the agent stating "now you're considered a domestic terrorist" [NPR, February 2026]. The Cambridge case operates on the inverse axis — civilians facing harassment from other civilians for perceived collaboration with enforcement — but both dynamics produce the same outcome: reduced willingness to be visibly present near immigration enforcement operations.

**Why This Case Matters for Information Integrity**

The Cambridge ICE roofing case illustrates several compounding failures in the 2026 information environment:

1. A live video delivered a first-person accusation that felt more credible than any subsequent official denial, regardless of the official denial's accuracy.
2. National outlets (Newsweek, Fast Company, Yahoo News UK) published the accusation before the ICE denial was available, creating an asymmetric record that Google searches still surface weeks later [9][18].
3. The secondary fake arrest video represented manufactured evidence designed to confirm the narrative after the initial accusation spread — a second-order fabrication that is harder to debunk because it requires viewers to be aware of both the original video and the splice.
4. The "felony charges possible" legal commentary, while technically framed as speculation, was presented in headlines and on social media as imminent fact, adding an additional false data point that compounded the harassment [8][18].
5. No major platform or media outlet issued a prominent correction or retraction with the same reach as the original claims as of April 3, 2026.
