## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

When Operation Epic Fury launched on February 28, 2026, the information war started faster than any boots hit the ground. Within hours, social media platforms were flooded with fabricated, recycled, and AI-generated footage falsely depicting the conflict — a pattern now familiar from Ukraine and Gaza, but dramatically amplified by the accessibility of generative AI tools. BBC Verify documented three top AI-generated videos accumulating over 100 million combined views in under two weeks [1]. NewsGuard found posts garnering more than 21.9 million views that falsely claimed to show Iran gaining military advantage over Israel and the United States [2]. One pro-Iranian account grew its X follower count from 700,000 to 1.4 million — an 85% surge — in under a week by farming engagement from conflict misinformation [3].

The fake content falls into four distinct categories: recycled footage from unrelated events (a 2015 Tianjin chemical explosion, a 2015 Sharjah apartment fire, a 2020 Algerian football celebration), video game clips from War Thunder and Arma 3 that accumulated over 27 million views combined, custom AI-generated videos using tools like Google Veo and Midjourney, and misattributed genuine footage from earlier conflicts. A War Thunder clip depicting a WWII-era USS Tennessee sinking a Nazi German aircraft was shared by Texas Governor Greg Abbott as real combat footage before deletion [4]. Arma 3 footage purporting to show a US aircraft carrier in flames racked up over 20 million views across X posts [5]. Iran's own state broadcaster IRIB aired footage unrelated to the current war, while Tehran Times and PressTV amplified misleading content systematically [3]. Separately, a Citizen Lab-documented Israeli-backed network called "PRISONBREAK" deployed AI-generated deepfakes to push regime-change propaganda targeting Iranian audiences [3].

The structural conditions enabling this flood are novel: Iran's near-total internet blackout (Cloudflare reported a 98% traffic drop on Feb 28) created an information vacuum that bad actors from every direction rushed to fill [6]. Unlike previous conflicts where citizen journalists provided ground truth, the near-absence of verified footage from inside Iran meant fabrications faced almost no competition from reality. Meanwhile, X's creator revenue-sharing program financially incentivized premium accounts to post the most sensational — and frequently most fabricated — content, with Grok, X's built-in AI assistant, compounding the damage by falsely confirming at least one fake Tel Aviv video as authentic and citing Reuters, CNN, and Euronews as supposed sources [7].

## FORENSIC ANALYSIS: CATALOG OF SPECIFIC FAKE VIDEOS

### FAKE 1: War Thunder WWII Clip as "US Ship Shoots Down Iranian Jet"
**Date First Circulated:** March 1, 2026
**Platform(s):** X (Twitter)
**Views:** 7.3 million
**Claim:** A US warship shot down an Iranian fighter jet in the Gulf

**Reality:** The clip is from War Thunder, a military simulation video game. The "warship" is the USS Tennessee, a US dreadnought built in 1910s, which served in WWII and was decommissioned in 1947. The "Iranian fighter jet" is a Messerschmitt Me 163B-1a Komet — a Nazi German aircraft from World War II. No Iranian plane attack on a US ship matching this clip occurred during the conflict.

**High-profile amplification:** Texas Governor Greg Abbott shared the clip captioned "Bye bye" before deleting the post.

**Confirmation:** Konstantin Govorun, head of PR at developer Gaijin Entertainment, confirmed to AFP: "Yes, this looks like War Thunder footage." [4]

**Debunk method:** Military vehicle identification by open-source researchers; developer confirmation.

### FAKE 3: Algeria Football Celebration as "Iranian Missiles Striking Tel Aviv"
**Date First Circulated:** March 2026 (earliest verified origin: August 2020)
**Platform(s):** X, TikTok
**Views:** 4+ million
**Claim:** Iranian missiles striking the center of Tel Aviv, with red-lit sky and fires across buildings

**Reality:** Football fans launching fireworks and flares in Algeria's capital Algiers after CR Belouizdad won the Algerian championship in 2020. **Geolocated to:** Al Mokrani Square, Algiers, Algeria.

**Debunk method:** Google reverse image search traced it to Berbere Television X post from August 7, 2020. Reuters corroborated red flares as characteristic CR Belouizdad supporter celebrations. [9]

## DEBUNKING METHODOLOGY REFERENCE

Techniques documented across multiple fact-checking organizations:

1. **Reverse image search** — Screenshot video frame, run through Google Images or TinEye; check if the image predates February 2026
2. **Google Lens / InVID-WeVerify** — Extract keyframes from video for batch image search
3. **Google SynthID** — AI-generated image/video metadata detection tool
4. **Visual artifact analysis** — Duplicated building elements, warped limbs, objects with wrong number of wheels/headlights, floating objects, unnatural smoke colors
5. **Audio analysis** — Suspiciously quiet background, unintelligible voices, absence of expected ambient sounds (sirens, crowd noise)
6. **Satellite cross-reference** — Compare claimed location to satellite imagery from commercial providers
7. **Military hardware identification** — Match depicted vehicles/aircraft to known hardware databases (used to identify USS Tennessee, Messerschmitt Me 163B)
8. **Google Veo clip length tells** — Google's Veo AI generates clips up to 8 seconds; suspicious if videos consist entirely of such short clips stitched together

## DEPTH EXPANSION — PASS C: Contemporary Context & Political Dimensions

### C1. Why This Is Happening NOW — The Structural Conditions

The Iran war disinformation surge is not simply "more of the same" from Ukraine and Gaza. Three conditions make it qualitatively different:

**1. The information vacuum is total, not partial.**
Iran's 98% internet traffic collapse (Cloudflare, Feb 28) is historically rare. In Ukraine, citizen journalists provided continuous ground-truth footage from Day 1. In Gaza, despite restrictions, journalists and aid workers maintained some documentation pipeline. In Iran, the near-complete blackout means fabrications face **essentially no competition from verified reality**. There is no Ukrainian Telegram channel equivalent providing authentic Iranian ground footage.

**2. Generative AI has crossed a usability threshold.**
The tools used for fakes in this conflict — Google Veo, Midjourney, Kling, RunwayML — are **commercially available, no technical expertise required**, and produce outputs convincing enough that even government officials (Abbott) and an AI chatbot (Grok) cannot immediately identify them as fake.

**3. Financial incentives now run in one direction.**
X's creator economy, TikTok's engagement monetization, and YouTube's ad revenue all **pay creators more for fake conflict content than for accurate reporting**. Accuracy requires investment (research, verification, time); fabrication is instant. The 550 TikTok accounts / 5,800 videos / 89M views documented by Maldita represent a functioning disinformation business model that did not exist at scale in 2022.

### C4. Russia's Double-Layered Disinformation Strategy

Russia's Operation Overload/Matryoshka operation in the Iran conflict demonstrated a novel two-layer strategy:

**Layer 1 (direct):** Fake Israeli intelligence warnings telling diaspora Jews in Germany and the US to avoid public spaces — designed to create fear and erode trust in Israeli institutions

**Layer 2 (parasitic):** Using Iran's own false victory claims as raw material for *separate* anti-Ukraine content — specifically circulating Iranian claims that "Iranian missiles destroyed Ukrainian military bases in Dubai," linking two unrelated conflicts to reinforce Russia's existing Ukraine narrative

This parasitic approach — using one conflict's disinformation as amplification fuel for a separate geopolitical goal — represents a documented evolution in multi-conflict information operations. It was not observed at this scale in Ukraine 2022 or Israel-Hamas 2023.
