## Executive Summary

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost on September 14, 1955, in Chicago, elected as the 267th pope and the first American pontiff on May 8, 2025 — became the most misinformation-targeted religious figure in the digital age within weeks of his election. His profile was uniquely exploitable: as both the first American pope and a fluent English speaker, his words carried direct, unfiltered resonance in the American media ecosystem at a moment of explosive tension with the Trump administration. Within 12 months of his election, Snopes documented at least 18 distinct rumors, and researchers counted dozens of AI-generated deepfake channels on YouTube and TikTok accumulating tens of millions of views.[1][2][3] The disinformation has operated on three parallel tracks: fabricated text quotes (primarily targeting his perceived opposition to Trump), AI-synthesized video deepfakes (weaponizing his papal authority for foreign political agendas), and false health claims (attempting to destabilize confidence in his leadership).[4][5][6]

The campaign's architecture reveals a convergence of ideologically opposed actors with the same technological toolkit. On one side, Vietnam-based content farms manufacture Trump-baiting fake papal quotes to generate advertising revenue from American and European audiences.[7] On another, pro-junta actors in West Africa deploy 36-minute deepfake videos of Leo endorsing military rulers.[8] In between, domestic U.S. actors — from progressive social media users to MAGA-aligned accounts — have each circulated fabricated quotes that confirm their preferred papal narrative. The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication has described the situation as an "exponential proliferation" of fake content, acknowledging that given the sheer volume, "it is impossible to publicly refute each and every one of them."[9] The scale and sophistication of this campaign represent a qualitative leap from previous papal disinformation episodes and a template for future AI-driven attacks on religious authority.

Pope Leo XIV himself has made combating AI disinformation a central pillar of his papacy. He chose the name Leo partly as a response to what he sees as a new industrial revolution driven by artificial intelligence, echoing Pope Leo XIII's defense of workers against industrial exploitation in *Rerum Novarum*.[10] His first World Communications Day theme — "Preserving Human Voices and Faces" — was a direct response to deepfake technology. In remarks to media executives, he warned that "people must not die because of fake news" and urged journalists to be "the barrier to protect the world from disinformation."[11] The bitter irony is that the pope most focused on combating AI-generated falsehoods has himself become their most prolific victim.

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The disinformation campaign targeting Pope Leo XIV began within hours of his election. The earliest confirmed fabricated quote appeared on May 8, 2025 — the very day of his election — when a Threads user with the handle @atheistadamx posted a meme claiming the new pope had embraced the concept of being "woke." The user later admitted the quote was fabricated, writing: "The fact that so many of you wanted this to be true shows how far Catholicism is from real progress. Yes, I made up these words, with the help of my AI friend." Within days, a separate fake quote — "You cannot follow both Christ and the cruelty of kings. A leader who mocks the weak, exalts himself, and preys on the innocent is not sent by God" — began circulating on Facebook and Instagram. Snopes confirmed the earliest posting of this meme was May 14, 2025, with no evidence the pope ever said it.

The first major deepfake video emerged on May 23, 2025. A 36-minute AI-generated video was uploaded to YouTube by a channel called "Pan African Dreams," falsely depicting Pope Leo XIV delivering a public address to Burkina Faso's military leader Ibrahim Traoré. The video used a "morphing" technique — transforming real footage of Leo to synchronize his lip movements with an AI-generated English voiceover featuring an accent distinctly not his own. The AI Incident Database (Incident #1092) classified this as part of a broader pro-junta information ecosystem, noting it emerged from "unknown actors" connected to Traoré's support network.

### Propagation

By March 2026, the campaign had evolved into a semi-industrial process. A Vietnam-based operation — referred to by Lead Stories as "Viet spam" — began manufacturing Pope Leo quotes targeting American political figures at scale. The operation's methods included creating Facebook pages with religious-sounding names (e.g., "Guided by Grace"), using AI tools to generate fabricated narratives, and publishing them on external blog pages that employed anti-crawling techniques (including substituting Latin letters with Cyrillic characters to evade detection). Facebook posts linked to these external blogs in comments, generating advertising revenue when users clicked through. GPTZero analysis of the blog posts confirmed "with high certainty" the content was AI-generated.

On TikTok, a Spanish-language deepfake video claiming to show Leo preaching about "supportive women" received 9.6 million views. Another AI-labeled sermon video accumulated 32.9 million views. The platform's required disclosure — a small label at the screen's bottom — proved largely ineffective. On YouTube, fake channels proliferated faster than platforms could remove them, with the Vatican's communications team noting that new accounts and videos appeared "as quickly as others are removed."

### Why It Spread

Several structural factors made Pope Leo XIV uniquely vulnerable:

1. **The Language Factor**: Previous popes communicated through translation layers that softened or ambiguated their statements for American audiences. Leo's native-level English removed this buffer entirely, making him a direct participant in American political discourse in a way no previous pope had been.

2. **Political Demand**: The escalating feud between Leo and the Trump administration created intense audience demand for dramatic papal statements. Trump accused Leo of being "WEAK on Crime, and terrible for Foreign Policy" and falsely claimed Leo endorsed Iran having nuclear weapons. This genuine conflict created fertile ground for fabricated escalations.

3. **Theological Authority as Exploitable Asset**: A papal statement carries unique moral weight that makes it a high-value target for bad actors. Word on Fire's theological analysis described AI-generated papal speech as "fabricating a false Magisterium — a shadow papacy of machines."

4. **Platform Economics**: The combination of high engagement potential (papal controversy drives clicks) and lax enforcement created favorable conditions for the Vietnam-based ad-revenue farms.

### Evidence of AI Generation

Multiple technical markers identified the fake content:
- GPTZero confirmed AI authorship "with high certainty" on blog posts containing fake Leo quotes
- Anti-crawling techniques (Cyrillic character substitution) in fake blog posts
- AI voice accents inconsistent with Leo's Chicago-inflected English in deepfake videos
- "Morphing" lip-sync technology applied to authentic papal footage
- Nearly identical post structures across dozens of Facebook accounts simultaneously

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### The Burkina Faso Deepfake: Technical Forensics

The 36-minute deepfake video depicting Pope Leo XIV addressing Ibrahim Traoré of Burkina Faso represents the most technically sophisticated disinformation attack in this campaign. Based on Vatican News and Full Fact's independent investigations:

**Source footage:** The video repurposed authentic footage from Pope Leo XIV's May 12, 2025 audience with journalists — one of his first public appearances as pope. The footage was then subjected to digital "morphing" to synchronize his lip movements with an entirely AI-generated English voiceover.

**The fabricated content:** In the AI-generated dialogue, Leo is made to say: *"I have read your words not once, but many times, and each reading has been deeper than the last, because in your voice I have heard not only the anger of a president, but the righteous cry of a continent long wounded by the twin blades of abandonment and exploitation."* This language — romanticizing a military coup leader who seized power in 2022 — serves a clear geopolitical agenda.[19][8]

**Technical forensic indicators** (identified by Dr. Dominic Lees, University of Reading):[19]
1. **Music masking**: An unlikely musical soundtrack was layered over the voiceover, a known technique for concealing the telltale artifacts of low-quality voice cloning
2. **Absent room ambience**: Authentic papal recordings have characteristic spatial audio; the deepfake audio lacks natural environmental sound
3. **Pronunciation inconsistency**: The AI voice mispronounced "Traoré" differently in consecutive sentences — an error impossible in human speech but characteristic of voice synthesis models
4. **Blurred mouth rendering**: The lip-sync AI "finds it very difficult to generate a natural look in the teeth," producing a blurred, smeared appearance around the mouth area

**Platform trajectory:** The video was uploaded May 17, 2025 to the channel "Pan African Dreams" with a buried disclaimer stating "This video is a work of fiction inspired by the life of Ibrahim Traoré." Despite the Vatican publicly confirming the video was fake, it surpassed 1 million views and remained accessible on YouTube.[8][19] The AI Incident Database classified the incident (#1092) as part of a broader pro-junta information ecosystem connected to Traoré's support network.[8]

**Geopolitical context:** The deepfake belongs to a documented pattern. HumAngle media has reported that deepfakes circulating in Africa promoting coup leaders frequently carry "European fingerprints," suggesting the production infrastructure for these videos may lie outside the African continent itself, complicating attribution.

### The TikTok 32.9 Million View Incident: Scale and Labeling Failure

The TikTok AI-labeled sermon video that accumulated 32.9 million views represents the single-largest audience reached by any piece of fake Pope Leo XIV content. Key details from AFP's investigation, reported by France 24 and The Journal:[2][3]

- The video carried the AI disclosure label required by TikTok's policy, but the label was placed at the bottom of the screen in a format that proved "largely ineffective" at preventing viewer deception
- For context, no video on Pope Leo XIV's official Instagram page has exceeded 6 million views — meaning this single piece of fake content reached more than five times the audience of any authentic papal video
- A separate Spanish-language deepfake on TikTok — falsely depicting Leo preaching about "supportive women" — reached 9.6 million views
- TikTok's own spokesperson acknowledged the platform "proactively removes policy-violating content and uses verified badges to signal authentic accounts," but critics noted the platform's AI labeling regime is self-reported by creators rather than algorithmically detected

The 32.9 million figure illustrates a structural failure: a platform's own disclosure mechanism, designed to prevent viewer deception, was present on the video, yet the content still misled viewers at massive scale. This suggests that label placement, size, and timing of disclosure — not merely the requirement to disclose — are critical policy variables.

### The AFP Investigation: YouTube Channel Takedowns

An AFP investigation — one of the most systematic independent audits of the pope deepfake problem — documented the scope of YouTube's response:[3]

- AFP identified **26 channels** posting predominantly AI-generated Pope Leo XIV content
- After AFP presented these channels to YouTube, the platform terminated **16** for violating policies against spam, deceptive practices, and scams
- An additional **six** channels were removed from YouTube's Partner Program (the monetization system), stripping creators of ad revenue while leaving content accessible
- A YouTube spokesperson stated the company would apply "more prominent labels" to videos on channels that were flagged but not found to violate platform guidelines

The AFP investigation also documented fake clips depicting Leo criticizing U.S. Vice President JD Vance and Peru's President Dina Boluarte — demonstrating that the deepfake operation targeted multiple political figures beyond the Trump-Leo feud, using the pope's authority as a versatile weapon against any politically useful target.

### The Vietnam Spam Operation: Revenue Architecture

The Vietnam-based operation producing fake Pope Leo quotes follows a documented commercial model reverse-engineered by Lead Stories fact-checkers:[4][5]

**Infrastructure:**
- Facebook pages with religious-sounding names ("Guided by Grace") serve as distribution hubs
- Posts on these pages link to external blogs — the actual revenue-generating destination
- Blog content is AI-generated (confirmed "with high certainty" by GPTZero analysis)
- Anti-crawling obfuscation employs **Cyrillic character substitution** (replacing visually identical Latin letters with Cyrillic equivalents) to evade automated AI detection tools
- Image headers use generative AI — Sightengine analysis confirmed they were "almost certainly created with generative technology"
- Facebook account managers have Vietnamese phone numbers (+84 country code)

**Revenue mechanism:** Each click from Facebook to the external blog generates advertising revenue. The fake papal quote is bait; the blog is the trap. The operation is profitable because papal controversy drives high-volume clicks from emotionally engaged American and European audiences.

**Scale of template reuse:** Lead Stories documented that in January 2026, at least 17 celebrities and public figures had the same fabricated Trump-critical statement attributed to them using identical post structures — the pope being one of the most lucrative "voices" to borrow, but not the only one.

### The Church's AI Policy Architecture

The Catholic Church's response to AI disinformation has developed on three parallel tracks:[16][17][21][22]

**1. Doctrinal framework — "Antiqua et Nova" (January 2025):** This roughly 30-page Vatican document, issued in the final months of Francis's pontificate, established the theological foundation: AI systems "operate largely through pattern recognition and lack the creative, spiritual and moral dimensions of human thought." It explicitly warned that AI "presents a serious risk of generating manipulated content and false information, which can easily mislead people due to its resemblance to the truth." Leo XIV inherited and expanded this framework.

**2. Practical governance — Vatican AI Framework:** The Vatican issued one of the world's first state-level AI governance frameworks, requiring systems to be ethical, transparent, and human-centered, explicitly prohibiting AI uses that "manipulate people, discriminate or threaten security." This positions the Holy See alongside national governments as a regulatory actor in global AI governance.[22]

**3. Communications response — Dicastery for Communication protocols:** Under Prefect Dr. Paolo Ruffini, the Dicastery has instituted: (a) a fake-content reporting system receiving and processing "dozens of reports every day"; (b) a public authentication protocol directing users to vatican.va as the sole authoritative source for all papal statements; (c) active platform reporting resulting in hundreds of account flaggings; and (d) Leo's January 24, 2026 World Communications Day message calling for mandatory AI content labeling, protection of "authorship, ownership and copyright," and global rollout of Media and Artificial Intelligence Literacy (MAIL) education.[16][17][18]

**Key institutional voice — Dr. Paolo Ruffini:** The Prefect of the Dicastery for Communication has been the Vatican's operational face in the fight against AI deepfakes. In December 2025 remarks in Hong Kong, he stated that true wisdom "cannot be artificial" and that current AI systems constitute "calculation" or "a statistical gamble" rather than genuine intelligence. He identified the locus of responsibility not in the technology itself but in "those who own them, those who programme them and those who use them."[18]

### Broader Pattern: Religious Authority as High-Value Disinformation Target

Pope Leo XIV is not the only major religious figure targeted in AI-enabled disinformation campaigns, though he is the most heavily targeted. A convergent pattern has emerged across multiple traditions:[20]

**The Dalai Lama — China's information warfare:** In early 2026, a viral claim circulated that the Dalai Lama's name appeared 69–169 times in Jeffrey Epstein court documents. The Central Tibetan Administration publicly attributed the campaign to Chinese state actors, noting that "the first sources of those smear campaigns were coming from China." Meta uncovered and removed an associated covert influence operation comprising 18 fake Facebook accounts, two pages, four groups, and five Instagram accounts operating as part of this network. The Digital Forensic Research Lab (DFRLab) documented a China-linked disinformation campaign targeting Tibetan elections, confirming state-level investment in undermining Tibetan religious and political leadership.[Search results: Phayul, The Diplomat, DFRLab]

**Pattern across Catholic bishops:** OSV News documented multiple fabricated videos depicting Catholic bishops "confronting ICE agents" — with identical scripts appearing "word for word" across numerous videos featuring different simulated clergy and congregations. Thousands of viewers believed these videos authentic despite their mass-produced, templated nature.[20]

**The structural logic:** Steven Umbrello of the Institute for Ethics and Emerging Technologies identified why religious figures are high-priority targets: *"Deepfakes aim straight at that credibility. They can make it appear that a pastor endorsed something he never endorsed."*[20] Religious authority is uniquely exploitable because: (1) it is built on trust rather than institutional verification; (2) statements attributed to religious leaders carry moral weight that amplifies their viral potential; (3) congregants worldwide often lack access to primary sources; and (4) the global reach of major religious figures means a single fake can be weaponized simultaneously in multiple countries and political contexts.

**The scale acceleration:** Resemble AI recorded 2,031 verified deepfake incidents in Q3 2025 alone. The broader deepfake landscape has grown from approximately 500,000 video and voice deepfakes shared globally in 2023 to an estimated 8 million by 2025 — a 16x increase in two years — providing the technical and commercial infrastructure within which religious figure deepfakes operate.
