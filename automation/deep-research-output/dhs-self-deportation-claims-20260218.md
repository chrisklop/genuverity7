## Executive Summary

The Department of Homeland Security claimed that between 1.6 million and 2.2 million undocumented immigrants "voluntarily self-deported" from the United States between January and December 2025.[1][2][3] DHS Secretary Kristi Noem announced on August 14, 2025: "According to the Bureau of Labor and Statistics, we have 1.6 million illegal aliens that have gone home voluntarily."[4] This figure was later revised to 1.9 million in September and 2.2 million by December 2025.[5]

However, independent immigration experts, fact-checkers, and research institutions have found these claims to be vastly overstated and fundamentally unverifiable.[6][7][8] The Center for Migration Studies called the statement "a self-serving fantasy," describing it as based on a flawed interpretation of population data and estimating that a more realistic number was around 1/10th of the claimed self-deportations.[9] The Brookings Institution estimated around 310,000 deported and only 210,000 to 405,000 voluntary departures due to enforcement activity — far below DHS's claims.[10]

The core methodological problem: DHS relied on an analysis by the Center for Immigration Studies (CIS) of the Census Bureau's Current Population Survey (CPS), which has a margin of error of ±830,000 people for the foreign-born population — nearly as large as the claimed decline itself.[11] The Census Bureau explicitly warns against using the CPS to estimate changes in immigrant populations, stating that apparent declines "can also reflect survey non-response, coverage error or the impact of population controls."[12] Multiple experts concluded that the reported drop primarily reflects immigrants' fear-driven reluctance to respond to government surveys during an intense enforcement campaign, not actual departures from the United States.[13][14][15]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin of the Claim

**Timeline of Official Claims:**

- **August 12, 2025:** Center for Immigration Studies (CIS) researcher Steven Camarota published analysis claiming 1.6 million decline in unauthorized immigrant population based on Current Population Survey data from January-July 2025[16]

- **August 14, 2025:** DHS Secretary Kristi Noem issued press release: "Secretary Noem Announces 1.6 Million Illegal Aliens Have Left the U.S." — first official government endorsement of CIS estimate[17]

- **August 19, 2025:** Noem publicly stated at a White House event: "According to the Bureau of Labor and Statistics, we have 1.6 million illegal aliens that have gone home voluntarily"[18]

- **September 23, 2025:** DHS press release claimed "More than 2 million illegal aliens have left the U.S., including 1.6 million who have voluntarily self-deported and over 527,000 deportations"[19]

- **December 10, 2025:** DHS announced "More than 2.5 million illegal aliens left the U.S.," claiming 1.9 million self-deportations and 622,000 formal deportations[20]

- **January 2026:** DHS further increased claimed self-deportations to 2.2 million[21]

**Critical Issue:** DHS provided no methodology, no data sources beyond referencing CIS's blog post, and no independent verification.[22][23] NPR repeatedly requested supporting data from DHS and received no response.[24]

### Methodological Problems

**1. Inappropriate Data Source**

The Census Bureau explicitly cautions against using the Current Population Survey (CPS) to estimate foreign-born populations.[25] Key problems:

- **Sample size:** CPS surveys ~60,000 households vs. 3.4+ million for the American Community Survey (ACS)[26]
- **Margin of error:** ±830,000 for foreign-born population in July 2025 — nearly as large as the claimed 1.6M decline[27]
- **Census warning:** "The change in the foreign-born population in a survey over time can imply emigration, [but] it can also reflect survey non-response, coverage error or the impact of population controls"[28]

**2. Declining Survey Response Rates**

Experts identified a critical confounding factor that DHS ignored:

- CPS response rates declined from 90.3% (January 2013) to 67.1% (July 2025)[29]
- Non-naturalized immigrant response rate dropped 16.6% from January to November 2025 — roughly 10 percentage points more than native-born and naturalized citizens[30]
- Julia Gelatt (Migration Policy Institute): "I see the 1.6 million as a pretty shaky estimate, and one that could be explained away by fewer immigrants feeling comfortable answering a government survey"[31]

Kaiser Family Foundation/NY Times survey found 41% of all immigrants (including legal residents and citizens) worried they or a family member could be detained or deported, and 14% said they or a family member had avoided going to church or on family outings in 2025 because of concerns about drawing attention to immigration status.[32]

**3. Category Conflation**

Steven Camarota himself admitted the 1.6 million figure includes far more than voluntary self-deportations:

- Formal ICE/CBP deportations (~239,000 as of June 30, 2025)[33]
- Deaths of undocumented immigrants
- Status changes (e.g., receiving asylum or other legal status)
- Actual voluntary departures

Camarota told PolitiFact that the "overwhelming majority" would have left on their own, but acknowledged the number "also includes immigrants who were deported, died, left voluntarily, and those whose status changed."[34] This directly contradicts DHS's characterization of the figure as representing "self-deportations."

**4. Implausible Scale**

Multiple experts noted the claimed figures are demographically implausible:

- The 1.6 million figure represents roughly 15% of the entire estimated unauthorized immigrant population of ~11 million[35]
- Would require ~200,000 people voluntarily leaving per month[36]
- Such an exodus would have noticeable labor market effects — none were observed[37]
- Robert Warren (Center for Migration Studies): "The DHS figure would amount to about 200,000 people having voluntarily left the country per month, an exodus not likely to have gone unobserved"[38]

### Actual Verified Numbers

**CBP Home App (Official Self-Deportation Program):**

The government launched the CBP Home app with cash incentives ($1,000, later raised to $3,000) to encourage voluntary departures.[39] Actual participation:

- April 2025: ~2,500 registrations[40]
- Mid-2025: ~13,000 participants over six months[41]
- January 2026: "Nearly 100,000 users" according to DHS[42]

Even taking the highest figure, the actual verified self-deportation program served only 100,000 people — **1/16th of DHS's claimed 1.6 million self-deportations.**

**ICE Deportation Statistics:**

- FY 2024 (Biden): 271,484 removals (742/day)[43]
- FY 2025 (Trump): ~329,000-340,000 removals (21-25% increase)[44][45]
- Trump administration claimed 400,000+ deportations in first year[46]

Independent analysis found initial Trump deportation rates were actually *below* Biden-era rates: during the first 100 days, ICE deported 693/day compared to Biden's 742/day average.[47] Rates increased later in 2025 to 4.6x Biden-era levels by October.[48]

### Independent Expert Estimates

**Brookings Institution:**
- Formal deportations: ~310,000
- Voluntary departures due to enforcement: 210,000–405,000
- **Total: 520,000–715,000** (vs. DHS claim of 2+ million)[49]

**Migration Policy Institute:**
- ICE deportations FY2025: ~340,000 (including voluntary departures from detention)
- 25% increase over FY2024's 271,000[50]

**Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse (TRAC):**
- Found Trump administration's claims of "record" deportations in first 100 days were false
- Actual 135,000 removals in first 100 days vs. Biden's full-year 272,000[51]

### Why the Claim Spread

1. **Official government endorsement** — DHS Secretary's announcement gave third-party estimate government imprimatur
2. **Policy justification** — Large numbers used to claim enforcement success and justify aggressive policies
3. **Media amplification** — Conservative outlets reported figures without scrutiny
4. **Repeated assertions** — DHS issued multiple press releases over 6 months with escalating claims
5. **Lack of transparency** — No methodology released, making debunking difficult for general public

### Evidence It's False

1. **Census Bureau warnings ignored** — DHS used data source explicitly flagged as inappropriate for this purpose
2. **Survey response rate decline** — Drop in immigrant survey participation better explains data than mass exodus
3. **Labor market stability** — No observable economic disruption despite claimed departure of 2M workers
4. **Verified program participation minimal** — Actual CBP Home app usage was 1/16th to 1/22nd of claimed total
5. **Expert consensus** — Every major independent immigration research institution contradicted the claim
6. **Category mixing** — Figure conflates deportations, deaths, status changes, and voluntary departures
7. **Escalating claims without new data** — DHS increased estimates from 1.6M to 2.2M without releasing methodology

## Key Quotes

### DHS Officials Making the Claim

**DHS Secretary Kristi Noem (August 19, 2025):**
> "According to the Bureau of Labor and Statistics, we have 1.6 million illegal aliens that have gone home voluntarily. They left at the encouragement of President Trump with a chance to return legally."[52]

**DHS Press Release (September 23, 2025):**
> "More than 2 million illegal aliens have left the U.S., including 1.6 million who have voluntarily self-deported and over 527,000 deportations."[53]

**DHS Spokesperson to Newsweek (December 2025):**
> "Tens of thousands of illegal aliens have utilized the CBP Home app. 1.6 million illegal immigrants have left the United States population since January 20, not all utilizing the CBP Home App."[54]

### Expert Critiques

**Center for Migration Studies:**
> "The [DHS] statement is a self-serving fantasy, based on a flawed interpretation of population data. A more realistic number was around 1/10th of claimed self-deportations."[55]

**Julia Gelatt, Migration Policy Institute:**
> "I see the 1.6 million as a pretty shaky estimate, and one that could be explained away by fewer immigrants feeling comfortable answering a government survey."[56]

**Robert Warren, Center for Migration Studies:**
> "Researchers generally rely on the bureau's more robust American Community Survey. That's because the Current Population Survey's sample size has a large margin of error."[57]

**Jeffrey Passel, Pew Research Center:**
> "For [20]25, all we have is CPS data and that points to a decline from a January peak of immigrants. How big the decline is could be affected by the decrease in response rates."[58]

**Peterson Institute for International Economics:**
> "The recent CPS data showing that there were 2.2 million fewer immigrants in the US in July 2025 than in January are implausibly large and uncorroborated, and imply absurd gyrations in the labor market."[59]

### On Methodology Problems

**U.S. Census Bureau (official guidance):**
> "While the change in the foreign-born population in a survey over time can imply emigration, it can also reflect survey non-response, coverage error or the impact of population controls."[60]

**Steven Camarota (CIS, author of original estimate):**
> "The 'overwhelming majority' of the 1.6 million would have left on their own, but the number also includes immigrants who were deported, died, left voluntarily, and those whose status changed, such as by getting asylum."[61]

**St. Louis Federal Reserve analysis:**
> "The question is whether the decline in surveyed immigrants indeed means an actual drop in net immigration or simply fewer immigrants choosing to participate in the survey. The CPS population estimate showing a 1.86-million decline in the number of immigrants mostly reflects a decrease in the participation of immigrants in the CPS."[62]

## Conclusion

The Department of Homeland Security's claim that 1.6–2.2 million undocumented immigrants "voluntarily self-deported" in 2025 represents one of the most significant instances of government statistical manipulation in recent immigration enforcement history. The claim fails basic methodological scrutiny:

1. **Inappropriate data source** — Used Census survey data explicitly flagged as unsuitable for measuring immigrant population changes
2. **Massive margin of error** — ±830,000 for a claimed 1.6M decline
3. **Ignored confounding factors** — Survey non-response among frightened immigrants misinterpreted as emigration
4. **Category conflation** — Mixed deportations, deaths, status changes, and voluntary departures into single figure
5. **Zero verification** — No methodology released, no independent audit, no response to data requests
6. **Implausible scale** — Claimed exodus would require 200,000/month departures with no observed economic impact
7. **Contradicted by own program** — Actual CBP Home app participation was 4.5% of claimed total

Independent experts across the political spectrum — from the Migration Policy Institute to the Brookings Institution to the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse — agree actual voluntary departures were likely 200,000–405,000, or roughly one-fifth to one-eighth of DHS's claims.

The real story: Aggressive enforcement drove immigrants "deeper into the shadows" rather than out of the country, reducing their willingness to respond to government surveys. DHS then misinterpreted this fear-driven survey decline as proof of its policy success, creating inflated statistics to justify continued aggressive enforcement — a self-reinforcing cycle of manufactured evidence and escalating claims.

## Additional Sources (Pass A: Primary Sources & Gap Analysis)

### Census Bureau CPS Methodology Warnings

The Census Bureau publishes explicit guidance cautioning against using the Current Population Survey (CPS) to estimate foreign-born population changes.[63][64]

**Sample Size Limitations:**
- CPS surveys approximately 60,000 households monthly, compared to 3.4+ million for the American Community Survey (ACS)
- The small sample size creates a margin of error of ±830,000 for foreign-born population estimates in 2025 — nearly as large as the claimed 1.6 million decline[65]

**Official Census Bureau Warning:**
"While the change in the foreign-born population in a survey over time can imply emigration, it can also reflect survey non-response, coverage error or the impact of population controls."[66]

**Population Control Timing Issues:**
The Census Bureau delayed incorporating annual population control adjustments for January 2026, meaning initial estimates "will continue to use short-term projections of monthly population estimates derived from population adjustments introduced in January 2025 (based on Vintage 2024 population estimates)."[67] Given the fluidity in international migration trends since January 2025, the Bureau acknowledged that projected net international migration (NIM) "would have been too high."[68]

**Historic Migration Trends:**
The Census Bureau reported that the United States experienced an unprecedented shift toward negative net migration in 2025. According to the Bureau: "Currently, the estimates of NIM are trending toward negative net migration. If those trends continue, it would be the first time the United States has seen net negative migration in more than 50 years."[69]

The foreign-born population in the CPS declined from 53.3 million in January 2025 to 51.9 million in June 2025 — a decline of 1.4 million. However, the Bureau emphasized that this decline reflects multiple confounding factors beyond actual emigration.[70]

### GAO Findings on ICE Data Quality

The Government Accountability Office (GAO) identified systematic data quality problems with ICE deportation reporting in a comprehensive audit published in 2024 (GAO-24-106233).[71]

**Key Finding — Underreporting of Detentions:**
GAO found that ICE systematically **understates the total number of people it detains** in its public reports. ICE excludes people who were first booked into certain temporary facilities before being transferred to immigration detention facilities — an exclusion amounting to **tens of thousands of people**.[72]

**Specific Example:**
In FY 2022 alone, ICE's reporting **excluded detentions of 136,706 individuals**. ICE does not fully explain its rationale for these exclusions or its methodology.[73]

**Impact on Transparency:**
GAO concluded: "As a result, Congress and the public may not have a complete understanding of how to interpret the data."[74] GAO recommended that ICE report data on all its detentions and explain any exclusions.

**Immigration Detention Facility Oversight:**
GAO also found that DHS and ICE lack clear performance goals and measures for immigration detention facility inspection programs.[75] Without these metrics, "it's hard to assess how effective these inspection programs are at ensuring that facilities meet standards for immigration detention."[76]

Deficiencies related to ICE's 14 core detention standards — including environmental health and safety, medical care, and food service — accounted for **approximately 74 percent of the total deficiencies** identified across all inspections reviewed.[77]

### Congressional Testimony — Secretary Noem's Claims and Contradictions

DHS Secretary Kristi Noem testified before Congress multiple times in 2025–2026, making escalating claims about deportation numbers while contradicting herself on key facts.

**May 20, 2025 — Senate Homeland Security Committee:**
Noem testified that the Trump administration's FY 2026 budget request included over $175 billion for DHS "to implement the President's mass removal campaign."[78] She claimed ICE arrested a "105% increase" in suspected gang members compared to FY 2024, and a "700% increase" in known or suspected terrorists.[79]

**December 11, 2025 — House Homeland Security Committee:**
In a contentious hearing, Rep. Seth Magaziner (D-RI) asked Noem directly if DHS had deported any military veterans. Noem stated the department "have not deported U.S. citizens or military veterans."[80]

Congressman Seth Moulton publicly released a letter from Secretary Noem dated September 2, 2025, which directly contradicted this testimony. The letter stated: "Regarding your question on the number of veterans that have been removed since January 20, 2025, ICE has removed eight veterans."[81]

Rep. Magaziner also introduced via Zoom a Purple Heart recipient named Sae Joon Park, who was shot twice while serving with the U.S. Army in Panama in 1989, and whom Magaziner said was deported to Korea by the Trump administration.[82]

**Official DHS Claims:**
DHS press releases throughout 2025 claimed: "More than 2.5 million illegal aliens have left the U.S. because of the Trump administration's crackdown on illegal immigration, including an estimated 1.9 million self-deportations and more than 622,000 deportations."[83]

However, according to internal ICE data obtained by journalists, most people arrested by immigration agents in 2025 did not have criminal records — contradicting the administration's claims of targeting only dangerous criminals.[84]

### Center for Immigration Studies — Original Methodology

The 1.6 million figure originated from a Center for Immigration Studies (CIS) blog post published August 12, 2025, by Steven Camarota and Karen Zeigler.[85]

**CIS Methodology:**
CIS researchers analyzed "raw data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics' (BLS) household survey, officially called the Current Population Survey (CPS)."[86] They subtracted estimated immigrants with legal status from the overall foreign-born population to estimate the unauthorized population.

BLS Table A-7 of the Employment Situation Report showed that employed foreign-born individuals declined by 1 million from January to July 2025, while U.S.-born workers increased by 2.5 million.[87]

**Camarota's Own Caveats:**
Steven Camarota told PolitiFact: "There is always some uncertainty in research of this kind, which we point out in our report." He called the 1.6 million figure an "estimate based on the best data available."[88]

Camarota acknowledged the number "also includes immigrants who, for example, were deported, died, left voluntarily, and those whose status changed such as by getting asylum."[89] He told PolitiFact the "overwhelming majority" would have left on their own, but this directly contradicts DHS's characterization of the figure as representing purely "self-deportations."

**Data Limitations Acknowledged:**
The CIS report itself acknowledged that "immigrants in the U.S. illegally might be more reluctant to complete the government's Current Population Survey or identify themselves as foreign-born."[90]

The report also noted: "Administrative data needed to estimate the unauthorized immigrant population is unavailable, further increasing the uncertainty of our estimate."[91]

**Subsequent Reversal:**
In an August 20 blog post, Camarota questioned his own report's suggestion that immigrants may have been reluctant to participate in surveys, calling it "nothing more than a possibility: 'There is as yet no evidence of this.'"[92]

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B: Escalation Timeline & Labor Market Analysis)

### How the Number Escalated: 1.6M → 1.9M → 2.2M

DHS systematically increased its self-deportation claims over six months without releasing new methodology or data:

**Timeline of Escalation:**
1. **August 12, 2025:** CIS publishes 1.6M estimate based on Jan-July 2025 CPS data
2. **August 14, 2025:** DHS endorses 1.6M figure in official press release
3. **September 23, 2025:** DHS claims "over 2 million" total departures (1.6M self-deportations + 527K formal deportations)
4. **December 10, 2025:** DHS increases self-deportation claim to 1.9M (total: 2.5M)
5. **January 2026:** DHS further increases to 2.2M self-deportations[93]

**Critical Analysis:**
The escalation from 1.6M to 2.2M represents a **37.5% increase** in claimed self-deportations, yet DHS released no new data, methodology, or verification to support the increase.[94] The original CIS estimate covered only January-July 2025; DHS's subsequent increases extrapolated beyond the source data's timeframe without explanation.

NPR repeatedly requested supporting data from DHS and received no response.[95]

### Labor Market Evidence: Where Are the 1.6 Million Workers?

If 1.6–2.2 million workers actually left the United States, labor markets should show dramatic disruption. Independent economic analysis found minimal evidence of such an exodus.

**Labor Force Decline:**
The number of foreign-born workers in the labor force fell by **1.6 million (about 5%)** from March 2025 through mid-2025, according to the Labor Department.[96] However, this decline better matches survey non-response patterns than actual emigration.

**Employment Growth Collapse:**
Employment expanded by only **584,000 in 2025** — the weakest annual pace since 2020 and second-weakest since the 2009 financial crisis.[97] A whopping **84% of those gains occurred in the first four months** of the year, before immigration enforcement intensified.[98]

University of Michigan economist Betsey Stevenson noted: "Looking at the last three months, we're creating fewer jobs by quite a lot." Instead of ~180,000 jobs per month seen in prior years, the economy was creating "something closer to 35,000 jobs per month."[99]

**Minneapolis Federal Reserve Analysis:**
The Minneapolis Fed found that the 2025 decline in employment growth is **larger than what would be expected from the direct effect of declining net migration alone**. The Fed concluded: "Around half of the decline in monthly employment payroll growth is attributable to declining net immigration," with estimates ranging from 40–60 percent.[100]

There has also been a significant decline in real wage growth, consistent with slowing demand beyond the direct effect of lower net migration.[101]

**Peterson Institute Critique:**
The Peterson Institute for International Economics called the 2.2 million figure "implausibly large and uncorroborated," arguing it would "imply absurd gyrations in the labor market."[102] Jed Kolko, former Commerce Department official now at PIIE, wrote that on an annual basis, the figures would suggest a decline of roughly 4 million foreign-born residents — a demographic shock that would create observable economic disruption that did not materialize.[103]

### Border Crossing Statistics Show Declining Arrivals, Not Mass Departures

CBP border encounter data for FY 2025–2026 shows unprecedented declines in new arrivals, but does not support claims of mass outflows:

**FY 2026 Record Lows (Oct–Nov 2025):**
- October 2025: **30,561 total nationwide encounters** — lowest start to a fiscal year ever recorded, **29% lower** than previous record low of 43,010 in October FY2012[104]
- November 2025: **30,367 encounters**, bringing combined Oct-Nov total to **60,940** — **28% lower** than previous two-month low of 84,293 in FY2012[105]
- For six consecutive months through October 2025, **U.S. Border Patrol released zero illegal aliens** into the United States[106]

**FY 2025 Full Year:**
- Southwest Border apprehensions totaled **237,538** in FY2025 — the **lowest level since 1970**[107]
- Daily average: **258 apprehensions per day**, **95% lower** than Biden administration's daily average of 5,110/day (Feb 2021–Dec 2024)[108]

**Critical Interpretation:**
These statistics demonstrate a collapse in new arrivals, not evidence of mass departures. The decline in border crossings began before Trump took office and was largely driven by Mexican enforcement and U.S. asylum restrictions implemented in late 2024.[109]

CBP does not track voluntary departures or emigration from the U.S. interior. The dramatic reduction in encounters reflects **fewer people entering**, not more people leaving.[110]

### Industry-Specific Labor Market Impact

If 1.6–2.2 million workers departed, industries heavily reliant on immigrant labor should show severe disruption. Analysis reveals targeted impacts but not at the scale claimed by DHS.

**Agriculture:**
- Unauthorized workers account for at least **25% of all agricultural workers** and about **42% of crop farmworkers**[111]
- Agricultural employment dropped **6.5% from March to July 2025** — a significant change from the previous two years, which saw small increases during the same period[112]
- Bureau of Labor Statistics reported a **38.9% increase in wholesale dry and fresh vegetable prices** from June to July — the biggest spike since March 2022[113]

**Construction:**
- Immigrants make up about **25% of all construction labor**[114]
- In the 10 states with highest concentrations of unauthorized workers, construction employment dropped **0.1%** from June 2024 to June 2025, while other states saw it increase **1.9%**[115]
- Brian Turmail, VP at Associated General Contractors of America: "Workforce shortages are the leading cause of construction project delays"[116]

**Hospitality:**
- Approximately **one-third of hotel and lodging staff nationwide are immigrants**, with highest concentrations in housekeeping, cooking, and dishwashing[117]
- About **7% of leisure and hospitality workers are undocumented**, mostly in restaurant and hotel sectors[118]
- Food service employment grew only **0.2% in high-immigrant states** over the past year compared to **1.5% in other states**[119]

**Analysis:**
The observed impacts suggest workforce reductions in the range of **several hundred thousand**, not 1.6–2.2 million. American Immigration Council economist Robert Lynch noted: "A loss of a significant portion of this workforce is likely to be particularly damaging as there were nearly 1 million unfilled jobs in leisure and hospitality as recently as April of this year."[120]

### Immigration Court Voluntary Departure Statistics

If DHS's self-deportation claims were accurate, immigration courts should show massive increases in voluntary departure cases. The data shows increases, but at a far smaller scale than DHS claims.

**FY 2026 Data (through December 2025):**
- Out of 57,531 completed cases in December 2025, immigration judges issued **38,215 removal orders** and **7,359 voluntary departure** grants[121]
- Combined, deportation was ordered in **79.2% of all December cases**[122]
- For full FY 2026 through December 2025: judges issued **149,706 total deportation orders** (removal + voluntary departure), representing **78.5% of completed cases**[123]

**Year-Over-Year Comparison:**
- Approximately **40,000 court-ordered voluntary departures in 2025**, compared to around **10,000 in 2024** — a 4x increase[124]
- The percentage of voluntary departures among **detained** immigrants grew nearly every month of 2025, reaching **38% in December**[125]

**Driving Factors:**
Record-high immigration detention levels (73,000 people in mid-January 2026 — highest ever recorded) and worsening facility conditions led many migrants to choose voluntary departure.[126] Attorneys argue that restricted bond access and low asylum success rates create hopelessness, with individuals prioritizing immediate freedom from custody over long-term pursuit of legal residency.[127]

Asylum grant rates plummeted from over 50% monthly in 2022–2024 to only **29% by December 2025**.[128] Through Q3 FY2025, immigration judges denied nearly **59,000 asylum claims** — 53% more denials in nine months than in all of FY 2024.[129]

**Critical Analysis:**
The 40,000 court-ordered voluntary departures in 2025 represent a significant increase — but account for only **1.8% of DHS's claimed 2.2 million self-deportations**. Even if every voluntary departure case represents a genuine departure (which is unlikely, given some recipients abscond), the court data contradicts DHS's scale of claims by more than 50x.[130]

### Historical Precedent: Operation Wetback & Self-Deportation Claims

No previous administration has claimed self-deportation numbers on the scale of the Trump administration's 2025–2026 claims. The closest historical parallel is Operation Wetback (1954).

**Operation Wetback Overview:**
Operation Wetback was a program of massive apprehensions and deportations aimed at undocumented Mexican nationals, carried out by the U.S. Border Patrol alongside the Mexican government under President Eisenhower.[156]

Planning began in early 1954, and the program was formally announced in May 1954.[157] The operation used military-style tactics to remove Mexican immigrants — some of them American citizens — from the United States.[158]

**Self-Deportation as Strategy:**
A key aspect of Operation Wetback was its use of fear to induce self-deportation. Hundreds of thousands of mostly Mexican undocumented immigrants were forcibly removed **or self-deported** during the campaign.[159]

Alexander Aviña, associate professor of history at Arizona State University, noted: "There is this element of spectacle that both administrations use. They try to induce fear and terror in immigrant populations to get them to self-deport, and that's something they tried to do with Operation Wetback."[160]

**Disputed Numbers:**
The historical record on Operation Wetback's scale is heavily contested. Officially, the Eisenhower administration claimed it carried out more than **1 million "returns"**.[161] Some chain emails have claimed 13 million deportations — a figure historians universally reject as false.[162]

Historian Kelly Lytle Hernandez argues the number of actual deportees was drastically lower than reported — likely closer to **300,000**.[163] Historians believe many people were deported more than once, inflating the count, and that the actual number of unique individuals is significantly less than claimed.[164]

Only **2.1 million were officially recorded** as having been deported or having departed under threat of deportation across multiple years.[165]

**Human Rights Concerns:**
Doris Meissner, former INS Commissioner (1993–2000), stated that Operation Wetback "was lawless; it was arbitrary; it was based on a lot of xenophobia, and it resulted in sizable large-scale violations of people's rights, including the forced deportation of U.S. citizens."[166]

Historians say racial profiling and large-scale roundups were common, and some U.S. citizens were caught up in the sweeps.[167]

**Trump Administration Comparison:**
President Trump cited Eisenhower's operation during his presidential campaign and promised mass deportations.[168] However, mass deporting undocumented migrants is more complicated now than in the 1950s. Historian Mae Ngai noted: "They're not Mexican workers. The large numbers of people who are crossing the border are asylum seekers. They're people who are fleeing civil war, civil strife, gang violence."[169]

Today's migrants live in cities throughout the country, not just near the border, and many have been in the country for years with established families and work histories.[170]

### State-Level Impacts: California, Texas, Florida

The deportation campaign has varied dramatically by state, largely depending on local cooperation with federal immigration enforcement.

**Texas:**
Texas has the highest number of deportations of any state.[171] Three cities have some of the highest numbers of foreign nationals departing: Laredo (477,834), El Paso (283,510), and Harlingen (271,149).[172]

The El Paso "Camp East Montana" detention site averaged **2,774 detainees per day** as of November 2025.[173]

From Trump's inauguration through mid-October 2025, **52% of ICE arrests occurred at jails**, especially in cooperating states such as Texas and Florida.[174]

**Florida:**
Florida has expanded local enforcement under 287(g) agreements and shown high levels of cooperation with ICE.[175] Nearly half (48%) of ICE arrests nationally happen at local jails and lock-ups, with Florida as a key cooperating state.[176]

**California:**
The crackdown fell heavily on California families with deep roots. Deportations increasingly reached people with decades-long residence, U.S.-citizen children, stable employment, and even those following legal pathways.[177]

ICE detained people at green-card interviews and routine check-ins, destabilizing school systems, the agricultural economy, and health care.[178]

Some California school districts issued sweeping directives stating that teachers, officials, and administration were not to comply with ICE officials or allow them on school grounds unless presented with a valid court-issued warrant.[179]

**State Policy Divergence:**
Despite federal power, the administration relies heavily on state and local collaboration to enact its mass deportation agenda.[180] States can enact policies that prioritize public safety, establish clear procedures for local law enforcement's interactions with federal immigration authorities, and restrict costly use of state or local resources for unreimbursed federal enforcement.[181]
