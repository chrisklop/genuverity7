## Executive Summary

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost of Chicago, elected May 8, 2025 as the first American pontiff in the 2,000-year history of the Catholic Church — has become the single most impersonated and misinformation-targeted religious figure in digital history. Within the first year of his pontificate, Snopes alone documented at least 18 discrete false claims requiring formal fact-checks [1], with a separate collection of nine fabricated quotations warranting their own dedicated investigation [2]. The primary vectors of attack are coordinated AI-generated content farms — many operating from Vietnam — that deploy text-to-speech narration, deepfake video technology, and GPT-generated blog posts to fabricate papal statements, manufacture political conflicts, and profit from advertising revenue generated by emotional engagement [3][4].

The disinformation ecosystem surrounding Leo XIV operates on two distinct tracks. The first is a purely commercial clickbait operation targeting American and European Catholic audiences with sensationalist AI-generated narratives — fake sermons, manufactured papal quotes, staged crises — designed to drive traffic to advertising-laden blog sites [5]. The second, more dangerous track involves the deliberate weaponization of Leo's political profile: his status as a Chicago-born American who has vocally opposed U.S. military strikes on Iran has made him a target for state-adjacent actors and domestic political operatives seeking to discredit his anti-war messaging [6]. The apex of this second track arrived on April 12, 2026, when President Donald Trump himself posted on Truth Social that Pope Leo "thinks it's OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon" — a claim rated "Pants on Fire" by PolitiFact and flatly contradicted by the Pope's documented statements calling nuclear weapons a threat to "our shared humanity" [7].

The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication has described the situation as a crisis of unprecedented scale, noting it receives "dozens of reports every day about fake accounts that use the pope's image and voice in a very realistic way, increasingly using artificial intelligence to make the pope say words he never uttered" [8]. Deepfake videos have surpassed one million views before removal, and the Vatican has acknowledged it is impossible to publicly refute every fabrication given the sheer volume [9]. The result is a documented erosion of public trust in papal communications, a significant media-literacy challenge for the world's 1.4 billion Catholics, and a stress test for platform content moderation that — by the evidence — is failing in real time [10].

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin and Structural Architecture

The misinformation campaign targeting Pope Leo XIV can be traced to three distinct origin structures that often overlap or cross-pollinate:

**1. Vietnamese "Viet Spam" Networks**  
Multiple fact-checking investigations — by Snopes, Lead Stories, and Yahoo News — have independently traced fabricated papal quotes to Facebook pages managed from Vietnam. These operations use Meta's ad-transparency tools, which reveal page management locations, to run coordinated clickbait campaigns targeting English-language Catholic communities. One operation tracked by Snopes had spread identical narrative templates to at least 17 different public figures simultaneously [3]. A key technical fingerprint: affected blog posts used Cyrillic character substitutions (replacing Latin "N" with Cyrillic equivalents) specifically to evade automated content moderation systems [5].

**2. AI Slop Content Farms**  
The "Guided By Grace" Facebook page — identified as a central node in multiple hoaxes including the 3 a.m. livestream claim and the "Do not let power turn leaders into kings" quote — represents a new model of AI-powered disinformation. These operations use large language models to generate content that mimics Catholic devotional vernacular, text-to-speech tools to add voice narration, and AI video tools to create deepfakes. GPTZero analysis confirmed blog articles supporting these claims were "100% certainly AI-generated" [4]. YouTube channels operating under titles like "Pope Leo XIV's Sermons" uploaded dozens of fabricated sermons using synthetic audio before platform enforcement [9].

**3. State-Adjacent / Politically Motivated Actors**  
The anti-nuclear weapons stance of Pope Leo, his criticism of the Iran war, and his rebuke of leaders whose "hands are full of blood" made him a geopolitical target. A 36-minute deepfake video — later confirmed fabricated by the Vatican — purported to show Pope Leo praising Burkina Faso's military president Ibrahim Traoré, accumulating over one million views on YouTube before removal. This content appears designed to weaponize Leo's moral authority for geopolitical audiences in sub-Saharan Africa. Trump's Truth Social missive of April 12, 2026 — directly misrepresenting Leo's position on Iranian nuclear weapons — represents the highest-status amplification of Leo-targeting misinformation, triggering a global fact-checking response from CNN, PBS, PolitiFact, and dozens of outlets simultaneously.

### Propagation Mechanics

The most viral false claims about Pope Leo XIV exploit a specific formula: a dramatic headline invoking a direct confrontation between the Pope and President Trump, a fabricated quote formatted to mirror authentic papal speech patterns, a link to an AI-generated blog article seeded in Facebook comment sections, and monetized advertising that rewards clicks regardless of content accuracy [5]. Lead Stories documented this template being applied to at least 30 different celebrities and politicians simultaneously, with the papal version generating outsized engagement due to the unique combination of religious authority and American political identity [4].

### Why It Spreads

Several structural factors make Pope Leo XIV uniquely vulnerable to viral misinformation:

- **Novel historical status**: As the first American pope, he occupies an unprecedented intersection of U.S. domestic politics and global religious authority that generates intense curiosity.
- **Active geopolitical conflict**: His documented opposition to the Iran war puts him in direct tension with the current U.S. administration, creating authentic news hooks that fake content can exploit.
- **Vast, digitally engaged audience**: Leo amassed 18 million X (Twitter) followers in a single day after his election, creating enormous reach for any content — real or fabricated — attached to his name.
- **Catholic content void**: The Catholic information ecosystem has limited rapid-response infrastructure compared to mainstream political fact-checking, creating a lag that disinformation exploits.
- **Emotional gravity of papal authority**: Claims about what the Pope said or did carry intrinsic moral weight for 1.4 billion Catholics globally, triggering immediate sharing behavior.

### Evidence and Documentation

The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication stated: "We are witnessing the exponential proliferation of a series of YouTube channels with fake videos, all similar to one another, some speaking in the voice of Leo XIV, others in that of his translators, still others in the third person. All use artificial intelligence to make the pope say things he never said" [8]. The dicastery further acknowledged: "Given the sheer volume of fake material, it is impossible to publicly refute each and every one of them" [9]. In a notable moment of self-awareness, Pope Leo XIV himself acknowledged that AI-generated images of him falling down the stairs of St. Peter's Basilica were "so good that they thought it was me" [10].

### The Fake "Cor Unum" Document — Complete Reform List

The most theologically impactful disinformation campaign against Pope Leo XIV was a viral video falsely claiming he had issued a sweeping reform document titled *Cor Unum* ("One Heart"). Posted on July 19, 2025, the video — which exceeded 300,000 views on YouTube — opened with: "Right now, as you are watching this, millions of Catholics..." and proceeded to enumerate 15 fabricated reforms [13].

The Catholic Herald identified the specific reforms listed in the fake document as follows [13]:

1. Vatican financial transparency  
2. Term limits for clergy  
3. Women deacons

6. Dissolution of papal nobility and titles

8. Liturgy adaptation to local cultures

10. Communion for divorced and remarried Catholics  
11. Binding joint council with Eastern Orthodox Churches

13. Abolition of the title "Vicar of Christ"

The Catholic Herald concluded that "these fabricated stories frame Pope Leo's alleged motivations as a caricature of the pontificate of Pope Francis" [13]. The Vatican had reported the channels involved to YouTube.

**What made this hoax particularly effective:** The fake reforms targeted real Catholic fault lines — celibacy, women's ordination, interfaith dialogue — making them plausible enough to spread within communities that follow Catholic doctrinal debates. The document name *Cor Unum* is itself the name of a real Vatican dicastery (for charitable works), lending false institutional credibility.

### The Burkina Faso Deepfake — Forensic Breakdown

The earliest and most-viewed single deepfake item targeting Pope Leo XIV was a 36-minute video falsely depicting the newly elected pontiff delivering a speech of support to Ibrahim Traoré, the military leader of Burkina Faso who came to power in a 2022 coup. The video was uploaded to YouTube on **May 17, 2025** — just nine days after Leo's election — and spread via the Facebook page "Ghanaba News" on June 6, 2025. It accumulated over **one million views** before YouTube terminated the posting account [8][9].

Full Fact's forensic analysis identified multiple technical indicators of fabrication [**25**]:

- **Mispronunciation inconsistency**: The Pope pronounces Captain Traoré's name differently in successive sentences — a telltale artifact of AI voice synthesis patching together phonemes.
- **Blurred mouth region**: The area around the mouth appeared blurry due to poor lip-sync rendering. As deepfake researcher Dr. Dominic Lees explained: "Artificial intelligence finds it very difficult to generate a natural look in the teeth so often leaves this blurry and out-of-focus."
- **Background music**: Dr. Lees noted this is "frequently used to mask the poor qualities of voice cloning," and observed that "No official video of a speech by a Pope has ever been given a music backing."

The source footage was genuine video from Pope Leo XIV's audience with journalists on May 12, 2025. The Vatican published the official English transcript of that audience — confirming the Pope "did not mention Africa" and was not addressing President Traoré. The video's description on the original channel included a disclaimer identifying it as "a work of fiction inspired by the life of Ibrahim Traoré," but this disclaimer was not visible in reshared versions [**25**].

The geopolitical significance: Burkina Faso is under significant influence from Russia's Wagner Group and has expelled French forces. A video showing the newly elected American pope endorsing its military leader would have significant propaganda value for anti-Western narratives across sub-Saharan Africa — where Leo's actual Africa trip (April 2026) was a carefully planned diplomatic outreach.

### Broader Pattern — AI Deepfakes Targeting Religious Leaders

Pope Leo XIV is not the only Catholic leader victimized by AI deepfakes, but he is by far the most targeted. Catholic Review's analysis documented a pattern of deepfakes targeting multiple Church figures simultaneously [16][**31**]:

- **Father Mike Schmitz** (popular Catholic speaker and author): Fabricated content circulated under his name.
- **Father Rafael Capó** (St. Thomas University, Miami): Impersonator accounts used his image to solicit money from followers.

A 2025 Vatican document on AI warned: "AI-generated fake media can gradually undermine the foundations of society." The Vatican's position is that for Catholics specifically, this is existential: "the faith is transmitted not only through ideas but through credible witnesses, via testimonies" — and deepfake technology directly attacks the credibility of those witnesses [**31**].

The pattern reveals a structural vulnerability: religious institutions rely heavily on the moral authority of named individuals (popes, bishops, priests) to communicate with their communities. This makes them particularly susceptible to deepfakes, which weaponize exactly that named personal authority. Unlike political organizations, which have press departments and rapid-response infrastructure, most parishes and dioceses lack the resources to monitor and rebut AI-generated impersonation content at scale.

Pope Leo XIV himself recognized this when advising Catholics in 2026: "Educate ourselves and others about how to use AI intentionally" and "protect our image (photos and audio), our face and our voice, to prevent them from being used in the creation of harmful content" [**31**].
