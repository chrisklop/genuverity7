## Executive Summary

Since his election on May 8, 2025, Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost, a Chicago-born Augustinian friar and the first American pope in history — has become one of the most exploited figures in the global AI disinformation ecosystem. A coordinated network of Vietnam-based Facebook pages, deploying AI-generated text and deepfake audio/video, has manufactured at least ten distinct categories of fabricated claims falsely attributing inflammatory statements and dramatic confrontations with U.S. President Donald Trump. The pattern — known to fact-checkers as "Viet Spam" — uses nearly identical formats to target dozens of different celebrities, replacing the famous name with whoever generates maximum traffic in a given news cycle. By early 2026, with the U.S. engaged in an active war against Iran and Pope Leo having issued genuine statements that God rejects "the prayers of leaders who wage war," the operators found fertile ground: real papal controversy, massively amplified by fabricated elaborations. [1][2][3]

Snopes debunked no fewer than six distinct Pope Leo XIV hoaxes between March and April 2026 alone, including: a "fiery sermon" in response to Trump calling him an "insult to Jesus" (FALSE — AI-generated, origin: Vietnam-based pages); a 3am urgent message livestream (FALSE — identical template used for 30 different individuals); a quote calling Trump "a vicious, old bastard draining America's soul" (FALSE — anti-crawling letter substitution confirmed as AI indicator); Trump's Wharton IQ test "revealed live on TV" (FALSE); Trump telling Leo to "sit down — you're just the pope" (FALSE — timeline impossible); and a "do not let power turn leaders into kings" attribution (FALSE). Independently, Catholic News Agency documented a 36-minute deepfake video praising Burkina Faso's president using manipulated lip movements, a "blessed salt" audio hoax denied by the Vatican, and AI-generated photos of the pope falling down stairs that Leo XIV himself remarked were "so good that they thought it was me." [4][5][6][7]

The Vatican's communications team has reported hundreds of fake accounts, predominantly on YouTube, and describes the situation as an "uphill battle" against operators who create new accounts and videos as quickly as others are removed. GPTZero, a leading AI-text detection tool, rated multiple Pope Leo XIV fake posts as "highly confident" AI-generated. The underlying economic model is straightforward: the Vietnam-based networks earn advertising revenue from traffic driven to external sites, with no financial risk from the fabrications. Platforms' reduced investment in professional fact-checking — replaced by user-generated community notes — has created a distribution infrastructure with "essentially no friction," in the words of Snopes analysts. [8][9][10]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The disinformation targeting Pope Leo XIV originates from a well-documented operation known to fact-checkers as "Viet Spam." Lead Stories published a guide titled *"Prebunk: Beware Of Fake Fan Pages Spreading False Stories About Your Favorite Celebrities — How To Spot 'Viet Spam'"* after identifying the pattern in January 2026, when the same statement about Trump was attributed to at least 17 different sports stars and celebrities in a single campaign wave. Snopes confirmed the link by contacting a manager of one Facebook page that posted an early version of the "vicious old bastard" quote attributed to Leo XIV — the page had a contact number with Vietnam's country code (+84). The blog posts spreading the false rumor used an anti-crawling technique involving visually substituting certain Latin letters with similar-looking characters from other alphabets — a known AI-generation fingerprint. [1][3]

### Propagation

The pipeline is consistent across all hoaxes: AI-generated text or deepfake video is published on a Facebook page or YouTube channel → the post or video includes a link to an external advertising-laden blog site → the post circulates virally, with each share generating impressions and traffic to the monetized destination → mainstream users screenshot and re-share without the original link, stripping context. In one documented case (the Charlie Kirk deepfake), a single fabricated video accumulated over 445,000 YouTube views in seven days before removal. The Vatican's communications team confirmed it has reported "hundreds of accounts" using this model. [7][8]

### Why It Spread

Three factors converged to make Pope Leo XIV exceptionally vulnerable to this exploitation:
1. **Genuine papal controversy**: Leo XIV made real statements opposing the US-Iran war — "God rejects the prayers of leaders whose hands are full of blood" — giving the fabrications a veneer of plausibility for casual readers.
2. **First American pope**: His unprecedented nationality during an active American military engagement made him a natural focal point for politically charged narratives.
3. **Platform complicity**: Meta and YouTube's reduction of professional fact-checking in favor of user-generated labels created a slow-response environment where fabrications typically go viral before any correction appears.

### Evidence of Fabrication

Confirmed AI-fabrication indicators across the hoaxes:
- GPTZero rated multiple posts "highly confident" AI-generated [4]
- Anti-crawling letter substitution (Latin letters replaced with visually identical Unicode characters from other alphabets) [3]
- Timeline impossibilities (e.g., "months" of confrontation in events that had occurred less than two weeks prior) [5]
- Identical template structure reused across 30+ different subjects [2]
- YouTube channels self-labeling "altered or synthetic content" at upload [1]
- Vatican officially confirming specific videos as fabricated [7]
