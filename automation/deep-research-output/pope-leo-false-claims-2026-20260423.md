## Executive Summary

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost in Chicago on September 14, 1955 — was elected to the papacy on May 8, 2025, becoming the first American pope in history and the first from the Order of Saint Augustine. He chose the name Leo XIV in honor of Leo XIII, who developed modern Catholic social teaching during the Second Industrial Revolution, signaling continuity on workers' rights, economic justice, and now artificial intelligence governance. His election was widely considered a surprise; Vatican insiders had dismissed the idea of an American pope given U.S. geopolitical influence. Almost immediately after his election, Leo XIV became one of the most impersonated and misrepresented public figures in the AI disinformation ecosystem. [1][2][3]

By early 2026, a coordinated — though apparently decentralized — wave of AI-generated false claims had spread across Facebook, YouTube, and TikTok targeting the new pope. Two claims emerged as particularly viral: that Pope Leo XIV delivered a "fiery sermon" in response to President Trump calling him "an insult to Jesus," and that the pope went live at 3am with an unspecified "urgent message." Both claims were debunked by Snopes. A third major false narrative — that Leo XIV called Trump a "vicious, old b******" — also spread widely, as did claims about a fake Wharton IQ score reveal, a fake hospitalization, a fake Trump threat to make him "sit down," a fake Pentagon threat against the Vatican, and dozens of AI-generated video sermons pushing false Church teachings on YouTube and TikTok. Snopes catalogued at least nine questionable or fabricated Leo XIV quotes for fact-checking by April 2026. [4][5][6]

The disinformation campaign exploited a genuine and tense real-world conflict between Leo XIV and Trump. In April 2026, the pope called for peace in response to the Iran war, prompting Trump to attack him on social media as "WEAK on Crime, and terrible for Foreign Policy." Trump subsequently shared an AI-generated image depicting himself as a Jesus-like figure, which was later deleted. This real conflict created the ideal viral substrate: audiences were primed to believe escalations that never actually occurred. Fabricators running AI content farms on Facebook seized on the tension to generate engagement-maximizing false stories, with suspicious links in comment sections directing readers to monetized blog sites. [7][8][9]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The two primary false claims both originated from AI content farms operating on Facebook. The "fiery sermon after Trump called him 'insult to Jesus'" claim was traced to a Facebook account called **"Pontiff Leo Era,"** described by fact-checkers as generating content "created by artificial intelligence." The posts followed a recognizable template: a sensational headline claiming the pope had delivered a remarkable anti-Trump speech, accompanied by blog links in the comment sections promising further details. The 3am "urgent message" claim followed the same structural pattern — posts originating from Facebook accounts and associated blog pages that use AI tools to generate fictional but emotionally resonant stories about public figures, frequently accompanied by images of Trump to exploit political grievance.

The broader AI video disinformation strand — fabricated sermons on YouTube and TikTok — originated from channels with names like "Pope Leo XIV Vision" and "Pope Leo XIV Sermons," which systematically published AI-generated video content depicting the pope delivering speeches he never gave. One detected deepfake showed a manipulated version of Leo XIV's first official press conference, with the audio altered to make him appear to praise Ibrahim Traore, the military leader of Burkina Faso.

### Propagation

Facebook served as the primary launch platform for the text-based false claims, using the architecture of page-and-blog-link farming: a Facebook page publishes a provocative post, generates shares and comments, then channels traffic to an external blog where ad revenue accrues. YouTube and TikTok served as the primary distribution channels for AI video content, with some material crossing over to WhatsApp via direct sharing among religious communities. Parishioners alarmed by fake papal sermons contacted diocesan staff to ask whether the claimed liturgical changes were real, demonstrating penetration into offline faith communities. A major Catholic TV network broadcast AI-generated content before staff identified it and issued warnings to viewers.

### Why It Spread

Several reinforcing factors drove the velocity and reach of this campaign:

1. **Real underlying conflict**: The genuine Trump-Leo XIV tension over the Iran war and immigration gave fabricators a plausible backdrop. Audiences already predisposed to believe the conflict was escalating found the fabricated escalations credible.
2. **Novelty of the subject**: As a newly elected, historically unprecedented American pope, Leo XIV had low public familiarity. Most Facebook users could not confidently say what he had or had not actually said, lowering the cognitive friction required to accept false claims.
3. **Religious and political polarization**: Claims pitting the pope against Trump mapped onto existing political and religious identity fault lines, incentivizing both sharing (to validate one's side) and emotional reaction.
4. **AI production at scale**: The low cost of AI text and video generation allowed farm operators to produce high volumes of content across multiple accounts, making any individual takedown ineffective.
5. **Platform recommendation algorithms**: YouTube and TikTok recommendation systems surfaced "Pope Leo XIV" content to users searching for legitimate information about the new pope, inadvertently amplifying fabricated channels alongside authentic ones.

### Evidence Against Claims

**"Fiery sermon / insult to Jesus" claim:**
Fact-checkers at Snopes and MEAWW confirmed that searches across Google, DuckDuckGo, and Bing returned no credible news sources reporting either that Trump called Leo XIV "an insult to Jesus" or that Leo XIV delivered any "fiery sermon" in response. No major news organization — including outlets that were actively covering the real Trump-Leo XIV conflict in April 2026 — reported these specific events. The origin Facebook account "Pontiff Leo Era" exhibited all hallmarks of an AI content farm. MEAWW concluded: "The claims made in viral Facebook posts are not true, as there is no credible evidence to prove that Pope Leo XIV gave a 'fiery sermon' after Donald Trump's insult." [10]

**"3am urgent message" claim:**
Snopes rated this claim false. The posts, originating from Facebook accounts and blog pages using AI generation, claimed the pope had started an unexpected livestream to warn of a threatening message from a powerful politician. No such livestream occurred. Vatican communications — fully documented — showed no such broadcast. The structure of the claim (3am urgency, threatening politician, live broadcast) follows a well-documented template for AI content farms targeting religious audiences with apocalyptic-feeling false alerts.

**AI-generated video sermons:**
The Catholic Times of Columbus documented that fabricated AI videos falsely depicted the pope announcing sweeping liturgical changes. Deacon Tom Berg Jr. identified the videos as AI-generated from their "animation-like look and semi-robotic sound and tone." A telltale error: the AI-generated content repeatedly referred to the pope as "Pope Leo I-dash-15" rather than "Pope Leo XIV." Mainstream news media had not covered the alleged changes, and the Vatican issued no implementation guidance — both reliable negative indicators.

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### The "Pontiff Leo Era" Content Farm

The Facebook account "Pontiff Leo Era" identifies itself as a "news and media website" but operates as a classic AI content farm. Its business model follows a documented template: publish a sensational, emotionally charged headline claiming the pope delivered a remarkable anti-Trump speech; generate shares and outrage-driven engagement; then channel traffic to an associated external blog where advertising revenue accrues. The posts contain all forensic markers of AI-generated text: vague, emotionally heightened language; no specific time, place, or named witnesses for the alleged events; no quotes from named officials; no links to video or audio evidence.

The independent AI-detection tool GPTZero analyzed posts from "Pontiff Leo Era" and associated blog content and assessed it as "highly confident" to be AI-generated text. This finding is consistent with the structural signatures Snopes identified: "This originated from Facebook accounts and blog pages that use artificial intelligence tools to create inspiring or shocking stories about public figures." The account targets the politically polarized overlap between American Catholic identity and Trump-era grievance politics, a demographic that is both highly engaged and algorithmically rewarded by Facebook's recommendation system. Comments on the posts frequently contain links to the monetized blog pages, completing the traffic-to-revenue loop.

### Scale of the Deepfake Problem

The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication has publicly described the deepfake problem as operationally overwhelming. According to its newsletter, the dicastery receives "dozens of reports every day" about fake accounts using the pope's image and voice. The statement acknowledged that "given the sheer volume of fake material, it is impossible to publicly refute each and every one of them" — a frank admission that the Vatican's reactive capacity has been outpaced by AI production volume.

Specific documented examples of the scale:

- A single YouTube channel called "Pope Leo XIV Sermons" accumulated over 230,000 views and 13,000+ subscribers before enforcement action was taken.
- YouTube ultimately banned at least one channel outright for AI-generated fake propaganda in the pope's name — an unusual escalation beyond content removal to full channel termination.
- "Hundreds" of fabricated video sermons circulated across YouTube and TikTok, per Republic World's reporting.
- Multiple Catholics reported being misled by fabricated content within a five-hour window during a single incident.
- A major Catholic TV network broadcast AI-generated content before staff identified and corrected it.
- Diocesan and parish staff received parishioner inquiries about whether fabricated liturgical changes were real, showing offline penetration of the disinformation.

The most illustrative fabricated content came from an AI-generated "end-times sermon" that began: "The sky is heavy with prophecy. The clock of heaven is ticking its final seconds. And I declare to you today: 2025 is not just another year." The vague, maximally ominous register is a recognizable signature of AI-generated religious disinformation designed to trigger sharing without triggering fact-checking.

Pope Leo XIV personally addressed the problem in his January 24, 2026 message for the 60th World Day of Social Communications: "It is important to educate ourselves and others about how to use AI intentionally, and in this context to protect our image (photos and audio), our face and our voice, to prevent them from being used in the creation of harmful content."

### The Real Trump-Leo XIV Conflict

Understanding the genuine tension is essential context: fabricators did not invent the conflict from nothing — they layered fictional escalations onto a real and high-profile clash, which is precisely what made the fake claims believable.

The actual sequence of events:

**On immigration (pre-papacy and ongoing):** As a bishop in Peru, Robert Prevost — before becoming Leo XIV — publicly criticized the Trump administration's mass deportation policies. In November 2025, he told reporters that the treatment of immigrants under the policy was "extremely disrespectful." These were real, on-record statements.

**On the Iran war (April 2026):** The main flashpoint. When Trump threatened to "destroy Iranian civilization," Leo XIV publicly called that comment "truly unacceptable." On Palm Sunday 2026, Leo stated: "Jesus is the king of peace, who rejects war, whom no one can use to justify war." These are real, documented papal statements.

**Trump's real responses:** Trump attacked the pope on social media, calling him "WEAK on Crime, and terrible for Foreign Policy." He separately posted: "Will someone please tell Pope Leo that Iran has killed at least 42,000 innocent, completely unarmed, protesters in the last two months." Trump also shared an AI-generated image depicting himself as a Jesus-like figure, later deleted; he claimed the image depicted him as a doctor.

**Leo's real response to Trump's attacks:** "I have no fear of the Trump administration or speaking out loudly of the message of the Gospel, which is what I believe I am here to do, what the church is here to do."

**What did NOT happen:** Trump never called Leo XIV "an insult to Jesus." Leo XIV never delivered a "fiery sermon" in response to that phrase. Leo XIV never called Trump a "vicious, old b******." No Wharton IQ test results were revealed. No 3am livestream occurred. No Vatican files were threatened. These are all fabrications layered atop a real conflict.

## Contemporary Context (Pass C)

### Why This Topic Is Critical Right Now (April 2026)

Pope Leo XIV is approximately eleven months into his pontificate as of this writing. He is still in the phase where his public record is thin enough that large segments of the global public — including practicing Catholics — lack a reliable internal baseline for what he actually sounds like, what he actually believes, and what he has and has not said. This low-familiarity window is the maximum-vulnerability period for disinformation: without a dense authentic record to contradict fabrications, false claims meet minimal cognitive resistance.

At the same time, Leo XIV is engaged in an active and high-visibility conflict with the sitting U.S. president over two of the most emotionally charged issues in American Catholic politics — immigration enforcement and a hot war with Iran. Both issues map precisely onto existing fault lines within American Catholicism: a significant portion of U.S. Catholics voted for Trump and hold sympathetic views of his immigration policies, while the Church's official teaching and Leo XIV's personal statements consistently oppose those policies. This creates a bifurcated audience in which both sides have strong motivations to share content about the conflict: Trump supporters want to see the pope attacked or discredited; Leo XIV supporters want to see him make strong anti-Trump statements. AI content farms exploit both audiences simultaneously.

Leo XIV's status as the first American pope adds a layer of nationalist identity politics that no previous pope has carried. His fluency in English — noted by Axios as "native-level" — means American audiences follow his statements directly, without the interpretive buffer of translation, amplifying both legitimate engagement and disinformation spread.

### Political Dimensions

The disinformation campaign around Pope Leo XIV serves identifiable political interests, even if it may not be centrally coordinated:

**Domestic U.S. political utility:** False claims that exaggerate or fabricate Leo XIV's attacks on Trump function to pre-emptively discredit him among Trump supporters — by depicting him as a partisan anti-Trump figure rather than a moral authority speaking from Church teaching. The more he appears to be a political combatant rather than a spiritual leader, the easier it is for supporters of administration policies to dismiss his criticisms of immigration enforcement and the Iran war.

**Content farm economics:** The primary driver of most identified disinformation is not sophisticated political strategy but ad-revenue farming. The Trump-Leo conflict is simply the highest-engagement topic in American Catholic digital media in 2026, making it the ideal substrate for traffic generation. The "Pontiff Leo Era" Facebook account and its associated blog pages monetize outrage from both political directions.

**Undermining Vatican authority on AI governance:** Leo XIV has made AI governance and media literacy a signature issue of his papacy — explicitly and repeatedly. The Vatican's Dicastery for Communication has been unusually candid about the scale of the deepfake problem. A pope who is himself a major victim of AI disinformation, while simultaneously being one of the world's most prominent voices calling for AI accountability, carries significant moral authority in that debate. Flooding his image with AI-generated fakes serves to muddy that authority and exhaust the Vatican's response capacity.

**The USCCB response:** Archbishop Coakley, responding on behalf of the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops to Trump's social media post targeting Leo XIV, issued a formal statement of support for the pope — indicating the conflict has mobilized institutional American Catholic leadership and elevated the stakes of the disinformation environment around it.
