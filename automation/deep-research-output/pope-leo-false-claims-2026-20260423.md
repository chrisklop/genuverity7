## Executive Summary

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost in Chicago on September 14, 1955 — was elected to the papacy on May 8, 2025, becoming the first American pope in history and the first from the Order of Saint Augustine. He chose the name Leo XIV in honor of Leo XIII, who developed modern Catholic social teaching during the Second Industrial Revolution, signaling continuity on workers' rights, economic justice, and now artificial intelligence governance. His election was widely considered a surprise; Vatican insiders had dismissed the idea of an American pope given U.S. geopolitical influence. Almost immediately after his election, Leo XIV became one of the most impersonated and misrepresented public figures in the AI disinformation ecosystem. [1][2][3]

By early 2026, a coordinated — though apparently decentralized — wave of AI-generated false claims had spread across Facebook, YouTube, and TikTok targeting the new pope. Two claims emerged as particularly viral: that Pope Leo XIV delivered a "fiery sermon" in response to President Trump calling him "an insult to Jesus," and that the pope went live at 3am with an unspecified "urgent message." Both claims were debunked by Snopes. A third major false narrative — that Leo XIV called Trump a "vicious, old b******" — also spread widely, as did claims about a fake Wharton IQ score reveal, a fake hospitalization, a fake Trump threat to make him "sit down," a fake Pentagon threat against the Vatican, and dozens of AI-generated video sermons pushing false Church teachings on YouTube and TikTok. Snopes catalogued at least nine questionable or fabricated Leo XIV quotes for fact-checking by April 2026. [4][5][6]

The disinformation campaign exploited a genuine and tense real-world conflict between Leo XIV and Trump. In April 2026, the pope called for peace in response to the Iran war, prompting Trump to attack him on social media as "WEAK on Crime, and terrible for Foreign Policy." Trump subsequently shared an AI-generated image depicting himself as a Jesus-like figure, which was later deleted. This real conflict created the ideal viral substrate: audiences were primed to believe escalations that never actually occurred. Fabricators running AI content farms on Facebook seized on the tension to generate engagement-maximizing false stories, with suspicious links in comment sections directing readers to monetized blog sites. [7][8][9]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The two primary false claims both originated from AI content farms operating on Facebook. The "fiery sermon after Trump called him 'insult to Jesus'" claim was traced to a Facebook account called **"Pontiff Leo Era,"** described by fact-checkers as generating content "created by artificial intelligence." The posts followed a recognizable template: a sensational headline claiming the pope had delivered a remarkable anti-Trump speech, accompanied by blog links in the comment sections promising further details. The 3am "urgent message" claim followed the same structural pattern — posts originating from Facebook accounts and associated blog pages that use AI tools to generate fictional but emotionally resonant stories about public figures, frequently accompanied by images of Trump to exploit political grievance.

The broader AI video disinformation strand — fabricated sermons on YouTube and TikTok — originated from channels with names like "Pope Leo XIV Vision" and "Pope Leo XIV Sermons," which systematically published AI-generated video content depicting the pope delivering speeches he never gave. One detected deepfake showed a manipulated version of Leo XIV's first official press conference, with the audio altered to make him appear to praise Ibrahim Traore, the military leader of Burkina Faso.

### Propagation

Facebook served as the primary launch platform for the text-based false claims, using the architecture of page-and-blog-link farming: a Facebook page publishes a provocative post, generates shares and comments, then channels traffic to an external blog where ad revenue accrues. YouTube and TikTok served as the primary distribution channels for AI video content, with some material crossing over to WhatsApp via direct sharing among religious communities. Parishioners alarmed by fake papal sermons contacted diocesan staff to ask whether the claimed liturgical changes were real, demonstrating penetration into offline faith communities. A major Catholic TV network broadcast AI-generated content before staff identified it and issued warnings to viewers.

### Why It Spread

Several reinforcing factors drove the velocity and reach of this campaign:

1. **Real underlying conflict**: The genuine Trump-Leo XIV tension over the Iran war and immigration gave fabricators a plausible backdrop. Audiences already predisposed to believe the conflict was escalating found the fabricated escalations credible.
2. **Novelty of the subject**: As a newly elected, historically unprecedented American pope, Leo XIV had low public familiarity. Most Facebook users could not confidently say what he had or had not actually said, lowering the cognitive friction required to accept false claims.
3. **Religious and political polarization**: Claims pitting the pope against Trump mapped onto existing political and religious identity fault lines, incentivizing both sharing (to validate one's side) and emotional reaction.
4. **AI production at scale**: The low cost of AI text and video generation allowed farm operators to produce high volumes of content across multiple accounts, making any individual takedown ineffective.
5. **Platform recommendation algorithms**: YouTube and TikTok recommendation systems surfaced "Pope Leo XIV" content to users searching for legitimate information about the new pope, inadvertently amplifying fabricated channels alongside authentic ones.

### Evidence Against Claims

**"Fiery sermon / insult to Jesus" claim:**
Fact-checkers at Snopes and MEAWW confirmed that searches across Google, DuckDuckGo, and Bing returned no credible news sources reporting either that Trump called Leo XIV "an insult to Jesus" or that Leo XIV delivered any "fiery sermon" in response. No major news organization — including outlets that were actively covering the real Trump-Leo XIV conflict in April 2026 — reported these specific events. The origin Facebook account "Pontiff Leo Era" exhibited all hallmarks of an AI content farm. MEAWW concluded: "The claims made in viral Facebook posts are not true, as there is no credible evidence to prove that Pope Leo XIV gave a 'fiery sermon' after Donald Trump's insult." [10]

**"3am urgent message" claim:**
Snopes rated this claim false. The posts, originating from Facebook accounts and blog pages using AI generation, claimed the pope had started an unexpected livestream to warn of a threatening message from a powerful politician. No such livestream occurred. Vatican communications — fully documented — showed no such broadcast. The structure of the claim (3am urgency, threatening politician, live broadcast) follows a well-documented template for AI content farms targeting religious audiences with apocalyptic-feeling false alerts.

**AI-generated video sermons:**
The Catholic Times of Columbus documented that fabricated AI videos falsely depicted the pope announcing sweeping liturgical changes. Deacon Tom Berg Jr. identified the videos as AI-generated from their "animation-like look and semi-robotic sound and tone." A telltale error: the AI-generated content repeatedly referred to the pope as "Pope Leo I-dash-15" rather than "Pope Leo XIV." Mainstream news media had not covered the alleged changes, and the Vatican issued no implementation guidance — both reliable negative indicators.
