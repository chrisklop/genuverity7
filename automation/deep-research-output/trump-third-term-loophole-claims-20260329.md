## Executive Summary

On March 29, 2025, a Daily Mail article titled "Revealed: How Trump could be president until 2037 due to a simple loophole in the Constitution" revived a 1999 academic paper arguing that the 22nd Amendment's use of the word "elected" — rather than "serve" or "hold" — means a twice-elected president could return to office via succession rather than direct election. The next day, March 30, 2025, President Donald Trump told NBC News's Kristen Welker in a Sunday morning phone interview that he was "not joking" about a third term, adding: "There are methods which you could do it." He described one scenario where Vice President JD Vance wins the 2028 election and then turns the presidency over to Trump.

The theory originates in a 1999 Minnesota Law Review paper, "The Twice and Future President," by Bruce Peabody and Scott Gant, which argued that the 22nd Amendment only bars *reelection* — not succession — to the presidency. Peabody, now a professor at Fairleigh Dickinson University, updated the argument in 2016. In 2025, Yale Law's Akhil Reed Amar — one of America's most-cited constitutional scholars — acknowledged "there is a possible loophole" while expressing alarm, and Harvard emeritus constitutional scholar Laurence Tribe warned the 22nd Amendment "doesn't bar *serving* a third term, only being *elected* three times."

However, the overwhelming consensus of legal scholars is that the loophole is "implausible." The primary constitutional barrier is the 12th Amendment (1804), which states: "No person constitutionally ineligible to the office of President shall be eligible to that of Vice-President of the United States." If Trump is ineligible to be elected president a third time, he is therefore also ineligible to serve as vice president — which collapses the VP-succession scheme at its foundation. Experts from Georgetown, Northwestern, UNC, Hofstra, and University of Wisconsin all independently reached this conclusion.

Beyond legal arguments, Trump's own public behavior demonstrates the claim's political rather than legal character: the official Trump Store began selling "Trump 2028" hats for $50 in April 2025, Trump said in May 2025 he would "be a two-term president," and as recently as March 22–24, 2026, Trump reposted third-term content on Truth Social with imagery reading "3RD TERM FOR TRUMP." The claim functions as political theater and merchandise opportunity, not a serious constitutional strategy.

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin and Lineage of the Claim

The "loophole" theory is not new. It traces to a 1999 law review article, "The Twice and Future President: Constitutional Interstices and the Twenty-Second Amendment" (Minnesota Law Review, Vol. 83, No. 3, pp. 565–635), co-authored by Bruce G. Peabody (then a graduate student, now a professor of politics at Fairleigh Dickinson University) and Scott E. Gant. The paper's core argument: Congress chose the word "elected" in the 22nd Amendment after considering and rejecting language that would have made a twice-elected president ineligible to *hold* the office again. Therefore, the amendment only restricts direct election — not succession.

Peabody revisited the argument in 2016. In 2025, the theory re-emerged when the Daily Mail published its article on March 29 — exactly the day before Trump's NBC interview. This timing suggests either the interview prompted the article or both reflected a coordinated communications moment. Peabody told media in 2025: "I think it's one of those surprisingly straightforward scenarios. What seemed crazy a couple of decades ago now seems all too plausible."

### The Vice President Succession Scenario (The "Vance Route")

The primary mechanism discussed:
1. Trump runs as Vance's vice-presidential running mate in 2028
2. Vance wins; Trump is inaugurated as VP
3. Vance resigns (or is otherwise removed)
4. Trump, as VP, succeeds to the presidency under the 25th Amendment's succession clause
5. Trump argues he was never *elected* president a third time — only *succeeded* to it

The argument's textualist hook: the 22nd Amendment says "no person shall be *elected* to the office of the President more than twice" — and Trump was never "elected" in step 4.

### Why the Loophole Fails (The 12th Amendment Block)

The dominant expert view is that this route is foreclosed by the 12th Amendment, ratified in 1804. Its final clause reads: "No person constitutionally ineligible to the office of President shall be eligible to that of Vice-President of the United States."

The argument: if the 22nd Amendment makes Trump ineligible to be elected president, then by virtue of the 12th Amendment, he is also ineligible to serve as VP. Barry Burden (University of Wisconsin-Madison Elections Research Center): "The 12th Amendment states that anyone who is ineligible to be president is also deemed to be ineligible to serve as vice president... This means that Trump could not serve as vice president, which is the post he would need for the Vance scheme to be executed."

David A. Super (Georgetown University Law Center) called the loophole argument "implausible, primarily because of its clear misinterpretation" of the 12th Amendment. Paul Gowder (Northwestern Pritzker School of Law) said it "defeats the clear intent of the 22nd Amendment" and is "pretty implausible."

### The Dissenting Expert Views

A small but notable group of scholars acknowledge theoretical possibility:

- **Akhil Reed Amar** (Sterling Professor of Law and Political Science, Yale): "There is a possible loophole. I wish it weren't true, but there is." He has identified a separate scenario: Vance wins, appoints Trump as Secretary of State, others in the line of succession resign, and Trump ascends. However, Amar describes this as deeply concerning, not as a legitimate path.

- **Laurence Tribe** (Harvard Law, emeritus): Warned via social media that "The 22d dsn't bar *serving* a 3d term, only being *elected* 3 times" and "The 12th dsn't bar running for VP unless 'ineligible' to serve as Pres, but Trump isn't ineligible." Tribe's interpretation: the 22nd Amendment makes Trump ineligible to be *elected*, but does not technically make him ineligible to *serve* — thus the 12th Amendment's "constitutionally ineligible" bar doesn't apply in the VP eligibility context. This is a minority view sharply contested by other scholars.

- **Michael C. Dorf** (Cornell Law): Describes the word "elected" in the 22nd Amendment as "an unfortunate drafting error" that creates ambiguity — but does not endorse the loophole as viable.

- **Brian Kalt** (Michigan State University): Acknowledges that succession rather than election could theoretically provide a path, but notes it would face overwhelming legal and political resistance.

### Congressional History and Legislative Intent

The legislative history is the loophole's weakest point. When Congress drafted the 22nd Amendment in 1947, it specifically considered and rejected language that would have made a twice-elected president ineligible to "hold" the office. However, Deborah Pearlstein (Princeton): the amendment's history "makes clear beyond doubt it was written that way to guard against the danger that anyone could use the office to assert long-term tyrannical control." The Congressional Research Service noted such debates "may be more unlikely than unconstitutional" — suggesting that even if the loophole technically existed, it would face profound practical obstacles.

Bruce Fein (constitutional law expert): "There are zero constitutional methods to circumvent the 22nd Amendment." He further warned: "If Trump flouts the 22nd Amendment, he will have no scruples defying court orders — summoning the military to arrest and imprison judges, legislators, and political opponents."

### The Amendment Repeal Path

Some Republican lawmakers pursued a straightforward approach: Rep. Andy Ogles (R-TN) introduced House Joint Resolution 29 in the 119th Congress to amend the Constitution to allow Trump to be elected up to three times. The resolution was referred to the House Judiciary Committee and has not advanced, as any amendment requires two-thirds approval of both chambers and ratification by 38 states.

### Trump's Own Inconsistency

Trump's statements have been systematically inconsistent, suggesting political signaling rather than legal strategy:

- **March 30, 2025**: "There are methods which you could do it... I'm not joking." (NBC Welker interview)
- **Late March 2025** (exact date varies): Regarding the Vance-resignation scenario: "I would rule that out because it's too cute." (PolitiFact)
- **May 4, 2025**: "I'll be an eight-year president, I'll be a two-term president. I always thought that was very important." (NBC Meet the Press)
- **August 2025**: Would like a third term, but would "probably not" run. (CNBC Squawk Box)
- **October 2025**: "I would love to do it" and "I have my best (polling) numbers ever." (PolitiFact interview)
- **March 22–24, 2026**: Reposts Truth Social image: "3RD TERM FOR TRUMP AS A REWARD FROM STOLEN ELECTION."

This oscillation — joking, not joking, two-term president, would love it — is consistent with using the third-term narrative as a political pressure release and attention mechanism, not as genuine legal planning.

## Depth Expansion — Pass A: Primary Legal Scholarship

### The Coenen Dissent: A Major Academic Counterpoint

The most significant academic counterweight to the mainstream "loophole fails" consensus is a 2015 article in the **Boston College Law Review** by **Dan T. Coenen** (University of Georgia School of Law): *"Two-Time Presidents and the Vice-Presidency."*

Coenen systematically analyzed four sub-questions: (1) Is a two-term president wholly ineligible for the vice-presidency? (2) Is such a person barred from *election* to the VP slot even if appointable? (3) Is a twice-elected president who reaches the VP role incapable of *succeeding* to the presidency? (4) If such succession occurs, does the resulting term expire after two years?

His **conclusion** — reached through close reading of Article II's qualifications clause, the 12th Amendment's VP eligibility language, and the 22nd Amendment's post-service limitations — is that **a twice-elected president may become VP (whether by election or appointment) and thereafter succeed to the presidency for the full remainder of the pending term.** Coenen's analysis turns on his reading of "constitutionally ineligible" in the 12th Amendment: he argues it refers only to the natural-born citizen, age, and residency requirements of Article II — not to the 22nd Amendment's electoral bar — because the latter applies to *election*, not to service.

This represents one of the most careful constitutional law treatments of the question, and its conclusion directly supports the loophole's viability — though Coenen's 2015 work predates the current political context and he has not publicly advocated for Trump to use it.

### 22nd Amendment Legislative History: The Anti-Tyranny Intent

The 22nd Amendment's drafting record reveals an explicitly anti-authoritarian purpose. Key congressional statements during the 1947 ratification debates (sourced from The Conversation's 2025 analysis of the legislative record):

- **Sen. Chapman Revercomb**: Holding power beyond two terms "would be a definite step in the direction of autocracy, regardless of the name given the office."
- **Rep. Edward McCowen**: The amendment would be "a great step toward preventing a dictatorship or some totalitarian form of government."
- **Rep. John Jennings Jr.**: Only through its adoption "can the people be assured that we shall never have a dictator."

The use of "elected" rather than "serve" in the amendment's text reflects a deliberate structural choice: it preserves a limited exception allowing a vice-presidential succession president to serve up to 10 years (if they inherit the office partway through a term) — but the legislative record shows this exception was not intended to enable a twice-elected president to return via the VP route. The FDR precedent driving the amendment was explicitly about preventing exactly that kind of long-term executive entrenchment.

### CREW's Legal Campaign (November 2025)

Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) published a comprehensive report on November 6, 2025, documenting the 22nd Amendment's ratification history and undertaking active legal work to defend it:

- **41 state legislatures** ratified the 22nd Amendment with bipartisan support across all regions
- **9 states never ratified** (CREW has published a separate investigation into those states)
- CREW submitted **FOIA requests to the Department of Justice** seeking OLC records about any internal legal analysis of Trump's third-term eligibility
- The report notes Trump's admission that "it is pretty clear" he cannot run again, while flagging that his public behavior contradicts this acknowledgment

## Depth Expansion — Pass C: Contemporary Context

### International Parallels: Putin and Bloomberg

**Vladimir Putin's "Zeroing" (Russia, 2020):** The Trump third-term scenario has a direct international parallel in Putin's 2020 constitutional maneuver. Putin oversaw a referendum that "zeroed" his existing presidential terms — allowing him to run for two more six-year terms, potentially keeping him in power until 2036 at age 83. The key mechanism: a late amendment introduced by cosmonaut **Valentina Tereshkova** into an omnibus constitutional revision package, framed as a minor technicality. The Council of Europe subsequently published analysis condemning "ad hominem term-limit waivers" — constitutional changes designed to benefit a specific individual — as illegitimate regardless of formal legality.

The Trump scenario differs structurally: rather than a constitutional reset, it would exploit a textual ambiguity. But the political logic is similar — using legal formalism to circumvent the anti-authoritarian intent of term limits.

**Michael Bloomberg's NYC Third Term (2008–2009):** In October 2008, the New York City Council voted to extend term limits, allowing Mayor Michael Bloomberg to run for a third consecutive four-year term. Bloomberg won in 2009. However, in 2010, **city voters reversed the extension by a landslide**, restoring the two-term limit. The Bloomberg case illustrates that even democratically enacted expansions of term limits face backlash — and that the public's instinct to preserve term limits is robust even when the specific officeholder is broadly popular (Bloomberg's approval was ~50% at the time of the Council vote).

### Bannon's Actual Strategy: 2028 Positioning, Not Legal Advice

Axios reported in January 2026 that Steve Bannon is laying groundwork for his *own* potential 2028 presidential run — with insiders acknowledging he likely isn't serious about winning but wants to shape the MAGA debate and pressure Republican candidates toward an "America First" platform (non-interventionist foreign policy, economic populism, anti-Big Tech).

This suggests the third-term narrative serves at least two political functions simultaneously: (1) it keeps Trump politically relevant and potentially deters primary challengers who fear running against him hypothetically, and (2) it gives Bannon a platform to position himself as the ideological custodian of MAGA if Trump ultimately doesn't (or can't) run in 2028.

### Structural Safeguards Beyond the Courts

If a third-term attempt were made, institutional barriers beyond judicial review include:

- **State election secretaries of state** — as the Bloomberg case showed, state-level officials have significant discretion over ballot eligibility; at least one state (California's SB-via-Tom Umberg) has introduced preemptive legislation to block Trump from the 2028 ballot
- **The Electoral Count Act** and federal election law would require any succession scenario to survive Congressional certification
