## Executive Summary

President Donald Trump has repeatedly claimed ahead of his February 24, 2026 State of the Union address that Americans are "now paying the lowest price anywhere in the world for drugs" thanks to his administration's negotiations with pharmaceutical companies. [1] This claim is false and misleading on multiple levels.

While the Trump administration did launch TrumpRx.gov on February 5, 2026, offering discounted cash prices for 43 brand-name drugs from five manufacturers, independent fact-checkers and health policy experts found no evidence that these voluntary deals have led to broad decreases in drug prices. [2] The median list price increase for hundreds of brand-name drugs in early 2026 was 4% — exactly the same as 2025, according to drug pricing research firm 46brooklyn. [3] All 16 pharmaceutical companies that signed "Most Favored Nation" (MFN) pricing deals with the Trump administration still raised prices on 872 brand-name drugs in January 2026, including cancer treatments, heart failure drugs, and COVID vaccines. [4]

The claim that Americans are paying the "lowest prices in the world" is contradicted by comprehensive international data. According to RAND Corporation analysis of 2022 data published in 2024, U.S. prescription drug prices are 2.78 times higher than other developed nations on average, and 4.22 times higher for brand-name drugs specifically. [5] The U.S. accounts for 62% of global pharmaceutical sales but only 24% of volume — a disproportionate share that has grown over time. [6] Trump has also made mathematically impossible claims about cutting drug prices by "400, 500, and even 600 percent" or "1,200 to 1,500 percent," which fact-checkers note would require drug companies to pay patients to take medications. [7] Multiple economists called these percentage claims "total fiction" and "not logical." [8]

The TrumpRx.gov website itself presents significant limitations: it only offers cash prices (purchases don't count toward insurance deductibles), explicitly excludes Medicare and Medicaid beneficiaries, and more than half of the 43 drugs listed have cheaper generic alternatives available through GoodRx or Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs. [9] For example, the antidepressant Pristiq costs $200/month on TrumpRx but only $16.65 for the generic version on Cost Plus Drugs — a savings of over $2,000 annually. [10] Health policy experts concluded that while TrumpRx may help a small share of uninsured patients, "it is not going to change the whole drug pricing landscape for most Americans." [11]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin: The Claim's Genesis

The core claim originated from President Trump himself in a January 27, 2026 speech in Iowa, where he stated: "We now are paying the lowest price anywhere in the world for drugs." [12] This claim was formalized and amplified through the February 5, 2026 launch of TrumpRx.gov, a government website that prominently features the tagline "the world's lowest prices on prescription drugs." [13]

The White House framed this as the culmination of Trump's May 12, 2025 Executive Order on "Delivering Most-Favored-Nation Prescription Drug Pricing to American Patients," which directed HHS to establish MFN price targets matching the lowest prices charged in OECD countries. [14] Following a September 29, 2025 compliance deadline with threats of tariffs and regulatory action, 16 pharmaceutical companies signed voluntary agreements to lower prices on select drugs for Medicaid and cash-paying customers. [15]

The claim escalated over time, with Trump making increasingly exaggerated percentage claims. He stated he would cut drug prices by "1,200, 1,300, 1,400, 1,500%" and "400, 500, and even 600 percent." [16] These mathematical impossibilities (cutting a price by more than 100% would mean negative costs) suggest either fundamental misunderstanding of price reduction math or deliberate exaggeration for political effect.

### Propagation: How It Spread

The claim propagated through multiple official and semi-official channels:

1. **White House Infrastructure**: Official fact sheets from the White House described TrumpRx as offering "the most impactful prescription price reset in the history of our country" and claimed patients would access "the world's lowest prices." [17]

2. **Government Website**: TrumpRx.gov itself became a propaganda vehicle, prominently displaying claims of "world's lowest prices" and discounts of 50-93% off list prices (though not off actual insured costs). [18]

3. **Presidential Speeches**: Trump repeated variations of the claim in multiple speeches leading up to the February 24 State of the Union, including at campaign-style rallies and official White House addresses. [19]

4. **Pharmaceutical Company Press Releases**: Companies including Pfizer, Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, AstraZeneca, and others issued press releases about their MFN agreements, lending corporate credibility to the narrative of price reductions. [20]

5. **Media Amplification**: Conservative media outlets and some mainstream news sources covered the TrumpRx launch, often repeating administration claims without sufficient independent verification.

The claim benefited from the complexity of drug pricing — most Americans don't understand the difference between list prices, net prices, rebates, and actual out-of-pocket costs, making misleading claims harder to immediately debunk.

### Why It Spread: The Perfect Storm

Several factors contributed to the claim's resonance:

**1. Kernel of Truth**: TrumpRx does offer real discounts on specific drugs. For example, Ozempic and Wegovy dropped from ~$1,000-1,300/month to as low as $199/month for cash-paying patients. [21] These headline-grabbing numbers made the broader claim seem plausible.

**2. Addressing Real Pain**: Prescription drug costs are a genuine crisis for millions of Americans. The U.S. spends $1,126 per capita on prescription drugs compared to $552 in peer nations. [22] Any promise of relief resonates deeply with struggling patients.

**3. Official Credibility**: Claims came directly from the President and official White House channels, with a professional government website (TrumpRx.gov) as tangible proof. This lent authority that made skepticism seem partisan.

**4. Pre-SOTU Timing**: The February 5 launch positioned the claim perfectly for the February 24 State of the Union address, creating a media narrative of "Trump delivers on drug prices" during peak political attention.

**5. Complexity Shields Scrutiny**: Drug pricing involves Byzantine systems of list prices, PBMs, rebates, formularies, and international comparisons. This complexity creates space for misleading claims to survive initial coverage before detailed fact-checks emerge.

**6. Political Incentives**: Both Trump (wanting a policy win) and pharmaceutical companies (avoiding tariffs and regulations) had incentives to portray the MFN deals as more impactful than they actually were.

### Evidence: What the Data Actually Shows

**Claim vs. Reality on "Lowest Prices in the World":**

The claim that Americans now pay the "lowest prices in the world" is directly contradicted by comprehensive international data:

- **RAND Corporation (2024)**: Analysis of 2022 data found U.S. drug prices are 2.78× higher than 33 OECD comparison countries on average. For brand-name drugs specifically, U.S. prices are 4.22× higher. [23]
- **Market Share Disparity**: The U.S. represents 62% of global pharmaceutical sales but only 24% of volume consumed — proof Americans are paying far more per unit. [24]
- **Country Comparisons**: U.S. prices range from 1.72× Mexico's prices to 10.28× Turkey's prices. For specific drugs, Humira costs 423% more in the U.S. than in the UK. [25]
- **Per Capita Spending**: Americans spent $1,126 per person on prescription drugs in 2019 vs. $552 average in comparable countries — roughly double. [26]

**Claim vs. Reality on "Broad Price Decreases":**

Trump's claim implies widespread drug price reductions. The evidence shows the opposite:

- **46brooklyn Research**: Drug companies raised prices on 872 brand-name drugs in January 2026, with a median increase of 4% — identical to 2025. [27]
- **All 16 MFN Deal Companies Raised Prices**: Every pharmaceutical company that signed an MFN deal with Trump still increased list prices on at least some drugs in January 2026. [28]
- **Specific Examples**: Pfizer (the first company to sign a deal) raised prices on 72 products, including a 15% increase on its COVID shot. Merck raised prices on 18 products including HIV and insomnia medications. [29]
- **Expert Assessment**: Rena Conti (Boston University health economist) concluded the negotiations "don't appear to have translated into actual savings for people at the pharmacy counter" except in rare cases. [30]

**Claim vs. Reality on TrumpRx "World's Lowest Prices":**

The TrumpRx.gov website claims to offer "the world's lowest prices," but independent analysis revealed:

- **Cheaper Generics Exist**: At least 18 of the 43 brand-name drugs on TrumpRx have cheaper generic alternatives available via GoodRx or Cost Plus Drugs. [31]
- **Specific Price Comparisons**:
  - Pristiq (antidepressant): $200/month on TrumpRx vs. $16.65/month generic on Cost Plus Drugs [32]
  - Annual Pristiq cost: $2,401.20 on TrumpRx vs. $320.88 generic on GoodRx/Cost Plus — saving $2,080 by avoiding TrumpRx [33]
  - Colestid (cholesterol): $67.20 on TrumpRx vs. $21.70 generic version [34]
- **GoodRx Overlap**: Many TrumpRx prices are powered by GoodRx's existing infrastructure, with identical or near-identical prices for brand-name drugs that were already available. [35]

**Mathematical Impossibility of Percentage Claims:**

Trump's claims of cutting prices by "400-600%" or "1,200-1,500%" are mathematically incoherent:

- **Math Reality**: Cutting a $100 drug price by 100% = $0. Cutting by 500% would = -$400 (drug company pays you $400). [36]
- **Expert Reactions**:
  - Geoffrey Joyce (USC): "total fiction" [37]
  - Timothy McBride (Washington University): "just not logical... no drug company in the world would give a person $400 to buy a drug they are now pricing at $100" [38]
  - Fox News Host John Roberts: "If you cut it by 4-, 5-, 600 percent, the drug companies are actually paying you to take their product" [39]
- **Probable Source**: Trump appears to be confusing the percentage by which U.S. prices *exceed* foreign prices (e.g., 520% more for Zepbound in U.S. vs. Europe) with the percentage by which prices would be *cut* — two completely different calculations. [40]

**Who Actually Benefits from TrumpRx:**

The impact is far narrower than Trump's universal-sounding claims suggest:

- **Cash-Only Limitation**: TrumpRx discounts only apply to cash-paying patients. Purchases don't count toward insurance deductibles or out-of-pocket maximums. [41]
- **Government Program Exclusion**: Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, and VA beneficiaries are explicitly excluded from TrumpRx cash-pay pricing. [42]
- **Limited Coverage**: Only 43 drugs from 5 manufacturers as of February 2026, compared to thousands of prescription drugs available in the U.S. [43]
- **Expert Assessment**: Yunan Ji (Georgetown): "For a small share of the market, it will deliver meaningful savings, but it is not going to change the whole drug pricing landscape for most Americans." [44]
- **Rare Genuine Wins**: GLP-1 weight loss drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Zepbound) and fertility medications saw meaningful reductions for uninsured patients, as these are often not covered by insurance to begin with. [45]

**The Biden IRA Connection:**

Trump's claims omit crucial context: the most significant drug price reductions in 2026 came from the Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), not Trump's MFN deals:

- **IRA Medicare Negotiation**: The first 10 drugs negotiated under the IRA (passed in 2022 under Biden) saw price discounts of 38-79% off list prices, with $6 billion in projected Medicare savings in 2026 plus $1.5 billion in patient out-of-pocket savings. [46]
- **Specific IRA Price Cuts**: Januvia (-79%), Eliquis (-43%), Jardiance (-44%), and Fiasp insulin (-75%) all saw major reductions in January 2026 due to IRA negotiations taking effect. [47]
- **Political Irony**: Trump's administration is implementing a program created by Democrats that Republicans opposed. KFF noted both parties can claim credit: Trump for delivering lower prices, Democrats for building the program. [48]

## Congressional Budget Office Analysis: Part D Cost Crisis

The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) revealed in a February 2026 analysis that **Medicare Part D spending is surging far beyond projections**, threatening to add **$500 billion in unexpected costs over the next decade**. [49]

Recent bids from private insurance plans anticipate a **35% increase in annual per-enrollee costs in 2026** — seven times higher than the 5% increase CBO had projected. [50] This massive discrepancy stems from several factors:

- **Insurer overhead costs** revised upward from 6.5% to 11.4% of net benefit costs, citing new cost-sharing limits and flexibilities from the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act [51]
- **Specialty drug spending surges** revealed in early 2025 claims data, particularly for beneficiaries whose cost-sharing was most affected by benefit changes [52]
- **Tariff uncertainty** creating instability for insurers, as potential pharmaceutical tariffs could increase the cost of providing Part D drug benefits [53]

From 2017 to 2022, annual growth in projected per-enrollee costs for Part D plans ranged from a 5% reduction to a 4% increase. Since then, projections have skyrocketed: **20% in 2024, 42% in 2025, and 35% in 2026**. [54]

The CBO has opened a formal avenue for independent researchers to submit new research on policies impacting patient access to new medicines, acknowledging that current drug development models need fundamental revision. [55]

## CMS Data: Medicare Negotiation Delivers Real Savings

While Trump's MFN deals produced limited impact, the Biden-era Inflation Reduction Act negotiations delivered substantial, verifiable savings:

- **10 drugs negotiated** under IRA saw price reductions of 38-79% off 2023 list prices [56]
- **$6 billion in Medicare savings** projected for 2026 alone [57]
- **$1.5 billion in patient out-of-pocket savings** for Medicare beneficiaries in 2026 [58]
- **19 million Medicare beneficiaries** saved an average of $400/year in 2025 from IRA provisions [59]

Specific negotiated prices taking effect January 1, 2026:
- **Januvia** (diabetes): $113/month (down 79% from $527 list price) — serving 850,000 beneficiaries who accounted for $4 billion in Part D spending [60]
- **Eliquis** (blood thinner): $231 negotiated price (43% discount) [61]
- **Jardiance** (diabetes/heart failure): 44% price reduction [62]
- **Fiasp** (insulin): 75% price reduction [63]

The next round of IRA negotiations will cover **15 additional drugs** in 2027, including blockbuster medications like Ozempic, accounting for $41 billion in Part D spending and serving 5.3 million beneficiaries. [64]

## HHS Most Favored Nation Implementation Details

The HHS press release on MFN pricing targets revealed enforcement mechanisms and specific expectations:

- **MFN target price**: The lowest price in any OECD country with a GDP per capita of at least 60% of U.S. GDP per capita [65]
- **Compliance deadline**: September 29, 2025, with threats to "deploy every tool in our arsenal" including tariffs, import expansion, and approval revocations [66]
- **Expected manufacturer commitments**: Extend MFN price to every Medicaid patient, contract with U.S. to guarantee Medicare/Medicaid/commercial payers receive MFN price for newly launched drugs, participate in direct-to-consumer distribution models [67]

The **GENEROUS Model**, launching in 2026, allows CMS to negotiate with participating manufacturers for lower prices while states adopt uniform, transparent coverage criteria to ensure Medicaid pricing "on par with those in other developed nations." [68]

However, legal experts note significant challenges: industry lawsuits challenge executive authority to impose international reference pricing, raising administrative overreach concerns. Additionally, confidential rebates and risk-sharing agreements cause international list prices to diverge from actual transaction prices, making accurate international price comparisons difficult. [69]

## Medicare Part D Out-of-Pocket Cap: Winners and Losers

The Inflation Reduction Act's out-of-pocket cap provides real relief for high-spending Medicare beneficiaries, but the structure creates winners and losers:

**2026 Cap Details:**
- **$2,100 annual out-of-pocket maximum** (up from $2,000 in 2025, reflecting 5% adjustment for drug spending increases) [70]
- **$615 maximum deductible** (up from $590 in 2025) [71]
- **$0 cost-sharing** after hitting the cap for remainder of calendar year [72]
- **3.2 million Americans** expected to benefit from the cap in 2025, rising to 4 million by 2029 [73]

**Premium trends:**
- Average stand-alone Part D premium: **$34.50 in 2026** (down $3.81 from 2025) [74]
- Average Medicare Advantage + Part D premium: **$11.50 in 2026** (down $1.82 from 2025) [75]

**Critical limitations:**
- Cap does **not** cover drugs not on the Part D formulary, Part B drugs, or monthly premiums [76]
- The shift from $8,000 catastrophic threshold (2024) to $2,000 cap (2025) dramatically improved affordability, but the 2026 increase to $2,100 represents a 5% erosion [77]

**Patient confusion:**
Multiple Medicare beneficiaries reported on forums that their 2026 costs for negotiated drugs like Eliquis and Xarelto remained unchanged or increased, questioning, "When does this reduction take effect?" — revealing a gap between announced savings and actual pharmacy counter experiences. [78]

## Congressional Action on Drug Pricing: 2026 Consolidated Appropriations Act

On **February 3, 2026**, Congress passed and President Trump signed the **Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026 (CAA 26)**, ending a partial government shutdown and including **landmark PBM (Pharmacy Benefit Manager) reform** that will reshape drug pricing infrastructure. [143]

### Key PBM Reforms (Effective 2028):

**1. Delinking PBM Compensation:**
Medicare Part D plans must compensate PBMs solely through **flat, bona fide service fees** that reflect fair-market value, eliminating rebate-linked compensation that incentivized high-list-price drugs. [144]

**2. 100% Rebate Pass-Through:**
PBMs must remit **100% of rebates, fees, alternative discounts, and other remuneration** from manufacturers to plan clients on a **quarterly basis** (within 90 days of quarter-end). [145]

**3. Transparency & Reporting:**
PBMs must provide employer health plans with **detailed, semiannual reports** on net drug spending, rebates, and "spread pricing" arrangements. [146]

**4. Enhanced Audit Rights:**
Prescription drug plan sponsors can conduct **comprehensive annual audits** of PBMs, with access to all relevant records, data, contracts, and supporting documentation from PBM affiliates. [147]

**5. "Any Willing Pharmacy" Provision:**
Starting in 2028, prescription drug plan sponsors must allow **any pharmacy meeting standard contract terms** to participate in their networks, opening doors for independent and community pharmacies. [148]

**Fiscal Impact:** Congressional Budget Office projects the legislation will **reduce the federal deficit by $2.1 billion over 10 years**. [149]

**Political Context:** The PBM reform passed with **bipartisan support**, representing rare common ground on healthcare between Republicans (who support market transparency) and Democrats (who support reducing drug costs). House Representative Deborah Ross stated: "This bill combats harmful PBM practices and lowers prescription drug prices for American families." [150]
