## Executive Summary

For more than six decades, fossil fuel companies including ExxonMobil, BP, Chevron, Shell, and the American Petroleum Institute maintained accurate internal knowledge that burning fossil fuels causes catastrophic climate change — and simultaneously funded, coordinated, and executed one of the most consequential disinformation campaigns in American history [1][2][3]. A 2024 joint bicameral staff report by Senator Sheldon Whitehouse and Representative Jamie Raskin documented that fossil fuel companies knew about climate change "for more than 60 years" while spending hundreds of millions of dollars to undermine public understanding, manufacture doubt about settled science, and block legislative action [4]. The 2023 Harvard-led analysis published in *Science* confirmed that ExxonMobil's internal models predicted global warming with 72% accuracy from the 1970s onward — while the company's public communications systematically promoted doubt [5]. This is not historical: ExxonMobil's 2025 Climate Report again failed scientific peer review according to the Union of Concerned Scientists [6].

The national security consequences of this sustained deception are now measurable and severe. The U.S. military manages approximately 300,000 buildings valued at $590 billion, with at least 30 installations already facing elevated sea-level rise risk — and three feet of rise, a conservative mid-century projection, would threaten 128 coastal bases worth $100 billion [7][8]. Naval Station Norfolk, the world's largest naval base, experiences sea-level rise at 5.6 millimeters per year — twice the global average — and faces a projected 280 tidal flood events annually by 2050, compared to 9 today [9]. Hurricane Michael alone inflicted an estimated $5 billion in damage on Tyndall Air Force Base in 2018; Typhoon Mawar caused approximately $10 billion in damage at Andersen Air Force Base in Guam in 2023 [10]. More than 10,000 troops have been sidelined by heat-related illnesses since 2018, and the fallout from warming has deployed the U.S. military to more than 240 extreme weather crises globally since 2022 [10].

Disinformation has now metastasized beyond industry funding into foreign adversary operations and AI-enabled content farms. Russia exploited Hurricane Helene, the Hawaii wildfires, and European flooding in 2024 to claim that Western support for Ukraine left nations vulnerable — using climate disasters as force multipliers for geopolitical destabilization [11][12]. China falsely attributed the Maui wildfire deaths to a U.S. weather control weapon [12]. The 2025 U.S. Annual Threat Assessment, under DNI Tulsi Gabbard, became the first intelligence community report since 2009 to contain no mention of climate change — a policy-driven blindspot that former officials and security experts warn will leave the United States tactically unprepared for the cascade of security challenges a destabilizing climate produces [13][14]. Former Homeland Security Secretary Michael Chertoff warned that Americans are "less able to protect" themselves when relying on false information from adversaries or ideologically compromised platforms, noting that "reliable information is a requirement for tackling any risk" [11].

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin and Patient Zero

The organized climate disinformation campaign traces to the fossil fuel industry, with the earliest documented incident predating common knowledge. Researchers discovered archival evidence that the American Petroleum Institute was distributing false and misleading information about climate change to Congress as early as **1980** — nearly a decade before the Global Climate Coalition was formed [15]. In 1977, ExxonMobil's senior scientist James Black briefed the company's management committee that "there is general scientific agreement that the most likely manner in which mankind is influencing the global climate is through carbon dioxide release from the burning of fossil fuels" [5]. Edward Teller had explained the same mechanism to an API symposium as early as **1959** [15].

Despite this knowledge, in **1989** ExxonMobil, Mobil, Chevron, BP, Shell, and other fossil fuel companies co-founded the **Global Climate Coalition (GCC)**, explicitly to oppose emissions reductions and cast doubt on the scientific basis for concern about climate change. The GCC operated as the primary organized denial vehicle through the 1990s. When the GCC dissolved in 2002 as science became undeniable, the industry's deception strategy evolved — pivoting from outright denial to delay tactics, greenwashing, false solution promotion, and what the 2024 congressional report termed "doublespeak."

### Propagation Network

The disinformation infrastructure operates through overlapping layers:

**Funding Layer:** The fossil fuel industry channels money through 501(c)(4) nonprofits not required to disclose donors, shell companies, political action committees, and limited liability companies. Inside Climate News documented that The Epoch Times — a major climate-skeptic media outlet — generated $1.5 million in advertising revenue despite violating Google's climate denial content policies [16]. FirstEnergy paid $61 million in bribes to Ohio House Speaker Larry Householder to secure a $1.3 billion ratepayer bailout; Alabama Power funneled $900,000+ to "news" websites to generate favorable coverage [17].

**Think Tank Layer:** The Heritage Foundation, Heartland Institute, and Cato Institute receive significant fossil fuel funding and produce policy papers, congressional testimony, and media commentary designed to manufacture uncertainty. BP's internal documents showed the company had budgeted $2.5–$4.5 million for "hard persuasion" tactics targeting Washington state [4].

**Media and Social Layer:** Research published in 2025 found that "New Denial" claims — attacking climate solutions rather than the underlying science — now constitute 70% of all climate denial content on YouTube, up from 35% six years prior [16]. Generative AI has lowered the cost of producing misleading content to near zero, enabling industrial-scale output [16].

**Foreign Amplification Layer:** Russian state media amplifies existing domestic climate denial narratives without needing to manufacture them, using four broad narrative frames: Climate Denialism, "Securitization" (framing fossil fuel dependence as security strength), Green movements as Western Neocolonialism, and Green Party political interference [12]. After the Maui wildfires killed more than 100 people, Russian media suggested aid to Ukraine had left Hawaii vulnerable; Chinese state media falsely claimed a U.S. weather weapon caused the deaths [12].

### Why It Spread

Climate disinformation is structurally advantaged over accurate information in the current media environment:

1. **Economic incentives:** Producing disinformation content is cheap and generates AdTech revenue; accurate climate reporting is expensive and politically risky in fossil-fuel-dependent markets.
2. **Complexity asymmetry:** Denial claims are simple ("it's natural cycles"); scientific accuracy requires explaining feedback loops, ice core data, and multi-decadal trend analysis.
3. **Political polarization:** Climate action became coded as a liberal cultural identity marker — enabling the right to treat science as partisan, rather than factual.
4. **Regulatory gaps:** Meta discontinued its U.S. fact-checking program in 2025; platform enforcement of climate misinformation policies remains inconsistent.
5. **Elite capture:** The 2025 Intelligence Community Annual Threat Assessment — the first in over a decade to omit climate change — demonstrates that disinformation has successfully captured enough of the policy apparatus to alter official government threat calculations.
