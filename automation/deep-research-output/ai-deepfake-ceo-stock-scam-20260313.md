## Executive Summary

A fabricated AI deepfake video falsely depicting Sundararaman Ramamurthy — Managing Director and CEO of the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), the world's oldest stock exchange in Asia — began circulating on WhatsApp, Telegram, and social media platforms in early January 2026, prompting an urgent public advisory from BSE on January 12, 2026.[1][2] The video depicts Ramamurthy appearing to offer exclusive stock tips and promises of "extraordinary or super-normal profits," including specific claims that viewers could "become multi-millionaires within a short time" and accumulate "Rs 8 million by 2027."[3] BSE categorically confirmed the video is "completely fabricated and created using deepfake technology to mislead investors," and that neither Ramamurthy nor any BSE official operates investment groups on messaging platforms.[4] Despite removal efforts, the video resurfaced in March 2026, forcing BSE to issue a second advisory on March 8, 2026.[5]

The BSE CEO scam is not an isolated incident but part of a rapidly escalating pattern of AI-powered financial fraud targeting India's surging retail investor population. The National Stock Exchange (NSE) was forced to issue similar warnings in April and June 2024 after deepfake videos of its CEO circulated online.[6] In October 2025, Mumbai Police arrested four employees of ad-tech firm Valueleaf for enabling a China-linked fraud syndicate that used deepfake videos impersonating Indian stock analysts to steal crores from retail investors via WhatsApp pump-and-dump operations.[7] These scams exploit India's specific vulnerability: 47% of Indian adults have been victimized by or know someone victimized by AI voice-cloning or deepfake fraud — nearly double the global average of 25%.[8]

The mechanics follow a consistent playbook: create a convincing deepfake of a credible financial authority figure, distribute it via paid social media ads or messaging apps, funnel victims into WhatsApp or Telegram groups controlled by overseas fraud operators, display fabricated profit screenshots to build trust, extract deposits into mule accounts, then block all withdrawal attempts. Globally, deepfake-enabled financial fraud drained $1.1 billion from U.S. corporate accounts in 2025 alone — triple the $360 million recorded in 2024 — with projected AI-facilitated fraud losses expected to reach $40 billion by 2027.[9][10]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The BSE CEO deepfake video first came to public notice in January 2026, when BSE issued a formal advisory dated January 11–12, 2026. The exchange stated: "It has come to the notice of BSE Limited that a fraudulent deepfake video is being circulated on social media and messaging platforms, falsely featuring Sundararaman Ramamurthy, Managing Director & CEO, BSE, and claiming to provide stock recommendations and investment advice."[2][4]

No independent attribution of authorship has been made public. However, the operational structure — WhatsApp funnel, deepfake celebrity endorsement, false profit dashboards — mirrors the Valueleaf case in July 2025, which was attributed to a China-based cybercrime syndicate.[7] SEBI noted in a November 2025 warning that fraudsters routinely "pose as SEBI-registered intermediaries, prominent public figures, celebrities, or chief executives or managing directors of reputed organisations to build credibility."[11]

### Propagation

The video was distributed across two primary channels:

1. **Social media platforms** — Facebook, Instagram, and X/Twitter. In the Valueleaf-linked scam model, fraudulent ads were pushed as Meta-paid advertisements, bypassing organic feed algorithms by purchasing ad placements through certified Indian advertising partners.[7]

2. **Messaging platforms** — WhatsApp groups and Telegram channels. Viewers who engaged with the video were redirected to private WhatsApp or Telegram groups where operators continued the scam with fabricated trading dashboards and pressure tactics.[3][4]

The video has resurfaced multiple times despite BSE's content removal efforts, with BSE explicitly acknowledging in its March 8, 2026 advisory that "such fraudulent videos have resurfaced multiple times" despite working with law enforcement and platforms.[5]

### Why It Spread

Several structural factors accelerated propagation:

- **Institutional credibility exploitation:** BSE is the world's oldest stock exchange in Asia, founded 1875. CEO impersonation carries high perceived legitimacy with India's 160+ million retail investors.
- **WhatsApp architecture:** India is WhatsApp's largest market. Closed group dynamics prevent algorithmic fact-checking. Content forwarding multiplies reach without individual verification.
- **India's retail investor surge:** Indian demat account holders crossed 170 million by 2025. Millions of new, inexperienced investors are actively seeking stock tips, making them prime targets.
- **Low production cost:** AI deepfake tools capable of producing convincing executive impersonation videos are commercially accessible for under $100 per production, or available via Deepfake-as-a-Service platforms.
- **Human detection failure:** Studies show humans correctly identify high-quality deepfakes only 24.5% of the time, with 60% of people overestimating their ability to detect them.[10]

### Evidence of Fabrication

BSE provided direct confirmation in its official advisory:
- The video is "fake, manipulated and maliciously created using deepfake technology to mislead investors" [4]
- BSE confirmed "neither Ramamurthy nor any BSE official provides stock tips or investment recommendations" [2]
- BSE does "not operate or endorse WhatsApp, Telegram, or similar groups offering investment advice" [3]
- The content is described as "unauthorised, fake, and intended to mislead investors" [4]

### Pass A-2: India IT Rules Amendment 2026 — Primary Regulatory Text

India's Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology notified the **Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Amendment Rules, 2026** on February 10, 2026, effective February 20, 2026. This is the most significant regulatory development directly relevant to the BSE CEO deepfake scam:

**Key provisions directly applicable to this case:**

- **Statutory deepfake definition:** For the first time, Indian law formally defines deepfakes as "content generated using algorithmic or computational techniques" producing convincing audio or visuals of real individuals. Applies directly to the BSE CEO video.
- **3-hour takedown mandate:** Platforms must remove unlawful synthetically generated content within **3 hours** of notice from a court order or authorized government officer (Joint Secretary rank or above) — a 92% reduction from the prior 36-hour window. Had this rule been in force in January 2026 when BSE first issued its advisory, platforms would have faced mandatory removal obligations within hours.
- **2-hour takedown:** Non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) — including AI-generated — must be removed within 2 hours.
- **Loss of safe harbor:** Platforms that miss the 3-hour window or fail to label AI content lose Section 79 IT Act protection and become liable as if they created the content.
- **Mandatory metadata traceability:** Intermediaries must embed provenance markers that persist through file-sharing, allowing investigators to trace deepfakes to the specific AI tool used to create them.
- **User declarations:** Users creating realistic AI content must declare it synthetic; platforms must deploy technical tools to verify and label such declarations.

**Critical gap:** The amendment does not specify penalties for non-compliance, creating enforcement ambiguity.

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### Pass B-1: NSE CEO Case — Bombay High Court Precedent

The NSE CEO deepfake incidents in 2024 produced the first significant Indian court ruling on deepfake financial fraud — a precedent directly relevant to BSE's legal options:

**Bombay High Court Order, July 16, 2024:**
The court granted NSE interim relief, restraining defendants "from further distributing the fabricated videos" and ordering Meta and other intermediaries to remove infringing content within **10 hours** of receiving complaints. Critically, the court also ordered platforms to **disclose the perpetrators' identities** — a disclosure mechanism that could, if used effectively in the BSE case, help trace video creators.

**What NSE proved to obtain the order:**
- Deepfake videos falsely depicted CEO Ashishkumar Chauhan endorsing fraudulent stock-picking services
- Content used NSE's trademark without authorization (intellectual property hook)
- The fake content "posed risks of market manipulation and threatened investor confidence"
- Despite NSE's prior reports to intermediaries, platforms had delayed removing the material

**Legal holding:** The court found intermediaries must exercise due diligence under IT Rules 2021 Rule 3(1) to prevent hosting misleading content that infringes intellectual property rights.

The NSE CEO videos promised investors the CEO would "reimburse any losses incurred" — an even more aggressive deception than the BSE video's "Rs 8 million by 2027" promise, and likely the template the BSE video creators followed.

### Pass B-2: WhatsApp/Telegram Pump-and-Dump — Operational Mechanics

Research confirms the precise operational playbook used in BSE CEO-style scams:

**Stage 1 — Bait (Deepfake Ad):** Fabricated video of credible authority figure (BSE/NSE CEO, SEBI official, RBI Governor, Infosys founder) pushed as paid Meta/Instagram/Facebook advertisement. The Valueleaf case confirmed operators paid ₹3 crore specifically to purchase certified Indian ad-tech access, allowing them to run WhatsApp-linked call-to-action ads that bypassed Meta's fraud detection by using a legitimate Indian intermediary account.

**Stage 2 — Funnel (Messaging App Groups):** Victims who click are directed to invite-only WhatsApp or Telegram groups of 50–200 members, most of whom are sockpuppet accounts controlled by the fraud ring. Group "members" post fabricated profit screenshots, celebrate fake returns, and create social proof.

**Stage 3 — Trust-building (False Dashboards):** Victims are given login access to professional-looking fake trading portals showing paper profits of 200–400%. Small "test withdrawals" of ₹5,000–₹20,000 are permitted to establish trust.

**Stage 4 — Investment Extraction:** Victims are pressured to invest larger sums — typically ₹1–10 lakh — into mule bank accounts framed as "brokerage deposits." Funds immediately dispersed to multiple downstream accounts.

**Stage 5 — Block and Exit:** Withdrawal requests are blocked with reasons including "tax compliance holds," "penalty fees," or "account verification." All contact then ceases.

### Pass C-1: India's Deepfake Crisis — Scope Beyond Financial Fraud

India's deepfake vulnerability is structural, not incidental:

- Deepfake cases in India have **surged 550% since 2019**, with projected losses having reached ₹70,000 crore (approximately $8.4 billion) in 2024 alone — making India one of the world's most targeted deepfake fraud markets.
- **83% of Indian victims** of AI voice scams suffered monetary loss; almost half lost over ₹50,000.
- India's digital financial sector is experiencing a **tenfold surge in fraud over three years**, fueled by deepfakes and voice cloning, while financial literacy has failed to keep pace with digital adoption.
- AI-powered deepfake scams now account for **nearly 40% of high-value crypto frauds** in India.
- More than **80% of deepfake scam losses globally** originated on social media — Facebook, WhatsApp, and Telegram — the exact platforms used in the BSE CEO fraud.

The BSE CEO video arrives in this context as a higher-credibility vector: impersonating the chief executive of a 150-year-old stock exchange carries more institutional weight than impersonating an individual analyst or celebrity.

### Pass C-2: Global Regulatory Architecture — What's Being Built

Regulators globally moved from observation to rules in 2025–2026:

**India (most aggressive):** IT Rules Amendment 2026 (February 20) — 3-hour deepfake takedowns, mandatory AI content labeling, provenance metadata requirements, loss of platform safe harbor for non-compliance. First major economy to regulate deepfakes with statutory definitions and operational mandates.

### Pass C-3: Deepfake vs. Traditional Stock Manipulation — What Changed

Traditional stock manipulation (pump-and-dump) relied on:
- Cold-calling boiler rooms
- Paid newsletter "recommendations"
- Forum/message board coordination (the GameStop era)
- SMS blast campaigns
- Fake analyst reports

AI deepfake manipulation replaces the core trust mechanism. Traditional scams required victims to trust an anonymous stranger. Deepfake scams make the victim believe they are receiving advice from a specific, recognizable, high-credibility authority — whose face, voice, mannerisms, and institutional identity are all verified by their own visual cortex. This is not an incremental improvement in fraud technique; it is a categorical shift.

**The detection gap:** Studies confirm humans correctly identify high-quality deepfakes only 24.5% of the time, while 60% overestimate their detection ability. Traditional stock manipulation relied on social engineering; deepfake manipulation exploits the fundamental human assumption that video evidence is real — a cognitive bias that cannot be easily trained away.

**The scale gap:** Traditional pump-and-dump required geographic reach (call centers, newsletters). Deepfake investment scams require only a social media ad budget and a convincing 90-second video. The Valueleaf operation ran 38 concurrent ad accounts during peak operations, each pointing to separate WhatsApp funnels — a parallelized fraud infrastructure impossible to replicate with traditional methods.
