## Executive Summary

The Department of Homeland Security launched "Arrested: Worst of the Worst" (wow.dhs.gov) on December 8, 2025, as a searchable public database of immigrants arrested by ICE. Secretary Kristi Noem and the White House heavily promoted the site on social media, presenting it as evidence that the Trump administration was removing dangerous criminals from American communities. CNN's February 19, 2026 exclusive investigation found the site was riddled with fundamental factual errors: hundreds of individuals listed as arrested for violent crimes — including homicide and sex offenses — had actually been arrested only for traffic violations, marijuana possession, or the civil infraction of illegal reentry. After receiving CNN's questions, DHS admitted in a Tuesday statement that charges against hundreds of people had been "described incorrectly," attributing the cause to a "glitch" affecting "about 5% of the entries." The agency claimed the issue was resolved by Wednesday and then, in a defiant tone, expanded the database to 30,000+ entries. [1][2][3]

The errors run deeper than the DHS-admitted "glitch." An NPR analysis of 130 Minnesota arrest cases highlighted on DHS social media found that roughly one-in-four subjects had decades-old convictions, only minor charges like DUIs, or no criminal convictions at all — and that NPR could not confirm a matching criminal history for 37 of the 130 individuals. [4] The Minnesota Department of Corrections independently released evidence documenting 68 specific cases in which DHS falsely labeled routine prison-to-ICE custody transfers as ICE "arrests," including at least one individual — Mauricio Morales-Morales — who was still incarcerated in a Minnesota state prison when DHS claimed he was in ICE custody. [5] Meanwhile, a separate incident first reported by NOTUS revealed that the White House posted a graphic on its X account falsely accusing a real man, Victor Manuel Carranza (whose actual convictions were larceny and identity theft), of committing child sex crimes — a crime belonging to an entirely different individual. The erroneous post received at least 257,000 views before being deleted. [6]

The broader context underscores why accuracy matters: independent data from FactCheck.org, the Migration Policy Institute, and ICE's own statistics show that as of January 2026, nearly 43% of ICE arrestees had no U.S. criminal convictions or pending charges — up dramatically from 22% in the first months of the Trump administration. [7] DHS Secretary Noem's repeated claim that approximately 70% of those arrested have "criminal convictions or pending charges" conflates convictions with mere charges — individuals who are constitutionally presumed innocent. The site's errors are not isolated technical failures; they are part of a documented pattern of government authorities overstating the criminal threat posed by immigrants to justify mass enforcement operations and shape public opinion. [8]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

DHS launched wow.dhs.gov on December 8, 2025, with an initial listing of 10,000 individuals arrested by ICE during Trump's second term. The site was framed explicitly as a public accountability and transparency tool, inviting citizens to search by state, crime type, and country of origin. DHS press releases used inflammatory language — "dirtbags," "pedophiles," "rapists" — in official government communications promoting the site. The website's official name, "Arrested: Worst of the Worst," was echoed by Secretary Noem and the White House to characterize all ICE arrests as targeting only the most dangerous offenders.

### Propagation

The site's data did not stay on a government webpage — it was actively weaponized across social media. DHS and ICE posted near-daily graphics on their X accounts showcasing specific individuals, often with mug shots, names, and alleged offenses. The White House X account (3.6 million followers) amplified the posts. Kristi Noem repeatedly cited site statistics in television appearances and press conferences. Local law enforcement and congressional Republicans referenced the data in public statements. The site was designed to be searchable by state, enabling localized fear narratives — "see who ICE arrested in your community."

### Why It Spread

The site exploited a structural information asymmetry: DHS controlled the data, published it with the authority of a government seal, and the individuals listed had no platform or legal recourse to dispute their public characterization. Immigration detainees facing removal proceedings have limited access to counsel, cannot easily contest public listings, and many lack the English-language capacity to alert media to errors. Advocates warn that public-facing criminal databases permanently damage reputations — particularly when the listed "crime" is later corrected or was never accurate — because corrections receive a fraction of the original story's reach.

### Evidence of Errors

**Category 1 — Crime Mislabeling ("The Glitch"):**
DHS admitted that "about 5% of entries" on the site — roughly 1,250 individuals out of 25,000 at time of admission — had crimes incorrectly described. CNN found hundreds of people listed as arrested for violent offenses who had only minor offenses on their records: single traffic violations, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry (a federal civil/criminal charge that involves crossing the border after a previous deportation, not a violent act). DHS claimed these individuals had additional serious offenses that the glitch failed to display — but did not provide transparency into which entries were affected or what the true records were.

**Category 2 — Individual Identity Errors (White House):**
On January 26, 2026, the White House posted a graphic on X linking Victor Manuel Carranza's mug shot to child sex crime charges. Carranza's actual convictions were larceny and identity theft. The crimes listed belonged to a different individual. The post received 257,000 views before deletion. NOTUS found that seven of eight graphics in the same post series appeared to misidentify individuals' crimes. A White House official said "two images of criminal illegal aliens were mistakenly swapped" and the error was corrected.

**Category 3 — False Arrest Claims (Minnesota):**
The Minnesota DOC identified 68 cases where DHS labeled routine prison-to-ICE custody transfers as ICE "arrests" — falsely implying federal agents had conducted dramatic enforcement operations to apprehend dangerous individuals lurking in communities. One listed individual, Mauricio Morales-Morales, was actively serving a state sentence in Minnesota when DHS claimed ICE had him in custody. DOC Commissioner Paul Schnell stated: "Publishing false arrest claims at this scale is a pattern that cannot be explained by mere incompetence."

**Category 4 — Inflation of "Criminal" Definition:**
CNN analysis found some of the cities with the highest apparent arrest concentrations on the site were small towns that coincidentally house large prisons — suggesting that many ICE "arrests" were simply individuals being transferred from completed state sentences to federal immigration custody, not active community enforcement. As of January 2026, ICE data shows 29% of detainees had criminal convictions (down from 54% in February 2025), and only 7% had violent convictions per a New York Times analysis. Nearly 43% had no U.S. criminal record at all.

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### The Morales-Morales Case: Full Timeline

The case of Mauricio Morales-Morales is the clearest documented instance of the DHS listing being factually impossible rather than merely inaccurate. The Minnesota DOC provided the following sequence:

- **June 2, 2025**: At the request of federal authorities, DOC coordinated transfer of Morales to a court hearing via a judicial writ (a temporary order allowing movement for legal proceedings)
- **September 24, 2025**: Morales was returned to state DOC custody in St. Cloud following the court hearing
- **By January 26, 2026**: DHS's wow.dhs.gov listed Morales as being in ICE custody, having been "arrested" by ICE agents
- **January 26, 2026**: Minnesota DOC confirmed in writing that Morales remained incarcerated in their St. Cloud facility, serving his state sentence

This is not a "glitch" in how a charge was categorized — it is DHS publicly claiming federal custody of a man who was, according to the state's own records, in a Minnesota state prison. The DOC provided documentary evidence: video, custody records, and detailed transfer logs for all 68 disputed cases. [16]

### The Three Distinct Categories of Error (Beyond "The Glitch")

DHS's February 19 admission of a "glitch affecting about 5% of entries" appears to describe only one of at least three distinct error categories identified by independent investigators:

**Error Type A — Charge Mislabeling (DHS-admitted "Glitch")**: Charges against approximately 1,250 individuals (5% of ~25,000) were listed incorrectly, displaying the wrong crime. DHS claimed a technical failure caused a mismatch between crime categories. No public list of affected entries was ever released.

**Error Type B — False Arrest Fabrication (Minnesota DOC)**: DHS claimed ICE had "arrested" individuals who were simply transferred from state prison to federal custody as routine post-sentence handoffs — a process that has existed for decades. The 68 documented Minnesota cases appear to represent not technical database errors but deliberate or systematically misleading classification of administrative custody transfers as active law enforcement operations. DHS has not addressed this category as an "error" at all. [16]

**Error Type C — Identity Swaps (White House/NOTUS)**: Seven of eight graphics in a January 26 White House X post series appeared to misidentify individuals' crimes. Victor Manuel Carranza's photo was linked to child sex crime charges belonging to a different person entirely. The post reached 257,000 views before deletion. The White House never explained how seven of eight graphics in a prepared series could all contain errors simultaneously. [6]

These three categories are additive, not overlapping. DHS's "5% glitch" admission addresses only Type A. The total error rate when including Types B and C is unknown because neither DHS nor any independent auditor has been permitted to conduct a full cross-category review.

### The ICE Detainer Number Fraud: 1,360 vs. 301

Beyond the wow.dhs.gov site itself, DHS made a related statistical claim that Minnesota DOC was able to precisely refute with state records:

DHS publicly claimed that 1,360 individuals with ICE detainers were being held in Minnesota custody. The Minnesota DOC conducted a systematic survey:
- State prisons: 207 individuals with ICE detainers
- County jails: 94 individuals with ICE detainers
- **Total: 301 — compared to DHS's claimed 1,360**

The DOC found the discrepancy inexplicable: individuals named by DHS included people who had never been in Minnesota DOC custody, people with no Minnesota court records, people in custody in other states, and people released to ICE in cases dating to the 1990s. [17]

This pattern — where DHS publishes a number roughly 4.5x the documented reality — mirrors the wow.dhs.gov pattern of inflating the scope of immigration enforcement for political messaging purposes.

### What DHS Actually Said in Its "Glitch" Response

After receiving CNN's questions on February 19, DHS issued a spokesperson statement. The statement:
- Admitted that charges against "hundreds" of individuals were "described incorrectly"
- Attributed the cause to a "glitch"
- Claimed the affected entries represented "about 5%" of all entries
- Did not name, identify, or publish a list of affected entries
- Did not explain the technical mechanism of the failure
- Did not acknowledge the Minnesota false-arrest category or the White House identity swap category
- Was followed the next day by DHS expanding the site by 5,000 entries and issuing a press release mocking CNN for "raising awareness" of the site

The complete absence of a public correction list — combined with site expansion — means the public has no way to identify which of the 30,000+ current entries may contain the original errors. [1][2][3]

## Contemporary Context (Pass C)

### The Fatal Enforcement Operations: Why This Matters on March 3, 2026

The wow.dhs.gov errors do not exist in isolation. They are part of a documented DHS pattern of making factually false public statements about immigration enforcement operations — and that pattern now has fatal consequences:

- **January 7, 2026**: ICE agent Jonathan Ross fatally shot **Renée Nicole Macklin Good**, 37, a U.S. citizen, in Minneapolis during an enforcement operation. Good was in her car; she reversed briefly and then moved forward; Ross fired three shots through her window.
- **January 24, 2026**: Two CBP officers fatally shot **Alex Jeffrey Pretti**, 37, a U.S. Navy veteran and VA ICU nurse, in Minneapolis. Pretti was filming law enforcement with his phone and directing traffic. He was pepper-sprayed, wrestled to the ground by six agents, and shot.
- DHS Secretary Noem publicly described Pretti as a "domestic terrorist." CBP Commissioner Rodney Scott later testified before Congress that no one under his command had provided that characterization and he could not explain why Noem made the claim. [22]

### The Political Architecture: Why "Worst of the Worst" Was Designed to Be Wrong

The wow.dhs.gov website serves a specific political function that is structurally incompatible with accuracy:

1. **The 3,000-a-day pressure**: Trump administration officials publicly stated they wanted ICE to reach 3,000 arrests per day. ICE's historical capacity was approximately 300 per day. Hitting 10x capacity in a compressed window required arresting people who were not violent criminals — but the site was designed to make all arrests appear to be violent criminal removals.

2. **The "criminal" definition inflation**: By defining "criminal" to include pending charges, decades-old minor convictions, and immigration civil violations themselves, DHS built a self-confirming narrative: everyone on the site is a criminal because DHS says they are.

3. **The demographic scope**: Of the 30,000+ entries as of March 1, 2026, almost half are from Mexico; 2,100+ are Honduran; Guatemala and Cuba account for ~1,900 each; El Salvador ~1,200. The site's geographic framing — searchable by state — is designed to enable localized messaging: "see who ICE removed from your community." This is not a transparency tool; it is an audience-engagement tool that happens to publish government data.

4. **No correction mechanism**: As of March 1, 2026, DHS has not established any process by which an individual falsely listed on the site can petition for removal or correction of their entry. This is the same structural failure documented in sex offender registry due process cases — courts have found that government registries without correction mechanisms can violate the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments when they carry reputational and legal consequences. [Sources: Indiana DOC due process case law; NPR 2020 sex offender registry error analysis]

### The Broader Pattern: DHS Misinformation Beyond the Website

The wow.dhs.gov errors are documented instances within a pattern of DHS factual misrepresentation that House Oversight Democrats have catalogued:

- DHS claimed Minnesota was "refusing to cooperate" with ICE detainers → State records showed DOC honored every detainer, including beyond state legal requirements [17]
- DHS claimed 1,360 individuals with ICE detainers in Minnesota → Actual count: 301 [17]
- DHS labeled routine state prison custody transfers as active ICE "arrests" in 68 documented cases [16]
- White House labeled Victor Manuel Carranza a child sex crime convict; he had larceny/identity theft convictions [6]
- DHS called Cato Institute's ICE data analysis "made up" — then DHS's own FOIA-released records confirmed Cato's numbers [21]
- DHS Secretary Noem publicly called a killed VA nurse a "domestic terrorist"; CBP Commissioner contradicted her under oath [22]

Each of these misrepresentations followed the same pattern: DHS made a public claim, independent investigators or state officials produced documentary evidence disproving it, DHS did not retract, and the agency then escalated its activities in the same area.

### Who Benefits, Who Is Harmed

**Who benefits from the current framing:**
- The Trump administration gains political narrative control: every ICE arrest can be presented as removing a dangerous criminal, regardless of the actual record
- Congress members who supported or promoted the site gain constituent communication vehicles ("ICE arrested 47 criminals in your county")
- DHS avoids accountability for arrests of people with no criminal records by obscuring the underlying data

**Who is harmed:**
- Individuals falsely labeled: their names and mugshots appear on a searchable federal government website linked to crimes they did not commit; corrections receive a fraction of the original story's reach; some individuals are detained during the legal process and cannot dispute the listing
- U.S. citizens in enforcement areas: Two have been killed. The false "worst of the worst" framing contributed to operational environments where agents appear to have perceived high threat levels
- State officials who contradict DHS: The Minnesota DOC Commissioner has been publicly attacked by Trump administration officials and accused of harboring criminals; his documentation-based rebuttals have received far less social media amplification than DHS's original false claims
- The public's right to accurate information: The site's claimed function — transparency about whom ICE is arresting — is actively undermined by the errors, making it a political propaganda tool dressed as a government database
