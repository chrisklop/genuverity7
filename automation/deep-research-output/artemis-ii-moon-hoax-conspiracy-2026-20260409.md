## Executive Summary

NASA's Artemis II mission — the first crewed lunar flyby in more than 50 years — launched April 1, 2026, carrying Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch (all NASA), and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA). Within days of launch, a coordinated wave of conspiracy content, dubbed "Deep Fake Nine" by some media outlets, spread across X, TikTok, and Reddit, recycling decades-old Apollo-era hoax arguments and layering in AI-generated imagery to create a veneer of new "evidence." The central claims — a green-screen studio glitch, a faked Earth photo matching Apollo 8's 1968 Earthrise, and an absence of stars proving the mission is staged — have each been independently debunked by digital forensics tools, NASA officials, astrophotographers, and the Artemis II crew themselves [1][2][3][4][5].

What makes the 2026 campaign qualitatively different from prior space conspiracy waves is the deployment of consumer-grade generative AI to manufacture convincing fake imagery and the speed at which altered video clips spread before corrections could reach comparable audiences. PolitiFact, Lead Stories, AFP, and CBC all published dedicated fact-checks within days [3][5][6][7]. NASA Spokesperson Jennifer Dooren directed the public to nasa.gov for authentic imagery, while mission control's documented flight-day blogs — covering trajectory corrections, suit demonstrations, the lunar flyby on April 6, and the return burn on April 7 — provide an unbroken record of verified mission activity incompatible with any studio fabrication narrative [8][9][10].

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin
The conspiracy movement coalesced around three distinct "evidence" items in the first week of April 2026:

1. **The "Green Screen" Clip (~April 3–4, 2026):** A modified version of a CNN live interview with the Artemis II crew circulated on X, purporting to show the letters "OW" and "TAN" flickering through a floating stuffed toy ("Rise," the crew's mascot), which conspiracy accounts claimed was proof of a digital overlay on a studio backdrop. Fact-checkers confirmed the original CNN broadcast contained no such artifacts; the viral clip was an edited version recorded off-screen at a mismatched refresh rate, causing text-overlay bleed. The plush toy's green landmass colouring likely contributed to the misinterpretation of standard chroma-key graphics. [2][7]

2. **The AI-Generated "Earthrise" Photo (April 6, 2026):** A Facebook account called "Space Voyager" posted an image dated April 6, 2026, claiming it was an authentic Artemis II photograph showing Earth rising over the Moon in the same composition as Apollo 8's 1968 Earthrise. Lead Stories' forensic analysis found a SynthID digital watermark (Google's AI content marker) embedded in the image; the Hive Moderation tool rated it 100% likely AI-generated, while the genuine 1968 Apollo 8 photo scored only 14.1%. NASA's official Artemis II gallery contained no matching image; the closest authentic photo from that date was titled "Earthset" with a different composition. [3][5]

3. **The "No Stars" Claim (April 7–8, 2026):** A recurring Apollo-era claim — that the absence of visible stars in spacecraft photography proves a terrestrial shoot — was revived during the Artemis II lunar flyby. The crew addressed it directly in a live Q&A. [4]

### Propagation
- Content spread first on X before migrating to TikTok and Reddit threads in conspiracy-adjacent communities.
- AI-generated imagery was shared on Facebook by accounts with large existing followings in space and "alternative news" niches.
- Astrophotographer Ildar Ibatullin confirmed his Earth-based telescope images (mineral-enhanced, colorized) were "misappropriated by various platforms to spread misinformation" — a separate but concurrent wave of fake mission photos. [6]

### Why It Spread
- **Trust deficit:** Surveys consistently show ~10% of Americans doubt Apollo-era moon landings (2022 data); Artemis II provided a fresh anchor for pre-existing scepticism.
- **AI accessibility:** Consumer generative AI tools can now produce photorealistic lunar imagery that passes casual inspection, lowering the barrier for fabricating "evidence."
- **Speed asymmetry:** Viral clips reached mass audiences in hours; formal debunks by fact-checking organizations took 1–3 days to publish and received lower organic amplification.
- **Political context:** The National's reporting noted that the current political climate — characterized by heightened distrust of government institutions — amplifies the receptivity to anti-NASA narratives.

### Evidence Contradicting the Hoax Claims
- NASA published dated flight-day blogs for every mission day (Days 1–8 confirmed), documenting specific engine burns, trajectory corrections, suit demonstrations, and crew activities with timestamps.
- The Artemis II crew set a new record for farthest distance traveled from Earth by a crewed mission, surpassing Apollo 13's 248,655-mile record (1970).
- Independent radio-telescope tracking by international partners is publicly logged and cannot be fabricated without coordinating deception across multiple sovereign space agencies.
- Splashdown is officially scheduled for April 10, 2026, off the coast of San Diego — with live coverage allowing independent verification.

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### B1. SynthID — Technical Mechanics of AI Watermark Detection

The AI-generated fake "Earthrise" photo was identified primarily through **SynthID**, a watermarking and detection system developed by Google DeepMind. Understanding how SynthID works strengthens the forensic case against the hoax.

SynthID embeds an **imperceptible, sub-pixel watermark** into AI-generated or AI-modified images at the time of generation. The watermark is designed to survive common post-processing operations including cropping, resizing, color adjustment, and JPEG re-compression — the standard manipulations a bad actor might apply to obscure an image's origin. SynthID has been applied to content generated by Google's Imagen, Veo, Gemini, and Lyria AI systems. As of early 2026, over **10 billion pieces of content** have been watermarked with SynthID across Google's AI product suite. [20]

In the Artemis II case, Lead Stories' Ed Payne ran the fake "Space Voyager" Facebook image through the SynthID detector and confirmed a positive watermark signal — meaning the image was either generated by or significantly modified using a Google AI tool. This finding was corroborated by the independent **Hive Moderation** tool, which returned a **100% AI-generation confidence score** on the fake image. For comparison, Lead Stories ran the genuine 1968 Apollo 8 Earthrise photograph through the same Hive tool; it scored only **14.1%** — consistent with a pre-digital-AI archival photograph. The gap between 100% and 14.1% is definitive at any reasonable threshold. [3]

### B2. The CNN Green Screen Artifact — Full Technical Explanation

The "green screen glitch" conspiracy rests on a specific technical misunderstanding of how broadcast video overlays and consumer screen-capture interact.

**What the viral clip showed:** Letters "OW" and "TAN" (partial fragments of the words "BELOW" and "CERTAIN," from an on-screen CNN chyron or lower-third graphic) appeared to flicker briefly through the floating plush toy mascot "Rise" — which has green-coloured landmasses on its surface — in a televised CNN interview with the Artemis II crew.

**Why this happened (not a hoax):** The effect is a **refresh rate mismatch artifact** arising from two separate video systems operating at slightly different frequencies:
1. CNN's live broadcast composites the crew video feed and graphic overlays (chyrons, lower-thirds) in real time. Each overlay layer has its own refresh cycle.
2. When a viewer recorded the broadcast **off a television screen with a smartphone camera**, the smartphone's camera shutter and the television display's refresh rate fell briefly out of sync. During these microsecond gaps, the graphic overlay was in a different phase than the background video, causing text that was meant to appear *beneath* or *beside* the video frame to briefly bleed into the video frame itself.
3. The bright green colouration of the plush toy's land-surface pattern made the brief text bleed visually prominent against the toy's surface — amplifying the false impression of a chroma-key failure.

**Confirmation:** NASA and CNN's original broadcast footage contains no such artifact. The anomaly is present **only** in versions recorded off-screen by third parties — which is the definitive diagnostic for a capture-device artifact, not a source-video defect. [2][7]

### B3. "No Stars" — Astrophotography Physics Explained

The claim that the absence of stars in spacecraft photographs proves a terrestrial studio shoot is the oldest and most thoroughly debunked Apollo-era argument. It persists because it sounds intuitively compelling to non-photographers.

**The physics:** Camera sensors and human eyes both operate on a fixed exposure/sensitivity range. The **lunar surface, Earth's daylight face, and the spacecraft exterior** in sunlight are approximately **250,000 times brighter** than the surrounding starfield. Any camera correctly exposed to show surface or daylight detail will underexpose the star background to invisibility — the same reason a daytime photograph from Earth's surface shows blue sky but no stars.

**Commander Wiseman's in-mission explanation (live Q&A, April 7–8, 2026):** "We have so much illumination from the Sun on the Moon and the Earth right now, it's hard at times to see stars. Just like when you walk out in the daytime, you see a blue sky but no stars." He further noted that with spacecraft lights off on the Moon's dark far side, stars became "fully visible — so many stars it's hard to see the constellations." [4]

**Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (same Q&A):** "I've definitely seen stars in outer space, and I was saying, 'I haven't seen as many as I thought I would'." — referring to the lit portions of the trajectory near Earth and Moon. [4]

**The Apollo precedent:** Identical camera physics applied during every Apollo mission from 1968–1972. Astrophotographers have explained the phenomenon publicly for over 50 years. The Institute of Physics (UK) maintains a dedicated explainer confirming this as standard photographic behaviour, not evidence of fabrication. [24]

## Contemporary Context (Pass C)

### Apollo Hoax Conspiracy — Historical Pattern and Modern Recurrence

Moon landing conspiracy theories are not new to Artemis II. The modern hoax narrative traces to **Bill Kaysing's 1976 self-published book** *We Never Went to the Moon: America's Thirty Billion Dollar Swindle* — written by a former U.S. Navy officer with an English degree, not a technical background. The theory gained significant popular traction through the 1978 fictional film *Capricorn One* (director Peter Hyams), which dramatised a faked Mars landing and remains a touchstone for hoax believers. Folklorist Linda Dégh identified the post-Watergate environment — widespread institutional distrust following government deception — as the cultural soil in which moon hoax theories took root and grew. [25]

The pattern repeating in 2026 follows the same structural template: a new, high-visibility space mission anchors pre-existing distrust to fresh "evidence." The National's reporting on "Deep Fake Nine" explicitly notes that "a small, dogged group of sceptics" is recycling "decades-old space hoax narratives." What is qualitatively new in 2026 is the deployment of consumer-grade generative AI to manufacture plausible-looking fabricated evidence — a capability Kaysing-era conspiracists did not have. [1]
