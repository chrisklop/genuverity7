## Executive Summary

NASA's Artemis II mission — the first crewed lunar flyby in more than 50 years — launched April 1, 2026, carrying Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch (all NASA), and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen (CSA). Within days of launch, a coordinated wave of conspiracy content, dubbed "Deep Fake Nine" by some media outlets, spread across X, TikTok, and Reddit, recycling decades-old Apollo-era hoax arguments and layering in AI-generated imagery to create a veneer of new "evidence." The central claims — a green-screen studio glitch, a faked Earth photo matching Apollo 8's 1968 Earthrise, and an absence of stars proving the mission is staged — have each been independently debunked by digital forensics tools, NASA officials, astrophotographers, and the Artemis II crew themselves [1][2][3][4][5].

What makes the 2026 campaign qualitatively different from prior space conspiracy waves is the deployment of consumer-grade generative AI to manufacture convincing fake imagery and the speed at which altered video clips spread before corrections could reach comparable audiences. PolitiFact, Lead Stories, AFP, and CBC all published dedicated fact-checks within days [3][5][6][7]. NASA Spokesperson Jennifer Dooren directed the public to nasa.gov for authentic imagery, while mission control's documented flight-day blogs — covering trajectory corrections, suit demonstrations, the lunar flyby on April 6, and the return burn on April 7 — provide an unbroken record of verified mission activity incompatible with any studio fabrication narrative [8][9][10].

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin
The conspiracy movement coalesced around three distinct "evidence" items in the first week of April 2026:

1. **The "Green Screen" Clip (~April 3–4, 2026):** A modified version of a CNN live interview with the Artemis II crew circulated on X, purporting to show the letters "OW" and "TAN" flickering through a floating stuffed toy ("Rise," the crew's mascot), which conspiracy accounts claimed was proof of a digital overlay on a studio backdrop. Fact-checkers confirmed the original CNN broadcast contained no such artifacts; the viral clip was an edited version recorded off-screen at a mismatched refresh rate, causing text-overlay bleed. The plush toy's green landmass colouring likely contributed to the misinterpretation of standard chroma-key graphics. [2][7]

2. **The AI-Generated "Earthrise" Photo (April 6, 2026):** A Facebook account called "Space Voyager" posted an image dated April 6, 2026, claiming it was an authentic Artemis II photograph showing Earth rising over the Moon in the same composition as Apollo 8's 1968 Earthrise. Lead Stories' forensic analysis found a SynthID digital watermark (Google's AI content marker) embedded in the image; the Hive Moderation tool rated it 100% likely AI-generated, while the genuine 1968 Apollo 8 photo scored only 14.1%. NASA's official Artemis II gallery contained no matching image; the closest authentic photo from that date was titled "Earthset" with a different composition. [3][5]

3. **The "No Stars" Claim (April 7–8, 2026):** A recurring Apollo-era claim — that the absence of visible stars in spacecraft photography proves a terrestrial shoot — was revived during the Artemis II lunar flyby. The crew addressed it directly in a live Q&A. [4]

### Propagation
- Content spread first on X before migrating to TikTok and Reddit threads in conspiracy-adjacent communities.
- AI-generated imagery was shared on Facebook by accounts with large existing followings in space and "alternative news" niches.
- Astrophotographer Ildar Ibatullin confirmed his Earth-based telescope images (mineral-enhanced, colorized) were "misappropriated by various platforms to spread misinformation" — a separate but concurrent wave of fake mission photos. [6]

### Why It Spread
- **Trust deficit:** Surveys consistently show ~10% of Americans doubt Apollo-era moon landings (2022 data); Artemis II provided a fresh anchor for pre-existing scepticism.
- **AI accessibility:** Consumer generative AI tools can now produce photorealistic lunar imagery that passes casual inspection, lowering the barrier for fabricating "evidence."
- **Speed asymmetry:** Viral clips reached mass audiences in hours; formal debunks by fact-checking organizations took 1–3 days to publish and received lower organic amplification.
- **Political context:** The National's reporting noted that the current political climate — characterized by heightened distrust of government institutions — amplifies the receptivity to anti-NASA narratives.

### Evidence Contradicting the Hoax Claims
- NASA published dated flight-day blogs for every mission day (Days 1–8 confirmed), documenting specific engine burns, trajectory corrections, suit demonstrations, and crew activities with timestamps.
- The Artemis II crew set a new record for farthest distance traveled from Earth by a crewed mission, surpassing Apollo 13's 248,655-mile record (1970).
- Independent radio-telescope tracking by international partners is publicly logged and cannot be fabricated without coordinating deception across multiple sovereign space agencies.
- Splashdown is officially scheduled for April 10, 2026, off the coast of San Diego — with live coverage allowing independent verification.
