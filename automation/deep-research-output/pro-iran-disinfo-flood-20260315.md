## Executive Summary

On February 28, 2026, hours after U.S. and Israeli strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, pro-Iran influence networks launched what researchers are calling the most sophisticated coordinated disinformation operation in the conflict's short history. The campaign exploited a pre-existing conspiracy theory — that Trump launched "Operation Epic Fury" to bury the Epstein files — and turbocharged it across X, Instagram, TikTok, and Telegram using AI-generated content, fake "news" account networks, and Iranian state media amplification. Within 48 hours, the phrase "Epstein Fury" had been mentioned over 91,000 times by more than 60,000 unique accounts, according to the Anti-Defamation League's Center on Extremism.[1] A network of at least 15 anonymous X accounts with blue checkmarks — including "HDX News" and "GPX News" — served as primary distribution nodes, with a single fabricated video depicting Trump alongside Epstein accumulating 6.8 million views before X suspended the account.[2]

The operation ran on two parallel tracks. The first was Iran's formal state-media infrastructure: PressTV, Tasnim, Mehr News, and IRIB TV1 flooded international feeds with fabricated battlefield claims — 650 U.S. troops killed, USS Abraham Lincoln struck by ballistic missiles, F-15s downed over Tehran — all false, all documented by NewsGuard's running tally of 18 verified false claims since February 28.[3] The second was a covert IRGC-affiliated network of fake personas documented by Clemson University's Media Forensics Hub: 62 accounts masquerading as Scottish independence supporters, Irish nationalists, and Latina women from Texas and Venezuela, which generated 59,403 original posts on X alone before pivoting entirely to war messaging.[4] The strategy, per Institute for Strategic Dialogue researcher Bret Schafer: "You come for the Epstein content, and you stay for the propaganda."[2] A Zeteo/Ipsos poll published March 10 found that a majority of likely American voters — 52% to 40% — believe Trump launched the Iran war at least in part to cover up his Epstein connections,[5] demonstrating real-world attitude shift driven by the operation.

The campaign represents a convergence of three distinct disinformation actors: Iran's formal media machine producing fabrications, the IRGC's covert influence infrastructure seeding divisive narratives, and engagement-farming accounts monetizing wartime attention — all of whose outputs reinforce the same set of narratives. Counter-operations also ran: the Citizen Lab's PRISONBREAK investigation documented an Israeli government-linked network of 50+ AI-enabled X accounts coordinating pro-regime-change narratives.[6] The result is the information environment researchers are calling "the first AI war" — a conflict in which no single source is trustworthy without independent verification, and where fabricated content accumulates hundreds of millions of views before fact-checkers can respond.[7]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The convergence of the Epstein narrative with the Iran war began immediately at 4:00 PM ET on February 28, 2026 — within hours of the first strikes. The ADL Center on Extremism documented 35,000 mentions of "Operation Epstein Fury" and 28,000 unique authors within that single afternoon.[1] This speed suggests pre-positioning: IRGC-linked content networks had been cultivating the Epstein disinformation environment for weeks prior, monitoring the rolling release of Jeffrey Epstein files by the Trump Justice Department and preparing to weaponize them.

The Clemson Media Forensics Hub's March 11 report ("From Texas to Tehran") traced the IRGC-affiliated account network's origins to December 2023, with accounts building credibility in niche communities (Scottish independence, Irish reunification, Latina political content) until February 2026, when they simultaneously pivoted to the war.[4] This "dormant persona" playbook mirrors techniques Clemson previously documented in a 2024 Iranian network promoting Scottish independence narratives — the current operation is broader and more multilingual.

Iranian state media's formal contribution was coordinated through IRGC-aligned outlets. Mehr News Agency reported four Iranian ballistic missiles struck USS Abraham Lincoln. Tasnim quoted an IRGC spokesman claiming 650 U.S. and wounded in the first two days. PressTV published a Pakistani drone photograph labeled as an "Israeli drone shot down near Natanz." IRIB TV1 muted Israeli attack footage and narrated it as Iranian counter-strikes. NewsGuard documented 18 distinct false war-related claims from these outlets since February 28, compared to just 5 in the two preceding weeks — a 360% acceleration.[3]

### The Epstein-Iran Narrative Architecture

The propaganda campaign exploited a three-part logical chain:

1. Trump released partial Epstein files, raising questions about his own documented connections to Epstein.
2. Trump launched "Operation Epic Fury" against Iran days later.
3. Therefore, the war was launched to bury the Epstein scandal.

Iranian state media formalized this as the "Epstein class" or "Epstein regime" framing — portraying Trump, Netanyahu, and Western elites as members of a depraved criminal network using military violence to distract from their crimes. The most visible artifact: a two-minute AI-generated Lego animation titled "Narrative of Victory," produced by Iran's Revayat-e Fath Institute and aired on Iranian state television March 11, depicting Lego-figure Trump, Netanyahu, and Satan reviewing a folder labeled "Jeffrey Epstein File" before Trump launches missiles at an Iranian classroom.[8]

A separate viral false claim — that Trump named the operation "Epic Fury" specifically so that "Trump ep" would autocomplete to "Trump Epic Fury" rather than "Trump Epstein" in Google searches — accumulated 1.7 million views despite being demonstrably false.[9]

### Propagation Mechanics

**Fake news account network:** Researcher analysis identified at least 15 anonymous X accounts with paid verification working in coordination. Key nodes: "HDX News" and "GPX News." These accounts posted a fabricated video depicting Trump and Epstein together, with HDX News alone accumulating 6.8 million views and GPX News receiving 4.7 million on the same fake content.[2] Nine of the 15 accounts held X Blue verification. Both primary nodes were suspended after The Washington Post contacted X for comment — but multiple others remained active.[2]

**State media amplification:** PressTV, Mehr, and Tasnim pushed false military claims with apparent IRGC sourcing, which were then picked up by IRGC-linked Telegram channels and hard-line Persian X accounts. The ADL documented convergence between "white nationalists, influencers, anti-war voices, and Islamist-aligned groups" around the same core messaging.[1]

**Engagement farming:** X's own creator revenue-sharing program incentivized fabrication. X Head of Product Nikita Bier identified "a guy in Pakistan managing 31 accounts posting AI war videos — all hacked and usernames changed on Feb 27 to 'Iran War Monitor.'" Bier noted "in nearly all cases, the users posting fake content were people looking to game monetization."[10] BBC Verify documented creators "churning out AI-generated videos and fake satellite imagery to farm views and revenue."[7]

**Cross-platform spread:** A single fake image can traverse X, Facebook, TikTok, Instagram, and Telegram within hours, accumulating tens of millions of views before fact-checkers can respond. The three most viral false AI-generated videos identified by BBC Verify collectively garnered over 100 million combined views.[7]

### A3: Zeteo/Ipsos Poll — Full Methodology

- **Sample:** 1,272 likely voters, national web panel
- **Dates:** March 6-8, 2026

## Contemporary Context — Pass C

### C1: Why Now — Post-Khamenei Power Vacuum and Information Strategy

Iran's unprecedented disinformation acceleration after February 28 reflects not just military strategy but a structural crisis in the regime's information apparatus. IRGC Deputy PR head Naeini's publicly documented concern about "cognitive warfare failures" predates this conflict — he was already warning internally that without better media operations, "we will face problems." The killing of Khamenei removed the single figure whose authority could coordinate the regime's media response from the top. What followed was a decentralized activation of pre-positioned assets: the IRGC's formal media machine (PressTV, Tasnim, Mehr, IRIB), the Revayat-e Fath propaganda production unit, the covert persona networks (Scotland, Texas, UK), and the monetized amplification ecosystem — all running simultaneously but without unified strategic direction.

The Iran-China-Russia trilateral pact signed January 29, 2026 — just 30 days before the strikes — provided the geopolitical backdrop. While the pact is not a mutual defense treaty, it included intelligence cooperation provisions. Russian intelligence was providing Iran with "precise locations of U.S. warships and aircraft." China had spent years shifting Iranian military navigation from GPS to China's encrypted BeiDou-3 constellation. Whether information warfare coordination was also part of the trilateral agreement has not been confirmed in open sources — but the pro-Russian hacktivist group NoName057(16) and Iranian Cyber Islamic Resistance ran a joint #OpIsrael DDoS campaign starting March 2, targeting Israeli defense entities including Elbit Systems, demonstrating at least tactical alignment in the cyber domain.

- **Sources:** Middle East Monitor (Iran-China-Russia pact, January 29, 2026); Al Jazeera ("The war of signals: How Russia and China help Iran see the battlefield," March 12, 2026); Flashpoint (Operation Epic Fury cyber tracking); HSToday (Iran layered response analysis)

### C2: State Actors Exploiting Real Grievances — The Epstein Mechanism

The most operationally significant aspect of this campaign — and the most politically durable — is that it weaponizes a real grievance with a fabricated causal link. Jeffrey Epstein's documented crimes, his documented relationships with powerful figures including Trump, and the incomplete Justice Department document releases are all real. The operation did not need to fabricate the underlying concern — only the causal link to the war.

Gizmodo's analysis of the HDX/GPX network identified this as the campaign's central strategic puzzle: authentic Trump-Epstein materials already exist. The use of AI fabrication was unnecessary for factual persuasion. This suggests the operation's true goal was not to convince skeptics but to intensify emotional investment among already-primed audiences — turning passive concern about Epstein into active certainty about a cover-up, and active certainty into the poll result: 52% of likely voters, 66% of under-45 voters, believing the war was launched to bury the scandal.

The Epstein narrative served as what ISD's Bret Schafer called "bait" — an entry point that required no persuasion because the underlying emotion (distrust of elite impunity) was genuine. The disinformation layer was the causal connection to the war, which was then reinforced by volume and apparent-consensus effects (91,000 mentions appearing organic because many amplifiers genuinely believed the narrative).

### C3: The First AI War — Scale, Cost, and Platform Failure

The EDMO characterization of the Iran conflict as "the first AI war" reflects a qualitative shift in disinformation production economics. Key structural factors:

**Production cost collapse:** AI-generated video content that would have required significant production budgets in 2020 can now be produced for near-zero marginal cost per item. The engagement-farmer in Pakistan running 31 accounts (documented by X's Nikita Bier) represents the commercial endpoint: individual actors, motivated entirely by Creator Revenue Sharing monetization, producing AI war content at industrial scale with no political allegiance.

**Verification reversal (EDMO finding):** The Iran conflict produced a documented paradox — authentic photographs from Tehran were being falsely labeled as AI fabrications, while AI-generated content was being presented as documentary evidence. The volume of fakes degraded audiences' ability to authenticate real content, not just fake content.

**Platform structural failure:** Meta's Oversight Board documented that Meta failed to act on six separate user reports of an AI-fabricated war video. X's Creator Revenue Sharing enforcement, announced March 3, was described by researchers as ineffective: "The feeds I monitor are still flooded with AI-generated content about the war." TikTok and Meta did not respond to CNN requests for comment on enforcement. The platforms' enforcement infrastructure was architecturally mismatched to the scale of the problem — designed for case-by-case review, confronting an automated content flood.

**The attention asymmetry:** Cyabra documented 145 million total views in under two weeks. NewsGuard documented 21.9 million views for fabricated visuals on X alone. BBC Verify documented 100+ million combined views for the top three AI-generated false videos. Against these numbers, fact-checks — which require search intent, reading comprehension, and trust in the fact-checking outlet — cannot achieve comparable reach. The information environment is structurally asymmetric against correction.

### C4: Who Benefits, Who Is Harmed

**Iranian regime objectives:** The operation served three simultaneous goals. First, domestic legitimation — portraying the conflict as imposed by a depraved Western elite rather than resulting from Iran's nuclear weapons program. Second, international positioning — framing Iran as a resistant power facing Zionist-American corruption, targeting sympathetic audiences in the Global South, UK political margins, and U.S. anti-war communities. Third, war-shortening — Clemson's Darren Linvill assessed the network's explicit goal was to "redirect its resources toward propaganda around the war, trying to make the war more painful for the United States," with the hope of shortening it by eroding American political will.

**Harm to U.S. public discourse:** The 52% poll finding represents a measurable distortion of American political reality. Twenty-five percent of Republicans — members of the party that controls the presidency — expressed belief that their own president launched a war as a personal cover-up. This creates a structural problem for democratic accountability: if the premise of the conflict is widely believed to be fraudulent, legitimate oversight of the war becomes impossible.

**Harm to Epstein accountability:** The most underanalyzed harm is the weaponization of the Epstein scandal itself. By attaching fabricated AI content and irresponsible causal claims to the Epstein story, the operation has made the real investigation harder. Real journalists covering the Epstein files must now navigate an environment in which "Epstein evidence" is associated with Iranian propaganda, AI fakes, and blue-checkmark bot networks. The Iranian operation did not need to suppress the Epstein story — it needed only to contaminate the information environment around it.
