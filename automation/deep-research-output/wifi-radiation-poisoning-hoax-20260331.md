## Executive Summary

On March 12, 2026, Children's Health Defense (CHD) — the nonprofit previously chaired by Robert F. Kennedy Jr., now serving as HHS Secretary — published an article headlined "1 in 8 U.S. Adults Say Wireless Radiation Is Causing Health Issues." [1] The article reported on a peer-reviewed study published in the journal *Next Research*, authored by Dr. Julie McCredden, Lyn McLean, and Prof. Anne Steinemann of the University of Melbourne. [2] The study surveyed 3,475 adults across the U.S., Australia, and Canada and found 12.8% of U.S. respondents self-reported experiencing adverse health effects from wireless radiation. Fringe health sites including Planet Today and NaturalNews then amplified the findings with a more alarming headline: "1 in 8 US Adults Now Have Radiation Poisoning from WIFI Hubs." [3] This headline is a significant mischaracterization: the study measured self-reported symptoms in an online survey, not clinically diagnosed "radiation poisoning."

The study itself contains critical methodological limitations that CHD's article underemphasized and fringe sites entirely ignored. The survey data was collected in early 2022 — four years before publication — from self-selected online respondents. The study explicitly acknowledged that its results could be inflated because people with self-reported electromagnetic sensitivity are more likely to complete an online survey about that topic. Most critically, the study measured perceived association between wireless exposure and health symptoms, not a proven causal link. The World Health Organization does not recognize electromagnetic hypersensitivity (EHS) as a medical diagnosis and states "no scientific basis currently exists for a connection between EHS and exposure to EMF." [4] Forty-six double-blind provocation studies involving 1,175 people with self-reported EHS consistently found that participants could not identify real EMF exposure better than chance. [5]

The timing and policy context of this claim make it particularly consequential. On January 15, 2026 — two months before the CHD article — HHS under Secretary Kennedy launched a new federal study into mobile phone radiation health effects and directed the FDA to remove webpages that had previously stated cellphones pose no danger. [6] Kennedy told USA Today in February 2026 that electromagnetic radiation is "a major health concern" and cited "more than 10,000 studies" documenting adverse effects. [7] Protect Our Care called the USA Today interview "a master class in misinformation." The CHD article and fringe amplification of the Next Research study thus forms part of a coordinated ecosystem in which a sitting cabinet secretary's advocacy is used to lend political authority to scientifically disputed health claims — with potential consequences for 5G infrastructure policy, school WiFi programs, and public trust in wireless safety regulation. [8]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The primary vehicle for this claim was a March 12, 2026 article published on the CHD website (childrenshealthdefense.org). CHD is the organization previously led by RFK Jr. and associated with a documented history of health misinformation including false claims that WiFi causes COVID-19, causes infertility, increases cancer risk, and is linked to autism — all previously debunked by NewsGuard. [9] The article accurately represented the study's headline findings but prominently featured lead author Julie McCredden's most alarming interpretations without adequately contextualizing the study's self-reported methodology or the broader scientific consensus.

Fringe health sites Planet Today and NaturalNews amplified the findings under a more inflammatory headline ("radiation poisoning"), which crossed a critical threshold from "self-reported symptoms" (what the study measured) to "radiation poisoning" (a clinical diagnosis not made by the study). NaturalNews, founded by health conspiracy promoter Mike Adams, has been rated as a "misinformation superspreader" by NewsGuard.

### Propagation

The CHD article spread through: (1) CHD's own subscriber network and social media following; (2) cross-posting by fringe health/anti-5G communities; (3) anti-5G Telegram channels and Facebook groups; (4) amplification through RFK Jr.'s existing HHS policy actions that primed audiences to take electromagnetic health claims seriously. The claim also circulated in Substack health and wellness newsletters with large subscriber bases.

### Why It Spread

1. **RFK Jr.'s institutional authority**: As HHS Secretary, Kennedy's public statements about electromagnetic radiation risk (USA Today interview, January 2026) provided political legitimacy to marginal claims. When a sitting cabinet health secretary says EMF is "a major health concern," CHD's subsequent amplification of a supporting study carries perceived institutional weight.

2. **Real study, inflated interpretation**: The study is genuinely peer-reviewed and published in a real journal (*Next Research*), giving the claim a credibility layer that purely fabricated claims lack. The distortion occurs in the headline — "radiation poisoning" vs. "self-reported health effects" — which most readers never investigate.

3. **Nocebo anxiety**: People who believe they are being harmed by invisible technology will seek and share confirming evidence. The study's 12.8% U.S. figure, divorced from methodology context, functioned as validation for this audience.

4. **Policy stakes**: The FDA's removal of webpages stating cellphones pose no danger was covered in mainstream media, signaling that official scientific positions are being reconsidered at the highest levels of government. This environment of institutional uncertainty made the CHD claim more credible to general audiences.

### What the Study Actually Found vs. What Was Claimed

The *Next Research* study (McCredden et al., 2026) was:
- A **self-reported online survey** of 3,475 adults (1,271 U.S., 1,104 Australian, 1,100 Canadian)
- Conducted in **February–May 2022** (4 years before publication)
- **Self-selected** — respondents were people willing to complete an online survey about wireless sensitivity
- The authors themselves acknowledged the sample may overrepresent wireless-sensitive individuals: "the true number could also be lower, as some people with EMR Syndrome have hard-wired internet and are more inclined to do online surveys"

What the study **did not find**: clinical diagnoses of "radiation poisoning"; causal proof that WiFi causes the reported symptoms; physiological biomarkers of EMF exposure; or results that contradict the 46 double-blind provocation studies in the scientific literature.
