## Executive Summary

NASA's Artemis II mission launched on April 1, 2026, sending astronauts Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen on a historic 10-day crewed lunar flyby — the first crewed mission beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972. Almost immediately, a vocal minority on social media platforms claimed the entire mission was an elaborate hoax. The most prominent trigger was a brief broadcast glitch in a live CNN interview with the crew, during which blocky text characters appeared to "stick" to the surface of a floating mascot ball. Conspiracy accounts froze and zoomed the clip, declaring it evidence of a failed green screen compositing effect — proof, they argued, that the crew was on a Hollywood soundstage rather than in deep space. [1][2]

The claims were rapidly and thoroughly debunked by digital forensics experts and fact-checkers. Hany Farid, co-founder of GetReal Security and professor at the University of California, Berkeley, identified the anomaly as "a failed text overlay on the part of the broadcaster" — a standard chromakey rendering error in the news station's retransmission chain that was entirely absent from the original NASA and CNN YouTube feeds. More ironically, many of the images circulated as "proof" that astronauts were filmed in front of green screens were themselves AI-generated, identifiable by anomalies including fused fingers, incorrect camera positioning, and Google's SynthID watermark embedded in the pixel data. [3][4]

The Artemis II hoax narrative is rooted in longstanding Apollo-era moon landing denial, newly amplified by widespread public anxiety about AI-generated media. The conspiracy's internal contradiction — fake AI images being used to "prove" that real footage is AI-generated — reflects a broader epistemic crisis in which fabricated evidence is now being deployed to discredit authentic events. Independent verification of the mission is possible through: published orbital telemetry, international tracking by ESA and JAXA, corroborating data from thousands of amateur radio operators worldwide, and the fact that the crew set a new human distance record (252,756 miles from Earth) verified against Apollo 13's known record from 1970. [5][6]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The hoax narrative coalesced in the first 48 hours after the April 1, 2026 launch. The primary catalyst was a clip from a live television broadcast of the Artemis II crew during a CNN interview on approximately April 5, 2026. The crew's zero-gravity indicator — a smiley-faced plush toy called "Rise" — drifted in front of Commander Reid Wiseman, and during playback a brief sequence showed blocky white letters appearing to overlay the ball's surface. Skeptical accounts on X replayed, froze, and zoomed in on these frames, claiming they represented a "failed blue screen" or "green screen" effect demonstrating the interview was staged. This clip circulated with captions like "Green screen??? this is from Artemis live on YouTube!" and quickly attracted millions of views.

A secondary origin vector was the emergence of AI-generated images — apparently produced using Google Gemini — that depicted astronauts posed in front of visible green screen rigs. These fabricated images were passed off as leaked behind-the-scenes "evidence" of staging. A third element was a fake "Earthrise" photo circulating on social media that appeared to duplicate cloud patterns from the Apollo 8 photograph taken in 1968, which Politifact and others confirmed was AI-generated and bore no relationship to any official NASA imagery from Artemis II.

The phrase "Deep Fake Nine" emerged as a social media label for the theory — a play on "Deep Space Nine" — suggesting NASA was deploying deepfake technology to simulate the mission.

### Propagation

The green screen clip spread first on Facebook and X, then was picked up by YouTube reaction channels and TikTok. The TikTok vector was particularly fast: CBC News documented that "fake images and videos of the Artemis II mission have been shared widely online" and produced a rapid fact-check video specifically for TikTok audiences. The AI-generated fake green screen images spread across X, Reddit, and TikTok simultaneously, with Vice, UNILAD, Delimiter Online, and BrobIble all documenting the wave of claims within the first week. Flat-earth adjacent Substack accounts (e.g., "NASA Liars on NASA Wires" on Substack) and alternative news sites such as The Burning Platform published long-form pieces arguing the mission was fabricated, providing additional fuel for shares on alternative social media networks.

### Why It Spread

Four factors explain the hoax's viral velocity:

1. **AI anxiety**: Public concern about deepfakes and AI-generated media made audiences primed to believe authentic footage could be fabricated. Ironically, this anxiety was exploited by actors who themselves used AI to generate fake "evidence."
2. **Broadcast artifacts as "proof"**: The green screen narrative required no special knowledge — viewers could see the glitch themselves. The clip was short, visually striking, and easy to share without context.
3. **Historical moon hoax tradition**: Apollo-era conspiracy theories (no stars visible, waving flags, studio lighting anomalies) have a robust online following that was immediately activated by the new mission.
4. **Political climate**: The article in The National (April 6, 2026) noted the rise of conspiracy theories was driven partly by the "political climate," suggesting anti-institutional sentiment lowered the threshold for belief in government deception.

### The Evidence

**On the green screen claim:** Digital forensics expert Hany Farid (UC Berkeley / GetReal Security) confirmed the anomaly was a broadcasting error — a failed text overlay by the news station syndicating the official feed. The original NASA live stream and CNN's YouTube upload showed no such glitch. The mascot "Rise" remained visually solid throughout all unaltered feeds.

**On AI-generated fake "evidence":** PolitiFact confirmed that moon photos circulating as "Artemis II images" were either Earth-based astrophotography (some stolen from Texas-based astrophotographer Adam Jackson) or AI-generated, with one image confirmed to contain Google AI's SynthID watermark. AI-generated "green screen" depictions showed telltale anomalies: fused fingers, misplaced green screen tracking markers, asymmetric positioning inconsistent with actual production equipment.

**On the fake Earthrise photo:** A "fact check" article confirmed a circulating image showing Earth with identical cloud patterns to the Apollo 8 photo from 1968 was AI-generated and not an official NASA photograph.

**On mission verification:** Staging a crewed lunar flyby would require fabricating engine burns, radio telemetry, orbital mechanics data, and thousands of independent tracking observations from international partners including ESA and JAXA. The crew broke the human distance record (252,756 miles / 406,771 km) — a figure corroborated by tracking stations worldwide and exceeding Apollo 13's 1970 record of 248,655 miles.

**Astronaut rebuttal:** Commander Reid Wiseman directly addressed the long-standing "no stars in space" conspiracy theory during the mission, explaining the optics of camera exposure in bright conditions — one of several crew-level debunks offered via live press conferences from space.
