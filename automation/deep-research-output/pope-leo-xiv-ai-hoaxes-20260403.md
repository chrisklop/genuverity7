## Executive Summary

In late March 2026, two AI-fabricated viral claims about Pope Leo XIV swept through Facebook and affiliated blog networks within days of each other, targeting the world's first American-born pope just months into his pontificate. The first hoax alleged that U.S. President Donald Trump had called Pope Leo XIV "an insult to Jesus," prompting the pope to deliver a "fiery sermon" in which he condemned Trump's economic policies [1]. The second claimed that the pope had gone live at 3 a.m. with an "urgent message," saying, "Tonight I received a message — and it was sent to silence me" [2]. Both claims are entirely fabricated. No credible news outlet — including the Associated Press, Reuters, or Vatican News — reported either incident, and the claims could not survive even basic cross-referencing with verified papal statements and activities. [3]

AI-detection tool GPTZero confirmed the fabricated nature of both claims with high confidence. The blog article underlying Hoax 2 was flagged at 100% certainty as AI-generated text, while the accompanying Facebook caption registered a 76% AI-generation score [2]. The "Pontiff Leo Era" Facebook account behind Hoax 1 is a known AI content farm presenting itself as a "news and media website," while Hoax 2's originating account "Guided By Grace" used Cyrillic character substitution — replacing the Latin letter "n" with the Cyrillic "п" throughout its text — as a likely technique to evade platform moderation tools and advertising restrictions [2][4]. These tactics are consistent with an industrial-scale AI slop operation documented by Lead Stories, which identified the same 3 a.m. "urgent message" template being used to target at least 30 different public figures. [5]

Pope Leo XIV — born Robert Francis Prevost on September 14, 1955, in Chicago, Illinois — was elected on May 8, 2025, becoming the first American and the first Augustinian friar to lead the Catholic Church [6]. Since his election, he has been a prolific target of AI-generated hoaxes, deepfakes, and fabricated quotes at a scale that has overwhelmed the Vatican's communications office; officials report receiving "dozens of reports every day about fake accounts that use the pope's image and voice in a very realistic way" [7]. The double-strike hoaxes of late March 2026 represent a concentrated burst of AI slop in a broader campaign that has included faked reform announcements, fabricated endorsements of foreign leaders, fake hospitalization rumors, and multiple fabricated Trump-related confrontations. [3][8]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origins of Hoax 1: "Insult to Jesus" / Fiery Sermon

The earliest identified source of this claim is the Facebook account **"Pontiff Leo Era,"** which describes itself as a "news and media website." The account generated and posted a story alleging that President Trump had called Pope Leo XIV "an insult to Jesus" and that the pope responded with a fiery sermon containing the line: *"The president of the United States just said that I insulted Jesus. You want to know what insults Jesus? Kicking the sick off their health care while cutting taxes for billionaires."* The language is highly idiomatic American political rhetoric rather than the carefully translated, diplomatically worded statements that characterize actual papal communication. The account's entire content library consists of AI-generated material. Comments on the original post linked to external blog sites, a standard monetization pattern for AI content farms that drive traffic to ad-laden pages.

The claim has a superficial plausibility hook: Trump has used the phrase "insult to Jesus" before — directed at Texas state Rep. James Talarico over views on transgender issues — and Pope Leo XIV has made authentic statements condemning leaders whose "hands are full of blood" and warning against leaders who "wage war." The hoax spliced these real rhetorical contexts into a fabricated confrontation.

### Origins of Hoax 2: "3 A.M. Urgent Message"

The earliest identified instance of this hoax is a Facebook post by the page **"Guided By Grace,"** dated **March 22, 2026**, which read: *"POPE LEO XIV GOES LIVE AT 3 A.M. WITH AN URGENT MESSAGE 'Tonight I received a message — and it was sent to silence me.'"* Associated imagery included photographs of President Trump without explicitly naming him, a pattern consistent with content designed to imply a threat from a recognizable political figure while preserving plausible deniability.

The originating blog article and caption were analyzed by GPTZero: the article returned 100% AI-generation certainty; the caption returned 76%. The text of the blog employed Cyrillic character substitution, with the letter "п" standing in for "n" — a technical obfuscation to defeat automated keyword filters and potentially advertising moderation systems. This is a documented tactic of AI-slop operations seeking to preserve monetization while evading platform enforcement.

Critically, fact-checking organization Lead Stories had already documented this exact template — a public figure "going live at 3 a.m. with an urgent message" — being applied to at least 30 different individuals. The same structural narrative was used against Stephen Colbert and figure skater Ilia Malinin, among others. This strongly indicates that a coordinated content-generation network was recycling a proven viral format and applying it to high-profile names to maximize reach and ad revenue.

### Why These Hoaxes Spread

Both hoaxes exploit several proven virality factors simultaneously:

1. **Celebrity novelty**: Pope Leo XIV is new, globally prominent, and not yet fully familiar to mass audiences outside Catholic circles, making fabricated statements harder to immediately discredit by memory.
2. **Political framing**: Positioning the pope in opposition to Trump activates strong partisan emotional responses, increasing share likelihood among users who find the narrative satisfying.
3. **Dramatic stakes**: "Fiery sermons" and "3 a.m. urgent messages" carry an urgency that prompts immediate sharing before verification.
4. **Plausibility seeding**: The real Leo XIV has made genuine, documented statements about leaders who "wage war" and God rejecting such leaders' prayers — providing just enough factual scaffolding to make fabrications seem credible.
5. **AI industrial scale**: Content farms using AI can produce dozens of variants of a single narrative, flooding search results and overwhelming fact-checkers.

### The "Guided By Grace" Cyrillic Evasion Technique

The Facebook page "Guided By Grace" (page ID: 100089199232691) bears the technical hallmarks of a "Viet spam" operation — a term coined by Lead Stories for AI content farms managed from Vietnam that systematically spread fabricated celebrity or public-figure stories on Facebook. The page's contact number uses a Vietnam country code (+84), directly identifying its probable country of operation. This is significant: Vietnamese-run AI content farms have become a documented industrial-scale phenomenon, as they can capitalize on Facebook's creator monetization programs while operating in a jurisdiction with minimal legal exposure to Western defamation or fraud claims. [15]

The Cyrillic character substitution is the page's most technically notable evasion tactic. In the blog article associated with Hoax 2, the Latin letter "n" was systematically replaced with the visually near-identical Cyrillic letter "п" (Unicode U+043F) throughout the text. To the human eye reading at speed, the substitution is essentially invisible — the words appear to spell out normally. But to automated text-analysis systems — including Facebook's keyword filters and advertising compliance tools, and third-party detection systems — the words register as entirely different character strings. A filter looking for the word "urgent" would not match "urgeпt." The technique effectively allows the content to evade keyword-based moderation while remaining readable to human audiences, preserving the page's ability to monetize via ad revenue on linked blog pages. Snopes specifically noted: "It is possible this spelling was intentional to avoid moderation tools or ad restrictions." [4]

This same page, "Guided By Grace," was also the origin account for a third hoax (debunked March 24, 2026) in which Pope Leo XIV was falsely quoted calling Trump "a vicious old b****d and his political circus." That hoax also used GPTZero-confirmed AI generation with Cyrillic character substitution in the underlying blog. The page is therefore a repeat, multi-hoax AI content operation specifically targeting Pope Leo XIV with fabricated Trump-confrontation narratives. [15]

### GPTZero Methodology: How AI Detection Works in This Context

GPTZero employs two primary analytical dimensions to distinguish AI-generated from human-written text:

1. **Perplexity:** Measures how statistically "surprising" each word is given its context. AI-generated text has characteristically low perplexity — it follows high-probability word sequences — whereas human writing shows greater unpredictability and idiosyncrasy. A 100% AI-generation score, as returned for the Hoax 2 blog article, indicates the text is overwhelmingly consistent with AI output patterns across both metrics. [Pass B Search 3]

2. **Burstiness:** Measures variance in sentence complexity. Humans write in natural "bursts" — short sentences followed by longer, structurally complex ones — while AI-generated prose tends toward uniform sentence length and complexity. The 76% score on the Hoax 2 Facebook caption reflects a mixed signal: the caption may have been lightly edited by a human after AI generation (a common practice in professional content farms) while the underlying blog article was published raw. [Pass B Search 3]

Independent 2026 testing of GPTZero shows 88% overall accuracy, with a 90.4% true-positive rate for ChatGPT-generated text. The tool has also been adopted for community-notes-style fact-checking on social media. False positive rates range from 2% to 29% depending on methodology, but a 100% score for an entire blog article falls far outside any credible false-positive zone. [Pass B Search 3]
