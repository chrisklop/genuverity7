## Executive Summary

On September 22, 2025, FDA Commissioner Dr. Marty Makary stood alongside President Trump and HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. at a White House briefing and announced that the FDA would relabel the drug leucovorin "so that it can be available to children with autism," claiming "hundreds of thousands of kids, in my opinion, will benefit." He added that "20 to 50% of kids with autism" might see improvement. The announcement caused leucovorin prescriptions to surge 71% within two and a half months, according to a study published in The Lancet, creating drug shortages and sending families scrambling for a treatment that had not been approved — and was based on evidence so weak that the largest supporting clinical trial would later be retracted. [1][2][3]

On March 10, 2026 — the day the FDA actually finalized the label change — the reality fell dramatically short of Makary's promises. The FDA approved leucovorin only for cerebral folate transport deficiency caused by a confirmed variant in the FOLR1 gene (CFD-FOLR1), a condition estimated to affect fewer than 1 in 1 million people — approximately 70 children in the entire United States. Senior FDA officials explicitly stated that "right now, we don't have sufficient data to say that we can establish efficacy for autism more broadly," and the head of the FDA's drugs division, Dr. George Tidmarsh, stated plainly: "We're not proposing to approve leucovorin for [people with] the diagnosis of autism." [4][5][6]

The gap between Makary's September claims and the March outcome reveals a pattern of politically driven health messaging under the Trump-RFK Jr. administration: a top government health official publicly overstated an unproven drug's benefits, causing a prescribing surge for a treatment lacking adequate clinical evidence, based on a mechanism theory (folate autoantibodies in autism) that had already been largely abandoned by researchers. The episode was fact-checked by FactCheck.org, covered by NBC News, PBS NewsHour, STAT News, CNN, and Scientific American, and generated widespread expert condemnation. [7][8][9]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The misinformation originated at the highest level of the FDA itself. On September 22, 2025, Commissioner Makary appeared at a White House briefing with Trump, RFK Jr., NIH Director Jay Bhattacharya, CMS Administrator Mehmet Oz, and HHS Deputy Secretary Dorothy Fink. Makary announced a planned Federal Register notice to update leucovorin's label, framing it in sweeping terms as an autism breakthrough for "hundreds of thousands of kids."

The underlying scientific theory — that some autistic children have folate receptor autoantibodies (FRAAs) that block folate transport to the brain — had circulated in academic literature for years. A subset of researchers argued leucovorin could help these children by bypassing blocked receptors. However, only four small clinical trials had ever tested leucovorin in autistic patients (the largest involving 48 children), the evidence was characterized by experts as "thin" and "middling," and a critical problem had emerged: autism-unaffected siblings of autistic children show the same low-folate antibody markers, strongly suggesting the antibodies are not causally related to autism. Despite all this, Makary publicly claimed the drug might help 20–50% of all children with autism.

### Propagation

The announcement was broadcast nationally as a White House press event. Within weeks, prescribing behavior had shifted dramatically. A Lancet study by Dr. Jeremy Faust of Brigham and Women's Hospital found outpatient leucovorin prescriptions for children ages 5–17 rose 71% in the 2.5 months following the September briefing. CNN reported in early March 2026 that parents were scrambling to find the drug amid growing shortages, with some turning to unregulated over-the-counter folate supplements when they couldn't obtain prescriptions.

### Why It Spread

- **Authority halo**: Claims came directly from the sitting FDA Commissioner, not a fringe source
- **Desperate audience**: Families of 2.5 million autistic children in the U.S. are vulnerable to treatment claims
- **Presidential amplification**: Trump personally said the label change "gives hope to the many parents with autistic children"
- **Media amplification**: The White House briefing format gave the claims enormous initial reach
- **FDA institutional capture**: Under RFK Jr., the FDA had removed its webpage of warnings about bogus autism treatments, signaling a shift toward accommodating fringe theories

### Evidentiary Collapse

The scientific basis was already collapsing before the March 2026 approval:

- **January 2026**: The largest randomized controlled trial on leucovorin and autism (77 children, published September 2024 in the *European Journal of Pediatrics*) was retracted after researchers found data table numbers "did not add up correctly," statistical analysis errors, and an inability to replicate results from the dataset provided.
- **Expert characterization**: Dr. Thomas Challman stated the retraction "removes a significant portion of the already weak evidence." Dr. Dorothy Bishop called the broader research quality "uniformly poor." Dr. Shafali Jeste noted the drug's prominence was "because of the publicity around it, not because we as scientists believe that this is the drug that we should be testing."
- **FDA's own finding**: The agency "narrowed its review to focus on uses backed by more robust evidence" and found it could not "establish efficacy for autism more broadly."

### Political Context

The leucovorin episode fits a broader pattern at the FDA under Kennedy's HHS:
- The FDA removed its webpage warning against false autism treatment claims (January 2026)
- The CDC overhauled its vaccine-autism website to suggest previously ignored studies supported a link
- One FDA career official described the culture as: "go find data to support this, go find data to support that" — reversing normal evidence-to-approval procedure
- The Genetic Literacy Project documented the situation in February 2026, calling it the "leucovorin autism 'cure' fiasco" and citing mass expert departures and ad hoc panels bypassing conflict-of-interest rules

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### What Exactly Is CFD-FOLR1, and How Rare Is It?

Cerebral folate transport deficiency (CFD-FOLR1) is an autosomal recessive neurological disorder caused by loss-of-function mutations in the FOLR1 gene. The FOLR1 gene produces folate receptor alpha, a protein embedded in cell membranes that transports folate across the choroid plexus and into cerebrospinal fluid. When both copies of FOLR1 are mutated, folate cannot reach the brain even if systemic folate levels are normal.

The condition is vanishingly rare. Peer-reviewed prevalence data from NCBI GeneReviews confirms fewer than 1 in 1 million individuals worldwide. As of the most recent published literature, fewer than 50 confirmed cases have been identified globally — with some estimates suggesting fewer than 35 fully confirmed cases from 32 families. Applied to the U.S. child population (~73 million), this implies roughly 70 eligible children — exactly the FDA's estimate.

Symptoms begin in late infancy (other mechanisms compensate prenatally), and include severe developmental delay, movement disorders, seizures, and progressive neurological deterioration. Treatment with high-dose oral leucovorin is highly effective at this indication — 87% of CFD-FOLR1 patients show clinical improvement — because the drug provides an alternative folate transport pathway that bypasses the defective receptor.

**Why leucovorin works for CFD-FOLR1 but not general autism:** The mechanism is receptor-bypass. Leucovorin (5-formyltetrahydrofolate) does not use the FOLR1 receptor — it enters cells via a different pathway. This only helps when the FOLR1 receptor is the problem. Most autistic children do not have FOLR1 mutations; they have normally functioning folate transport. Giving them leucovorin does not address any underlying mechanism.

The "folate receptor autoantibody" theory Makary invoked — that immune antibodies block the FOLR1 receptor in autistic children without a genetic mutation — remains unverified. Critically, autism-unaffected siblings of autistic children show the same antibody prevalence, which strongly suggests the antibodies are not causally linked to autism.

### AAP Formal Position: Not Recommended

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) published interim guidance in October 2025 — less than a month after Makary's White House briefing — explicitly declining to recommend leucovorin for routine autism use. The AAP FAQ for prescribing clinicians contains critical safety and evidentiary warnings that directly contradict the administration's framing:

**On evidence:** "Current evidence is insufficient to support prescribing leucovorin for autism in the absence of CFD." Key limitations identified: all studies involve fewer than 100 patients; most originate from "the same group of researchers"; no "large multicenter phase 3 randomized controlled trial" has been completed.

**On safety risks — high-dose folate masking B12 deficiency:** "High-dose folinic acid can mask hematologic signs of vitamin B12 deficiency while allowing neurologic damage to continue." This is an active harm risk in children receiving off-label prescriptions without proper monitoring.

**Critical contraindication:** Leucovorin is contraindicated in MTHFS (5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate synthetase) deficiency — a separate metabolic disorder where folinic acid cannot be processed, causing toxic buildup. Children with undiagnosed MTHFS deficiency receiving leucovorin for presumed autism could be harmed.

**Side effects documented:** Irritability, gastrointestinal disturbance (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), dermatological reactions, and anaphylaxis.

### The Shortage's Real Victims: Cancer Patients

Leucovorin has a decades-long primary use in oncology — it is given alongside the chemotherapy drug 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) to treat colorectal cancer and other malignancies. The demand surge created by the White House briefing created a direct competition with cancer patients for a finite drug supply.

By early December 2025, the FDA took the unusual step of issuing a "Dear Provider" letter authorizing emergency imports of leucovorin tablets from Canada and Spain to help ease shortfalls — a rare intervention typically reserved for declared drug shortages. Despite this, CVS Health spokesperson Roslyn Guarino confirmed: "We are seeing supply challenges among certain versions of generic leucovorin." The American Society of Health-Systems Pharmacists (ASHP) shortage list showed most leucovorin manufacturers had placed tablets on allocation or backorder.

The Oncology News Central noted the FDA does not have leucovorin officially listed as being in shortage — a designation that would trigger additional mitigation — creating a gap between the supply reality and the regulatory response.
