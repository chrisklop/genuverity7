## Executive Summary

On February 17, 2026, the Department of Homeland Security posted a statement on its official X account claiming that up to 4 million "illegal" immigrant households would be removed from Medicaid under a new DHS public charge policy. The post reproduced a graphic from the Kaiser Family Foundation (KFF) while fundamentally misrepresenting what the data shows. Georgetown University's Center for Children and Families (CCF) published a point-by-point fact-check the same day, documenting multiple layers of misrepresentation: the KFF data concerns "noncitizen households" — a category that includes US citizens, lawful permanent residents, refugees, and other lawfully present immigrants — not "illegal immigrants" as DHS claimed. Undocumented immigrants have never been eligible for full federal Medicaid coverage under any existing law [1][2][3].

The misrepresentation is compounded by a second layer of deception: the 4 million figure does not represent people who will be "removed" from Medicaid. It represents the upper-bound estimate of people who might voluntarily disenroll due to fear generated by DHS's own public charge rule — and DHS itself acknowledged in its regulatory filing that the enrollment impacts "relate predominantly to enrollment decisions made by those who are not subject to the public charge" test [4][5]. In other words, the administration cited fear it deliberately created as evidence of a problem it invented. Georgetown CCF noted the DHS post was "nearly identical" to one posted a day earlier by the White Papers Policy Institute, an organization whose stated beliefs include replacement theory — a white nationalist doctrine [6].

The real-world harm from this disinformation is substantial. According to KFF analysis, 13.4 million Medicaid and CHIP enrollees live in households with at least one noncitizen, and 5.9 million of them are US citizen children [7]. If projected disenrollment rates of 10–30% materialize, between 600,000 and 1.8 million US citizen children would lose health coverage — not because they are ineligible, but because their parents are too frightened by enforcement rhetoric to enroll them. The claim has also been used to justify the "One Big Beautiful Bill" reconciliation package, which CBO projects would result in 10.3 million people losing Medicaid and CHIP coverage by 2034 [8].

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin of the Claim

The DHS X post appeared on February 17, 2026, just one day after an identical post appeared from the White Papers Policy Institute (WPPI), a far-right organization associated with replacement theory ideology. Georgetown CCF's Joan Alker and colleagues noted the DHS post was "almost identical" to the WPPI post and appeared to have been copied from it. The DHS version was, however, described by researchers as "even more inaccurate" — where the original WPPI post mentioned "non-citizen households," DHS added the explicit relabeling to "illegal" immigrants.

The underlying data graphic DHS shared came from KFF's analysis of the 2023 American Community Survey (ACS). KFF estimated that 3.5 million Medicaid enrollees and approximately 600,000 CHIP enrollees lived in a household with at least one noncitizen — a combined 4.1 million. This is the number DHS rounded to "4 million" and described as "illegal immigrants." The KFF data has nothing to do with immigration enforcement and does not identify any enrollee as undocumented.

### The Three-Layer Misrepresentation

**Layer 1 — Wrong Population Label:** "Noncitizen household" is not synonymous with "illegal immigrant household." A noncitizen household under the ACS definition is any household in which at least one member is not a US citizen. This includes: US citizen children with a noncitizen parent, lawful permanent residents, refugees, asylees, DACA recipients, TPS holders, people on work visas, and mixed-status families with no undocumented member whatsoever. KFF data shows there are more lawfully present noncitizens in the US than undocumented immigrants. Applying the label "illegal" to this population is demonstrably false.

**Layer 2 — Wrong Mechanism:** DHS framed the 4 million figure as people who "will be removed" from Medicaid. In reality, the 4 million is the upper-bound estimate of people who might voluntarily disenroll due to fear of the public charge rule. These are people who choose to leave a program they are eligible for because they are frightened — not people who are removed because they were ineligible. The distinction is the difference between enforcement and fear-induced behavior.

**Layer 3 — Circular Logic / Manufactured Pretext:** Most devastatingly, DHS's own regulatory language admits the disenrollment would "relate predominantly to enrollment decisions made by those who are not subject to the public charge" test. The people who would leave Medicaid are not the undocumented immigrants the rule is supposedly targeting — they are US citizens and lawfully present immigrants disenrolling voluntarily because the enforcement environment is too threatening. DHS created the fear, then cited the resulting fear-driven disenrollment as evidence of a Medicaid "illegal immigrant" problem.

### Why the Claim Spread

1. **Official authority:** The claim came from a federal agency's verified account, lending institutional credibility to a demonstrably false statistic.
2. **Number simplicity:** "4 million illegal immigrants on Medicaid" is memorable and emotionally activating; the actual explanation ("upper-bound estimate of voluntary disenrollment from mixed-status households due to public charge chilling effect") is complex and unrewarding for viral spread.
3. **Legislative utility:** The claim provided political cover for budget reconciliation cuts to Medicaid — framing $1 trillion in cuts as "protecting the program for Americans" rather than slashing coverage for children.
4. **Media amplification without adequate context:** Many mainstream outlets reported the controversy without emphasizing the most important fact: undocumented immigrants were never eligible for Medicaid in the first place.
5. **Pre-existing misinformation ecosystem:** The claim echoed a long-running false narrative that immigrants drain Medicaid, creating confirmation bias that accelerated spread. The DHS post followed the White House "One Big Beautiful Bill" messaging campaign by just weeks.

### Evidence of the Misrepresentation

Georgetown CCF: "The graphic clearly states it pertains to 'non-citizen households,' which include mixed-status households with U.S. citizens and lawfully present individuals with no undocumented members."

DHS's own regulatory text on the public charge rule: The enrollment impacts "relate predominantly to enrollment decisions made by those who are not subject to the public charge" test.

KFF: Of the 4.1 million enrollees in noncitizen households estimated by DHS, approximately 1.8 million are US citizen children.

Federal law (established since 1996 PRWORA): Undocumented immigrants are not eligible to enroll in federally funded Medicaid, CHIP, or Medicare. The only exception is Emergency Medicaid, which reimburses providers for emergency care and represents less than 1% of total Medicaid spending.

## Evidence Deep-Dive — Pass B

### B.1 — The ACS Methodology: How "Noncitizen Household" Was Constructed

The KFF analysis relied on the 2023 American Community Survey (ACS). The ACS does not include a direct measure of whether a noncitizen has legal status. KFF uses a methodology drawing on the 2013 analysis by the State Health Access Data Assistance Center (SHADAC) and recommendations from Van Hook et al., using the Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP) to develop a predictive model for immigration status. This methodology means the 4.1 million figure is a household-level estimate — counting any household with at least one noncitizen member — not a count of undocumented individuals enrolled in Medicaid.

**Critical composition breakdown (KFF, ACS 2023):**
- 13.4 million total Medicaid/CHIP enrollees in noncitizen households
- 5.9 million are US citizen children
- 1.8 million are eligible but currently uninsured individuals in these households (not yet enrolled)
- The undocumented sub-population within this 13.4 million is already legally ineligible for full Medicaid; they are only present in the household, not enrolled
- DHS's projected disenrollment of 460,000 was itself a gross underestimate — KFF's parallel methodology projects 1.3 to 4.0 million disenrollments depending on rate (10-30%)

**The KFF-DHS methodology gap:**
DHS used a household inference methodology that projected only 460,000 total disenrollments/forgone enrollments. KFF's direct ACS analysis projected 1.3 to 4.0 million. The DHS figure was 3-10x lower than KFF's independent estimate — while paradoxically DHS used KFF's own data graphic to support its post.

### B.2 — The Historical Evidence That Chilling Effects Are Real

The 2019-2020 public charge rule provides documented precedent that the DHS post's projections are not theoretical. Migration Policy Institute analysis of Census Bureau ACS data found that during the first three years of the first Trump administration, participation in TANF, SNAP, and Medicaid declined twice as fast among noncitizens as among citizens (Migration Policy Institute, 2020).

**Specific documented impacts (2016-2019):**
- Participation in Medicaid and CHIP fell by **18%** among low-income US citizen children with a noncitizen in the household, compared to **8%** among those in citizen-only households — a 10-point gap explained by the public charge chilling effect, not by eligibility changes
- Benefits use among noncitizen adults fell faster than for citizen children, with program participation dropping nearly twice as fast among noncitizen adults as among US-born counterparts
- Approximately **25%** of noncitizen immigrants (95% CI: 15-35%) avoided public benefit enrollment due to the changes in the public charge rule definition, per peer-reviewed research
- The 2019 rule expanded the share of noncitizen immigrants who could be considered a public charge from **3% to 47%** — a 16-fold increase in the population facing potential denial of legal status

**The 2025 environment is demonstrably more severe** than 2019. Key data from KFF/New York Times 2025 Survey of Immigrants (n=1,805, nationally representative, August-October 2025):
- **29%** of immigrant adults skipped or postponed healthcare in the past 12 months — up from 22% in 2023

- **40%** of immigrant adults overall experienced negative health impacts (stress, anxiety, sleep or eating problems, worsening chronic conditions)

- **18%** of immigrant parents report their child's well-being has been negatively impacted since January 2025

### B.3 — H.R. 1 "One Big Beautiful Bill": The Legislative Context and Floor Debate

H.R. 1 — the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — was signed by President Trump on July 4, 2025, after passing the House 215-214 and the Senate 51-50 (with Vice President Vance casting the tie-breaking vote). The bill contains the largest cuts to Medicaid in the program's 60-year history.

**CBO projections (final enacted version):**
- $911 billion reduction in federal Medicaid spending over 10 years
- 10.9 million more people uninsured in 2034 compared to baseline (updated CBO score)
- 7.5 million coverage losses attributable specifically to Medicaid provisions
- 2.1 million additional losses through ACA marketplace provisions
- The bill simultaneously allocated $170 billion to ICE and immigration detention expansion

**The legislative framing:**
The ACLU summarized the core political logic: "H.R. 1 is the biggest cut to Medicaid in history — slashing approximately $1 trillion, which will result in at least 12 million people being removed from the program... The politicians who supported H.R. 1 took health care away from millions, while giving $170 billion in taxpayer dollars to an immigration police force larger than most of the world's militaries."

The DHS "4 million illegal immigrants" claim provided political cover by framing cuts affecting primarily US citizens, disabled individuals, veterans, and low-income workers as a purge of ineligible immigrants — a fundamentally false characterization. According to KFF analysis, 1.4 million lawfully present immigrants — not undocumented people — are the primary non-citizen population losing Medicaid coverage under the 2025 law.

**Who the cuts actually affect:**
- US citizens: the overwhelming majority of the 10.9 million projected to lose coverage
- Lawfully present immigrants (LPRs, refugees, DACA, TPS, work visa holders): approximately 1.4 million losing coverage per KFF
- Undocumented immigrants: already ineligible for full Medicaid; minimally impacted by the cuts
- Children: particularly vulnerable; 1 in 4 US children has an immigrant parent, and parental coverage loss produces cascade effects on child enrollment

### B.4 — The Circular Logic Dissected

The most analytically damning aspect of the DHS disinformation: it describes the consequences of its own fear-creation as evidence of a pre-existing problem. The sequence:

1. DHS proposes public charge rule → immigrants and mixed-status families disenroll from Medicaid out of fear
2. DHS cites projected disenrollment numbers as "proof" of 4 million illegal immigrants on Medicaid
3. The disenrollment, per DHS's own regulatory text, "relates predominantly to enrollment decisions made by those who are not subject to the public charge test"
4. The people disenrolling are US citizens and lawfully present immigrants — the exact population the rule is NOT targeting
5. DHS presents this fear-driven disenrollment as a "cleanup" of ineligible enrollment

Georgetown CCF's Joan Alker documented this circular logic explicitly: the DHS X post claimed the policy "will remove up to 4 million from Medicaid" but the people who would leave Medicaid are not the people being legally removed — they are eligible enrollees being frightened out of their own coverage.

## Contemporary Context & Political Dimensions — Pass C

### C.1 — The CMS-ICE Data Sharing Regime: The Structural Enforcement of the Chilling Effect

The February 2026 DHS disinformation post did not exist in isolation. By February 2026, a parallel data-sharing infrastructure had been built that gave the chilling effect institutional teeth. In summer 2025, CMS entered into a formal data-sharing agreement with ICE, giving deportation officers access to Medicaid enrollee data — a direct reversal of prior explicit assurances to the public, applicants, providers, and states that Medicaid data would never be used for immigration enforcement.

**Timeline of the CMS-ICE data regime:**
- June 2025: CMS begins sharing Medicaid patient data with ICE
- July 2025: CMS and ICE formalize a data-sharing Information Exchange Agreement
- November 25, 2025: Federal Register publishes the Notice of Medicaid Information Sharing Between CMS and DHS
- Summer 2025: California files suit; 21 other states join; judge issues preliminary injunction
- December 2025: Judge issues narrower preliminary injunction — allows sharing of limited data (name, address, citizenship status, DOB, Medicaid ID) only for individuals not lawfully present, in the 22 plaintiff states
- January 6, 2026: CMS authorized to resume data sharing with ICE while litigation continues
- 28 non-plaintiff states: full data access for deportation officers, without court restrictions

**The data-sharing technical problem:** The CMS T-MSIS data system — the database being shared — cannot reliably distinguish undocumented immigrants from lawfully present immigrants who share the same eligibility category. Georgetown CCF warned this means the data sharing likely captures lawfully present immigrants in violation of the court order, adding to the chilling effect by demonstrating that enrollment creates a searchable record regardless of legal status.

**Impact on enrollment:** 51% of immigrant adults were "very concerned" or "somewhat concerned" their health providers would share information with ICE. 14% of all immigrant adults avoided seeking medical care since January 2025 specifically because of these concerns.

### C.2 — Pattern of Repeated Misrepresentation: DHS Was Not a One-Off

Georgetown CCF documented a pattern of White House and administration officials misrepresenting immigrant Medicaid data throughout 2025-2026:

- **May 2025** — FactCheck.org documented a false claim circulating on social media and amplified by official accounts claiming illegal immigrants were on Medicaid (Georgetown CCF, "The Truth About Medicaid Coverage for Immigrants," May 21, 2025)
- **June 2025** — Georgetown CCF published "More Fact Checking on Medicaid Spending for Immigrants" after additional White House misrepresentations (June 4, 2025)
- **June 2025** — Georgetown CCF fact-checked a White House "Mythbuster" document about Medicaid cuts and immigrants: "Factchecking The White House 'Mythbuster' on Medicaid Cuts and Immigrants" (June 10, 2025)
- **October 2025** — Georgetown CCF published "Fact Checking Claims about Medicaid and Marketplace Health Coverage for Immigrants — Government Shutdown Edition" (Oct. 2, 2025)
- **February 2026** — The DHS X post (the subject of this report)

The DHS February 2026 post was at least the fifth distinct episode of major disinformation about immigrant Medicaid access within a single year, all following a consistent pattern: mischaracterizing the population in KFF or government data from "noncitizens" or "noncitizen households" to "illegal immigrants."

### C.3 — State Responses and Legal Landscape

Twenty-two states sued the Trump administration over the CMS-ICE Medicaid data sharing, representing the strongest organized resistance to the enforcement regime. States have also taken independent action to protect immigrant health access:

- As of April 2025, **37 states plus DC** cover lawfully residing immigrant children without a five-year waiting period in Medicaid and CHIP — a protection directly threatened by H.R. 1 cuts
- State attorneys general in the 22 plaintiff states have sought to limit ICE's access to Medicaid data while litigation continues
- California (Medi-Cal) and other states using state-only funds to cover immigrants not eligible for federal Medicaid face indirect pressure as federal matching funds are reduced under H.R. 1

**The H.R. 1 impact on lawfully present immigrants specifically:**
KFF estimates 1.4 million lawfully present immigrants will lose health coverage under the enacted 2025 tax and budget law — including victims of human trafficking, domestic violence survivors, and other protected populations who had previously been eligible under state Medicaid expansions. This represents a direct policy outcome rooted in the false framing that only "illegal immigrants" were affected.

### C.4 — Who Benefits, Who Is Harmed: The Political Economy

**Who benefits from the disinformation:**
- The "4 million illegal immigrants on Medicaid" claim provided a politically popular justification for the largest Medicaid cut in the program's history
- The framing recast cuts predominantly affecting US citizens, disabled Americans, veterans, and low-income working families as immigration enforcement — polling-positive framing that obscured the actual population of people losing coverage
- Congressional floor debate used the DHS framing to deflect amendments that would have protected citizen coverage from the cuts

**Who is harmed:**
- **5.9 million US citizen children** in noncitizen households whose coverage is destabilized by the chilling effect — they are eligible for Medicaid but may not remain enrolled when parents are afraid
- **1.4 million lawfully present immigrants** losing coverage directly under H.R. 1 — legal residents, refugees, and domestic violence survivors
- **10.9 million total** projected to lose Medicaid/CHIP by 2034 under CBO's updated H.R. 1 estimate, the overwhelming majority of whom are US citizens
- **Hospitals and healthcare systems**: The AHA noted that when insured patients disenroll and show up uninsured in emergency rooms — sicker than if they had received preventive care — it increases uncompensated care costs and wait times for all patients
- **Emergency rooms**: Without preventive Medicaid coverage, conditions like uncontrolled diabetes, hypertension, and prenatal care gaps generate more expensive emergency presentations that cost hospitals and states more than the enrollment would have
- **1 in 5 immigrant parents** report their child's well-being has been negatively impacted since January 2025 — a direct measurable harm to American children attributable to enforcement rhetoric

**The fiscal counter-narrative:**
Emergency Medicaid for noncitizens cost $3.8 billion in FY2023 — 0.4% of total Medicaid spending. The program being cut is 250 times larger than the problem being cited to justify the cuts. If the stated goal were actually fiscal responsibility regarding noncitizen enrollment, the maximum achievable savings from eliminating all noncitizen Medicaid costs would be approximately 6% of total spending (the share attributable to the 6% of enrollees who are lawfully present noncitizens eligible for Medicaid). The $911 billion in H.R. 1 Medicaid cuts represents 97% of the program's 10-year baseline — the vast majority falling on US citizens.
