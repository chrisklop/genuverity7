## Executive Summary

The Department of Homeland Security launched wow.dhs.gov on December 8, 2025, as a public database of approximately 25,000 arrested immigrants whom the agency branded the "worst of the worst." The site was actively promoted by DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, the White House, and administration officials on social media to justify aggressive immigration enforcement operations. A CNN investigation published February 19, 2026, forced DHS to admit the website contained widespread errors — with hundreds of individuals incorrectly described as having been arrested for or convicted of serious crimes, when in reality they faced only minor charges such as single traffic offenses, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry. DHS blamed the inaccuracies on a "glitch" affecting "about 5% of entries" but declined to explain how a technical glitch could cause individuals to be mischaracterized. [1][2][3]

The errors on wow.dhs.gov are not isolated to the website. Independent data from the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse (TRAC), the Cato Institute, and the Center for Migration Studies have systematically debunked the administration's broader enforcement narrative. As of February 7, 2026, 73.6% of people held in ICE detention had no criminal conviction — a figure that directly contradicts Secretary Noem's January 2026 claim that "70% of them have committed or have charges against them on violent crimes." The administration's claim that 1.6–2 million undocumented immigrants had voluntarily self-deported has been called "vastly overstated and based on conjecture, not facts" by the Center for Migration Studies, with no supporting data provided to journalists who requested it. [4][5][6][7]

The pattern of factual inaccuracy extends beyond databases to field operations. At least five ICE shooting incidents in 2025–2026 featured initial official accounts that were later directly contradicted by video evidence. The most prominent case — the shooting of Venezuelan immigrant Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis in Minneapolis on January 14, 2026 — resulted in DHS admitting its agents appeared to have made "untruthful statements" under oath. The charges DHS brought against Sosa-Celis and another Venezuelan man were dismissed after the U.S. Attorney found newly discovered evidence "materially inconsistent" with FBI affidavits and sworn court testimony. ICE's acting director said the agents could face termination and criminal prosecution. [8][9][10]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin: The WOW Website as Propaganda Infrastructure

DHS launched wow.dhs.gov on December 8, 2025, with an initial list of 10,000 arrested immigrants. The website was framed from inception as a public accountability tool — "letting you find criminal illegal aliens removed from your community" — but functioned primarily as a propaganda instrument to justify mass enforcement operations. DHS press releases used extreme inflammatory language, with headlines like "ICE Law Enforcement Risk Their Lives to Arrest Worst of the Worst Criminal Illegal Alien Pedophiles, Sex Criminals, and Armed Robbers."

Secretary Noem personally promoted the site repeatedly on social media. The White House amplified it. DHS continued adding entries, reaching approximately 25,000 by mid-February 2026 and then 30,000 after the CNN story broke. The database listed individuals alongside the crimes the agency claimed they were arrested for or convicted of.

### The Errors: What CNN Found

CNN reviewed the website and found it fell into two categories of misrepresentation:

1. **Serious crime overcounting:** Thousands were described as convicted of or arrested for sex crimes or homicide. DHS's retrospective defense was that individuals listed with only one minor crime actually had multiple offenses on their record — but the website only showed the minor charge, making them appear to be low-level offenders misplaced in the database.

2. **Minor crimes in a "worst of the worst" database:** Hundreds were genuinely arrested only for traffic offenses, marijuana possession, or illegal reentry — a federal felony that criminalizes crossing the border after a prior deportation. These individuals were labeled "worst of the worst" alongside violent criminals.

3. **Geographic inflation:** Jurisdictions with the highest arrest numbers often contained large federal prisons. DHS was claiming credit for people already incarcerated — implying they had been lurking in communities as public safety threats when they had already been in custody.

### DHS Response: Minimal Accountability

DHS spokesperson's response to CNN: "This is a glitch on the WOW website the impacted about 5% of the entries." The spokesperson did not explain what type of technical glitch could cause human beings to be described by incorrect crimes. DHS claimed the glitch was "resolved" by February 18, 2026 — one day before the CNN story published.

The following day, February 20, DHS published a press release titled: "To Award the Mainstream Media for Raising Awareness About DHS's Worst of the Worst Website, DHS Drops Another 5,000 Criminals." Rather than respond to the accountability reporting with contrition or correction, DHS added 5,000 more entries and sarcastically thanked the press for publicizing the site.

### Why It Spread

The website benefited from top-down amplification by the administration's communications infrastructure. It tapped into an existing media frame ("immigrant criminals") that required no supporting evidence to circulate. The specific language — "worst of the worst" — is emotionally loaded and requires no substantive engagement with who is actually on the list. Corrections require reading data; the original claim required only a headline. Right-wing media outlets reproduced DHS framing without independent verification for 73 days before CNN's investigation forced a public admission.

### Evidence: The Broader Pattern of False ICE Enforcement Claims

The wow.dhs.gov errors are one data point in a documented pattern:

- **Kristi Noem's 70% violent crime claim:** Noem stated on January 18, 2026 that "70% of them have committed or have charges against them on violent crimes." TRAC data shows 73.6% of ICE detainees as of February 7, 2026 had no criminal conviction. Only 5% of detainees from October-November 2025 had been convicted of violent crime, per Cato Institute analysis.

- **The "2 million deportations/self-deportations" claim:** DHS stated 2.5 million illegal aliens had "left the U.S." under Trump. The Center for Migration Studies analyzed this as "vastly overstated," noting that the 1.6–2 million self-deportation figure had no data backing it, and even the analysts whose chart DHS cited included "prominent caveats" that the data might be a "statistical artifact." The Deportation Data Project found actual deportations since January 2025 totaled approximately 350,000 — not 2 million.

- **DHS vs. Cato Institute:** When Cato published data showing 73% of ICE detainees had no criminal conviction, DHS called the report "made up" with "no legitimate data behind it." Cato subsequently obtained FOIA data from ICE directly, which confirmed 71% of ICE arrests from October 1–15, 2025 had no criminal convictions. DHS's denial was itself false.

- **ICE agents lying under oath (Minneapolis shooting):** DHS initially portrayed the January 14, 2026 shooting of Venezuelan immigrant Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis as an officer defending himself against a violent ambush. Noem called it "an attempted murder of federal law enforcement." Video evidence showed Sosa-Celis was shot while closing his front door. Two agents were placed on administrative leave; charges against Sosa-Celis and co-defendant Alfredo Alejandro Aljorna were dismissed. A federal criminal probe was opened against the agents.

## Evidence Deep-Dive (Pass B)

### B1: The WOW Website — Scope and Scale of Errors

The WOW website's geographic distribution reveals a pattern beyond random error: almost half the listed individuals are from Mexico, followed by Honduras (2,100+), Guatemala and Cuba (~1,900 each), El Salvador (~1,200), and smaller numbers from Iran, China, Nicaragua, Haiti, and Jamaica. The geographic inflation finding — that jurisdictions with the highest arrest numbers often contain large federal prisons — is compounded by the national origin data: the website's top countries of origin align with U.S. immigration enforcement priorities, not necessarily with the criminal backgrounds of those listed. [Source: CNN investigation, DHS.gov website data, Feb 2026]

A Common Dreams analysis found that the total number of individuals on the WOW website (approximately 25,000) represents less than 5% of all ICE arrests since the start of the Trump administration — meaning even by DHS's own framing, over 95% of ICE arrests were *not* "worst of the worst." [22]

### B2: CU Boulder Study — Peer-Reviewed Analysis of ICE Arrest Data

A February 2026 study by CU Boulder labor economist Chloe East analyzed federal FOIA data on all ICE arrests from October 2015 through October 2025. Key findings published February 18, 2026:

- Daily ICE arrests nearly tripled under Trump's second term: from 304 per day (Biden final year) to 821 per day (Trump Jan–Oct 2025), an increase of 170%.
- The percentage of ICE arrests involving people with a criminal record reached a near-historic low: approximately 30% in the most recent data, down from 52% during Biden's final year, 70% during Trump's first-term first year, and 79% during Obama's final year.
- Nearly half of all ICE arrests in 2025 were community arrests (up from only 19% in 2024 under Biden), meaning agents were arresting people in neighborhoods, workplaces, and homes rather than at jails or after criminal proceedings.
- Democratic-leaning jurisdictions saw disproportionate spikes in community arrests, suggesting political targeting rather than purely criminal enforcement prioritization. [23]

### B3: Brennan Center Analysis — Scale of Detention Expansion

The Brennan Center for Justice documented the financial and operational scale of the detention expansion in 2026:

- Between October 1, 2024 and June 16, 2025, ICE arrested approximately 204,000 people. Approximately 65% (133,000 individuals) had no criminal record at all.
- Congress allocated $75 billion to ICE over four years (~$18.7 billion/year), with $28.7 billion available in FY 2025 alone — exceeding the entire DOJ budget request for the federal prison system ($11.25 billion), which holds 155,000 people.
- By end of November 2025, ICE was using 104 more detention facilities than at the start of the year — a 91% increase in facilities, adding tent camps with documented brutal conditions.
- ICE detention on any given day increased over 75% in one year, from ~40,000 to ~70,000+. [24]

### B4: PolitiFact and FactCheck.org — Systematic Fact-Checking of the 70% Claim

Multiple independent fact-checking organizations examined Noem's January 18, 2026 claim that "70% of them have committed or have charges against them on violent crimes":

- **PolitiFact** (January 23, 2026): Rated the claim as relying on conflation of convictions with pending charges. "Someone with a pending charge who is not convicted is not usually called a 'criminal' in our criminal system." Even including pending charges, the figure did not reach 70%.
- **FactCheck.org**: Found that of ICE arrests in Trump's first 10 months, only 37% involved individuals with criminal convictions — down from 52% under Biden's final year.
- **Econofact**: A UC Berkeley School of Law assistant professor noted the administration's definition problem: charges are not convictions. The claim misrepresents how criminal justice terminology works.
- **Wisconsin Watch** (January 2026): Found that as of early January 2026, approximately 52% had either criminal convictions *or* pending criminal charges combined — still below Noem's 70% claim — and only 29% had actual convictions.

The consistent finding across five independent fact-checkers: Noem's "70%" figure inflates the number by including pending charges as "criminal," and even that inflated figure was inaccurate as of the date she made the claim. [25][26][27]

### B5: The "Cannibal" Story — A Case Study in DHS Fabrication

On February 23, 2026, The Intercept published an investigation revealing that a story Noem repeatedly told — about ICE detaining and deporting a cannibal who then "began to eat himself" on a deportation flight — was entirely fabricated. Three federal law enforcement officials, including one from within DHS itself, told The Intercept the story was "completely false." When DHS was asked for records, none could be found. DHS's explanation was that Noem was told the story by an air marshal on a deportation flight — meaning she based repeated public claims (including one alongside President Trump at a press conference) on an unverified, third-hand story she never sought to confirm. The incident illustrates that the pattern of false DHS enforcement claims is not confined to statistical misrepresentation — it extends to fabricated anecdotes used for rhetorical effect at the highest levels of the agency. [28]

## Contemporary Context (Pass C)

### C1: Congressional Oversight — Hearings, Letters, and Obstruction

The Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee held an oversight hearing on February 12, 2026, chaired by Senator Rand Paul (R-KY). Witnesses included ICE Acting Director Todd Lyons, CBP Commissioner Rodney Scott, and USCIS Director Joseph Edlow, alongside Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison and Minnesota Corrections Commissioner Paul Schnell. Key outcomes:

- Senators questioned DHS officials about use-of-force policies and deescalation training in the wake of the Minneapolis killings.
- DHS officials declined to directly address the Minneapolis agents' false testimony under oath.
- The hearing also addressed DHS's decision to cut the Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties, an internal watchdog, as ICE enforcement expanded.

Separately, a federal judge twice blocked Secretary Noem's attempt to require 7 days' advance notice before members of Congress could inspect immigration detention facilities — a move federal courts found conflicted with Congress's constitutional oversight authority. As of February 2026, a bipartisan impeachment resolution sponsored by Rep. Robin Kelly (D-IL) had attracted support from over 180 House Democrats, citing obstruction of oversight, personal use of her position, circumventing federal contracting law, and repeated civil rights violations. [29][30][31]

### C2: DHS Leadership and the WOW Website's Origins

Secretary Kristi Noem personally launched and championed the WOW website from its December 8, 2025 inception. ICE Acting Director Todd Lyons testified before Congress without addressing the website's factual errors. DHS's February 20, 2026 press release — sarcastically "awarding" mainstream media for publicizing the site — was authored by DHS communications staff under Noem's leadership and published on the official DHS.gov domain, indicating institutional endorsement rather than a rogue communications decision.

The pattern of false claims attributed to Noem personally includes: the "70% violent criminals" claim (Jan 18), the "2 million self-deportations" claim (ongoing), the false characterization of the Minneapolis shooting as an ambush (Jan 14), and the fabricated "cannibal deportee" story (multiple occasions, including alongside the President). Three of these four claims were publicly contradicted within weeks by data, video evidence, or federal law enforcement officials from within DHS itself. [32]

### C3: Impact on Immigrant Communities

Arrests and aggressive ICE enforcement operations created measurable fear and community disruption by early 2026:

- Thousands of Minneapolis residents joined a church-run grocery delivery program for immigrant families too afraid to appear in public near federal agents.
- Two DHS-related fatal shootings in Minneapolis (Renee Nicole Good, January 7 — an American citizen killed; and Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis shooting, January 14) ignited large-scale protests in the Twin Cities.
- January 2026 alone saw at least 8 ICE-related deaths, with 2026 on track to exceed 2025's record of 31 deaths in ICE custody.
- The ACLU filed a class-action lawsuit against the Trump administration on behalf of Minnesotans subjected to warrantless arrests, racially profiling, and unconstitutional stops by ICE and CBP — a direct legal response to the enforcement surge that produced many WOW website entries. [33][34]

### C4: Transparency Under Previous Administrations — A Comparative View

The current administration's enforcement data practices represent a break from prior norms in specific ways:

- **Under Obama**: DHS published regular monthly enforcement statistics through the Office of Homeland Security Statistics (OHSS). Enforcement was criticized for high volume but data was publicly available. Obama oversaw 5.3 million total removals across two terms.
- **Under Biden**: OHSS continued publishing monthly data; enforcement ran approximately 1.3 million removals in Biden's first year. Biden's numbers were also large but driven heavily by Title 42 border returns rather than interior enforcement.
- **Under Trump (second term)**: DHS stopped publishing regular in-depth monthly data as of early 2025. TRAC noted that "DHS stopped publishing in-depth monthly data" making independent analysis harder. The Deportation Data Project obtained records only through FOIA litigation, not voluntary disclosure. Trump administration deported far fewer people than Biden in comparable periods while claiming vastly higher numbers — a documented inversion of the enforcement-to-claims ratio of prior administrations. [35][36]
