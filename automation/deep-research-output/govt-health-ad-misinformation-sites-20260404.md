## Executive Summary

A peer-reviewed Yale University study published April 3, 2026 in *JAMA Network Open* — and reported as a major investigation by CNN — documented that health organizations and government agencies spent a combined $35.7 million advertising on 11 news websites that routinely promote health misinformation between 2021 and 2024. [1][2] The affected advertisers include some of the most credible names in American public health: the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), pharmaceutical giants Pfizer and Eli Lilly, the American Heart Association, and the Alzheimer's Association. These 11 misinformation-promoting websites generated a total of $336 million in advertising revenue during the study period, meaning that health-sector advertisers accounted for more than 10 percent of that revenue — an ironic and consequential subsidy from the very institutions fighting health misinformation. [3][4]

The mechanism behind this counterintuitive phenomenon is programmatic advertising: an automated, algorithm-driven system in which ads are bought and placed at machine speed across thousands of websites simultaneously, leaving brand managers with little or no visibility into exactly which sites carry their content. [5][6] Researchers at Yale, working with the media-credibility analytics firm NewsGuard, identified that the 11 studied sites spread a variety of false health claims — including anti-vaccine narratives, unproven remedies, and distorted accounts of the causes of disease. [1][7] Named misinformation sites in the dataset include Newsmax and ZeroHedge. The researchers warned that "advertisements from these organizations may enhance trust in misinformation or diminish trust in the government or health organization" — a dual harm that threatens both the efficacy of public health messaging and the credibility of institutions that produce it. [2][3]

All major organizations named in the study characterized the ad placements as unintentional. The HHS, whose advertising predated the current administration, acknowledged through spokesman Andrew Nixon that "the fact that taxpayer dollars were directed to outlets flagged for spreading health misinformation is a serious failure of oversight and accountability," while stating HHS is "doing things differently" under Secretary Kennedy's leadership. [4] The American Heart Association and Alzheimer's Association each issued statements confirming their ads were placed by automated systems without their direct knowledge. [8] Independent commentary from University at Buffalo communications professor Yotam Ophir and former CDC official Demetre Daskalakis reflected a real tension in public health outreach: reaching audiences who consume misinformation requires being present on the platforms those audiences use — but appearing on those platforms carries its own reputational and epistemic risks. [3]

## Forensic Analysis

### Origin

The story originates from a research letter by Neeraj G. Patel (Yale School of Medicine, fourth-year medical student and first author) and colleagues, with corresponding author Dr. Joseph S. Ross (joseph.ross@yale.edu), published in *JAMA Network Open* on April 3, 2026 (DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2026.5068). [9] The researchers partnered with NewsGuard — a firm that employs trained journalists to rate the reliability of news and information websites — to identify a dataset of health-misinformation-promoting news sites for which advertising expenditure data was also available via platforms like Google DV360 and measurement provider Comscore. [1][7]

### Propagation

The study analyzed 11 news websites with NewsGuard "red" (unreliable) ratings specifically related to health misinformation. These sites collectively attracted $336 million in advertising revenue from 2021 to 2024. The advertising came overwhelmingly through programmatic channels: automated ad-buying platforms that run real-time auctions for ad impressions across millions of websites simultaneously. Individual advertisers — even major government agencies — often have no knowledge that their campaigns are appearing on specific sites. [5][6]

The most common category of health-sector advertisers on these sites was nonprescription wellness products (supplements, weight management products), which spent more than $19 million on these sites over the study period — nearly 6% of the sites' total advertising revenue. Government and nonprofit health organizations, while spending far less individually, contributed the symbolically significant share that drew headlines. [2][3]

### Why It Spread

Three structural factors explain why this problem is endemic to the modern advertising ecosystem:

1. **Scale and automation**: Programmatic advertising systems run trillions of ad auctions daily. At that velocity, human review of individual placements is impossible. Advertisers rely on keyword blocklists and content category exclusions, but these tools are imperfect and can be circumvented by sites that change content after being whitelisted. [5]

2. **Economic incentives for misinformation sites**: For every $2.16 in ad revenue flowing to legitimate newspapers, advertisers inadvertently send $1 to misinformation publishers, according to a prior NewsGuard/Comscore analysis of 7,500 websites. The total annual flow industry-wide is estimated at $2.6 billion globally. [7] This revenue subsidizes the operations of sites that actively spread false health information.

3. **Organizational knowledge gaps**: A 2024 Stanford/Carnegie Mellon study published in *Nature* found that two-thirds of advertisers unknowingly bought ads on misinformation sites, and that only 41% of surveyed C-suite decision-makers believed consumers would react negatively to such placements — suggesting brands underestimate the reputational and public-health cost. [10]

### Evidence

- Primary study: Patel et al., *JAMA Network Open*, April 3, 2026, DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2026.5068. [9]
- Covered by CNN, CIDRAP, MediaPost, CTV News, ABC Columbia, and dozens of local affiliates on April 3, 2026. [1][2][3][4]
- Cross-verified by EurekAlert! press release from Yale University. [9]
- Consistent with earlier research: Stanford/CMU (2024, *Nature*) found 66% of advertisers bought ads on misinformation sites. [10]
- HHS spending ($623,000+) confirmed by HHS spokesman Andrew Nixon. [4]
- American Heart Association and Alzheimer's Association confirmed their placements and issued apologies. [8]

| Only fringe or disreputable advertisers fund misinformation sites | False: Pfizer, Eli Lilly, HHS, CDC, American Heart Association, Alzheimer's Association, and others were identified among the advertisers on 11 misinformation sites | FALSE — major, credible institutions are involved |
